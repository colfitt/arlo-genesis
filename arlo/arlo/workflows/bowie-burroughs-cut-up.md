# Workflow: The Bowie / Burroughs Cut-Up

## The premise

Write prose. Cut it into fragments. Rearrange. The juxtapositions that survive are stranger and often more truthful than anything you'd have written linearly. The technique was invented by Brion Gysin in 1959, systematized by William Burroughs through the 1960s, and adopted by Bowie around *Diamond Dogs* (1974), used through *Outside* (1995, via the custom-built Verbasizer software) and as late as *Blackstar* (2016).

For songwriters: a linear lyric reaches for the obvious line. The cut-up sidesteps the reach. The fragments collide; the writer becomes an editor picking the lines that hit.

Expect maybe 5–20% of the output to be usable. The other 80% is noise the technique generates so the gold can surface. If you expect 80% to be usable, the technique will seem broken.

## When ARLO should offer this

- User reports their lyrics feel "obvious" or "too on the nose"
- User says every line is the line they'd expect themselves to write
- User has piles of prose / journal entries about the song's subject and no lyrics yet
- User is writing for a sonically-strange production and the lyric feels too straightforward
- User wants dream-logic, imagistic, non-narrative lyrics

Do NOT offer when:
- User is writing a story-song with narrative through-line (Lucinda Williams / John Prine / Townes school) — the cut-up will destroy the throughline
- User is writing for a specific listener — use `nick-cave-letter-method`
- User is writing a beat-first topline that has to scan rhythmically — cut-up output rarely scans
- User wants direct emotional clarity — cut-up produces obliqueness, not directness

## The steps

1. **Pick a subject.** Even vague is fine. "The dream I had Tuesday." "The year my dad got sick." "What leaving a city felt like."

2. **Write 3–5 paragraphs of prose.** Don't think in songs. Just write. Optional but often better: also gather 1–2 paragraphs of unrelated text — a newspaper page, a passage from a novel, a forum thread. The collision of personal + found is often more productive than personal alone.

3. **Cut into strips of 3–7 words each.** Not single words (produces word-salad). Not whole sentences (preserves the original structure too much). Scissors on paper, or a tool like Verbasizer.com, or ARLO running as the cut-up partner.

4. **Rearrange.** Mix all strips. Draw randomly, or arrange by feel. Lay out a new sequence.

5. **Read aloud.** Slowly. Some collisions sound dead; some hit.

6. **Mark the lines that surprise you.** The ones you would never have written linearly. The ones that make you laugh in recognition. The ones that hold two unrelated images and the friction is the meaning.

7. **Discard the rest.** Most of the output will be noise. This is correct.

8. **Build the song around the marked lines.** Choruses, verses, bridge — let the marked lines suggest structure. Connective tissue (rhymes, transitions, scan-fixes) is yours; the seed is the cut-up's.

9. **Resist sanding.** In revision, the temptation is to smooth strange lines back into expected lyrics. Don't. The strangeness is what the technique produced; sanding destroys it.

## ARLO prompts for each step (verbatim)

**Step 1 (subject):**
> "Pick a subject. Vague is fine. One phrase. What is this lyric circling?"

**Step 2 (write prose):**
> "Write three to five paragraphs of prose about it. Don't think songs yet. Just write what comes. If you want, paste in one or two extra paragraphs from somewhere outside your own writing — a newspaper, a novel, a forum thread. The friction between personal and found often helps."

**Step 3 (cut):**
> "Now cut the prose into strips of three to seven words each. Not single words — that's word salad. Not full sentences — that preserves too much of your original. If you want me to be the cut-up engine, paste in your prose and I'll do the randomization."

**Step 4 (rearrange):**
> "Mix the strips. Lay them out in a new order. You can draw at random or arrange by feel."

**Step 5 (read aloud):**
> "Read the new sequence slowly, out loud. Some collisions are dead. Some hit. You'll feel it."

**Step 6 (mark):**
> "Mark every line that surprises you. Every line you'd never have written on your own. Every line that holds two unrelated images and the friction means something."

**Step 7 (discard):**
> "Discard the rest. Most of the output is noise. The cut-up produced the noise so the gold could surface. You'll likely keep 5–20% — that's correct."

**Step 8 (build):**
> "Now build the song around the marked lines. Which lines feel like chorus? Which feel like verse? Which is the bridge? The connective tissue — rhymes, transitions, scan-fixes — is yours. The strange center is the cut-up's."

**Step 9 (resist sanding):**
> "In revision, when a strange line bothers you and you want to smooth it, stop. The strangeness is the point. If you sand it, you'll have an expected line you could have written without doing any of this."

## When to abandon this workflow

- After 30+ minutes of cutting, nothing has hit — the source prose may be too sterile; restart with more emotionally-charged or more disparate source text
- The user can't bring themselves to use anything strange — they're a directness-first writer; switch to `nick-cave-letter-method` or `aimee-mann-specificity-first`
- The song wants narrative clarity — switch to a linear-draft approach
- The user is using the cut-up to avoid committing to a subject — name it; the cut-up doesn't fix subject-avoidance
- The user has produced a lyric they like but the production is still pulling toward conventional pop — the lyric and production may not be matched; consider whether the song wants the production or the lyric to give
