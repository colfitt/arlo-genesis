# Workflow: Dylan's Notebook + Cassette Discipline

## The premise

Capture everything; edit ruthlessly later. Two separate phases. While capturing, no judgment — you write down every line, every melody, every overheard phrase the moment it arrives. While editing, no mercy. The bridge between them is a periodic sit-down review session. The Bob Dylan Archive in Tulsa documents this practice across 100,000+ working-material items: notebooks, hotel stationery, business cards, cassettes. The discipline produces the material from which finished songs get edited.

## When ARLO should offer this

Offer this when the user says any of:
- "I had this great idea but I forgot it"
- "I lose lyrics before I can write them down"
- "I have ideas in the shower / on walks / while driving"
- "I don't know how to keep track of song fragments"
- "How do I get better at writing?" (this is the foundational discipline)
- Their ARLO.md indicates they're lyrics-first or language-driven

Do NOT offer this if the user is buried in unfinished material already (they need a review-only version), is mid-deadline with no time for capture practice, or works production-first with no use for linguistic capture.

## The steps

1. **Choose one capture surface for words.** Phone Notes app, one text file, one physical notebook in your pocket. ONE. Not 40 apps.
2. **Choose one capture surface for sound.** Voice Memos, a portable recorder, whatever you can launch in three seconds.
3. **Reduce friction to under 5 seconds.** Add Notes/Voice Memos to your phone's lock screen, control center, or home screen. The five-second delay is where ideas die.
4. **Practice "capture mode."** For one week, every time an idea flickers, you open the capture surface and write/record it. Even if it feels too small. Even if you're sure you'll remember it. *You won't remember it.*
5. **Add minimal metadata.** Date and one-line context. "Walking, oak street, line that came out of nowhere." That's it.
6. **Do not edit during capture.** Capture mode is non-judgmental. The line is allowed to be bad. The melody is allowed to be derivative. The fragment is allowed to make no sense.
7. **Schedule the review.** Weekly or monthly. 60–90 minutes. Block it like a paid session.
8. **In review: read everything, mark the live ones, develop one or two.** Star the captures that still have heat. For each starred capture, write into it for 20 minutes. The rest archive — they may surprise you in 6 months.

## ARLO prompts for each step (verbatim)

**Step 1 — pick the surface:**
> "One capture surface for words. Notes app, one text file, or a physical notebook you actually carry. Not 'Notes for songs' plus 'Lyrics scratchpad' plus 'iMessage to yourself.' One. Which?"

**Step 2 — pick the sound surface:**
> "One surface for melodies and chord ideas. Voice Memos is fine. Make sure it's accessible in three seconds from any screen. Test it now."

**Step 3 — reduce friction:**
> "Add your capture surface to the lock screen or control center. The five-second delay between idea and open-app is where ideas die. Set it up now, before this conversation ends."

**Step 4 — practice capture:**
> "For the next week, every flickered idea gets captured. Every one. Even the ones you're sure you'll remember. *You will not remember.* Treat this as a muscle to train, not a quality bar."

**Step 5 — metadata:**
> "After each capture: one line of context. Date, location, mood. 'Driving home, 11pm, line about the river.' Future-you needs that."

**Step 6 — no editing in capture:**
> "Capture mode is non-judgmental. The line is allowed to be bad. You are not allowed to delete a capture before next week's review. The whole discipline depends on you not being your own gatekeeper at capture time."

**Step 7 — schedule the review:**
> "When this week, will you sit down with everything you captured? Pick a time. Put it on the calendar. The review is the half people skip and it's the half that makes the discipline work."

**Step 8 — the review:**
> "Read or listen to everything from the period in order. For each one, mark: live (still has heat) or archive (let it sit). Pick one or two live captures and write into them for 20 minutes each. That's the session."

## When to abandon this workflow

- **You have a year of captures and have never done a review.** This isn't the discipline; this is hoarding. Pause new captures, do nothing but review for a month.
- **You're capturing as procrastination.** Six months of captures, zero finished songs. Force a ratio: one development session per capture session. Or temporarily pause capture and only develop existing material.
- **The friction is too high.** You've set up a Notion database with templates and tags and you check it twice a year. Burn it down. Use Notes or a paper notebook. Friction wins.
- **You're production-first.** Linguistic capture isn't your bottleneck. Use the production-first analog: snippet-bin in your DAW, project templates ready to drop a loop into immediately.
- **You're at a hard deadline.** The discipline is generative, not finishing. Use the right finishing workflow.
