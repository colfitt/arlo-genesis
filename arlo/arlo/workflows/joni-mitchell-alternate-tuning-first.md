# Workflow: Joni Mitchell's Alternate-Tuning-First

## The premise

The tuning is the song. Change the tuning, the chord shapes change. Change the chord shapes, the harmony, melody, and emotion change. Joni Mitchell used 50+ tunings across her catalog and explicitly described tuning as a "strange attractor" — a random element that forces discovery. For guitarists in a writing rut, retuning is the most reliable lateral move available.

## When ARLO should offer this

Offer this when the user says any of:
- "All my songs sound the same"
- "I'm stuck in the same chord shapes"
- "I'm a guitarist and I want to write something different"
- "I want to write something more harmonically interesting but I don't want to learn jazz chords"
- They reference Joni, Nick Drake, Richard Thompson, John Martyn, Andy McKee, or DADGAD-tradition writers
- They've been writing folk/indie/acoustic songs that feel formulaic

Do NOT offer this to non-guitarists (suggest the piano/DAW adaptation), users who need quickly-portable songs for jam contexts, or co-writers working with standard-tuning players.

## The steps

1. **Pick a tuning.** Don't overthink. Start with a documented Joni or DADGAD-tradition tuning: D modal (DADGBD), open G (DGDGBD), the Hejira tuning (BF#C#EF#B), or DADGAD itself.
2. **Tune up. Strum the open strings.** Listen. The open-string chord IS the tuning's identity. Sit with it for two minutes.
3. **Find the first shape.** One finger on one fret. Listen. Move it. Find a shape that produces a sound you want to keep hearing. Don't name the chord.
4. **Find the second shape.** Something that moves naturally from the first shape, with your hand on this guitar today. Move between the two.
5. **Set the tempo and meter by feel.** Strum or fingerpick at the pace the tuning suggests. Don't impose a meter.
6. **Sing nonsense into it.** Improvise vocal melody over the two shapes. Let the open tuning's resonance pull your voice somewhere unfamiliar.
7. **Capture, don't edit.** Record everything. The first usable melodic idea may show up in minute 12.
8. **Notate the tuning and the shapes immediately.** Joni notation (low-note + fret numbers) or photos of your hand on the neck. You will forget tomorrow.

## ARLO prompts for each step (verbatim)

**Step 1 — pick a tuning:**
> "Start with a documented one. DADGAD if you've never been there. Open G (DGDGBD) for the warm folk feel. The Hejira tuning (BF#C#EF#B) for ambiguity and floating. Pick one — don't research, just pick."

**Step 2 — strum and listen:**
> "Tune up. Now strum the open strings. Don't fret anything. Listen for two full minutes. What does this tuning *want*? Drone? Pastoral? Underground?"

**Step 3 — first shape:**
> "One finger on one fret. Strum. Move the finger. Strum again. You're looking for a shape that surprises you — not a shape you recognize. Don't try to name the chord."

**Step 4 — second shape:**
> "Now a second shape, something your hand reaches for naturally on this tuning. Move between the two shapes. Is the movement satisfying? If yes, you have a progression. If no, find a different second shape."

**Step 5 — tempo and meter:**
> "Strum at the speed the tuning wants. Don't force 4/4. Sometimes these tunings want 6/8 or a free-time feel. Trust what your right hand does."

**Step 6 — sing nonsense:**
> "Hit record. Sing into the chords for 10 minutes. Nonsense syllables, fragments, whatever. The open-string resonance will pull your voice somewhere. Follow."

**Step 7 — capture, don't edit:**
> "Don't stop the recorder. The good melody often shows up in minute 12. Keep going past the first instinct to assess."

**Step 8 — notate:**
> "Before you stand up: write down the tuning (low-note + fret numbers, Joni-style) and take a photo of your hand making each shape. You will forget by tomorrow. Future-you will thank you."

## When to abandon this workflow

- **You keep playing standard-tuning shapes in the new tuning.** You're using the tuning cosmetically. Discipline-fail. Either commit to using only shapes that *only work in this tuning*, or abandon and try a different writing approach.
- **You can't find a shape that surprises you after 30 minutes.** This tuning isn't for this song or this day. Try a different tuning, or come back tomorrow.
- **The song wants standard tuning.** Some songs do. If after 2 sessions the tuning is fighting the song, transcribe to standard and finish there.
- **You need to perform the song tonight and the tuning makes that impossible.** A practical constraint outweighs the workflow.
- **You're a non-guitarist.** Adapt the principle: write in a key with all black keys (piano), or pick an unfamiliar synth patch, or write in an unfamiliar meter.
