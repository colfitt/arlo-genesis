# Workflow: Confessional / Performance-First (Indie-Folk)

## The premise

The song exists, fully, before any production touches it. Your job is to **capture a performance**, not build a track. The first emotionally-true take is usually the finished thing — the instinct to "do it properly later" is the enemy of finishing. Build outward sparingly; every addition earns its place against the bare performance. Bon Iver's *For Emma* demos became the record; Lenker cut *songs* AAA to an eight-track in a one-room cabin; Sufjan tracked *Carrie & Lowell* fragments on an iPhone. The thread: protect the capture. (Full rationale and sources: `corpus/songwriting/cluster-workflows/indie-folk-confessional.md`.)

## When ARLO should offer this

Offer when the user says any of:
- "My voice-and-guitar demo sounds better than the produced version."
- "I have a really personal lyric and I don't know how to frame it."
- "I want it to sound intimate, like Bon Iver / Sufjan / Big Thief."
- "I keep over-producing and ruining my songs."
- "My song doesn't have a chorus — is that broken?"

Do NOT offer when the value is in the production (groove-first, beat-first, sound-design-led). For those, route to `arlo/workflows/stems-first-production-first.md` or the electronic / art-rock clusters. Also skip when a genuinely weak song is being protected as a "raw take" to avoid the work of developing it.

## The steps

1. **Get the song whole on one instrument first.** Voice + guitar or voice + piano. No production. It has to move someone in a room before anything else happens.
2. **If words won't come, chase the melody first.** Mumble a wordless melody, record it, harvest words from the syllables you sang. (Vernon's *For Emma* method.)
3. **Make the lyric specific.** Replace any named emotion with the object or moment that caused it. Stack concrete images. (See `corpus/songwriting/lyric/image-stacking-technique.md`.)
4. **Let form follow feeling.** If the material develops continuously, let it be through-composed; don't force a chorus the feeling has moved past. (See `corpus/songwriting/form/through-composed-song-construction.md`.)
5. **Capture the performance early and protect it.** One honest take, one or two mics, your real room. This recording is the master until proven otherwise.
6. **Build outward sparingly — voice first.** When it's thin, add *voice* (a double, a harmony, a soft stack) before you add instruments.
7. **Edit for feeling, not accuracy.** Stop the moment it stops sounding like a person.
8. **Live with it for a few days. Require strong evidence before re-recording.** Most of the time, the first version is the song.

## ARLO prompts for each step (verbatim)

**Step 1 — song whole first:**
> "Play it for me as just voice and one instrument. Does it move you like that? If it doesn't work bare, no production will save it — let's fix the song first."

**Step 2 — melody-first when stuck:**
> "Don't write words yet. Hum or mumble the melody and record 30 seconds on your phone. Now: what words are hiding in the sounds you just sang? Start there."

**Step 3 — specificity:**
> "Find the line that names a feeling. Delete it. What object, place, or exact moment caused that feeling? Put that in instead. The thing, not the word for the thing."

**Step 4 — form follows feeling:**
> "Does this material keep moving forward, or does it circle back to something? If it keeps moving, you might have a through-composed song — that's not broken, that's a choice. End it when the feeling is spent."

**Step 5 — capture and protect:**
> "Set up one mic (or two), hit record, and play it for real. This take is the master now. Set a low bar for 'recorded' and a high bar for 're-recorded.'"

**Step 6 — voice first:**
> "It feels thin? Before you reach for another instrument, try doubling the vocal or stacking a harmony. In this school the voice is the arrangement."

**Step 7 — edit for feeling:**
> "Are you fixing this take for accuracy or for feeling? The breath and the waver are presence. Stop editing the moment it stops sounding like a person."

**Step 8 — resist re-recording:**
> "Sit with it for three days. If you still want to re-record, what specifically is wrong — and can you fix that one thing without redoing the whole take?"

## In Ableton

Concrete moves that translate this school. ARLO links the mechanics rather than re-teaching them.

- **Capture voice + instrument together, near-mono, in your real room.** One or two mics into a couple of tracks; don't over-isolate. The room tone is part of the aesthetic.
- **Use Live as a performance/looper instrument for the capture, not a construction grid.** Track full takes, build harmony stacks live, keep the session lightweight. See `corpus/daw/ableton/timeless/live-as-looper-and-performance-instrument.md`.
- **Comp gently, for feeling, not for surgical perfection.** Take lanes let you assemble the most *alive* performance — the goal is the truest take, not the most accurate one. See `corpus/daw/ableton/editing/comping-in-live-11-plus.md`.
- **Resist warping the performance straight.** Tempo flex, rubato, and the natural push/pull are the feeling. Warp only to fix genuine problems, and preserve the human timing. See `corpus/daw/ableton/editing/warping-discipline.md`.
- **Keep the vocal chain minimal and intimate.** Light compression, gentle EQ, a touch of room/plate — build *voice* outward (doubles, soft stacks) before adding instruments. See `corpus/daw/ableton/patterns/vocal-chains-in-live.md`.
- **Impose a track limit on yourself** (Lenker's eight-track discipline) to force commitment and avoid infinite-layer paralysis.

## When to abandon this workflow

- **The bare performance doesn't move anyone after honest effort.** The song, not the production, is the problem — go back to writing or shelve it.
- **The song's real value is in a groove, beat, or sound-design idea.** Wrong cluster; switch to a production-first workflow.
- **You're using "it's a raw take" to dodge developing weak material.** If a part genuinely needs arranging to become good, arrange it.
- **You've protected a rough capture that actually should be re-cut** — pitch is off, the mic was wrong, the take fights the song. Re-record once, deliberately, then re-apply the high bar.
- **You're stacking endless layers chasing "fullness."** Stop adding; the school finishes by subtraction.
