# Workflow: Oblique Strategies as Stall-Breaker

## The premise

Creative stalls are pattern-locks. The writer has been making the same shape of decision for the last hour and can no longer see other shapes. The Eno/Schmidt deck (1975) offers external, lateral instructions that refuse the local logic of the problem and force a sideways move. The cards aren't wise; they're *interrupting*.

The deck is for emergencies, not daily production. One or two cards per session, at known stuck-points. If you're drawing cards every five minutes, the cards have eaten the work.

## When ARLO should offer this

- User has been on the same section for more than 30 minutes
- User reports making the same kind of choice repeatedly ("what if X… no, Y… no, Z…" where X/Y/Z are all the same shape)
- User has finished the song but can't stop tweaking
- User keeps writing the same chord progression on every demo
- User has a section that almost works but won't commit to identifying what's wrong
- User says they need an external nudge

Do NOT offer when:
- User hasn't started (cards break locks; they don't create starting energy)
- User is in a strict discipline workflow (`tom-cosm-8-bar-loop-cycle`, `spoon-two-demos-a-week`)
- User is in a co-write with someone not in on the practice
- User has already drawn 3+ cards this session (the deck is being abused as procrastination)

## The steps

1. **Name the stuck-point clearly.** Before drawing, the user states out loud (or types) the exact decision they're stuck on. One sentence. "I can't decide whether the bridge should go to the IV or stay on the I." Naming it sharpens what the card will land on.

2. **Pick the mode.** Draw-and-obey: whatever the card says is the next move, no negotiation. Draw-and-consider: the card is a prompt to think differently but doesn't dictate. ARLO default: draw-and-obey for the first card.

3. **Draw one card.** Physical deck preferred. If digital, use obliquestrategies.ca or write the result down on paper before continuing.

4. **Read it out loud.** Slow. Don't skim. Some cards take seconds to interpret in the context of the current stuck-point.

5. **Spend 15 minutes applying it.** Make the move the card dictates (or that it inspires, in consider-mode). Do not redraw during these 15 minutes.

6. **Listen / read what changed.** Is the lock broken? Is the section moving again? If yes, finish the section before drawing again. If no, you may draw a second card.

7. **Cap at two cards per session.** Two cards in, no lock broken → cards aren't the right tool today. Switch workflows.

8. **Log the draw.** Brief note: which card, what stuck-point, did it work. Across many sessions this becomes a map of which cards work for which stuck-points.

## ARLO prompts for each step (verbatim)

**Step 1 (name the stuck-point):**
> "What is the exact decision you're stuck on? One sentence. Don't generalize — get specific. 'I can't decide whether ___' or 'I can't make ___ feel like ___.'"

**Step 2 (pick the mode):**
> "Two modes: draw-and-obey (whatever the card says, you do it) or draw-and-consider (the card is a prompt, you reserve the right to ignore it). I recommend draw-and-obey for the first card — it's the only way to know if the cards actually work. Pick."

**Step 3 (draw):**
> "Draw one card. Don't redraw if you don't like it — that defeats the deck."

**Step 4 (read):**
> "Read the card slowly. Out loud. Let it sit. Some cards take a minute to interpret against your specific stuck-point."

**Step 5 (apply):**
> "Now spend 15 minutes applying the card. Make the move the card dictates (or, in consider-mode, the move it suggests). Don't redraw during these 15 minutes. If the card seems absurd, lean harder into it — the absurdity is sometimes the lock-breaker."

**Step 6 (assess):**
> "Listen / read what changed. Is the section moving again? If yes, keep going — don't draw another card until the section is finished. If no, what specifically didn't shift?"

**Step 7 (cap):**
> "If two cards in and the lock hasn't broken, the cards aren't the right tool today. Try `waits-break-the-demo` or `rubin-reduction-philosophy` instead."

**Step 8 (log):**
> "Write down which card you drew, what stuck-point you applied it to, and whether it worked. Over time this becomes a map of which cards work for you."

## When to abandon this workflow

- Two cards in and no lock has broken
- The user is drawing cards every five minutes (the deck has eaten the work)
- The user is in a co-write and the other person finds the cards disruptive
- The user is rationalizing every card into the original lock ("the card says X but that doesn't apply here")
- The user is finished and using cards as a procrastination ritual — switch to `finishing-work-and-completion` (general corpus) or `spoon-two-demos-a-week`
