# Workflow: Rubin's Reduction Pass

## The premise

Most stuck songs are over-built, not under-built. The essence is buried under additions made for adequacy rather than necessity. Rubin's move is to subtract until something speaks. Saint-Exupéry, via Rubin: "Perfection is finally obtained not when there is no longer anything to add, but when there's no longer anything to take away."

For each element in the song, the question is: if I removed this, would the loss be greater than what's revealed? Most over-built songs fail this test for at least a third of their elements.

## When ARLO should offer this

- User says they keep adding things and the song keeps getting worse
- User says they can't tell what the song is about anymore
- User has 12+ tracks open and the demo feels cluttered
- User's chorus isn't landing because there's too much in it
- User is in mix-procrastination, stacking plugins to avoid finishing
- User describes the song as "almost there" and won't ship

Do NOT offer when:
- Song is not yet structurally complete (subtract from what?)
- Song feels under-realized rather than over-built (different problem — add, don't subtract)
- User is early in exploration (use `david-lynch-catching-big-fish` instead)
- Genre identity depends on density (some shoegaze, some maximalist pop)

## The steps

1. **State what the song is about.** One sentence. Out loud or on paper. The Rubin question every subtraction has to pass: does this element serve what the song is about?

2. **Mute every track.** Save a backup first.

3. **Unmute the kick and the lead vocal.** Play the song through. Note: even this stripped, the song's spine is audible.

4. **Add one track back at a time.** For each: does it make the song more itself? "Yes" = leave it in. "Neutral" = mute it. "No" = mute it. The neutral verdict is the trap — neutral usually means subtract.

5. **By the end of the pass, expect 30–60% of original tracks still active.** If you're at 90%, you cheated; you didn't actually judge. Run it again.

6. **Bounce and listen elsewhere.** Phone, car, headphones, kitchen speaker. The subtracted version often sounds *bigger* than the original.

7. **Apply the same move to the lyric.** Read every line: if removed, does the song lose something? If no, cut. Aim for a 20% cut on the first pass.

8. **Sit with it 24 hours.** Then either ship the reduced version, or note specifically what the reduction is missing and add back only that.

## ARLO prompts for each step (verbatim)

**Step 1 (state the song):**
> "In one sentence, what is this song about? Not the genre, not the style — what does it say or feel? Every cut has to be judged against this sentence."

**Step 2 (backup + mute):**
> "Save a copy of the current session first. Then mute every track. Yes, every track. We're going to add back one at a time, and each one has to earn its place."

**Step 3 (start with spine):**
> "Unmute the kick and the lead vocal. Play through. The song's spine is here. Notice what it's like with this much room."

**Step 4 (add back, judge):**
> "Add one track back. Does it make the song more itself, or is it just there? 'Yes, more itself' — keep it. 'Neutral, doesn't hurt' — mute it. The neutral verdict is the trap; neutral usually means subtract."

**Step 5 (expect a real cut):**
> "If you're keeping 90% of the tracks, you're being polite, not honest. Real subtraction usually leaves 30–60% of the original. Run the pass again if you cheated."

**Step 6 (bounce and listen):**
> "Bounce the reduced version. Listen on a phone speaker, then in the car, then on headphones. Often the reduced version sounds bigger than the original. The remaining elements have room to be heard."

**Step 7 (lyric subtraction):**
> "Same move on the lyric. Read every line slowly. If removed, would the song lose something essential? If no, cut. Aim for a 20% cut on the first pass."

**Step 8 (24-hour sit):**
> "Sit with the reduced version for 24 hours. Don't decide tonight. If tomorrow it still feels right, ship. If something specific is missing, add back only that — not the whole layer back."

## When to abandon this workflow

- Two passes in and the user can't bring themselves to mute anything — they may not be ready to ship; switch to `finishing-work-and-completion` (general corpus)
- The reduced version feels genuinely worse (not just thinner) — the song may need additions, not subtractions; the diagnosis was wrong
- The genre identity required the density that's been removed — re-add intentionally and document why
- The user is using subtraction as procrastination — name it; subtraction is for shipping, not for further tweaking
- The lyric is the issue, not the production — switch to a lyric workflow (`aimee-mann-specificity-first`, `nick-cave-letter-method`)
