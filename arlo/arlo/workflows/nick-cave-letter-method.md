# Workflow: Nick Cave's Letter Method

## The premise

When a lyric keeps coming out vague or universal, the writer has lost contact with the actual person or moment that originated it. Writing the song *to* a specific recipient — as an unsent letter — restores the contact. The song then hides inside the letter, and the writer's job is to extract it.

This is a Cave-adjacent synthesis. Cave has not publicly described this exact recipe, but each constituent move is documented: he treats songwriting as a daily desk job, his songs typically emerge from a single concrete image, and his ongoing Red Hand Files project is itself a letter discipline. ARLO bundles the three into one workflow.

## When ARLO should offer this

- The user says their lyric "sounds like every other song"
- The user is writing about grief, love, anger, or longing and the drafts keep landing on platitudes
- The user has a real person or real event in mind but won't or can't name them in the song
- The user has a strong feeling and no song yet
- The user is stuck on the second verse and the first verse is generic

Do NOT offer this when:
- The user is working beat-first or production-first (offer beat-first-topline-workflow instead)
- The user explicitly wants a non-personal song (storytelling, character work, world-building)
- The user is in a sonic-exploration mode without a subject

## The steps

1. **Pick the recipient.** A specific real person. Dead, alive, estranged, present — doesn't matter. Has to be one person, named in the writer's head. If the user can't pick, this workflow isn't the right one yet.

2. **Pick the recipient mode.** Letter the recipient will never see, or letter they could in theory read? The choice changes what the writer can say. Decide before writing.

3. **Set a timer for 30–60 minutes.** Open a fresh page. Date in the corner. "Dear ___" at the top. Handwriting is preferred over typing if possible; the slower hand resists self-editing.

4. **Write the letter — prose, not lyrics.** Address the recipient directly. Present tense if they're alive, past tense if they're not. No rhymes, no line breaks for songs, no choruses. Sensory specifics — what the room smelled like, what they said, the color of the jacket. Two prohibitions: no abstractions (*love, loss, hope*) and no universals (*we, everybody, somebody*).

5. **Walk away.** Print or save. Sleep on it if you can. Minimum a few hours.

6. **Read the letter as a stranger.** Mark the most concrete passages. The chorus often appears as the line the writer keeps almost-saying and pulling back from. The verses are the surrounding scenes. The bridge is often the paragraph where the writer admits something they hadn't planned to admit.

7. **Extract.** Reshape marked passages into meter and rhyme. Most of the letter will be cut. That's correct — the letter was the contact-restoration tool, not the finished artifact.

8. **In revision, replace sanded-down lines with letter verbatim.** When a line feels too pop-polished, swap in the original prose sentence. The pop will improve. The song will live.

9. **Archive the letter.** Keep it in a folder. It's the record of what the song was trying to do, useful for the next song too.

## ARLO prompts for each step (verbatim)

**Step 1 (pick recipient):**
> "Who is this song actually for? Name one specific person — not a type, not an audience. Dead, alive, estranged, present — doesn't matter. The recipient has to exist."

**Step 2 (pick mode):**
> "Is this a letter they will never read, or one they could? The unreachable letter lets you say anything. The reachable letter forces consequence. Pick before you start."

**Step 3 (set the page):**
> "Open a new page. Write the date in the corner. Write 'Dear [their name]' at the top. Set a timer — thirty to sixty minutes. We are not writing a song yet. We are writing a letter."

**Step 4 (the letter itself):**
> "Tell them what happened. Use the senses — what the room smelled like, what they were wearing, what they said. No abstractions: cut every instance of 'love,' 'loss,' 'hope.' No universals: cut every 'we,' 'everybody,' 'somebody.' If a sentence could be about anyone, throw it out. Keep going until the timer ends."

**Step 5 (walk away):**
> "Stop. Save the letter. Don't read it until tomorrow, or at minimum after dinner. The distance is part of the method."

**Step 6 (read as stranger):**
> "Read the letter slowly. Mark every sentence that is so specific only this recipient would recognize it. Find the line you keep almost-saying — the one you build up to and then back away from. That line is often the chorus. Find the paragraph where you admit something you hadn't planned to admit. That's often the bridge."

**Step 7 (extract):**
> "Take the marked passages and shape them into verses, a chorus, a bridge. You will cut most of the letter. That is correct. The letter did its job."

**Step 8 (resist sanding):**
> "When a line feels too smooth, too pop, too 'song-like,' swap it back to the verbatim sentence from the letter. The unpolished line is almost always better than the polished one."

**Step 9 (archive):**
> "Save the letter somewhere you can find it. It's not waste — it's process you'll reuse on the next song."

## When to abandon this workflow

- After 45 minutes the page is still blank and the recipient choice was correct — try `david-lynch-catching-big-fish` instead; the subject may not be ready yet
- The user can't choose a recipient — try `aimee-mann-specificity-first` (start from an image, not a person)
- The user is on a producer-led track with a beat already — switch to `beat-first-topline-workflow`
- The letter becomes a journal entry that's no longer addressed to the recipient — re-anchor with "Dear ___" or stop
- The extracted song is structurally a song but emotionally dead — the letter wasn't honest enough; the workflow can be retried with a different recipient or different mode
