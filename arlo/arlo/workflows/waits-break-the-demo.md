# Workflow: Break the Demo

## The premise

The polish is hiding the song. When a demo sounds correct but emotionally inert, the problem usually isn't that the mix needs better EQ — the problem is that the polish has smoothed away the friction the song needs.

Tom Waits and Kathleen Brennan's solution since 1983: replace conventional elements with broken or unconventional ones, route through wrong gear, record in wrong places, capture amateur performances of professionals. The goal isn't lo-fi for its own sake. It's *juxtaposition*: high-fidelity-vs-low, clean-vs-broken, intentional-vs-accidental. The friction is the texture.

## When ARLO should offer this

- User has a finished, polished demo they should love but don't
- User keeps trying to fix the mix and nothing helps
- User's demos all sound the same — defaults have hardened into a polish they can't see
- User describes their song as "professional but boring"
- User is producing indie / alt-folk / art-pop / bedroom electronic where friction is genre-coherent
- User says some version of "this should sound more like a Tom Waits song" or "I want this to feel less safe"

Do NOT offer when:
- Genre demands polish (Top 40 pop, K-pop, mainstream country, polished R&B)
- Song's structure isn't finished yet — this is a production-stage move
- User is making a record for a label or sync that wants conventional polish
- Vocal is the only thing the song has — proceed with extreme caution

## The steps

1. **Save the polished demo.** Don't overwrite. The workflow may not work and you'll want the original.

2. **Identify the most "professional" element.** The cleanest drum loop, the most pop-correct synth pad, the doubled vocal. The element that sounds most like the production decade you're trying to escape.

3. **Pick a way to break it.** Options: route through a broken plugin (Decapitator at 24, RC-20 maxed, a free amp sim used wrong); re-record through a phone speaker into a room mic; replace the sample with something you found around the house (knife on glass, dropped book, fridge hum); play it through a megaphone; record it outdoors.

4. **A/B against the original.** Most of the time the broken version is better — not because broken is better, but because broken has texture and polished didn't.

5. **Repeat with the next element.** By the third or fourth break, the song is no longer the demo. It might be a song.

6. **Preserve juxtaposition.** Keep one or two elements clean — usually the vocal, sometimes a single melodic instrument. Fully-broken has no friction; everything's at the same level of grit. Contrast is the move.

7. **Stop and listen elsewhere.** Phone, car, kitchen. The juxtaposition usually translates well.

8. **Decide what stays.** Not everything you broke needs to survive. Some breaks were experiments. Keep what makes the song more itself; revert the rest.

## ARLO prompts for each step (verbatim)

**Step 1 (preserve original):**
> "Save the polished demo as a separate bounce or session. This workflow has a real chance of not working, and we want to be able to go back."

**Step 2 (identify the most professional element):**
> "What is the cleanest, most pop-correct element in this demo? The one that sounds most like a modern production trend. That's the candidate for breaking first."

**Step 3 (pick the break):**
> "Pick one way to break it. Through a broken plugin pushed past spec. Re-recorded through a phone speaker. Replaced with a found-sound from around the house. Megaphone. Outdoors. Pick one and commit."

**Step 4 (A/B):**
> "Bounce both versions and listen to them back to back, level-matched. Which has more texture? Which makes you want to keep listening?"

**Step 5 (next element):**
> "Now do the same to the next most-professional element. By the third break, the song should no longer feel like the original demo. If it still does, you're breaking too gently."

**Step 6 (preserve juxtaposition):**
> "Keep one or two elements clean. Usually the lead vocal stays clean while everything around it gets broken. Or the reverse — broken vocal, clean instrumental world. Fully-broken loses the contrast."

**Step 7 (listen elsewhere):**
> "Stop the session. Listen on phone, in the car, on headphones. Does the juxtaposition translate? Does the broken version sound more alive than the polished one?"

**Step 8 (decide what stays):**
> "Walk through each broken element. Which ones made the song more itself? Which were just noise? Keep the wins; revert the rest. You're aiming for break-with-purpose, not break-for-its-own-sake."

## When to abandon this workflow

- The song can't survive any breaking — diagnose the structure; the polish was holding it together because the bones aren't there. Stop producing; go back to writing.
- The user can't bring themselves to commit to a break — they're a polish-first writer; switch to `rubin-reduction-philosophy` instead (cleaner-but-fewer rather than dirtier-but-same-count)
- The genre actively penalizes friction — don't fight the genre; ship the polished version
- The vocal is the song's only asset — be very careful about breaking it; consider clean-vocal-broken-world variant
- The user is using "break the demo" as procrastination from finishing — name it; sabotage isn't finishing
- Two passes in, nothing has improved — the diagnosis was wrong; the song doesn't have hidden life under the polish
