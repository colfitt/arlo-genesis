# Workflow: Catching the Big Fish

## The premise

Ideas are fish. Shallow water has small fish; depth has big ones. The job of the writer is to (a) build a daily practice that lets the mind settle deep enough to surface big fish, and (b) catch them ruthlessly when they arrive. Lynch's specific practice is Transcendental Meditation; the generalizable practice is *daily defended interior time + immediate capture discipline*.

This is not a fix-tonight workflow. It's a bed for ideas to land in. Expect the first useful catch in weeks, not minutes.

## When ARLO should offer this

- User says they feel creatively dry, blocked, or barren
- User reports their last several drafts all feel "thin" or "small"
- User hasn't finished a song they liked in a month or more, and has been trying
- User is between projects and wants the next thing to surface organically
- User has been deep in editing/production for weeks and the writing well is dry

Do NOT offer when:
- User has a near-term deadline (this isn't a sprint tool)
- User has plenty of material and needs structure or finishing (offer `rubin-reduction-philosophy` or `spoon-two-demos-a-week`)
- User is procrastinating finishing an in-progress song by starting a meditation practice (name it gently)
- User wants to write a topline over a beat tonight (offer `beat-first-topline-workflow`)

## The steps

1. **Pick the practice.** Lynch's specific path is TM (a paid technique taught by a certified teacher). The generalizable version is 20 minutes of unguided sitting, twice daily, eyes closed, comfortable seated position, no podcast or guided audio. Pick one; commit.

2. **Pick the daily times.** Morning is most common for the first session, late afternoon or early evening for the second. Lynch has held to twice-daily for decades. The point is the practice is non-negotiable on its scheduled times.

3. **Set up the capture system.** A pocket notebook, a notes-app shortcut on the phone home screen, or a voice-memo folder labeled `fragments`. The capture tool must be within arm's reach during waking hours. Pick one tool; don't split across many.

4. **Begin the daily practice.** Sit for 20 minutes, twice daily, for a minimum of two weeks before judging. During the sit, do not chase ideas. If a fragment surfaces, note it (one word, internally), return to the practice, capture it afterward.

5. **Capture fragments the moment they arrive — anywhere, any time.** Walking, dish-washing, driving, talking on the phone. The discipline is *immediate* and *verbatim* — don't elaborate, don't develop, just preserve the fish exactly as it arrived. A fish is a phrase, a sonic image, a title, a character, a juxtaposition, a tempo-and-feeling — not a finished song.

6. **Weekly review pass.** Once a week, sit with the fragment notebook and read everything. Mark the fragments that still spark. The ones that spark are the songs that want to be written next.

7. **Develop one fragment per week into a song.** Cross-hand this to a separate workflow once you pick the fragment — `nick-cave-letter-method` if it's a person/feeling, `eno-oblique-strategies` if it's a sonic seed, `aimee-mann-specificity-first` if it's an image.

8. **Archive the cold ones, don't delete them.** Some fragments warm up months later. The notebook is permanent.

## ARLO prompts for each step (verbatim)

**Step 1 (pick the practice):**
> "Lynch's specific practice is TM — twenty minutes, twice a day, taught by a certified teacher. The generalizable version is unguided sitting, twenty minutes twice a day, eyes closed. Pick one. Don't blend half-versions."

**Step 2 (schedule):**
> "What time will you sit, morning and evening? Pick specific times. Lynch has held twice-daily for fifty years; the discipline is the schedule, not the willingness."

**Step 3 (capture system):**
> "Pick one tool: a pocket notebook, a notes-app shortcut, or a voice-memo folder. Not all three. The tool has to be within arm's reach all day. Where will yours live?"

**Step 4 (begin):**
> "Sit for twenty minutes, eyes closed, comfortable. Don't chase ideas. If a fragment surfaces, note it briefly to yourself and return to the practice. Capture it afterward. Do this twice a day for at least two weeks before judging the practice."

**Step 5 (the catch):**
> "When a fish surfaces — during the sit, on a walk, doing dishes — write it down immediately. Verbatim, the way it arrived. Don't develop it yet. Don't extract a song from it yet. The fish is the fish. Get it on the page before it's gone."

**Step 6 (weekly review):**
> "Once a week, read the entire fragment notebook from the top. Mark the fragments that still spark. Most won't. The ones that do are the next songs."

**Step 7 (development handoff):**
> "Pick one sparking fragment for this week. Is it a person or feeling? Use the Cave letter method. Is it a sonic seed? Use Oblique Strategies. Is it a concrete image? Use the Aimee Mann excavation."

**Step 8 (archive):**
> "Don't delete the cold fragments. Move them to an 'archive' section of the same notebook. They warm up months later more often than you'd guess."

## When to abandon this workflow

- User has tried the practice consistently for four weeks with no surfacing fragments and the daily sit feels actively bad — try `dylan-notebook-cassette-discipline` instead (different capture entry point, no meditation requirement)
- User has a deadline this week — switch to `spoon-two-demos-a-week` or `aimee-mann-specificity-first`
- User catches fragments but never develops any of them — the workflow is succeeding; move to a development workflow (Cave / Mann / Eno)
- User is using meditation as procrastination from finishing a song that needs to ship — switch to `rubin-reduction-philosophy` or `waits-break-the-demo`
- The practice produces psychological distress (rare, but possible) — stop, the workflow is not appropriate
