# Workflow: Spoon's Demo-as-Writing Discipline

## The premise

Demoing IS the writing. Britt Daniel says he "puts together the words as I'm figuring out the one element of the song" — the demo isn't a draft, it's the workshop. Commit to a sustainable weekly cadence of finished demos and treat the demo as the unit of completion. The discipline of the deadline beats the quest for the masterpiece. The "two demos a week" framing is a useful starting target; the documented practice is high-volume, constant demoing, with the album emerging from selection.

## When ARLO should offer this

Offer this when the user says any of:
- "I haven't finished a song in [weeks / months]"
- "I keep starting things and not finishing them"
- "I have 40 unfinished sketches"
- "I'm a perfectionist about my demos"
- "I need to force myself to actually finish stuff"
- "I want to write more"

Do NOT offer this when the user has a specific finished song that's stalled (use a stall playbook instead), is mid-production on an album with deadline pressure, or is co-writing.

## The steps

1. **Pick the cadence.** Realistic for your week. Default suggestion: 2 demos per week. Lower (1/week) if life is full. Higher only if you've sustained 2/week for a month.
2. **Define your demo bar.** Uncomfortably low. The bar is *all sections present, vocal on every section (even placeholder lyrics), bounces to one audio file you could play for a friend with apologies only for rough, not missing*.
3. **Block the time.** Demo sessions on the calendar. Treat them as immovable.
4. **Single-session commitment.** Each demo gets one session. You finish in the session or you accept the demo as it stands.
5. **Recording-as-writing.** Lyric, melody, arrangement, and production decisions happen together. You're not writing a song then producing it — the recording IS the writing.
6. **Hit save and walk away.** When the demo bounces to one file, you're done. No tweaking. Open the next idea.
7. **Weekly review.** End of week, 30 minutes. Listen to every demo from the week in order. Mark each: *develop further*, *archive*, *kill*.
8. **Process the marks.** Killed demos go to a graveyard folder. Archived demos sit. Develop demos get one more session next week; if they don't earn it, they archive.

## ARLO prompts for each step (verbatim)

**Step 1 — pick the cadence:**
> "Honest question: how many sessions per week can you realistically protect? Pick a demo number you can hit. Two is a good default. One is fine if life is full. The discipline only works if the cadence is real."

**Step 2 — define the bar:**
> "Your demo bar is uncomfortably low. All sections present, vocal on every section, bounces to one file. Not mixed. Not in tune to the cent. Not on real drums. Are you willing to ship at that bar?"

**Step 3 — block the time:**
> "Put your demo sessions on the calendar now. Two of them. Treat them like you'd treat a paid gig — they don't move."

**Step 4 — single-session commitment:**
> "Today's session is one demo. You finish in this session or you ship what you have. No 'I'll come back to this Tuesday.' What's the seed — chord, lyric, drum loop, image?"

**Step 5 — recording-as-writing:**
> "Don't write the song and then record it. Write *as* you record. The patch choice is a songwriting choice. The drum sound is a songwriting choice. Commit early."

**Step 6 — ship and walk away:**
> "Bounce it. One file. Move it to your 'this week' folder. Close the project. Open the next idea or shut down. Do not tweak."

**Step 7 — weekly review:**
> "End of week. Play every demo from the week in order, once each. For each, you say: develop, archive, or kill. No re-listening, no agonizing — first instinct."

**Step 8 — process the marks:**
> "Kills go to the graveyard folder. Archives sit. Develops get one more session next week — and if they don't earn it then, they archive. You're allowed at most three active 'develop' songs at once."

## When to abandon this workflow

- **You've missed the cadence four weeks in a row.** The number is wrong, not the discipline. Drop to 1/week before quitting.
- **Your demos are creeping toward album quality.** The bar drifted up. Reset the bar low or abandon the discipline.
- **You have 12 simultaneous "develop" songs and nothing's finishing.** The review pass isn't ruthless enough. Force-archive everything that hasn't moved in 3 weeks.
- **You're mid-album with deadline-quality demands.** This is a generative discipline, not a finishing-an-album discipline. Switch to a session-methodology approach (general corpus).
- **A specific song has stalled.** Don't use this workflow to push through a stall — use the right stall playbook (B1–B8).
