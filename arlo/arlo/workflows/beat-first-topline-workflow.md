# Workflow: Beat-First / Topline-Over-Beat

## The premise

The track exists first. The topline (melody + lyric) is written over it. The pocket of the track dictates phrase length, syllable count, and where the vocal can sit. This is the dominant workflow in hip-hop since its inception and in pop since the Cheiron / Max Martin era of the 1990s.

For singer-songwriters used to lyric-and-music-together, this feels backwards. It isn't. It's a separate discipline with its own muscle.

## When ARLO should offer this

- User has a beat, loop, or instrumental sketch and wants a song over it
- User is a producer who's made a track and doesn't know how to write the topline
- User is working remotely with a producer or beat-maker
- User writes better with constraints — the open page is hostile, the track is friendly
- User is in a genre where beat-first is norm: hip-hop, modern pop, R&B, electronic, dance, Latin
- User has been forcing lyric-first writing and producing the same kind of song

Do NOT offer when:
- User has no track yet (use `david-lynch-catching-big-fish` or `tom-cosm-8-bar-loop-cycle` to build to one)
- User is in fingerpicking singer-songwriter mode where lyric/melody/guitar develop together
- User is writing a story-song with narrative arc as the spine
- The track is uninspiring — no topline saves a bad track; get a different one

## The steps

1. **Listen 5–10 times without writing.** Let the pocket land. Notice the snare, the chord changes, the empty space, where vocal energy belongs. Do not start writing yet — premature writing fights the pocket.

2. **Identify the empty space.** Where in the loop is the production sparse? That's where the vocal lives. Where is the production dense? Stay out of those moments unless you're playing call-and-response.

3. **Mumble before writing.** Hum and grunt over the track. Vocal melody on nonsense syllables. The melody has to fit the pocket; if you start with words you'll fight it.

4. **Find the chorus first if you can.** The hook is the song's identity. Once the chorus is real, verses are written to lead into it.

5. **Mirror meter line-to-line.** If verse line one is 8 syllables, verse line two is 8 syllables. Verse 2 should match verse 1's syllable shape. Max Martin's discipline: changing one syllable changes the melody.

6. **Land strong words on strong beats.** Mark the downbeats; the accented words go on them.

7. **Plant hook previews.** A fragment of the chorus melody or lyric appearing in the verse or pre-chorus makes the chorus feel inevitable when it arrives.

8. **Engineer the climax on the title.** Chorus's highest note typically lands on the title or key phrase. If it doesn't, consider whether the title is doing its job.

9. **Demo on scratch vocal.** Get it down. Listen back. Edit. Repeat until the topline sounds inevitable.

## ARLO prompts for each step (verbatim)

**Step 1 (just listen):**
> "Play the track at least 5–10 times before writing anything. Notice the pocket — where the snare hits, where the chord changes, where the empty space is. Don't open a lyric document yet. The first job is to hear what the track is asking for."

**Step 2 (find empty space):**
> "Where in the loop is the production sparse? Those are the moments where the vocal naturally goes. Where is it dense? Stay out of those, unless you're doing call-and-response. The track's emptiness is the writer's room."

**Step 3 (mumble first):**
> "Start humming and grunting over the track. Nonsense syllables. Don't write words yet. The melody has to fit the pocket — words written first will fight it."

**Step 4 (chorus first):**
> "Try to find the chorus first. The hook is the song's identity. Once the chorus is real, the verses get written backwards from it."

**Step 5 (mirror meter):**
> "If line one of the verse is 8 syllables, make line two 8 syllables. When you get to verse 2, match verse 1's syllable count line by line. Max Martin treats a one-syllable change as a different melody entirely. Discipline."

**Step 6 (strong-beat landing):**
> "Mark the downbeats of the bar. Accented words — the ones you stress when speaking — go on the downbeats. If the strong word lands on a weak beat, the line will feel wrong even if the listener can't say why."

**Step 7 (hook previews):**
> "Plant a fragment of the chorus melody or lyric somewhere in the verse or pre-chorus. When the chorus arrives, it will feel inevitable instead of arbitrary."

**Step 8 (chorus climax):**
> "The chorus's highest note should land on the title or the key phrase. If it doesn't, ask why. Either the title isn't in the right place, or the melody isn't built around the title."

**Step 9 (demo):**
> "Track a scratch vocal. Listen back. Note what doesn't fit the pocket; rewrite those lines. Repeat until the topline sounds inevitable. Inevitability is the test."

## When to abandon this workflow

- The track itself doesn't inspire after 10 listens — get a different track
- The user keeps writing lyric-first and the meter never matches the pocket — switch the order; mumble melody, words after
- The user is a hardcore singer-songwriter and the workflow feels actively bad — they may be better off recording their song first and matching production to the existing topline (inverse of this workflow)
- The track has too many production elements competing for vocal space — talk to the producer about subtracting (cross to `rubin-reduction-philosophy`)
- The user produces a topline they like but it's not landing emotionally — the song may want lyric-first specificity; switch to `aimee-mann-specificity-first` for the lyric revision pass after the topline structure is set
