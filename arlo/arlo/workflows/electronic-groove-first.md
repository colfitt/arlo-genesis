# Workflow: Groove-First / Arrangement-Driven (Electronic Method)

## The premise

Build an eight-bar world that is undeniable, then find the song inside the arrangement. The rhythm section is the hook — you do not need a topline to know whether you have a song. Daft Punk start with the drums and let the jam become "the canvas for the entire record." LCD Soundsystem build by patient accumulation, so the arrangement delivers the arc a chorus would carry elsewhere. The Postal Service wrote toplines *to* finished instrumentals. The writing is largely arrangement: what enters, what drops out, how long the listener waits. Full school: `corpus/songwriting/cluster-workflows/electronic-groove-first.md`.

## When ARLO should offer this

Offer when the user says any of:
- "I can make beats but I can't write the melody/lyrics"
- "I have a hundred loops and nothing finished"
- "I keep writing the same lyric-first song and want a different result"
- "I'm working in Session view / clips and don't know how to turn it into a track"
- They're in a groove-friendly genre (house, disco-not-disco, synth-pop, indie-electronic, dance)

Do NOT offer when the user already has a lyric or melody driving the song, is writing a narrative story-song, or is on an acoustic instrument with no loop. Those want a lyric-first or performance-first path.

## The steps

1. **Foundation first — drums, then bass.** Get a groove moving before any melody. Interlock kick and bass rhythmically and spectrally.
2. **Build the eight-bar world.** Add harmony, one top element with a clear rhythmic shape, one texture. Stay in the loop.
3. **Apply the undeniable test.** Loop it two minutes. If a stranger sent it, would you keep listening? The groove alone should pass.
4. **Fix the loop, not the arrangement.** Subtract the laziest element or swap one sound. No risers. Commit sounds early.
5. **Decide if a topline is wanted.** If yes, write vocals to the loop's empty spaces — mumble melody first, words after.
6. **Find the song in the arrangement.** Minus-one: intro = loop minus drums/chords; verse = minus lead; chorus = full; breakdown = minus drums.
7. **Hold the repetition.** One change — a hat pattern, a filter open, a dropped element — marks a section. Don't change too much.
8. **Commit and finish.** Bounce, listen elsewhere, ship.

## ARLO prompts for each step (verbatim)

**Step 1 — foundation first:**
> "Start with the rhythm section. Get a kick and groove moving before any melody. Does the bass interlock with the kick — one owns the sub, the other the punch? That's your foundation, the way Daft Punk's drums became 'the canvas for the entire record.'"

**Step 2 — eight-bar world:**
> "Build one eight-bar loop: drums, bass, harmony, one top element with a clear rhythmic shape, one texture. Stay in the loop — don't open the arrangement yet."

**Step 3 — undeniable test:**
> "Loop it two minutes. If a stranger sent you this, would you keep listening, or skip? Be brutal. The groove alone should make you move — you don't need a vocal to know it's a song."

**Step 4 — fix the loop:**
> "Not there? Don't add a riser. Subtract the laziest element or swap one sound. And stop re-choosing the kit — pick the kick, tune it, move on. The loop can't get undeniable while the sounds are a question."

**Step 5 — topline (optional):**
> "Want vocals? Write them to the loop's empty spaces. Mumble the melody on nonsense syllables first so it fits the pocket; words come after. That's how Gibbard wrote over Tamborello's skeletons — the track came first."

**Step 6 — find the song:**
> "Copy the loop across the whole timeline. Now subtract to make sections: intro is the loop minus drums and chords, verse is minus the lead, chorus is full, breakdown is minus drums. The contrast is what's missing, not new material."

**Step 7 — hold repetition:**
> "Hold the loop. Let one change — a new hat pattern, a filter opening, one element dropping out — mark each section. If you're tempted to change everything, the loop probably wasn't strong enough; go back to step 4."

**Step 8 — finish:**
> "Bounce it, listen on your phone or in the car, fix the two things that bug you, and ship. Murphy's whole thing was getting past the fear and finishing. Done beats perfect."

## In Ableton

Maps cleanly onto Live. These corpus files own the mechanics — do not re-derive them:

- **Build the loop in Session view; arrange in Arrangement.** The loop phase is Session-view-only. See `corpus/daw/ableton/workflows/session-vs-arrangement-view.md` for the boundary and `corpus/daw/ableton/timeless/eight-bars-first-discipline.md` for the undeniable-loop discipline.
- **Foundation first via the Drum Rack.** Build the kick/percussion interlock on a multizone Drum Rack — see `corpus/daw/ableton/timeless/drum-rack-as-multizone-instrument.md`.
- **Make the groove feel human.** Apply or extract swing rather than leaving hats on the grid — see `corpus/daw/ableton/timeless/groove-pool-and-extracted-grooves.md`.
- **Lock kick and bass with sidechain.** The classic duck (or the Envelope Follower for finer control) carves the low end — see `corpus/daw/ableton/patterns/sidechain-ducking-classic-vs-modulator.md`.
- **Audition variations with clip launching.** A/B lead patches, drum kits, and chord voicings via scenes and follow actions before committing — see `corpus/daw/ableton/workflows/clip-launching-and-follow-actions.md`.
- **Arrange by minus-one.** Drag the undeniable loop into Arrangement, duplicate across sections, and delete elements per section to make the form.

## When to abandon this workflow

- **Three-plus sessions without the loop improving** — archive it, seed a new one.
- **No variation passes the undeniable test** — the seed isn't strong enough; strip to one element and restart.
- **A lyric or melody wants to drive the song** — switch to a lyric-first or performance-first path.
- **Broke into arrangement and stalled on the topline** — hand off to `beat-first-topline-workflow`.
- **The track competes with itself for space** — subtract before you arrange.
