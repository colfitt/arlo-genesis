# Workflow: Sufjan Stevens' Research-Then-Write

## The premise

Deep, structured non-songwriting research produces specifics — names, dates, images, juxtapositions — that direct-write-from-self can't invent. Sufjan Stevens read Sandburg and Bellow, studied immigration records, traveled, and interviewed Illinois friends for most of 2004 before he wrote *Illinois*. The same principle in inverted form ran *Carrie & Lowell* — internal "research" into memory and imagined possibilities. Specificity is the path to universality; research is one way to produce specificity.

## When ARLO should offer this

Offer this when the user says:
- "I want to write an album about [a place / a person / a historical event / a body of work]"
- "I have a topic but no songs yet"
- "I want my lyrics to feel less generic / more specific"
- "I want to write about something bigger than my own life"
- They mention concept albums or named research-grounded writers (Sufjan, Joanna Newsom, Andrew Bird, Phoebe Bridgers)
- They have time (this is a slow workflow)

Do NOT offer this for tight deadlines, immediate-feeling pieces, or writers who are prone to research-as-procrastination without strong self-discipline.

## The steps

1. **Pick the subject.** One sentence. Specific. "An album about my hometown's railroad history" or "Songs from the perspectives of women in my grandmother's village." Not "loss" or "the city."
2. **Set the research period.** Default: 6–8 weeks. Put a deadline on the calendar. The deadline is *when songwriting becomes the primary activity*, not when research ends.
3. **Cap daily research at 60 minutes.** Force the rest of the day to be living or other writing.
4. **Keep one capture file** (cross-link Dylan notebook discipline). Everything goes in: quotes, images, names, dates, fragments, anecdotes.
5. **Read widely in the subject.** Literature by/about, history, primary documents, oral histories, your own memory if applicable. Take notes; don't write songs yet.
6. **Make field contact.** Trips, interviews, conversations with people who lived it. Notes only.
7. **At week 2, start drafting weekly.** One rough song-draft per week minimum, even if bad. Drafts surface what research you need next.
8. **At the research deadline, flip the ratio.** Songwriting becomes primary; research continues only when a specific song demands a specific fact.
9. **Weave personal into research.** Every song needs a *you* — implicit or explicit — even when the foreground is documentary. Research is the texture, not the theme.

## ARLO prompts for each step (verbatim)

**Step 1 — pick the subject:**
> "One sentence: what is this album about? If you can't name it in 12 words, pick something narrower."

**Step 2 — set the deadline:**
> "How long do you have? Block a 6–8 week research period on your calendar. The deadline is when songwriting becomes the primary activity. Research doesn't stop; its rank changes."

**Step 3 — cap daily research:**
> "60 minutes a day on research, no more. The other writing hours go to drafts (eventually) and the rest of your life. The cap is what prevents this from eating your year."

**Step 4 — one capture file:**
> "One file. Everything. Quotes, images, names, dates, fragments. Date each entry. You'll be surprised what surfaces 3 months in."

**Step 5 — read widely:**
> "What are the five things you'd read first if you had to give an undergraduate seminar on this subject? Read those. Then the second layer of five. Take notes. Don't write songs yet."

**Step 6 — make field contact:**
> "Who can you talk to about this who has lived it? Family, friends, online communities, archivists. Schedule one conversation per week. Bring questions; take notes."

**Step 7 — start weekly drafts at week 2:**
> "By week 2 you'll feel like you need more research. Start drafting anyway. One rough draft per week, even if bad. The drafts tell you what research you still need."

**Step 8 — flip the ratio at the deadline:**
> "Today's the deadline. From now on, you write first and research only when a specific song demands a specific fact. The library is closed by default."

**Step 9 — weave personal into research:**
> "Read your draft. Where are *you* in it? If the song is all research and no you, it'll read like Wikipedia. If it's all you and no research, you wasted the research. Find the seam."

## When to abandon this workflow

- **You're 12 weeks in and have zero drafts.** Research is procrastination. Stop reading; write a bad first draft today.
- **Your drafts read like Wikipedia.** You're loading research onto songs that have no emotional spine. Pause; identify the personal stake.
- **The subject was too narrow.** You've exhausted it before the album is full. Expand the subject or accept the EP.
- **The subject was too wide.** You can't research everything about "loss." Pick a narrower instance.
- **You're under deadline pressure.** This workflow won't fit. Use a generative workflow (A11 Spoon-school demos) instead.
- **Your immediate emotional life wants a song NOW.** Stop researching; write the immediate song. Come back to the project tomorrow.
