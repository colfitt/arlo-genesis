# Workflow: Specificity-First

## The premise

Stop trying to write *about* the feeling. Start from one concrete, observed thing that's been on your mind, and excavate outward. Mann's catalog repeatedly starts in small specifics — a Hollywood meeting with an actor newly arrived in town ("Patient Zero"), an Instagram photo of a friend's cat ("Goose Snow Cone") — and grows naturally into the larger emotional territory. The abstraction *emerges*; it isn't declared.

Three premises:
1. Abstractions in lyrics are the listener's job; the writer provides specifics.
2. The seed is *observed*, not invented.
3. The song excavates outward; the meaning surfaces on its own.

## When ARLO should offer this

- User says they want to write about a feeling and every draft sounds generic
- User has piles of journaled abstractions and no songs
- User describes their lyrics as "positions on" the subject rather than "experiences of" the subject
- User keeps starting songs with a declared theme and abandoning them at line three
- User is writing character songs and the characters feel like archetypes, not people

Do NOT offer when:
- User is doing beat-first topline (use `beat-first-topline-workflow`)
- User wants to write a letter to a specific recipient (use `nick-cave-letter-method`)
- User has a story-song with a known arc — specificity still helps but the workflow doesn't add structure
- User is in pure sonic exploration where lyric is incidental

## The steps

1. **Don't pick a theme.** Resist the instinct to declare what the song will be about. Just sit down.

2. **Write down what's actually been in your head this week.** A specific thing — a photo you keep returning to, a person you met, a conversation, a smell, a chair. Not "loneliness." A specific instance of being lonely. If nothing comes — if the well is dry — this isn't the right workflow today; try `david-lynch-catching-big-fish`.

3. **Describe the thing in plain prose. Two paragraphs.** What you saw. What they said. Where it was. Use the senses. This is the same discipline Pattison's object-writing teaches; use it here as preparation for the song.

4. **Find the line that hits.** Inside the prose, one image or phrase you can't stop looking at. That's the seed.

5. **Start the song from the seed.** Not from the seed's *meaning* — from the seed itself. Often the seed becomes a chorus line or the opening verse couplet.

6. **Surround it with adjacent specifics.** Not abstractions. When the first image is exhausted, find the next concrete image — the hotel window, the dressing room, the call home — not a more abstract claim about loss.

7. **Let abstraction surface late, if at all.** Around the third verse or the bridge, you may find the song has revealed what it's about. You didn't declare it; it emerged. If it never emerges and the song is still specific and alive, the song doesn't need it.

8. **Stress-test every line with the noun-verb-noun diagnostic.** Concrete nouns? Specific verbs? "The pain inside is breaking me" fails. "I'm peeling labels off the bottles I won't drink" passes. Replace failing lines with adjacent concrete images.

## ARLO prompts for each step (verbatim)

**Step 1 (don't pick a theme):**
> "Don't tell me what this song is about yet. Sit down without a theme. The workflow depends on you not deciding upfront."

**Step 2 (find the actual recent specific):**
> "What specific thing has actually been in your head this week? A photograph you keep returning to. A person you met. A line someone said. A chair. Not a feeling — a *thing.* If nothing comes, the workflow isn't right today."

**Step 3 (describe in prose):**
> "Write two paragraphs of plain prose about it. What you saw. What was said. Where you were. The smell, the color of the jacket, the time of day. The senses. Don't write a lyric yet."

**Step 4 (find the line that hits):**
> "Read your prose. Which sentence is the one you can't stop looking at? Which image won't let you go? That's the seed."

**Step 5 (start from the seed):**
> "Start the song from the seed image itself — not from what it means. Often the seed wants to be a chorus line or the opener of the first verse. Place it and write into it."

**Step 6 (adjacent specifics):**
> "When the first image is exhausted, don't reach for an abstraction — reach for the next concrete thing. The hotel window. The dressing room. The call home. Build outward from specific to specific."

**Step 7 (let meaning surface):**
> "Don't declare the song's theme. If the song is doing its work, you'll notice around the bridge or the third verse that it has *become* about something larger. That's enough. The listener does the rest."

**Step 8 (noun-verb-noun test):**
> "Read every line. Are the nouns concrete? Are the verbs specific? 'The pain inside breaks me' fails — pain and breaking aren't things you can see. 'I'm peeling labels off the bottles I won't drink' passes. Replace failing lines with adjacent concrete images."

## When to abandon this workflow

- User can't find any specific thing recently on their mind — the well is dry; try `david-lynch-catching-big-fish` (the practice that fills the well)
- User keeps following each concrete image with a declared abstraction — they're fighting the workflow; offer the lyrics-only revision pass instead
- The song has a story-arc that requires telling — switch to a narrative-first draft, then apply Mann's specificity in revision
- User produces a lyric they like but the song's bigger structure isn't surfacing — that's normal; move to `tom-cosm-8-bar-loop-cycle` or `spoon-two-demos-a-week` for the structural work
- User is using "find the right seed" as procrastination — name it; pick a seed that's *adequate* and start; the writing reveals the right seed in retrospect
