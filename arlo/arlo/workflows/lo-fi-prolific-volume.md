# Workflow: Volume Over Polish (Lo-Fi / Prolific Method)

## The premise

You don't get good songs by perfecting one — you get them by finishing many, fast, ugly, and letting the volume drag your taste and hit-rate up. The demo IS the song. Will Toledo built Car Seat Headrest's whole Bandcamp catalogue this way: roughly a dozen lo-fi albums made largely alone, vocals tracked in his family's car, often on a stock laptop microphone, over a few years before Matador signed the project. The roughness became a preference, not an apology — "I wasn't going for clean, but I wanted sounds that were interesting to me." This workflow is the finish-ugly permission (commit the demo as the master) plus a volume engine (do it again, now). The thing being protected is momentum, and momentum dies the moment one song becomes precious.

## When ARLO should offer this

Offer when the user says any of:
- "I keep polishing one song and never writing new ones"
- "I haven't finished anything in weeks / months"
- "I have 40 unfinished sketches"
- "I'm a perfectionist about my demos"
- "every time I bounce it I hear something new to fix"
- "I want to write more / finish more"

Strong fit when their taste_cluster is lo-fi/indie/bedroom-pop (Car Seat Headrest, Modest Mouse, Guided By Voices lineage).

Do NOT offer when the user is making radio pop, EDM, modern country, or sync-targeted work (the last 20% of polish is the product there — route to the 80/20 finishing rule), is mid-album with deadline-quality demands, or has one specific song stalled on a *structural* problem (use a stall playbook).

## The steps

1. **Set the time cap before you start.** Decide the song gets one session. When the session ends, it ships at whatever state it's in.
2. **Answer the gear question once.** Whatever's in the room. Don't reopen it. The gear question is a stall in disguise.
3. **Lay the seed and commit it fast.** Chord loop, drum loop, riff, or vocal idea. Commit the sound immediately — don't audition forty patches.
4. **Build all sections — placeholder is fine.** Every section exists with a vocal on it, even mumbled/placeholder lyrics. Missing parts are not allowed; rough parts are.
5. **Refuse the upgrade impulse.** When you hear "this needs real drums / a better mic / a proper vocal," note it and keep moving. That impulse is usually the stall.
6. **Bounce ugly and file it.** One audio file you could play for a friend, apologizing only for rough, never for missing. Move it to a "done this week" folder. Close the project.
7. **Open the next idea or stop.** Do not reopen the file you just finished. The next song is worth more than this one.
8. **Weekly review (30 min).** Play the week's demos once each, in order. Mark each develop / archive / kill on first instinct. Kills go to a graveyard; at most three active "develop" songs at a time.

## ARLO prompts for each step (verbatim)

**Step 1 — set the cap:**
> "This song gets one session. When the session's over, it ships at whatever state it's in. Agree to that now, before you're attached to it. How long is the session — 90 minutes? Two hours?"

**Step 2 — gear question, once:**
> "Whatever mic, whatever DAW, whatever's already open. Car Seat Headrest's early records were a laptop mic in a parked car. The gear question is a stall — answer it once and don't reopen it."

**Step 3 — commit the seed:**
> "Lay the seed and commit the sound right now — bounce it to audio if you have to. Don't audition forty patches. The first interesting-not-clean thing is the thing. What's the seed?"

**Step 4 — all sections, placeholder ok:**
> "Get every section in: intro, verse, chorus, bridge, whatever the shape is. Put a vocal on each one even if the words are mumbled. Missing parts aren't allowed. Rough parts are encouraged."

**Step 5 — refuse the upgrade:**
> "I hear the 'this needs real drums' voice. Write it on a sticky note for next time and keep moving. Re-recording the keeper take is how you lose the energy that made it good."

**Step 6 — bounce ugly:**
> "Bounce it. One file. You should be able to play it for a friend and only apologize for it being rough — never for it being unfinished. Move it to 'done this week.' Close the project."

**Step 7 — next or stop:**
> "Do not reopen that file. The next song is worth more than a better mix of this one. Open a new idea or shut it down for today — your call."

**Step 8 — weekly review:**
> "End of week: play each demo once, in order. Develop, archive, or kill — first instinct, no agonizing. Kills go to the graveyard. You get three 'develop' slots, max."

## In Ableton

The volume school lives or dies on *commitment speed*, and Ableton's commit tools are the engine. Concrete moves (do not re-teach mechanics — the linked files own those):

- **Commit sounds the moment they're decided.** Bounce instruments and effect chains to audio as you go so you stop fiddling with parameters and start arranging. This is the core discipline of the whole method. See `corpus/daw/ableton/timeless/bounce-in-place-commit-discipline.md`.
- **Build the song one 8-bar block at a time and move on.** Don't perfect bar one; get a section working, duplicate, vary, advance. The 8-bars-first habit keeps you finishing sections instead of looping the intro forever. See `corpus/daw/ableton/timeless/eight-bars-first-discipline.md`.
- **Use saturation to make rough material sound intentional, not broken.** A pass of Saturator (or Roar for character-forward grit) turns thin laptop-mic and stock-sound material into the lo-fi texture that is the aesthetic — "interesting, not clean." Reach for Saturator first; it's the fast, transparent move. See `corpus/daw/ableton/devices/saturator-vs-roar.md`.
- **Resample to lock in happy accidents and force forward motion.** Bounce a loop or a glitchy chain back to audio and treat the result as a new, fixed instrument — you can't endlessly tweak what's already audio. Resampling is commitment by another name. See `corpus/daw/ableton/timeless/resampling-discipline.md`.

The throughline: every Ableton move here removes a way to keep tweaking. Commit, advance, bounce, file.

## When to abandon this workflow

- **You're making radio pop / EDM / modern country / sync work.** Polish is the product there. Switch to the 80/20 finishing rule (`corpus/songwriting/finishing/80-20-finishing-rule-mix-master.md`).
- **The roughness is hiding a broken song, not adding texture.** If it wouldn't be good at high fidelity, more volume won't save *this* song — it's an abandon-or-rewrite question, not a polish one.
- **"Develop" has become a graveyard of 12 permanent half-projects.** The review pass isn't ruthless enough. Force-archive anything that hasn't moved in three weeks.
- **You've missed the cadence four weeks running.** The number's wrong, not the method. Drop to one a week before quitting.
- **A specific song stalled on structure.** Don't grind through a real structural stall by sheer volume — use the right stall playbook.
