# Workflow: Nashville Co-Writing Protocol

## The premise

Music Row co-writing operates on a documented protocol: 2–3 writers in a room for ~3 hours, one song per session, chorus written first, split sheet signed before anyone leaves. The protocol exists to *finish songs that can be cut by an artist*. It works because the rules are clear, the time is bounded, and the contractual layer (the split sheet) gets handled in-room. Adapt the protocol — don't import it whole — if you're working in pop, indie, or remote contexts.

## When ARLO should offer this

Offer this when the user says:
- "I have a co-write tomorrow / next week"
- "I've never co-written before"
- "What are the rules for [Nashville-style / country / pop] co-writing?"
- "I'm writing with someone for the first time"
- "How do split sheets work?"
- They mention NSAI, Music Row, or named Nashville writers

Do NOT offer this for solo writing sessions, established collaborative pairs who have their own working practice, or experimental indie collabs where the protocol's commercial framing doesn't fit.

## The steps

1. **Pre-write list.** Before the session, write down 5–10 title ideas, hook concepts, or partial lyric/melody seeds you'd bring to the room. Bring the list.
2. **Set the room expectations.** Agree on session length (default 3 hours), target output (one song), and whether you're writing for a specific artist/slot.
3. **Connect first.** First 15–30 minutes: catch up, get to know each other if new, share what you brought. Don't dive straight into writing.
4. **Pick the concept.** Together, choose a title / hook / angle that all writers can imagine the chorus from. Don't start writing until this is agreed.
5. **Write the chorus first.** Title in the chorus. Hook in the chorus. If the chorus doesn't work, no verses are going to save it.
6. **Verses follow.** Verse 1 sets up; verse 2 develops. Write to the chorus, not away from it.
7. **Bridge or tag (if needed).** Many modern songs skip the bridge. Decide based on what the song wants.
8. **Sign the split sheet before leaving.** All writers, full legal names, agreed percentages summing to 100%, PRO and IPI numbers, song title. Photo or digital — but signed.
9. **Agree the takeaway plan.** If unfinished: agree a return date and what's outstanding. If finished: agree who's making the demo, when, and how the demo gets shared.

## ARLO prompts for each step (verbatim)

**Step 1 — pre-write list:**
> "Before the session: 5–10 title ideas, hook concepts, partial seeds. Write them in a single list. You're going to share these in the room — don't show up empty-handed."

**Step 2 — set expectations:**
> "When you arrive: 'Let's plan 3 hours for one song. Are we writing for [artist / cut / open]?' Get the time and the target on the table in the first five minutes."

**Step 3 — connect:**
> "Don't open with songwriting. Coffee, catch up, share what you brought. Fifteen to thirty minutes. The trust that builds is what makes the next two hours work."

**Step 4 — pick the concept:**
> "Together, pick a title/hook/angle. Check: (a) can we all imagine the chorus? (b) is it specific enough to write toward? (c) is it not too close to a recent hit? If yes to all three, start. If not, hunt longer."

**Step 5 — chorus first:**
> "Chorus before verses. The title is in the chorus, usually as the first line or the last line. Get the chorus working before you write a single verse line."

**Step 6 — verses:**
> "Verse 1 sets up the situation. Verse 2 develops it — time-shift, POV-shift, zoom, or consequence. Never restate verse 1. (See B2 second-verse problem if you stall here.)"

**Step 7 — bridge or tag:**
> "Does this song want a bridge? If yes, what does it contrast — lyric, harmony, melody, or arrangement? If no, write a tag or a stripped-down final chorus. Don't add a bridge by default."

**Step 8 — sign the split sheet:**
> "Before anyone leaves: split sheet. Equal splits is the Nashville default; agree differently only if you all want to. Full legal names, percentages summing to 100%, PRO + IPI numbers, song title. Photo or e-sign it. This is non-negotiable."

**Step 9 — takeaway plan:**
> "Either: 'Demo by Friday, here's who's tracking it.' Or, if unfinished: 'V2 and bridge by [date], and we meet [day] to finish.' Without a deadline, the song dies in email rounds."

## When to abandon this workflow

- **The room dynamic isn't working.** Two writers carrying a third, mismatched stakes, genuine creative mismatch. Finish what you have, sign the sheet, and don't book again.
- **You're under hour 1 with no concept.** Either reset to a much smaller-stakes "let's just play around" session, or end early.
- **You're solo-writing now, not co-writing.** If the room dynamic has collapsed and one writer is doing all the work, name it and either reset or split.
- **The song wants to be a solo write.** Some songs do. Pause the session, write the song alone tonight, share the next day.
- **You're in pop/indie/remote and the protocol's commercial framing doesn't fit.** Take the room-discipline structure (chorus first, split sheet, time-bounded) but drop the Music Row commercial layer.
- **You're in a stem-trading remote collaboration.** Different workflow entirely. The split-sheet discipline still applies; the room discipline doesn't.
