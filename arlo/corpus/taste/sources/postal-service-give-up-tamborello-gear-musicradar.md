# The Postal Service *Give Up* / "Such Great Heights" — how the beats were made

**Primary instrument:** Jimmy Tamborello's **Kurzweil K2000RS** rack sampler-synth — "mostly the only thing I used for sounds back then." It generates the signature **beeping melodies and glitchy rhythms**.

**Sequencing/recording:** Apple laptop running **Cubase (5)** + a **Mackie 1604** mixer. **NI Battery** for drum kits throughout the album.

**Effects:** a **Line 6 delay pedal** and an **Alesis MIDIverb** — analog/hardware FX, not plugins. Crucially: "**There's none of that [compression/side-chaining] on the album. I didn't really understand how any of that worked.**" So the glitchy, slightly-clattery feel is raw programmed beats + delay, NOT sidechain pump (the opposite of Daft Punk's pumped house).

**"Such Great Heights" origin:** started as a **cover song** — they liked the track and "used the same palette of sounds" but reworked it into an original. The intro = a fast cluster of **beeping/glitchy programmed percussion** (the K2000) under Ben Gibbard's soft, dry, close vocal.

**The mail method (the band's whole identity):** Tamborello mailed **finished instrumental tracks via post (CD-R)** to Gibbard, who added vocals remotely; back and forth until done. "We didn't even know each other that well… that helped us figure out our parts on our own."

**Glitch lineage (Dntel):** Tamborello = a '90s glitch practitioner ("every song had some distortion or something broken-sounding in it"), influenced by Autechre and Oval. Early tools: Vision, then Cubase, then Logic; D50, MicroKorg.

**Rig translation:** program a **fast, clattery, slightly-glitchy beat** on the Digitakt/MPC from short beeps/clicks (resample a VG-800/OP-1 blip, chop it), run it through **delay** (Big Time / EchoBoy), keep it dry-forward under a soft lead — NO pump (this is the un-sidechained counterpoint to Daft Punk). Conditional/probability trigs + micro-glitch retrigs = the Dntel cut-up feel.
