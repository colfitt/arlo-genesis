## The V-of-X concept

A **secondary dominant** (also called an **applied dominant**) is a chord that acts as the V (or V⁷) of some chord other than the tonic. Notation: **V/X** means "the dominant of X." So in C major, **V/V** is D (the V chord of G, which is the V of C); **V/vi** is E (the V chord of A minor, which is the vi of C). The technique is called **tonicization** — you're treating a non-tonic chord *as if it were tonic* for one or two beats, then resolving to it. Open Music Theory defines tonicization as "the process of making a non-tonic chord temporarily sound like tonic" through chromatic chords borrowed from a secondary key ([Open Music Theory, *Tonicization*](https://viva.pressbooks.pub/openmusictheory/chapter/tonicization/)). The tonal center doesn't change; you just visit a chord through its own back door.

## How to spot one

Secondary dominants nearly always introduce a chromatic note (an accidental not in the key signature). In C major, V/V is D major — which contains F♯, a note that doesn't belong to C major. That F♯ is the leading tone *of* G, and it's what makes the D chord pull strongly to G. The diagnostic move: when you see a major chord built on a scale degree that "shouldn't" be major (II, III, VI, VII in a major key — these are normally minor or diminished), check whether the next chord is a fifth below — if so, you're almost certainly looking at a secondary dominant. "Applied chords are readily identifiable because they contain chromatic pitches—for example, a V/V contains an F♯, which is the leading tone in G major and is indispensable for implying pseudo-tonic status for the G major chord" ([AP Music Theory study guide on Fiveable](https://fiveable.me/ap-music-theory/unit-7/tonicization-through-secondary-dominant-chords/study-guide/rLCBduMA9EmMPYSSNiwd)).

## The three pop workhorses: V/V, V/vi, V/IV

In pop and rock, three secondary dominants account for the vast majority of usage:

- **V/V** — tonicizes the V chord. In C major: D7 → G. Adds tension before the dominant.
- **V/vi** — tonicizes the relative minor (vi). In C major: E7 → Am. Adds drama and "minor pivot" feel.
- **V/IV** — tonicizes the IV chord. In C major: C7 → F. Adds bluesy color (because the C7 has a B♭, borrowed from C Mixolydian).

These three handle 80%+ of pop secondary dominants. **V/ii** and **V/iii** appear occasionally but are rarer because they tonicize already-minor diatonic chords, which feels less surprising. Popgrammar's pop-music harmony reference frames these three as the standard set ([Popgrammar, *Secondary Dominants*](https://popgrammar.com/secondary-dominants/)).

## V/V in action

**V/V** is the most common secondary dominant in mainstream songwriting because the V chord is already a tension chord — adding a chord that pulls *into* V doubles down on the setup-and-release. The Beatles' **"Oh! Darling"** has V/V at the climactic half cadence and again in the bridge, where B7 (V/V in E) sets up the E (V) before the verse return ([Popgrammar, *Oh! Darling*](https://popgrammar.com/the-beatles-oh-darling/)). The intro chorale of Queen's **"Bohemian Rhapsody"** uses V⁷/V to emphasize the third chord (V⁷) of the progression. The Beatles' **"Her Majesty"** uses E7 → A7, where E7 is V/V of D major ([Music Theory Bridges, *Secondary Dominants in Rock and Pop*](https://musictheorybridges.wordpress.com/2014/11/03/secondary-dominants-in-rock-and-pop/)). The functional move: a half cadence on V becomes a *much* bigger event when you precede the V with its own dominant — the listener hears "I'm coming home to V" before V even arrives.

## V/vi in action

**V/vi** is the secondary dominant that makes a major-key song dip into the relative minor's emotional gravity without modulating. The Beatles' **"Yesterday"** uses V/vi (E7) in the verse to pivot toward the vi chord (Am) and the wistful character of the song ([Music Theory Bridges, *Secondary Dominants in Rock and Pop*](https://musictheorybridges.wordpress.com/2014/11/03/secondary-dominants-in-rock-and-pop/)). The Beatles' **"Something"** uses E7 (V/vi in C major) as a featured bridge chord. Sublime's **"Santeria"** loops I–V/vi–vi–V (E–G♯7–C♯m–B in E major), placing the V/vi inside an otherwise diatonic four-chord cycle for color ([Music Theory Bridges, *Secondary Dominants in Rock and Pop*](https://musictheorybridges.wordpress.com/2014/11/03/secondary-dominants-in-rock-and-pop/)). The Foundations' **"Build Me Up Buttercup"** is built on V/vi → IV. When you want a major-key song to briefly *feel* like it's in the relative minor without paying the cost of a real modulation, V/vi is the cheapest move.

## V/IV in action

**V/IV** is the bluesy secondary dominant — it works by adding a flat seventh to the tonic chord, turning I into a dominant-seventh that pulls to IV. In C major, the chord is C7 (which contains B♭, borrowed from the parallel minor / Mixolydian). The 12-bar blues structure is built on this move: I⁷ → IV. Pop and rock writers borrow it constantly. The Beatles' "Got to Get You into My Life" has I⁷ → IV. Most blues-rock songs in major keys use this as their default I→IV approach. The catch: V/IV (i.e., I⁷) is so common in blues-influenced music that listeners barely register it as chromatic; it functions almost as a diatonic chord in the blues-major hybrid mode. Use it when you want the I chord to feel like motion rather than rest.

## Applied diminished and half-diminished chords

The other half of the applied family is the **secondary leading-tone chord** — written **vii°/X** or **vii°⁷/X** — which is the leading-tone diminished chord *of* the target. It functions like a secondary dominant minus the root, sharing all the other notes. **vii°⁷/V** in C major is F♯° (F♯–A–C–E♭), resolving to G. These chords are rarer in pop but appear in songwriter-jazz hybrids. Garth Brooks' **"Friends in Low Places"** uses vii°⁷/ii (the diminished leading-tone chord of ii), creating a chromatic motion A → A♯ → B in the verse that contrasts with the bright chorus ([Quora, *vii diminished pop songs*](https://www.quora.com/What-popular-songs-use-the-vii-diminished-chord)). The Beach Boys' **"God Only Knows"** uses both a fully diminished and a half-diminished chord in the verse, including a D♯°⁷ that creates tension before ascending by half-step. Use applied diminished chords when you want the chromatic-pull effect of a secondary dominant but darker and more unstable.

## Standard resolutions

A secondary dominant resolves like a regular V → I cadence, just inside the secondary key:

- **V/V → V** (D7 → G in C major)
- **V/vi → vi** (E7 → Am in C major)
- **V/IV → IV** (C7 → F in C major)
- **V/ii → ii** (A7 → Dm in C major)
- **V/iii → iii** (B7 → Em in C major)

In voice-leading terms: the chromatic note (the borrowed leading tone of the target) resolves up by half step, and the seventh of the dominant resolves down by step. So in V/V → V (D7 → G in C): F♯ → G, and C → B. Both moves are stepwise. This is the entire mechanism — the chromatic pitch is the "promise," and the resolution to the target chord is the "delivery."

## Deceptive resolutions

Secondary dominants don't have to resolve where they "promise" to. A **deceptive resolution** of a secondary dominant is when V/X resolves to something other than X — usually a chord that shares notes with X. The Wallflowers' **"One Headlight"** sets up B minor with an F♯ major (V/vi), then resolves to G (IV) instead, subverting the expectation ([Pacific Music Theory, *Irregular Resolutions of Secondary Chords*](https://musictheory.pugetsound.edu/mt21c/IrregularResoltionsOfSecondaryDominants.html)). **CeeLo Green's "Forget You"** resolves V⁷/V to IV instead of V. **John Lennon's "Imagine"** and **Otis Redding's "(Sittin' On) The Dock of the Bay"** both use V⁷/vi → IV (instead of → vi) as a signature move. The Beatles' **"Yesterday"** similarly resolves V/V to IV at a key moment. For songwriters: the deceptive resolution is where the *most expressive* moment lives — the listener expects one chord and gets another that's harmonically reasonable but emotionally different. Use sparingly so the surprise actually registers.

## Extended chains: V/V/V backcycling

You can stack applied dominants — V of (V of V), etc. — and follow them back to tonic. In C major, the chain **A7 → D7 → G7 → C** is V/V/V → V/V → V → I. Each chord is the dominant of the next, walking down through the cycle of fifths. This is called **backcycling** in jazz pedagogy: "the convergence of dominant chords through the cycle of fourths so they meet on a tonic or target chord" ([Lotus Music, *Backcycling*](https://www.lotusmusic.com/lm_backcycling.html)). The chain doesn't have to be long — even two links (V/V → V → I) is a backcycle. Bridge sections in standards and pop-jazz frequently chain three or four. The intro chorus of "Bohemian Rhapsody" uses a short chain. Eric Carmen's "All By Myself" (verse) walks through extended secondary dominants. Use a chain when you want a section to feel like it's "winding up" toward a destination — every chord in the chain telegraphs forward motion.

## V/IV versus the dominant seventh on I

A frequent point of confusion: **I⁷ in a major key** can be analyzed two ways. In standard tonal analysis, it's V/IV (the dominant of the IV chord). In blues-influenced pop, it's just *the I chord with a flat 7* — a Mixolydian color that doesn't necessarily resolve to IV. Hooktheory and similar pop-music references typically only call it V/IV when it actually resolves to IV; otherwise they treat it as a modal color. Practical rule: if I⁷ is followed by IV, label it V/IV and read it as a secondary dominant. If I⁷ is just sitting there sounding bluesy and doesn't lead to IV, label it I⁷ (Mixolydian) and treat it as modal color, not tonicization. Same chord, different function depending on what comes next.

## Identifying them in songs you know

Quick mental checklist when analyzing a chord chart:

1. Find the key. The chord chart should already give you this.
2. Mark every chord that contains an accidental (a note outside the key signature).
3. For each non-diatonic chord, check if it's a *major* chord built on a scale degree where the diatonic version would be minor (II, III, VI, VII).
4. If yes, look at what follows. Is the next chord a fifth below the chromatic chord? If so, that's a secondary dominant.
5. If the next chord is something else, it's either a deceptive resolution or a borrowed chord (modal interchange).

Practice on songs you already know. "Yesterday," "Something," "Bohemian Rhapsody," "Build Me Up Buttercup," and "Don't Stop Believin'" are all loaded with secondary dominants. Once you can spot V/V, V/vi, and V/IV in the wild, the rest of applied harmony is variations on the same pattern.

## When to reach for one (and when not to)

Songwriters tend to under-use secondary dominants, because diatonic loops are easier and pop ears are forgiving. But three situations almost always benefit from one:

- **Pre-chorus that needs lift** — a V/V right before the chorus's V or I pumps up the energy without changing key.
- **Bridge that needs a different harmonic world** — V/vi pivots toward the relative minor for free, giving a "darker bridge" feel.
- **Turnaround at the end of a section** — V/IV at the I-chord-headed-back-to-IV moment adds bluesy motion.

When not to: if a section is already harmonically busy (lots of modal interchange, lots of motion), an additional secondary dominant adds clutter without adding meaning. The technique works best as occasional spice in an otherwise diatonic context — the chromatic note has to feel like an *event*, not a constant condition.
