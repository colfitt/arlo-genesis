## The function model in plain language

Functional harmony groups chords into three jobs: **tonic** (home, rest), **predominant** (departure, preparation), and **dominant** (tension, pull-back-home). The standard cycle is **T → PD → D → T**. In a major key the default assignments are tonic = I and vi, predominant = ii and IV, dominant = V and vii°. The model is descriptive, not prescriptive — pop writers loop and substitute freely — but it explains why most progressions resolve and which moves feel restless versus settled. Open Music Theory's pop unit treats classical function as one lens among several, alongside loop-based schemas that don't always end on tonic ([Open Music Theory, *Introduction to Harmonic Schemas*](https://viva.pressbooks.pub/openmusictheory/chapter/intro-to-pop-schemas/)). For songwriting, the practical move is: ask which function the next chord serves, then pick from that bucket. A vi swapped for I keeps the section tonic-flavored; a ii swapped for IV keeps it pre-dominant; both feel like the "same move."

## What Hooktheory's database actually shows

Hooktheory's TheoryTab database — user-submitted chord-and-melody analyses of pop, rock, and game music — is the closest thing to public frequency data on real songs. In their analysis of 1,300 songs, the I chord made up roughly 18.9% of all chords and IV came in next at about 17.2%, with V and vi close behind ([Hooktheory blog, *I analyzed the chords of 1300 popular songs*](https://www.hooktheory.com/blog/i-analyzed-the-chords-of-1300-popular-songs-for-patterns-this-is-what-i-found/)). The headline finding for songwriters: **IV → I appears about as often as V → I**, which is a real divergence from classical practice and one reason pop sounds "rounder" than common-practice tonal music ([Hooktheory blog, part 2](https://www.hooktheory.com/blog/music-theory-analysis-1300-songs-for-songwriting-part2/)). Plagal motion isn't a special-occasion cadence in pop — it's a default resolution.

## The axis progression (I–V–vi–IV)

Hooktheory ranks **I–V–vi–IV** as the single most popular four-chord progression in their database ([Hooktheory, *Popular Chord Progressions*](https://www.hooktheory.com/theorytab/popular-chord-progressions/1)). It threads tonic → dominant → tonic-substitute → predominant, which is functionally a loop that never demands resolution — every chord can begin or end the cycle. Verified examples include "Let It Be" (The Beatles), "With or Without You" (U2), "Don't Stop Believin'" (Journey, verse) and "Someone Like You" (Adele) ([Wikipedia, *I–V–vi–IV progression*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/I%E2%80%93V%E2%80%93vi%E2%80%93IV_progression)). The Axis of Awesome comedy medley made the ubiquity into a meme but the pattern predates them — "To Know Him Is to Love Him" (Teddy Bears, 1958) is an early example. The progression's superpower is that it balances bright (I, V) and dark (vi) inside one bar-pair, so the same loop carries verses, choruses, and bridges with only arrangement changes.

## Rotations of the four-chord axis

The same four chords yield distinctly different songs depending on which one you start on. **vi–IV–I–V** is the so-called "sensitive female chord progression" — named by Boston Globe columnist Marc Hirsh — and it shows up in "Africa" (Toto), "One of Us" (Joan Osborne), and "Building a Mystery" (Sarah McLachlan) ([Wikipedia, *I–V–vi–IV progression*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/I%E2%80%93V%E2%80%93vi%E2%80%93IV_progression)). **IV–I–V–vi** powers "Umbrella" (Rihanna), "Bad Blood" (Taylor Swift), and is common in country-pop hits. **V–vi–IV–I** is the "Wannabe" (Spice Girls) rotation. Practically: rotating the axis lets you keep the same harmonic palette while changing where the listener feels the "downbeat" of the loop. Starting on vi makes the loop feel like it's in the relative minor even though the chords are unchanged.

## The doo-wop / '50s progression (I–vi–IV–V)

The **I–vi–IV–V** progression — sometimes called the "Heart and Soul changes," the "Stand by Me" changes, or "ice cream changes" — was the dominant template of late-1950s and early-1960s pop ([Wikipedia, *'50s progression*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/%2750s_progression)). Verified examples include "Blue Moon" (Marcels), "Earth Angel" (The Penguins), "Stand by Me" (Ben E. King), and "Every Breath You Take" (The Police, an adapted version). Open Music Theory groups this with two other four-chord schemas under the **Doo-Wop schema** label and notes that "Duke of Earl" (Gene Chandler, 1962), "Total Eclipse of the Heart" (Bonnie Tyler, 1983), and "Friday" (Rebecca Black, 2011) use it across decades ([Open Music Theory, *Four-Chord Schemas*](https://viva.pressbooks.pub/openmusictheory/chapter/4-chord-schemas/)). Functionally it's T → T (vi as tonic substitute) → PD → D, looping back to I — a textbook function cycle with one prolongation of tonic.

## Other high-frequency progressions

Beyond the axis and doo-wop, a handful of progressions show up so often they're worth memorizing as defaults. **I–IV–V–I** is the bedrock blues/rock progression and the harmonic core of folk-rock. **ii–V–I** is the jazz-and-standards anchor, common in pre-choruses and bridges of pop songs that lean sophisticated ("Just the Two of Us," "Sunday Morning"). **vi–V–IV–V** (or vi–IV–V) is the "lament-style" loop heard in "Hit the Road Jack" and many minor-leaning pop bridges. **I–V/vii–vi–V–IV–V/iii–IV–V** (the Pachelbel descent) appears in "Don't Look Back in Anger" (Oasis), "Basket Case" (Green Day), and "Graduation (Friends Forever)" (Vitamin C). Each of these can substitute into a verse where the writer wants more harmonic motion than a four-chord loop provides.

## Cadence types and what they do at section ends

A **cadence** is how a phrase ends; it's the harmonic punctuation. The four practical types: **authentic** (V → I, strongest resolution, default for chorus endings and song endings); **plagal** (IV → I, the "Amen cadence," softer landing, common in pop chorus-outs and gospel-flavored songs like Pink's "Just Give Me a Reason"); **deceptive** (V → vi, sets up the listener for resolution then sidesteps it, common in bridges and second-verse setups — The Beatles' "P.S. I Love You" has four of them); and **half** (anything → V, ending on the dominant, the standard verse and pre-chorus exit because it pulls forward into the next section) ([Musicnotes, *4 Types of Cadences*](https://www.musicnotes.com/blog/cadences-in-music-theory-the-4-types-explained/), [Open Music Theory, *Cadences*](https://viva.pressbooks.pub/openmusictheory/chapter/cadences/)). Songwriting rule of thumb: a half cadence at the end of a verse or pre-chorus is what makes the listener "lean forward" into the chorus; an authentic cadence at the chorus ending creates closure.

## Inversions and bass-line shape

Hooktheory's separate study of inversions in their database found that **I⁶ (with the third in the bass — e.g., C/E) and V⁶ (e.g., G/B)** are by far the most-used inversions in pop ([Hooktheory, *Statistical study of inversions*](https://www.hooktheory.com/blog/statistical-study-inversions-slash-chords-popular-music/)). The reason is bass-line shape: inversions let songwriters write stepwise descending or ascending bass under a chord loop. A classic move is **I – V/vii – vi – IV** (C – G/B – Am – F) where the bass walks C → B → A → F — much smoother than C → G → A → F. Coldplay's "Viva la Vida" famously uses a IV-rooted axis rotation with stepwise bass; "Don't Look Back in Anger" walks a Pachelbel-style descending bass under its verse. When a progression sounds "stuck" or jumpy, the fix is often an inversion to fill in the bass steps, not a chord change.

## Substitutions that keep the function intact

The cheap, immediately useful moves: **vi for I** (tonic substitution — anywhere the I sits comfortably, vi will work but darker), **iii for I or V** (a weaker substitute, used to ease into vi), **ii for IV** (predominant swap, more "jazz" flavor), **IV for ii** (predominant swap, more "pop"), and **V⁷ for V** (adds the b7 leading tone, sharpens the pull home). Pedler's analysis of the Beatles catalog highlights how often the Lennon-McCartney moves are simple swaps within function rather than genuinely new chords — "Let It Be" uses I–V–vi–IV with vi acting as tonic-substitute, "Hey Jude" uses I–V–IV–I with IV as both predominant and plagal resolver. The discipline is to know which function you're in, then swap within the bucket; cross-function swaps (V for IV or vice versa) tend to break the progression's pull.

## Modal flavor without leaving the key

You can color a diatonic progression toward Mixolydian or Dorian without modulating, just by reordering. **I–♭VII–IV–I** (Mixolydian flavor in major) shows up in "Sweet Child o' Mine" (Guns N' Roses) and "Sympathy for the Devil" (Rolling Stones) — borrowing one chord (♭VII) from the parallel minor adds rock attitude without changing key center. **i–♭VII–♭VI–♭VII** in minor (the "Andalusian cadence" softened) gives a moody but not melodramatic feel. Loop-based pop frequently lives in this borderland: the song isn't strictly major or minor, it's a rotation of chords that lets the melody decide. Open Music Theory calls these **modal schemas** and treats them as a parallel system to classical functional analysis ([Open Music Theory, *Modal Schemas*](https://viva.pressbooks.pub/openmusictheory/chapter/modal-schemas/)).

## How section function maps to progression choice

Pop song sections have harmonic conventions that hold across genres. **Verses** tend to use longer or more varied progressions (six to eight chords, or a four-chord loop with melodic motion doing the work). **Pre-choruses** want forward motion, so they often climb (IV–V or ii–V) or use a half cadence to set up the chorus. **Choruses** lean on the four-chord axis or doo-wop schema with strong tonic landings — the listener wants the home-feeling on the title line. **Bridges** push toward instability via modal interchange, secondary dominants, or rotation to the relative minor; they almost never use the same progression as the chorus. **Post-choruses and outros** often drop to a two-chord vamp (I–IV or I–vi) that lets a hook repeat without harmonic competition. Writing a song = picking the right progression shape per section, not the same progression for the whole thing.

## Diagnosing a progression that's not working

If a section feels static, the issue is usually one of three things: (1) the bass line is jumping instead of stepping — try inversions to smooth it; (2) the predominant function is missing — every loop benefits from at least one IV or ii before going to V; (3) the loop never lands on tonic, so it has no rest point — even loop-based pop usually visits I at least once per cycle. If a section feels too "stock," try a deceptive cadence (V → vi instead of V → I) at the phrase end, or borrow ♭VII once. If the chorus doesn't lift, check whether the melody is landing on chord tones during tonic — pop choruses lift when the title syllable lands on the root or third of I, not a passing tone.
