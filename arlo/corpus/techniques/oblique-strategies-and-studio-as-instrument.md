## What Oblique Strategies actually is

Oblique Strategies (subtitled *Over One Hundred Worthwhile Dilemmas*) is a deck of cards co-authored by Brian Eno and the painter Peter Schmidt, first published in January 1975 as a numbered, signed limited edition of 500 sets containing 113 cards each. Each card carries a single short aphorism — "a gnomic suggestion, aphorism or remark which can be used to break a deadlock or dilemma situation," as the Wikipedia entry summarizes it — and the deck is meant to be consulted, I Ching–style, when you are stuck ([Oblique Strategies, Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oblique_Strategies)).

The collaboration was not coincidence. Schmidt had made a related work in 1970 — *The Thoughts Behind the Thoughts*, 55 letterpress aphorisms on discarded prints — and Eno, working independently in 1974, had been writing his own handwritten cards on bamboo with the same title. When they compared notes, the overlap was so close they merged the two projects ([Oblique Strategies, Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oblique_Strategies); [Eye Magazine: Oblique Strategies](https://eyemagazine.com/feature/article/oblique-strategies)). Six editions exist (1975, 1978, 1979, 1996, 2001, 2013), with card counts ranging from 100 to 128.

## The problem the cards were designed to solve

Eno's stated motivation is that the panic of a studio session — expensive time, watching musicians, label pressure — makes you forget that you know other ways of working. The cards externalize that knowledge so you can find it again under stress. As Eno has put it in multiple interviews, the cards exist because "in working situations…the panic of the situation…made him forget there were other ways of working and tangential ways of attacking problems that were often more interesting than the direct head-on approach" ([Production Advice: Oblique Strategies](https://productionadvice.co.uk/oblique-strategies/)).

The deeper logic is that creativity under deadline collapses into habit; a card forces a non-habitual move. The card is not an answer — it is a *redirection*.

## Sample cards (verified)

A representative selection from across editions. These are widely reproduced from the official decks; treat them as the *flavor* of the deck rather than a complete list:

- **Honour thy error as a hidden intention.** (The first card in the deck — treat mistakes as if you meant them.)
- **Use an old idea.**
- **State the problem in words as clearly as possible.**
- **Try faking it!**
- **Work at a different speed.**
- **Abandon normal instructions.**
- **Ask your body.**
- **Use unqualified people.**
- **Twist the spine.**
- **Repetition is a form of change.**
- **Reverse.** (variants include the action "reverse roles")
- **Only one element of each kind.**
- **What would your closest friend do?**
- **Give the game away.**

The full deck is documented in Gregory Taylor's authoritative archive and reproduced in *A Year with Swollen Appendices* ([Wikipedia: A Year with Swollen Appendices](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/A_Year_with_Swollen_Appendices); [Oblique Strategies, Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oblique_Strategies)).

## Bowie, *"Heroes"*, and "Sense of Doubt"

The most famous documented card use is on the Berlin Trilogy — *Low*, *"Heroes"*, *Lodger* — recorded with Tony Visconti producing and Eno contributing textures, synth parts, and methodology. The standout single-session example is the *"Heroes"* instrumental "Sense of Doubt." Eno and Bowie each drew an Oblique Strategy card, kept it secret from the other, then took turns overdubbing — each player observing only the instruction on their own card. The two cards' instructions are reported to have been directly opposed, and the friction of the two contrary directives is audible in the track's brooding stasis ([Louder: The Story Behind 'Heroes'](https://www.loudersound.com/features/the-story-behind-the-song-heroes-by-david-bowie); [Oblique Strategies, Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oblique_Strategies)).

Visconti has described the broader Berlin working method as "building the frame before the picture" — laying down layered backing tracks first, with melody and lyric grafted on top after the sonic landscape was already speaking. *Low*'s drum sound, processed through an Eventide H910 Harmonizer in pitch-shift feedback (Visconti's reply when Bowie asked what it did: "It fucks with the fabric of time") came out of exactly that approach — the production gesture *was* the compositional gesture ([Wikipedia: Low (David Bowie album)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Low_(David_Bowie_album))).

## *Lodger* and "Boys Keep Swinging" — the canonical role-swap

The cleanest documented example of an Oblique Strategies card directly determining a take is on *Lodger* (1979). After a few takes of "Boys Keep Swinging," Bowie complained the band "sounds like professionals!" — he wanted a garage-band looseness, the sound of "young kids in the basement [who were] just discovering their instruments." The card pulled was "Reverse Roles." Carlos Alomar (normally guitar) moved to drums; Dennis Davis (drums) moved to bass; George Murray (bass) moved to keyboards ([Wikipedia: Boys Keep Swinging](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boys_Keep_Swinging); [Wikipedia: Lodger (album)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lodger_(album))).

Davis's bass performance was deemed *too* amateurish and was later replaced by Visconti's overdub — but Alomar's stiff, leaden drumming stayed, and is the reason the track sounds the way it does. *Lodger* is also where Eno's other oblique moves landed: "Move On" was built from the chords of Bowie's 1972 "All the Young Dudes" *played backwards*; multiple tracks share chord progressions ("Boys Keep Swinging" and "Fantastic Voyage") ([Wikipedia: Lodger (album)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lodger_(album))).

Biographer Chris O'Leary describes the cards as "part-fortune cookie, part-*Monopoly* 'Chance' cards" — apt because the role of the card is not insight but *intervention*.

## Talking Heads and *Remain in Light* — the studio-first songwriting move

*Remain in Light* (1980) is the cleanest example of the broader "studio as compositional instrument" philosophy in action — even where Oblique Strategies as a deck is less directly documented. Previous Talking Heads records had come in with songs already written. For *Remain in Light*, Byrne and Eno reversed the order: the band recorded long jams at Compass Point in the Bahamas, isolated the strongest one- and two-bar fragments, looped them, and then *learned* to play those loops back as repeating one-chord vamps. Byrne later put it bluntly: "We were human samplers" ([Wikipedia: Remain in Light](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Remain_in_Light)).

Byrne's own 1980 description of the method is direct: "Everything became collaboration because of the method of recording and composition as we just went into the studio with nothing and gradually evolved things and added bits. Also a good part of the music was done before the lyrics and so I had to take my cues from the music." The chord-change architecture of conventional pop song form was simply dropped: "The underlying principle is very different, the way the songs hold together as they don't have any chord changes" ([More Dark Than Shark: Byrne/Eno interview, Nov 1980](https://moredarkthanshark.org/eno_int_riu-nov80.html)).

Eno's documented in-studio moves on the record include having overdubbing musicians play *without hearing what the others had added* (a direct oblique-strategy logic), and applying Fela Kuti–derived polyrhythmic displacement — starting different parts at different positions in the bar, so the downbeat ambiguity becomes the song's identity ([Wikipedia: Remain in Light](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Remain_in_Light)).

## Eno's framing: the studio as a compositional instrument

The methodology behind both Berlin and *Remain in Light* was formalized in Eno's lecture "The Recording Studio as a Compositional Tool," delivered at the 1979 New Music America Festival and later published in *Down Beat* in 1983. The central thesis: multi-track recording is not a documentation medium — it is a compositional medium of its own kind. Eno's own framing of the shift: "you no longer come to the studio with a conception of the finished piece. Instead, you come with…a bare skeleton of the piece, or perhaps with nothing at all. I often start working with no starting point" ([Beat Patrol: The Studio as Compositional Tool](https://beatpatrol.wordpress.com/2010/02/10/brian-eno-the-studio-as-compositional-tool-1983/); [Open Culture: Eno's 1979 Lecture](https://www.openculture.com/2017/12/brian-eno-presents-a-crash-course-on-how-the-recording-studio-radically-changed-music.html)).

His comparison was to painting: "It puts the composer in the identical position of the painter — he's working directly with a material, working directly onto a substance, and he always retains the options to chop and change." Composition becomes *additive*: "you can begin to compose in relation to those facilities. You can begin to think in terms of putting something on, putting something else on, trying this on top of it" ([Beat Patrol](https://beatpatrol.wordpress.com/2010/02/10/brian-eno-the-studio-as-compositional-tool-1983/)).

Recording, Eno argued in the same lecture, takes music "out of the time dimension and puts it in the space dimension" — a take is no longer ephemeral, it is *material* that can be cut up, looped, reversed, layered, and treated like clay ([Open Culture](https://www.openculture.com/2017/12/brian-eno-presents-a-crash-course-on-how-the-recording-studio-radically-changed-music.html)).

## The "happy accident" doctrine

Closely linked: Eno's belief that mistakes are the most valuable raw material in the studio. The first card in the deck — "Honour thy error as a hidden intention" — encodes this: when something goes wrong, you treat the wrong thing as if you meant it and follow it. Eno on his own practice: "I can't duplicate my own successes, because part of the creation of that effect is making something happen that you didn't expect" ([Pro Audio Files: 19 Eno Quotes](https://theproaudiofiles.com/brian-eno-quotes/)).

Eno distinguishes this from what he calls "screwdriver work" — the meticulous, conscious, knob-twisting labor that comes after. The interesting part is making something surprising happen; the screwdriver work is just refining what surprised you ([Sound on Sound: Brian Eno](https://www.soundonsound.com/people/brian-eno)).

## Adaptations for a modern DAW workflow

The cards were designed for a tape-based studio with a fixed crew and a clock. In a DAW with infinite undo, every Oblique Strategy needs to be translated into a move that costs you something — otherwise the constraint dissolves. Concrete adaptations:

- **Pull a card before every overdub, blind from the rest of the project.** Don't let yourself listen back between layers. Eno used this on "Sense of Doubt"; the DAW equivalent is to mute the existing tracks while recording the next part, only un-muting once the take is committed.
- **Reverse roles.** Swap your DAW templates: produce the song as if you were a different producer in your library. Better: hand your project to a collaborator with a different toolset for one session.
- **Commit takes — disable undo for a window.** Bounce the take to audio, delete the source, and continue. This is the DAW translation of "Honour thy error as a hidden intention."
- **Build the track before the song.** Spend the first session producing a two-bar loop with no chord changes, no lyric, no melody. Only after you have a sound-world do you write *into* it. This is the *Remain in Light* method literally executed.
- **Reverse, slow, transpose old material.** Pull a stem from a finished song, reverse it, pitch it down a fourth, use it as the foundation for the next one. This is "Move On" from *Lodger*.
- **Use unqualified people.** Hand a take to a non-musician collaborator and have them perform a part. Or play an instrument you can't play. The amateurism is the signal.
- **Repetition is a form of change.** Loop a single one-bar groove for eight minutes before introducing variation — let the listener's attention be the variation. (This is also the LCD Soundsystem move; James Murphy's debt to Eno here is direct.)
- **Deletion as composition.** When stuck, mute or delete tracks rather than add more. Often the song's identity is what's *missing*.
- **State the problem in words.** Before opening the DAW, write the problem down in one sentence. Often the act of articulation makes the move obvious.

## Other documented users

Coldplay used the cards extensively with Eno on *Viva la Vida* (2008) — Eno reportedly handed each band member a different card and asked them to perform to its instruction without revealing the card to the others. Phoenix bought a deck for the *Wolfgang Amadeus Phoenix* sessions (2009). Carlos Alomar has said his students at the Center for Performing Arts have access to a wall of the cards ([Oblique Strategies, Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oblique_Strategies); [Coldplaying: Coldplay used Oblique Strategies](https://coldplaying.com/newsarchive/coldplay-news/coldplay-used-oblique-strategies-cards-with-brian-eno-during-studio-sessions/)).

## Why this matters for an emotional-but-experimental aesthetic

The Bon Iver / LCD Soundsystem / Charli XCX / mk.gee axis all share a common methodology trait: **production gesture precedes the song.** Justin Vernon assembles *22, A Million* from manipulated sample fragments before the lyrics solidify. James Murphy builds LCD tracks by looping a drum machine for hours until the song emerges from the groove. mk.gee's records sound the way they do because the *reamping decisions* were the composition. Charli's hyperpop catalog is unimaginable without the producer-first writing model. All of these are downstream of Eno's "you come to the studio with…perhaps nothing at all."

Oblique Strategies are the explicit, portable, deck-of-cards form of that worldview. The methodology underneath them — production-as-composition, mistakes-as-raw-material, deliberate friction in the workflow — is the foundation a modern hybrid record can be built on. Pull a card. Honour the error. Make the studio the instrument.
