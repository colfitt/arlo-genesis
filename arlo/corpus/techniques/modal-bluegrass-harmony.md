# Modal Bluegrass Harmony — Beyond I-IV-V

Bluegrass, as Monroe and Scruggs codified it in the 1940s, sits mostly
inside functional major-key harmony — I, IV, V, with the occasional vi,
ii, or secondary dominant. But the music's older substrate (Appalachian
ballads, fiddle tunes, mountain modal singing) is full of modal material,
and the post-Newgrass generation — Fleck, Bush, Thile, Pikelny, Strings
— has spent fifty years pulling that substrate back to the surface and
grafting it onto jazz and classical harmonic vocabulary. This file is
the bluegrass-specific extension of
`corpus/harmony/modal-interchange-and-borrowed-chords.md`; consult that
file for the underlying theory of borrowed chords and parallel modes.

## What "Traditional" Bluegrass Harmony Actually Is

The mythical "three-chord" bluegrass song is real and common, but the
honest description of mainstream bluegrass harmonic vocabulary is:

- **I, IV, V** as the backbone (G–C–D in G; A–D–E in A).
- **vi** (Em in G) as the standard minor color.
- **V/V** and other secondary dominants — Banjo Hangout posters note that
  bluegrass "typically uses V-of-V progressions and secondary dominants,
  allowing harmonic movement beyond strict modal boundaries" — meaning
  even straight-ahead bluegrass tunes are tonal/functional rather than
  purely modal.[^bhang-modes]
- Occasional **ii** (Am in G) in more sophisticated writing.
- **bVII** as a borrowed/modal color — usually arriving from old-time and
  fiddle-tune sources rather than from functional theory.

The interesting move is that the *tunes* are often modal even when the
*chord progressions* are not. Fiddlers, as one forum participant observes,
historically played "between 3min and 3maj and between 7min and 7maj,"
producing ambiguous modal inflections that don't fit neatly into either
major or minor.[^bhang-modes] Modern progressive players turn those
melodic ambiguities into harmonic decisions.

## Mixolydian — The "Country" Mode

Mixolydian is the major scale with a flat 7 (in G: G A B C D E **F**).
On a string band, this is the home of the *bVII* chord — the F major
chord in G, the G major chord in A, the D major chord in E. It is the
single most common modal flavor in bluegrass and old-time repertoire.

**Roman-numeral kit for Mixolydian:**

- **I** — tonic major triad
- **bVII** — major triad a whole step below tonic (the defining color)
- **IV** — usually still major
- **v** (minor) — occasionally, derived from the natural seventh
- **I – bVII – IV – I** — the canonical "Old Joe Clark" / mountain-modal
  loop

**Canonical example — "Old Joe Clark."** Acoustic Guitar Magazine's
flatpicking lesson treats the tune explicitly as G Mixolydian (sounding
in A with a capo at the second fret), with the melody built on
"G A B C D E F."[^ag-ojc] Banjo Hangout's modes thread agrees: "Old Joe
Clark and Little Maggie" are the textbook Mixolydian bluegrass
tunes.[^bhang-modes] The chord vocabulary that emerges from this is the
A–G–A movement (I–bVII–I) for the B section, against an A major-ish A
part.

Other Mixolydian bluegrass / old-time tunes commonly cited: "Cluck Old
Hen," "June Apple," "Salt Creek." The bVII chord is the giveaway every
time.

## Dorian — The "Folk-Minor" Mode

Dorian is the minor scale with a raised 6 (in A: A B C D E **F#** G).
Compared to Aeolian (natural minor), Dorian sounds brighter and less
funereal — the natural 6 keeps it from settling into a fully dark
sound. It is the second-most-common modal flavor in bluegrass.

**Roman-numeral kit for Dorian:**

- **i** — tonic minor triad
- **bVII** — major triad a whole step below (same chord as in
  Mixolydian, different function)
- **IV** (major!) — the major-quality IV is the Dorian fingerprint; in
  Aeolian it would be minor (iv)
- **i – bVII** vamp — the most common Dorian loop in bluegrass
- **i – IV** — the brighter Dorian move

**Canonical example — "Shady Grove."** The tune most commonly circulates
in A Dorian (or D Dorian on mandolin), though, as Doc Watson, Jerry
Garcia / David Grisman, and bluegrass arrangers have shown, it is
routinely re-harmonized into straight A minor or even A major depending
on the band.[^shady-grove] The Dorian reading uses Am–G (i–bVII) as
the basic vamp, sometimes with a C (bIII) or D (IV) to brighten the B
phrase. The "IV major over a minor i" sound is the Dorian giveaway.

Other Dorian bluegrass / old-time tunes commonly cited: "Cold Frosty
Morning," "Ducks on the Pond," "Elzic's Farewell," "Betty Liken."[^bhang-modes]

A note on rigor: many "Dorian" tunes are actually built on a **minor
pentatonic** melody (G Bb C D F in G minor) rather than a full Dorian
scale.[^bhang-modes] That ambiguity is why they re-harmonize as modal,
minor, or bluesy without breaking. ARLO should treat "Dorian" and
"minor-pentatonic-over-modal-vamp" as functionally adjacent in this
idiom.

## Aeolian and Phrygian Flavors

**Aeolian** (natural minor) shows up in murder ballads and lonesome
material — "Pretty Polly" is most accurately analyzed as G minor
pentatonic over an Aeolian-leaning context rather than full Aeolian,
but the i–bVI–bVII / i–bVII–bVI cadential moves come from this
mode.[^bhang-modes] In banjo terms, the Aeolian tonic forces the player
out of open-G chord shapes and into closed positions or alternate
tunings (G modal / "sawmill" tuning gDGCD is built for this).

**Phrygian** is rare in bluegrass proper, but Punch Brothers, Fleck,
and the chromatic edge of the genre pass through it as momentary color
— the bII–i move (Bb to A in A Phrygian) as a brief darkening before
resolving back. Closer to a modal-interchange gesture than a
Phrygian-key composition.

## Modal Vamp vs. Functional Progression

The structural distinction that matters for songwriting:

- **Functional progression** — chord movement is goal-directed; V wants
  to resolve to I, ii wants to lead to V, secondary dominants tonicize
  briefly. Most mainstream bluegrass is functional even when it sounds
  modal in passing.
- **Modal vamp** — a small set of chords (often two, sometimes three)
  loops indefinitely, and harmonic interest comes from melody, rhythm,
  and texture rather than from cadential motion. "Shady Grove"'s
  Am–G vamp is the archetype.

Progressive bluegrass routinely splices these. A Punch Brothers or
Fleck composition might open with a modal vamp (i–bVII for sixteen
bars), pivot to a functional ii–V–I cadence to set up a new key, drop
back into a modal vamp in the new key, then chromatically slide a tone
upward without resolving. ARLO, when generating bluegrass-flavored
suggestions, should distinguish between "give me a vamp to ride" and
"give me a progression that goes somewhere."

## How Fleck, Pikelny, Bush, and Thile Pushed the Vocabulary

**Béla Fleck.** *My Bluegrass Heart* (2021) is Fleck's third bluegrass
trilogy entry, following *Drive* (1988) and *The Bluegrass Sessions*
(1999). Coverage of the record notes that "there's stuff going on
rhythmically and harmonically on this record that definitely pushes
the boundary of where Béla was on *Drive* or *The Bluegrass
Sessions*," and that Fleck described it as "not a straight bluegrass
album, but written for a bluegrass band" — bluegrass instrumentation
with harmonic vocabulary borrowed from his Flecktones and large-form
compositional work.[^mbh-review] The track "Doctor Gradus" is
specifically cited as carrying "the kind of harmony Béla might do in
the Flecktones." Fleck's modal moves tend to extend the Mixolydian /
Dorian core with jazz-style chromatic approach chords, tritone subs,
and modal mixture across phrase boundaries.

**Noam Pikelny.** Pikelny is the technical bridge: the player who
brought single-string and advanced melodic technique into Punch
Brothers' chamber-bluegrass context. Reverb's three-styles primer
positions him among the modern players (with Fleck, Tony Trischka)
who took Eddie Adcock and Don Reno's single-string vocabulary "to new
heights of virtuosity," and notes that "advanced melodic positions
are used by players like Noam Pikelny."[^reverb-styles] The harmonic
significance is that single-string and melodic style let the banjo
play modal scales linearly rather than only arpeggiating chord tones
— meaning the banjo can finally articulate Dorian and Phrygian
melodies note-for-note rather than implying them through rolls.

**Sam Bush.** The "King of Newgrass" (Wikipedia / AllMusic), Bush is
the bridge from Monroe's mandolin tradition to the rock-influenced
modal jam vocabulary that defined New Grass Revival and the festival
circuit.[^sambush] His tunes routinely sit on extended modal vamps
that invite long improvisations — "Whisper My Name" (Ebo Walker, first
cut by New Grass Revival in 1972, re-cut by Bush on *Circles Around
Me* in 2009) is one example, though specific harmonic analysis of the
chart is outside sourced material here.

**Chris Thile / Punch Brothers.** See
`corpus/artists/punch-brothers-progressive-composition.md` for the
full compositional picture. For modal harmony specifically: Punch
Brothers extend bluegrass by borrowing from twentieth-century
classical and from modern jazz, using "intricate harmony and
counter-melody arrangements that are both meticulously structured
and flexible enough to allow for some jazz-like improvisations."
Thile's own framing — "It's a fragile tonality that Punch Brothers
employ. I love giving people something that seems very stable, and
then whipping the rug out from underneath them" — is the operative
description.[^punch-wiki] Jerry Douglas, quoted in the same coverage,
characterizes Punch Brothers as "more complex and cerebral than
traditional progressive bluegrass." For ARLO's purposes this means:
Punch Brothers' harmonic moves often start from a modal vamp,
chromatically reharmonize the cadence (much closer to Ravel or to
contemporary jazz than to Monroe), and resolve into a different mode
than the one the section began in.

## Voice Leading on Bluegrass Instruments

Modal harmony only sounds modal if the *voice leading on the
instruments* reflects it. The three string-band instruments handle this
differently:

**Mandolin — the chop chord.** The mandolin's chop is a percussive,
dampened four-note chord on beats 2 and 4. As Mandolin Cafe's glossary
explains, "four note chord forms are popular for chop chords because
after striking the chord the entire sound can be dampened by letting
up slightly with the left hand."[^mando-chop] For modal work the chop
becomes the carrier of modal color: a Dorian tune calls for a chop
voicing that *includes* the natural 6 (the Dorian fingerprint), not
just a generic minor triad. Thile-era voice leading routinely moves
inner voices chromatically inside the chop while the bass note holds,
producing the slow modal shifts that characterize Punch Brothers
arrangements.

**Banjo — three styles, three harmonic implications.**

- **Scruggs style.** Roll-based, forward and backward rolls
  arpeggiating the chord. The harmonic implication is *the underlying
  chord*; rolls don't typically articulate modal scale tones outside
  the chord, so modal flavor in Scruggs style is carried by the chord
  changes themselves and by hammer-on / pull-off ornaments to scale
  tones.[^scruggs-style]
- **Melodic style (Bill Keith, early 1960s).** Plays "consecutive
  scale notes on different strings," letting the banjo articulate
  fiddle and flatpicking melodies note-for-note.[^reverb-styles] This
  is the technique that makes Mixolydian and Dorian fiddle tunes
  playable as melodies on the banjo. Pikelny, Trischka, and Fleck all
  live here.
- **Single-string style (Reno, Eddie Adcock).** Plays scale runs on a
  single string before crossing. Harmonically this is the most
  flexible of the three — single-string lets the banjo execute jazz
  lines, chromatic passing tones, and any mode without rearranging
  the right hand.[^reverb-styles] Pikelny and Fleck push the modern
  edge of this technique.

**Fiddle — drones and double stops.** The open strings (G D A E) are
a built-in modal tool: a drone on open A under an A-Mixolydian melody
is the canonical sound. Double stops outline modal harmonies (a 5th
with the bottom note on the bVII is a Mixolydian flag). Cross-tunings
(A E A E, sawmill A E A D) tilt the instrument toward specific modes.

**Guitar — flatpicking and rhythm.** See
`corpus/artists/billy-strings-flatpicking-stompboxes.md` for Strings'
right hand. For modal harmony: the boom-chuck bass note carries the
root; the chord shape carries the quality. First-position open shapes
voice the bVII naturally (G-shape F, A-shape G) and keep modal
cadences inside the resonant acoustic sound.

## Extending With Classical-Borrowed Chromaticism

Punch Brothers' innovation, and where bluegrass modal harmony bleeds
into broader modal-interchange vocabulary, is the use of non-diatonic
borrowed chords against a modal background. Examples (generic, not
transcribed from specific tracks):

- A Dorian vamp on i–bVII (Am–G) suddenly substitutes a **bVI maj7**
  (F maj7) for the bVII — borrowing from Aeolian and adding extension.
- A Mixolydian I–bVII–IV loop adds a **diminished passing chord** on
  the way back to I (so I–bVII–vii°–I rather than I–bVII–I).
- A modal section pivots to its **parallel mode** for a single bar
  (Mixolydian I becomes Phrygian I — same root, completely different
  scale) before resolving back.

For the general theory of these moves see
`corpus/harmony/modal-interchange-and-borrowed-chords.md`. The
bluegrass-specific point is that string-band instrumentation makes
these substitutions sound *folk-strange* rather than *jazz-slick* —
the open strings and acoustic timbre keep them inside the bluegrass
sound-world even when the harmony has left functional tonality
behind.

## Composition Heuristics for ARLO

When ARLO is helping write a progressive-bluegrass-flavored section:

1. **Pick the mode before the chords.** Dorian or Mixolydian first;
   derive the vamp from the modal Roman-numeral kit above.
2. **Decide vamp vs. progression early.** Vamps stay home; functional
   progressions go somewhere. Mixing them needs a pivot point.
3. **Voice the modal color on the right instrument.** Dorian's natural
   6 belongs on the mandolin chop or fiddle double stop, not buried
   in the banjo roll.
4. **Let the bVII do the work.** In Mixolydian and Dorian it's the
   diagnostic chord — feature it.
5. **Borrow sparingly.** One chromatic chord per section is a Punch
   Brothers move; four is a jazz tune in a bluegrass shirt.
6. **Cross-check.** Jazz-functional reharmonization routes to
   `corpus/harmony/secondary-dominants-and-applied-chords.md`.
