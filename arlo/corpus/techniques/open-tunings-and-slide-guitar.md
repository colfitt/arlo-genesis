## What "Open Tuning" Actually Means

An open tuning re-tunes the six strings so that strumming them unfretted produces a complete major (or sometimes minor) chord. The payoff for slide work: laying a glass or metal bar straight across the strings at any fret yields an in-tune major chord. Fretting individual notes accurately with a slide is hard; barring six strings at once and trusting the open-tuned intervals is easy ([Guitar World — Open D](https://www.guitarworld.com/lessons/open-d-chords)).

The three workhorses for slide guitar are **open G**, **open D**, and **open E** — each with a different sonic personality, neck tension, and historical canon.

## Open G — D-G-D-G-B-D ("Spanish" Tuning)

Open G tunes the standard six strings, low to high, to **D-G-D-G-B-D**. From standard E-A-D-G-B-E, that's: drop the low E down to D, drop the A down to G, leave D-G-B alone, drop the high E down to D. Strum it open and you get a G major chord with G in the bass.

Open G has been called "Spanish" tuning for over a century and is the most-used open tuning in Delta blues. Robert Johnson recorded "Crossroad Blues," "Walkin' Blues," and "Come On in My Kitchen" in Spanish/open G; Charley Patton, Son House, Willie Brown, and the rest of the Delta canon are full of it ([Acoustic Guitar — Open Tunings of Robert Johnson](https://acousticguitar.com/exploring-the-open-tunings-of-robert-johnson-and-other-blues-guitarists/); [American Blues Scene — Cross-Spanish](https://www.americanbluesscene.com/language-of-the-blues-cross-spanish/)). The lineage continues through Muddy Waters and into Keith Richards, who stripped the low D off his Telecaster entirely and played a five-string open G on "Honky Tonk Women" and "Brown Sugar."

Open G also doubles as the standard bluegrass dobro/resonator tuning when raised an octave to **G-B-D-G-B-D** (high-bass) ([ArtistWorks — Dobro Tuning](https://artistworks.com/blog/dobro-tuning-complete-guide)). Billy Strings doesn't play resonator himself, but his collaborators — Jerry Douglas on the *Renewal* sessions especially — operate in this exact tuning.

**What open G is good at:** mid-string riffs (I-IV-V sit on the inner four strings nicely), Keith-style five-string chord-melody rhythm, and Delta-blues bottleneck on the higher strings while the bass strings drone the I.

## Open D — D-A-D-F#-A-D ("Vestapol" Tuning)

Open D tunes, low to high, to **D-A-D-F#-A-D**. From standard: drop low E to D, leave A, leave D, drop G to F#, drop B to A, drop high E to D. Strum it open and you get a wide-voiced D major spread across all six strings.

Open D has been called "Vestapol" tuning since the 1850s, named after a parlor-guitar piece ("The Siege of Sebastopol") that popularized it ([Raga Junglism — Open D](https://ragajunglism.org/tunings/menu/open-d/)). Robert Johnson used it ("Walking Blues" in some takes, "Come On in My Kitchen" in others), and the tuning's full bottom-end voicing made it the natural home for acoustic bottleneck blues, Joni Mitchell's open-tuned ballads, and Neil Young's heavier acoustic moments ([Wikipedia — Open D](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Open_D_tuning)).

**The structural argument for open D over open G:** because the D root sits on the bottom string and the open strings span two octaves of D with a high D on top, open D suits **fingerstyle** — the thumb drones the bass D while the fingers handle melody on top. Open G has D (the fifth) on the bottom string and G (the root) on the next — a less stable bass pedal, which is part of why open G tends toward strummed/slid riff playing and open D toward fingerpicked accompaniment.

**Tension note:** open D is the lowest-tension of the three, *reducing* total neck tension by roughly 15–20% versus standard — friendly to fragile acoustics ([theacousticguitarist.com](https://theacousticguitarist.com/the-best-tuning-for-acoustic-slide-guitar-open-e-tuning/)).

## Open E — E-B-E-G#-B-E

Open E tunes, low to high, to **E-B-E-G#-B-E**. From standard: leave low E, raise A up to B, raise D up to E, raise G up to G#, leave B, leave high E. Open E is the **interval twin of open D** — identical intervals, just pitched a whole step higher. Capo open D at the second fret and you've effectively got open E.

The catch is the structural opposite of open D: raising three strings adds roughly 15–20 pounds of tension, hard on acoustics and some short-scale electrics ([Telecaster Guitar Forum thread](https://www.tdpri.com/threads/open-e-tuning-too-much-tension.268660/)). Many acoustic players who want the open-E sound tune to open D and capo II rather than stress the neck.

Open E is most associated with **Duane Allman** (the *Layla* slide passages, "Statesboro Blues") and his heir **Derek Trucks**, who plays open E exclusively on stage with .011–.049 strings and a glass slide on his ring finger ([MusicRadar — Derek Trucks on the art of slide](https://www.musicradar.com/news/guitars/derek-trucks-on-the-art-of-slide-598954); [Guitar Player — Derek Trucks on Open E](https://www.guitarplayer.com/guitarists/derek-trucks-on-open-e-tuning)). The brighter pitch and tight high-E top string give open E a cutting, electric-friendly character — slide notes ring out over a band rather than blend into an acoustic mix.

## The Lineage: Johnson → Cooder → Trucks → Raitt

Slide-guitar history is short and unusually traceable. Each generation transmitted something specific forward:

- **Robert Johnson (1911–1938)** — the Delta canon in Spanish/open G and Vestapol/open D. Used the slide sparsely, punctuating vocal phrases on the "ands" between beats rather than as a constant lead voice. Wore a metal slide on his pinkie ([Guitar World — Unlocking Robert Johnson](https://www.guitarworld.com/lessons/deep-unlocking-guitar-mysteries-delta-blues-great-robert-johnson)).
- **Ry Cooder (b. 1947)** — translated Delta and gospel slide vocabulary into a meticulous, dynamic, microtonal style; brought slide into film score and world-music contexts. See [Ry Cooder Slide Guitar and the Coodercaster](../artists/ry-cooder-slide-guitar-and-coodercaster.md) for instrument-specific details.
- **Bonnie Raitt (b. 1949)** — early-'70s blues-revival cohort. Famously self-taught: "I didn't see anybody play slide, so I just figured it out in my room — and I put the bottleneck on the wrong finger" ([Guitar World — Bonnie Raitt interview](https://www.guitarworld.com/features/bonnie-raitt-just-like-that)). Wears her slide on the **middle finger** rather than the more common ring or pinkie, which lets her flip between slide and fretted chords fluidly — at the cost of balancing it precariously when fingering chords behind it.
- **Derek Trucks (b. 1979)** — absorbed Duane Allman's open-E vocabulary, then extended slide into modal jazz and Indian classical territory. Fingerstyle (no pick), glass slide, exclusively open E. His stated obsession is **intonation**: "it's a lot like singing. You can go after all these runs, but unless you're hitting the notes and sliding into them the right way, and the inflections are right, then you lose the whole purpose" ([MusicRadar interview](https://www.musicradar.com/news/guitars/derek-trucks-on-the-art-of-slide-598954)).

The through-line: every player in this lineage learned by ear, treated the slide as a singing voice rather than a guitar effect, and built a vocabulary around intonation and vibrato rather than speed.

## Slide Materials — Glass vs. Brass vs. Steel

A slide is a tube of dense material worn on a finger. The two big variables are **material** and **weight**, and they trade off against each other.

- **Glass (bottleneck, Pyrex, borosilicate)** — Warmer, rounder, mellower tone with smoother high-string response. Less raspy on the wound bass strings than metal. Lighter, so easier to control at speed but with less natural sustain. The classic "bottleneck" — early Delta players literally cut the necks off glass medicine bottles; Bonnie Raitt famously soaked the label off a Coricidin cold-medicine bottle ([Acoustic Guitar — How to Choose a Slide](https://acousticguitar.com/lesson-how-to-choose-the-right-guitar-slide-video/); [Sweetwater — Choose the Right Slide](https://www.sweetwater.com/insync/choose-right-guitar-slide/)).
- **Brass** — Heavier, darker than steel but brighter than glass. Long sustain, aggressive vibrato character, but harder to control because of the mass. Favored by players who want their slide notes to *cut*.
- **Steel / chrome-plated steel** — The brightest of the common metals, sharpest attack, most sustain per gram. Robert Johnson used a metal slide. Square-neck dobro players use a bullet-nose steel **tonebar** (different from a finger slide — it's held in the hand, not worn).

**Heavier slide = more sustain, less control; lighter slide = more agility, less sustain.** A practical starting point is a medium glass slide for acoustic and a heavier brass or steel for electric. Specific brand/weight claims attached to specific players are mostly folk knowledge — verify any "Trucks uses exactly an X-gram Y" statement before treating it as fact.

## String Gauge and Action — Why Slides Eat Light Strings

Slide playing fights against standard "easy to fret" setup on two fronts:

1. **The slide presses down on the strings** with its own weight. Light strings (.009s, .010s) deflect and contact the frets, producing buzz and inconsistent pitch.
2. **The slide moves across all six strings at once.** If action is too low, the slide drags on the frets rather than gliding above them.

The fix: **heavier strings, higher action.** Common slide setups run **.011–.049** or **.012–.052**, with dedicated acoustic slide players going as heavy as **.013–.056** or **.014–.058** ([Guitar World — Slide setup](https://www.guitarworld.com/features/how-to-set-up-your-guitar-for-slide); [One Finger Guitarist — String Gauges](https://www.onefingerguitarist.com/blog/string-gauges)). Action gets raised by 1/16" to 3/32" at the high-E end of the bridge.

Billy Strings runs heavy strings on his Preston Thompson flattop (.013-and-up territory), part of why his rare slide gestures sit so confidently — the strings can handle a slide without a setup change. The compromise for players who alternate slide and fretted on the same guitar is around **.011–.049 medium**: stiff enough under a slide, light enough to bend conventionally when it comes off.

## Vibrato — The Mark of an Intermediate Slide Player

Slide vibrato is the single technique that separates beginners from everyone else. The mechanic: with the slide held parallel to the fret, **oscillate the slide back and forth along the length of the string** — left-right relative to the fret line, *not* perpendicular like a fretted-hand bend ([Guitar World — Slide Vibrato](https://www.guitarworld.com/lessons/how-to-develop-a-great-slide-vibrato)). The movement is small, controlled, and comes from the whole forearm-and-hand assembly, not just the fingertip.

The classical-violin analogy is exact: a violinist rolls the fingertip along the string to create pitch oscillation; a slide player does the same with the bar. Wider oscillation = dramatic, vocal vibrato (Trucks-style). Narrower, faster = blues-traditional shimmer (Cooder, Raitt).

A novice typically slides *into* a note and holds it dead-still — which sounds amateurish and exposes any intonation problem. A small controlled vibrato around the target pitch both disguises minor intonation drift and animates the note. Trucks has said his vibrato study came from vocalists and sarangi players, not other guitarists.

## Intonation — The Slide Goes *Over* the Fret, Not Behind It

Standard fretted guitar: press the string down *behind* the fret, and the fret stops the string at the correct length. Slide guitar: the *slide* is the stop. Therefore the slide must sit **directly over the metal of the target fret**, not behind it ([Derek Trucks — Learning Guitar Now](https://www.learningguitarnow.com/slide-guitar/derek-trucks-open-e-tuning-slide-guitar-lesson/)). Hold it perfectly parallel so all strings stop at the same length — tilting even slightly detunes the chord across strings, a giveaway of an inexperienced player.

## Muting and Damping — Why Slide Demands Fingerstyle

The slide doesn't choose which strings ring. Without muting, every open string in your tuning sustains forever — a wash of drone that swallows the melody. Good technique uses **both hands** ([Peghead Nation — Damping](https://www.pegheadnation.com/string-school/courses/blues-guitar/slide-guitar-basics-part-2-damping/)):

- **Fretting-hand damping:** fingers *behind* the slide (closer to the nut) rest lightly on the strings, muting whatever isn't being played.
- **Picking-hand damping:** thumb and unused fingers rest on bass strings; the palm damps aggressive bass drone.

This is most of the reason serious slide players prefer **fingerstyle over flatpicking**: with bare fingers, each finger plucks *and* mutes, and unused fingers rest on adjacent strings as live mutes. Cooder, Trucks, Raitt, and Sonny Landreth all play fingerstyle for this reason.

## Bottleneck vs. Lap Steel vs. Resonator

Same slide-bar principle, three different instruments and posture conventions:

- **Bottleneck (Spanish position)** — Standard guitar held against the body, slide on a finger. Can switch between slid and fretted notes freely. This is the Johnson → Cooder → Trucks → Raitt lineage above ([Long & McQuade — Slide Guitar Types](https://www.long-mcquade.com/blog/96/Slide-Guitars-What-types-are-there-and-what-defines-them.htm)).
- **Lap steel** — Guitar laid flat on the lap or a stand, very high action (so high you can't fret it at all), heavy bullet-nose steel **tonebar** held in the palm. No fretting option — slide all the time. Different vocabulary: slants (tilting the bar for non-parallel intervals), behind-the-bar pulls, palm-blocked staccato attacks. The pedal-steel sound descends from here.
- **Resonator / dobro** — A guitar with a metal cone amplifying the strings acoustically. Square-neck resonators are played lap-style in high-bass G-B-D-G-B-D (Jerry Douglas's world); round-neck resonators can go either Spanish-style or lap-style. Cooder's *Paris, Texas* tone comes from a round-neck metal-bodied National.

Choosing between these isn't really choosing a technique — it's choosing how much fretboard chord work you want available alongside your slide vocabulary. Bottleneck keeps both; lap steel commits fully to the bar.
