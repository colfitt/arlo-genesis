## The Session Story In One Paragraph

*Who Will Cut Our Hair When We're Gone?* is the second and final studio album by The Unicorns — Nicholas Thorburn, Alden Penner and drummer Jamie Thompson — recorded in Montreal in June and July 2003 with engineer-producer Mark Lawson and released on Alien8 Recordings on 21 October 2003 ([Wikipedia, *Who Will Cut Our Hair When We're Gone?*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Who_Will_Cut_Our_Hair_When_We%27re_Gone%3F); [Alien8 Recordings, release page](http://alien8recordings.com/releases/who-will-cut-our-hair-when-were-gone)). The album re-arranged and re-tracked a number of songs from the band's self-released 2003 CD *Unicorns Are People Too* and added enough new material to constitute a real debut on a proper indie label ([Wikipedia, *The Unicorns*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Unicorns)). Its sonic identity is Casiotone keyboards, lo-fi guitar and organ, programmed and acoustic drums layered together, two contrasting voices trading and overlapping leads, and a closed-system lyric world saturated with ghosts, death, and self-conscious mortality jokes ([Cokemachineglow, *Who Will Cut Our Hair When We're Gone?*](http://cokemachineglow.com/records/unicorns-whowillcutourhair-2003/); [The Collegian, *Playback: The Unicorns — Who Will Cut Our Hair When We're Gone?*](https://tucollegian.org/playback-the-unicorns-who-will-cut-our-hair-when-were-gone/)). It received a Pitchfork Best New Music designation with an 8.9 score, sat in the Montreal indie scene that produced Arcade Fire's *Funeral* the following year (Richard Reed Parry plays on the Unicorns record), and remains the definitive document of a band that broke up — bitterly, on tour — within 14 months of its release ([Stereogum, *Who Will Cut Our Hair When We're Gone? Turns 20*](https://www.stereogum.com/2238841/the-unicorns-who-will-cut-our-hair-when-were-gone-turns-20/reviews/the-anniversary/); [Wikipedia, *The Unicorns*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Unicorns)).

## The Hotel2Tango Question — What The Record Says And Doesn't Say

A note on studio attribution worth getting right. The Wikipedia article for *Who Will Cut Our Hair When We're Gone?* specifies the recording dates ("June – July 2003") and the producer (Mark Lawson) but does *not* name a specific studio ([Wikipedia, *Who Will Cut Our Hair When We're Gone?*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Who_Will_Cut_Our_Hair_When_We%27re_Gone%3F)). The Stereogum 20th-anniversary feature describes the sessions as taking place "with Grammy-winning audio engineer Mark Lawson" in Montreal but offers no further room-level detail ([Stereogum, *Who Will Cut Our Hair When We're Gone? Turns 20*](https://www.stereogum.com/2238841/the-unicorns-who-will-cut-our-hair-when-were-gone-turns-20/reviews/the-anniversary/)). Lawson's own published biography confirms he moved to Montreal in 2003 explicitly to work with bands like The Unicorns and Arcade Fire, and his subsequent career as the Arcade Fire camp's go-to engineer cements him as a Hotel2Tango-orbit figure ([SoundBetter, Mark Lawson profile](https://soundbetter.com/profiles/87758-mix-monster-mark); [Performer Magazine, *Arcade Fire Engineer Mark Lawson Takes UA Hardware On A Trip To The Suburbs*](https://performermag.com/music-news/arcade-fire-engineer-mark-lawson-takes-ua-hardware-on-a-trip-to-the-suburbs/)). Hotel2Tango was operational by 2003 — Arcade Fire would track much of *Funeral* there over eight months starting later that year — but the Unicorns sessions appear to predate the heavy band-incumbency that defined the room from 2004 onward, and no source consulted for this entry explicitly places *Who Will Cut Our Hair* at Hotel2Tango. The room attribution should be treated as plausible-but-undocumented; the scene attribution is firm. For the broader Hotel2Tango build-out and Constellation/Montreal context, see the Godspeed *Lift Yr. Skinny Fists* sibling file.

## Two Voices — Thorburn And Penner

The album's compositional identity is built on the contrast between its two singer-songwriters. Nicholas Thorburn (writing under the stage name Nick "Neil" Diamonds) and Alden Penner (Alden Ginger) had been friends since high school in Campbell River, British Columbia, where they met in 1998; Thorburn enrolled at McGill in 1999 and the band formed in Montreal around Christmas 2000 ([Wikipedia, *The Unicorns*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Unicorns); [Stereogum, *Who Will Cut Our Hair When We're Gone? Turns 20*](https://www.stereogum.com/2238841/the-unicorns-who-will-cut-our-hair-when-were-gone-turns-20/reviews/the-anniversary/)). Their voices are sharply differentiated on the record — Thorburn's higher and more nasal, Penner's softer and more melodic — and almost every track trades or doubles between them, producing a call-and-response feel that doubles as a structural pun on the band's two-headed creative core. Drummer Jamie Thompson, who joined later as a third member, plays a mediating role in the songs the same way he reportedly did in the band's internal politics ([Stereogum, *Who Will Cut Our Hair When We're Gone? Turns 20*](https://www.stereogum.com/2238841/the-unicorns-who-will-cut-our-hair-when-were-gone-turns-20/reviews/the-anniversary/); [Substack, stübermania, *A Conversation with Nick Thorburn of The Unicorns from 2003*](https://stubermania.substack.com/p/a-conversation-with-nick-thorburn)).

## The Casiotone Sound

The album's instrumental signature is the consumer-grade Casio keyboard, played as an actual lead instrument rather than as ironic prop. The opening track is driven by toy keyboards, squiggling sound effects and false-start guitar licks; "Jellybones" pivots on what one contemporary review called "crackling, evil keyboard phrasing"; "Tuff Ghost" leans on toy keyboards and synth-pop undercurrent to undercut its sinister tone ([Cokemachineglow, *Who Will Cut Our Hair When We're Gone?*](http://cokemachineglow.com/records/unicorns-whowillcutourhair-2003/); [The Collegian, *Playback: The Unicorns — Who Will Cut Our Hair When We're Gone?*](https://tucollegian.org/playback-the-unicorns-who-will-cut-our-hair-when-were-gone/)). The Casio aesthetic was a 1990s Daniel Johnston / K Records cassette-pop inheritance reactivated for an early-2000s indie context — the kit is cheap, the sound is identifiable, the gesture is anti-virtuosic. That the band would later cover Daniel Johnston's "Rocket Ship" as a 2014 reissue bonus track makes the lineage explicit ([Consequence, *The Unicorns announce reissue of Who Will Cut Our Hair When We're Gone?*](https://consequence.net/2014/07/the-unicorns-announce-reissue-of-who-will-cut-our-hair-when-were-gone/)).

## Programmed Drums And Acoustic Drums Together

Thompson's drum tracks alternate between programmed beats and live kit playing, sometimes within a single song. The album's loose live feel is enabled by the live drumming; its lo-fi consumer-electronics feel is enabled by the programmed sections; the willingness to swap between them mid-track is what gives songs like "Jellybones" their structural unpredictability — "extremely simple keyboard lines and pounding drums" yielding to "electronic drone" and then "breakbeats" without a verse-chorus signpost to mark the transition ([Cokemachineglow, *Who Will Cut Our Hair When We're Gone?*](http://cokemachineglow.com/records/unicorns-whowillcutourhair-2003/)). The drum approach is itself a stylistic position — the album refuses the choice between a band that plays and a band that programs.

## The Track Sequence And The Death Cycle

The thirteen tracks are sequenced as a single mortality cycle: the album opens with "I Don't Wanna Die" and closes with "Ready to Die," with eleven songs of varying confidence about the question in between ([Wikipedia, *Who Will Cut Our Hair When We're Gone?*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Who_Will_Cut_Our_Hair_When_We%27re_Gone%3F); [Alien8 Recordings, release page](http://alien8recordings.com/releases/who-will-cut-our-hair-when-were-gone)). "Tuff Ghost," "Ghost Mountain" and "Sea Ghost" form a haunted triptych across the sequence; "Jellybones," "Inoculate the Innocuous" and "Child Star" handle disease, infection and burnout; "Les Os" (French for "the bones") and "I Was Born (a Unicorn)" sit closer to nursery-rhyme territory; "The Clap," "Let's Get Known" and "Tuff Luff" lean into showbusiness fatalism ([Wikipedia, *Who Will Cut Our Hair When We're Gone?*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Who_Will_Cut_Our_Hair_When_We%27re_Gone%3F)). The genius of the record is that the morbid material is delivered with what Pitchfork's Eric Carr described as the ability to "continually and effortlessly sap the drama from rock's favorite, most maudlin topics, and transform them into simple, charming, light-hearted *fun*" ([Stereogum, *Who Will Cut Our Hair When We're Gone? Turns 20*](https://www.stereogum.com/2238841/the-unicorns-who-will-cut-our-hair-when-were-gone-turns-20/reviews/the-anniversary/)).

## "I Don't Wanna Die" And The Arcade Fire Trumpet

The opening track sets the album's terms: a Casio-driven mid-tempo pop song whose chorus is literally the title repeated as a refrain, with Richard Reed Parry's trumpet adding a thin lyrical horn line that prefigures Arcade Fire's later use of brass on *Funeral* ([Wikipedia, *Who Will Cut Our Hair When We're Gone?*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Who_Will_Cut_Our_Hair_When_We%27re_Gone%3F); [Stereogum, *Who Will Cut Our Hair When We're Gone? Turns 20*](https://www.stereogum.com/2238841/the-unicorns-who-will-cut-our-hair-when-were-gone-turns-20/reviews/the-anniversary/)). Parry plays bowed bass on "Sea Ghost" as well, and is credited with additional recording assistance — these are not just guest spots but evidence of the very small Montreal scene the album emerged from. Arcade Fire would record *Funeral* across late 2003 and early 2004 with Mark Lawson at Hotel2Tango while The Unicorns toured *Who Will Cut Our Hair* into oblivion; the two records form a paired document of the same Mile End moment from inside and just outside the Hotel2Tango orbit ([MusicTech, *Landmark Productions: Arcade Fire — Funeral*](https://musictech.com/guides/essential-guide/landmarks-arcade-fire/); [Performer Magazine, *Arcade Fire Engineer Mark Lawson*](https://performermag.com/music-news/arcade-fire-engineer-mark-lawson-takes-ua-hardware-on-a-trip-to-the-suburbs/)).

## "Jellybones" And The Structural Refusal

"Jellybones" is the album's most-cited single-track example of its structural restlessness. The song opens with a scrambled synthesizer figure and excellent live drumming over a backing guitar riff, accumulates energy past the two-minute mark, then breaks abruptly into a lo-fi acoustic passage that functions less as a bridge than as an interrupted second song hiding inside the first ([The Collegian, *Playback: The Unicorns — Who Will Cut Our Hair When We're Gone?*](https://tucollegian.org/playback-the-unicorns-who-will-cut-our-hair-when-were-gone/)). The track is built like a medley by a band that doesn't have separate songs yet. This is the move that Pitchfork was singling out when it praised the album's "essentially formless compositions" that nonetheless deliver "beautiful, memorable lines with such remarkable ease" ([Stereogum, *Who Will Cut Our Hair When We're Gone? Turns 20*](https://www.stereogum.com/2238841/the-unicorns-who-will-cut-our-hair-when-were-gone-turns-20/reviews/the-anniversary/)). The structural refusal is the band's main compositional asset.

## "Tuff Ghost," "Ghost Mountain," "Sea Ghost"

The three ghost songs operate as a thematic spine across the sequence. "Tuff Ghost" — track two — is the brisk one, with stark tempo changes that whiplash between drive and shuffle under sinister toy-keyboard riffs and synth-pop undercurrent ([The Collegian, *Playback: The Unicorns — Who Will Cut Our Hair When We're Gone?*](https://tucollegian.org/playback-the-unicorns-who-will-cut-our-hair-when-were-gone/)). "Ghost Mountain" sits later in the sequence and is the most overtly haunted in arrangement — long sustained organ tones under the vocal. "Sea Ghost" features Parry's bowed bass and reads as the trilogy's elegy, slower and stranger than its siblings ([Wikipedia, *Who Will Cut Our Hair When We're Gone?*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Who_Will_Cut_Our_Hair_When_We%27re_Gone%3F)). The recurring "ghost" framing is one of the album's identifying conceits — the band literally sings about its own future absence, which the December-2004 breakup retrofitted as prophecy.

## "Inoculate the Innocuous" And The Lyric Density

The album's longer titles — "Inoculate the Innocuous," "I Was Born (a Unicorn)" — perform their own kind of structural joke, packing alliteration and parenthetical aside into ostensibly disposable pop. "Inoculate the Innocuous" runs noticeably slower and stranger than the surrounding tracks, with Penner's softer voice leading; the title's tongue-twister density mirrors the song's wandering shape. The lyric world is built from juvenile-rhyme components that the album then weaponises into something genuinely uncanny — death rhymes, ghost rhymes, body-part rhymes ("Les Os"), the "Unicorn" naming-joke deployed as self-mythology in "I Was Born (a Unicorn)" ([Wikipedia, *Who Will Cut Our Hair When We're Gone?*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Who_Will_Cut_Our_Hair_When_We%27re_Gone%3F)). The album never quotes a piece of canonical pop, but it eats every era of pop's lyric conventions.

## Alien8 Recordings And The Montreal Independent Layout

Alien8 Recordings was the right label for the moment: a Montreal independent with deep avant-garde and experimental catalogue (Tim Hecker, Khanate, Acid Mothers Temple), willing to release a pop record from a band whose pop instincts were filtered through a punk-show-promoter sensibility ([Alien8 Recordings, release page](http://alien8recordings.com/releases/who-will-cut-our-hair-when-were-gone); [Stereogum, *Who Will Cut Our Hair When We're Gone? Turns 20*](https://www.stereogum.com/2238841/the-unicorns-who-will-cut-our-hair-when-were-gone-turns-20/reviews/the-anniversary/)). The CD was issued in a custom gatefold sleeve with original artwork by Thorburn (under his Nicholas Diamonds visual-art identity); the album's physical-object aesthetic mirrors the music — handmade, charming, more deliberate than it looks ([Alien8 Recordings, release page](http://alien8recordings.com/releases/who-will-cut-our-hair-when-were-gone)). The Arcade Fire / Alien8 handshake deal that almost happened the following year is a useful indicator of how small the Montreal indie ecosystem still was in 2003 — Alien8 was effectively the most plausible release home in town for an album like *Who Will Cut Our Hair*, and the Arcade Fire connection through Parry made the transaction frictionless.

## Critical Reception And The Pitchfork Effect

Critical reception was strong on release — Metacritic 77 across professional reviews — and grew through the 2010s into broadly canonical status for the early-2000s indie-pop generation ([Wikipedia, *Who Will Cut Our Hair When We're Gone?*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Who_Will_Cut_Our_Hair_When_We%27re_Gone%3F)). Eric Carr's 8.9 review in Pitchfork — published when Pitchfork's tastemaking grip on the indie underground was at its tightest — carried disproportionate audience-aggregation weight ([Stereogum, *Who Will Cut Our Hair When We're Gone? Turns 20*](https://www.stereogum.com/2238841/the-unicorns-who-will-cut-our-hair-when-were-gone-turns-20/reviews/the-anniversary/)). The album's retrospective reputation has if anything outgrown its initial reception: it is now a routine entry on best-Canadian-indie-rock lists and was the central artifact of The Unicorns' 2014 reunion narrative ([Consequence, *The Unicorns announce reissue of Who Will Cut Our Hair When We're Gone?*](https://consequence.net/2014/07/the-unicorns-announce-reissue-of-who-will-cut-our-hair-when-were-gone/); [Billboard, *The Unicorns Return: Nick Thorburn Talks Arcade Fire, Album Reissue & Possible New Music*](https://www.billboard.com/music/music-news/unicorns-interview-nick-thorburn-arcade-fire-6188032/)).

## The Breakup And The Reissue

The Unicorns toured *Who Will Cut Our Hair* aggressively across North America, Europe and Australia through 2004, with internal tension escalating throughout. The band's final performance was on 20 December 2004 at the Engine Room in Houston, Texas; on 28 December the website announced "THE UNICORNS ARE DEAD, (R.I.P.)" and the breakup was confirmed in January 2005 ([Wikipedia, *The Unicorns*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Unicorns)). Thompson would later describe the touring experience as: "we all hated it like 70 percent of the time" ([Wikipedia, *The Unicorns*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Unicorns)). The album was reissued on 26 August 2014 (CD) and 7 October 2014 (vinyl) on the band's own Caterpillar Records imprint, with four bonus tracks including a Daniel Johnston "Rocket Ship" cover, two outtakes from the original 2003 sessions ("Let Me Sleep" and "Evacuate the Vacuous"), and a 2004 live recording ([Consequence, *The Unicorns announce reissue of Who Will Cut Our Hair When We're Gone?*](https://consequence.net/2014/07/the-unicorns-announce-reissue-of-who-will-cut-our-hair-when-were-gone/)). The reissue coincided with a brief reunion tour supporting Arcade Fire at arena shows in Los Angeles and Brooklyn — the closing of a circle that Richard Reed Parry's 2003 trumpet contribution had opened ([Billboard, *The Unicorns Return*](https://www.billboard.com/music/music-news/unicorns-interview-nick-thorburn-arcade-fire-6188032/)).

## Mix And Production Logic

Three observations a careful listener can verify. First, the mix is intentionally narrow and busy: Casios, organs, guitars and drums are mostly mid-range-heavy, with little of the wide-stereo separation a more conventional indie-rock production would deploy. The crowdedness is the point — a basement-pop record needs the basement claustrophobia. Second, vocal layering is the album's main spatial trick: Thorburn and Penner double, harmonise and trade leads constantly, and the vocal stack is where most of the album's frequency width lives. Third, Mark Lawson's signal-chain instinct, on display here in primitive form, is the one he would refine on Arcade Fire's *Funeral* and *Neon Bible* — preserve the band's compositional left-turns rather than smoothing them, mic the room rather than the source where possible, mix to retain the eccentric instrument's identity rather than to make it sit politely ([Performer Magazine, *Arcade Fire Engineer Mark Lawson*](https://performermag.com/music-news/arcade-fire-engineer-mark-lawson-takes-ua-hardware-on-a-trip-to-the-suburbs/); [SoundBetter, Mark Lawson profile](https://soundbetter.com/profiles/87758-mix-monster-mark)).

## What This Record Teaches The Corpus

- **A consumer-grade instrument can be the lead voice of a record.** The Casio is treated as a legitimate compositional centre, not as a sonic gag. The album's identity collapses if the Casio is replaced with a "proper" synth.
- **Programmed and live drums can co-exist within a single track without irony.** The Unicorns refuse the indie-rock-vs-electronic divide of their moment by simply playing both, sometimes in the same song.
- **Two-singer bands can compose around the contrast rather than rotating leads.** Thorburn and Penner trade and overlap within songs, making the dual-voice structure a recurring sonic event rather than a track-level assignment.
- **A breakup can retrospectively organise an album.** The death/ghost lyric cycle, in 2003, read as morbid pop wit; after December 2004, it reads as accidental autobiography. The album's mortality material aged forward into a different valence.
- **Studio attribution requires care for tight-knit scenes.** *Who Will Cut Our Hair* is a Mark-Lawson-engineered, Arcade-Fire-adjacent, Mile-End Montreal record from 2003 — but no available source places it specifically at Hotel2Tango, and the scene-attribution-vs-room-attribution distinction matters when handing down a corpus.

- [Billboard, *The Unicorns Return: Nick Thorburn Talks Arcade Fire, Album Reissue & Possible New Music*](https://www.billboard.com/music/music-news/unicorns-interview-nick-thorburn-arcade-fire-6188032/) — Thorburn's reflections on the 2004 breakup ("egos getting involved," "young band without a clear vision"), Arcade Fire as catalyst for the 2014 reunion, the album's continuing reputation.

- [Performer Magazine, *Arcade Fire Engineer Mark Lawson Takes UA Hardware On A Trip To The Suburbs*](https://performermag.com/music-news/arcade-fire-engineer-mark-lawson-takes-ua-hardware-on-a-trip-to-the-suburbs/) — Lawson's signal-chain approach on Arcade Fire's *The Suburbs*, refined version of the production logic first deployed on the Unicorns record; confirms Lawson as Arcade Fire's engineer through *Funeral*, *Neon Bible* and *The Suburbs*.
- [Cokemachineglow, *The Unicorns: Who Will Cut Our Hair When We're Gone?*](http://cokemachineglow.com/records/unicorns-whowillcutourhair-2003/) — contemporary review; observations on Casio-keyboard centrality, the "extremely simple keyboard lines and pounding drums" production posture, the album's avoidance of verse-chorus structures, the dynamic shifts and electronic-drone-into-breakbeat transitions, the morbid-meets-sugary tonal trick.
- [The Collegian, *Playback: The Unicorns — Who Will Cut Our Hair When We're Gone?*](https://tucollegian.org/playback-the-unicorns-who-will-cut-our-hair-when-were-gone/) — track-level descriptions of "Tuff Ghost" (toy keyboards, synth-pop undercurrent, stark tempo changes), "Jellybones" (scrambled synthesizer, two-minute pivot to lo-fi acoustic), Casiotone identification across the record.
- [MusicTech, *Landmark Productions: Arcade Fire — Funeral*](https://musictech.com/guides/essential-guide/landmarks-arcade-fire/) — Hotel2Tango as the room where Arcade Fire would track *Funeral* across eight months from late 2003; Mark Lawson's role; used as scene-context confirmation rather than direct attribution for the Unicorns sessions.
- [Substack, stübermania, *A Conversation with Nick Thorburn of The Unicorns from 2003*](https://stubermania.substack.com/p/a-conversation-with-nick-thorburn) — period-document interview from 2003 itself; Thorburn on the controlled-vs-innocuous production posture, the band's "making pop music" framing, Jamie Thompson as inter-band mediator.
