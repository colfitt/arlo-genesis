# LCD Soundsystem — *This Is Happening* (2010)

## What the record is, and where it sits in the catalog

*This Is Happening* is the third LCD Soundsystem album, released 17 May 2010 on DFA / Virgin (US) and Parlophone (rest of world), recorded 1 April 2009 – 24 February 2010 primarily at **the Mansion** in Los Angeles with additional sessions at DFA in New York ([Wikipedia — This Is Happening](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/This_Is_Happening)). Credits read **produced by James Murphy** — no Goldsworthy co-production this time — with mixing on four tracks ("Drunk Girls," "I Can Change," "You Wanted a Hit," "Somebody's Calling Me") handled by **Dave Sardy** and the rest mixed by Murphy; engineering assists from **Matthew Thornley, Gunnar Bjerk, and Alec Gomez**. The record sits between [*Sound of Silver* (2007)](./lcd-sound-of-silver.md) — Plantain, Murphy + Goldsworthy — and *American Dream* (2017). For three years it was officially the *last* LCD record. The 2 April 2011 Madison Square Garden farewell show and the *Shut Up and Play the Hits* documentary closed that chapter; the 2015–2017 reunion reopened it. ARLO should treat *This Is Happening* as an album made *believing it was the end*, regardless of what the catalog later did to that framing.

## The Mansion at 2451 Laurel Canyon — and why live-where-you-record matters

The band tracked at **the Mansion**, a four-bedroom 1918 house at **2451 Laurel Canyon Boulevard**, owned by **Rick Rubin** and run as a residential recording facility ([Wikipedia — The Mansion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Mansion_(recording_studio)); [Rock and Roll Roadmap](https://rockandrollroadmap.com/places/miscellaneous/los-angeles-area-miscellaneous/the-mansion/)). It is *Laurel Canyon*, not the Hollywood Hills proper, and is distinct from the Houdini Estate at 2400 Laurel Canyon — a confusion that has persisted in the press for decades. Rubin has used the Mansion as his production base since cutting Red Hot Chili Peppers' *Blood Sugar Sex Magik* there in 1991; prior residents include the Mars Volta (*De-Loused in the Comatorium*), Audioslave, System of a Down, Linkin Park. LCD's sessions were documented in eight clips Murphy uploaded to the band's YouTube channel ([Fuse TV](https://www.fuse.tv/v/lcd-soundsystem-recorded-this-is-happening-in-an-all-white-mansion-tbt-2010/6jM2vd9p)). Murphy on why he does residencies: *"I always have to live where I'm recording to make an LCD record otherwise I get distracted, I don't get to work 'til three"* ([NME](https://www.nme.com/news/music/lcd-soundsystem-22-1293072)). The same logic governed the early Plantain era when Murphy lived in the DFA building for ~2.5 years (see [James Murphy Analog Synth Philosophy](../artists/james-murphy-analog-synth-philosophy.md)). Relocating the whole band to Laurel Canyon was an escalation — the band "slept, partied and recorded all within one setting" ([audiosnobbery](https://www.audiosnobbery.com/tripping-out-on-lcd-soundsystem/)). ARLO should say "the Mansion, Rick Rubin's residential studio at 2451 Laurel Canyon" — *not* "Hollywood Hills," *not* "the Houdini house."

## The supposed-final-album framing

Murphy was unusually public about *This Is Happening* being LCD's last record while it was still being made. He had told himself privately he'd be done by forty; the album was framed in interviews as the end of LCD as a touring, album-cycle entity. He later qualified that the language had been imprecise — what he meant was the end of "the band being a professional entity… like a professional rock band that makes albums and videos and tours," not the literal dissolution of the people involved. After the 2017 reunion he said: *"For the rest of my life, no matter what happens in this band, we'll never 'break up' again. One day we'll just stop making music, but no one is going to say a fucking word about it ahead of time"* ([SPIN](https://www.spin.com/2017/08/lcd-soundsystem-james-murphy-breaking-up-interview/)). The retroactive correction matters, but it doesn't change what the *record itself* was made under: every song was tracked believing this was the last time. That belief is audible in "Home," in the second-person addresses of "I Can Change" and "All I Want," and in the gallows showmanship of "Pow Pow."

## Personnel and the Russom transition

The credited personnel lock in the lineup that would walk onstage at MSG the following April: **James Murphy** (vocals, drums on tracks 2–4, 6, 9, synthesizer on most tracks, Wurlitzer, percussion, guitar, piano, bass), **Pat Mahoney** (drums, vocals — see [Pat Mahoney Dance-Punk Drumming](../artists/pat-mahoney-dance-punk-drumming.md)), **Nancy Whang** (vocals, yells), **Tyler Pope** (bass), **Al Doyle** (guitar), **Gavilán Rayna Russom** (synthesizer, vocals), **Morgan Wiley** (piano), **Jason Disu** (trombone) ([Discogs](https://www.discogs.com/master/248221-LCD-Soundsystem-This-Is-Happening)). The record is dedicated to **Jerry Fuchs (1974–2009)** — the !!!/Maserati/Juan Maclean drummer and DFA-family member who died in November 2009 during the writing — the only printed acknowledgement that grief was in the room. Russom's contribution is worth separating: through prior LCD records she had been the DFA in-house synth technician and instrument-builder (modifying the CS-60's back panel for CV-from-audio, building custom modular instruments). For *This Is Happening*, Murphy invited her out to the Mansion, and over those sessions she crossed from technician to credited band member ([NPR — LCD Soundsystem's Synth Genius Gavin Rayna Russom](https://www.npr.org/sections/therecord/2017/10/26/559112386/there-is-no-done-gavin-rayna-russom-on-the-dialogue-between-creation-and-identit)). The most-cited specific contribution: the "out of time synth thing" — an experimental, off-grid modular part — edited into "Drunk Girls," which she also co-wrote with Murphy and Mahoney. ARLO move: *let one synth part drift deliberately out of tempo and chop it back in* — a documented Mansion-era technique.

## Engineering — the Goldsworthy gap, the Sardy mix split

This is the record where Murphy is most visibly working without early producer-partner Tim Goldsworthy, who had co-piloted the first two LCD albums. Murphy produces alone here. Equally absent is **Eric Broucek**, Murphy's Plantain signal-flow partner on *Sound of Silver* — by 2009, Broucek's "most fertile era" at DFA (roughly 2003–2008) had ended and he was working on his own Stickydisc imprint ([SonicScoop — Inside Stickydisc Recording with Eric Broucek](https://sonicscoop.com/2011/02/17/inside-stickydisc-recording-with-eric-broucek/)). Mansion engineering fell to **Matthew Thornley, Gunnar Bjerk, and Alec Gomez** as assists, with Murphy as primary engineer. The four-track mix-split with **Dave Sardy** — a rock mixer with credits ranging from Oasis to Marilyn Manson — is unusual for an LCD record: the Sardy-mixed tracks ("Drunk Girls," "I Can Change," "You Wanted a Hit," "Somebody's Calling Me") sit slightly hotter and rockier in the master than the Murphy-mixed tracks. **Bob Weston** mastered.

## "Dance Yrself Clean" — the slow-burn-into-detonation opener

"Dance Yrself Clean" opens the album at **8:58** — the most-cited LCD arrangement after "All My Friends." The structure is famous for the *patience* it asks: the first ~3 minutes are vocals and a near-skeletal rhythm bed at low volume; around the 3:00 mark the song detonates into a synth-bass-and-claps groove that runs the next five minutes ([The Current review](https://www.thecurrent.org/feature/2010/05/17/lcd-soundsystem-this-is-happening)). The bass synth at the drop is documented in [LCD Synth Sound Design](../artists/lcd-soundsystem-synth-sound-design.md) — "all three rectangular waveforms together, one dropped an octave as sub, filter cutoff at 4, resonance at 2, short loudness decay (200ms) for that punchy stab" per [Reverb Machine's breakdown](https://reverbmachine.com/blog/lcd-soundsystem-synth-sounds/) — and the arpeggio uses "saw-triangular waves at 2' and 4' with resonance and a noise oscillator under reverb." The "Atari 2600" character of the opening synth bleeps is the strategy: cheap-sounding miniatures in the cold open, monstrous full-spectrum punch on the drop. **The impact of the drop is purchased by the restraint that came before.**

## "Drunk Girls" — the Velvet Underground homage (caveat on the source song)

"Drunk Girls" is built around a call-and-response, ramshackle, two-chord pulse that LCD-press has consistently traced to the Velvet Underground — specifically, to **"White Light/White Heat"** (the 1968 album title track), not to anything on *Loaded* ([Wikipedia — Drunk Girls](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Drunk_Girls); [WhoSampled — Drunk Girls / White Light/White Heat](https://www.whosampled.com/sample/75282/LCD-Soundsystem-Drunk-Girls-The-Velvet-Underground-White-LightWhite-Heat/); [Village Voice — Wooden Shjips Cover Drunk Girls](https://www.villagevoice.com/wooden-shjips-cover-lcd-soundsystems-drunk-girls-succeed-in-making-a-tribute-to-the-velvet-underground-sound-like-its-actually-being-performed-by-the-velvet-underground/)). A casual community framing of "Loaded-era Velvets" is around the internet but does not appear in primary press, and *Loaded* (1970) is a different, cleaner, more pop-rock Velvets record than the noisier *White Light/White Heat* feel "Drunk Girls" actually adopts. ARLO should describe the homage as "the *White Light/White Heat* Velvets — call-and-response, two-chord scuzz." The song is credited to **Mahoney, Murphy, and Russom**, was the second single, and the **Spike Jonze + James Murphy** co-directed video at Factory Studios in Brooklyn features the band trying to perform while being assaulted by people in panda suits ([Wikipedia — Drunk Girls](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Drunk_Girls)).

## "All I Want" — the Bowie / Fripp "Heroes" lift, acknowledged

Of the album's lifts, "All I Want" is the most openly admitted. Murphy is on record that the song is *especially* indebted to the **Bowie–Eno–Fripp** material on the 1977 *"Heroes"* album — the descending feedback-and-sustain guitar line Robert Fripp played on the title track is the source of the searing single-note guitar voice running through "All I Want" ([Substack — Song of the Week: All I Want](https://camhexter.substack.com/p/song-of-the-week-all-i-want-by-lcd)). Murphy: *"As a little kid, 'Heroes' was one of my favorite songs, and as a bearded hipster, Bowie/[Brian] Eno collabs in Berlin are, you know, 'check' as cool stuff to listen to that you like."* LCD subsequently covered "Heroes" live at Coachella 2010 on the same tour cycle ([Rolling Stone — LCD Soundsystem Cover David Bowie's 'Heroes' at Coachella](https://www.rollingstone.com/music/music-news/lcd-soundsystem-cover-david-bowies-heroes-at-coachella-59937/)). The Murphy method here is *steal the riff, ground it in a different rhythm bed, and cover the source live* — pastiche-as-honor rather than pastiche-as-theft.

## "I Can Change" and "Home" — the synth ballad and the closer

**"I Can Change"** is the album's heaviest synth-pop heart. The main patch — *"two differently tuned oscillators to give the impression of two parts, melody and bass"* — is documented in [Reverb Machine](https://reverbmachine.com/blog/lcd-soundsystem-synth-sounds/) as a Gary Numan *Pleasure Principle*-era voicing, with Murphy reaching for early-80s reference points: Eurythmics' *Sweet Dreams*, Bronski Beat, early OMD, a Jimmy Somerville-style falsetto on the chorus ([Grokipedia — I Can Change](https://grokipedia.com/page/I_Can_Change_(LCD_Soundsystem_song)); [Spectrum Culture review](https://spectrumculture.com/2010/05/17/lcd-soundsystemthis-is-happening/)). The lyric reads as a marriage-in-trouble plea — *"Love is a murderer."* The production move worth flagging: an acid-beat-style 16th-note synth line with filter movement (very TB-303-adjacent) anchors what would otherwise float into pure ballad territory — the bass does dance-floor work while the vocal does heartbreak work. **"Home"** closes the album at 7:53 — the *quiet* ending, Mahoney's hat keeping time, a single chord rocked back and forth, Murphy talking through the song before singing it. Critics consistently invoke Talking Heads' **"This Must Be the Place (Naive Melody)"** as the obvious touchstone ([Redbrick — 10 Years On](https://www.redbrick.me/10-years-on-lcd-soundsystem-this-is-happening/)). The lyric reads as Murphy addressing band, listeners, and himself in one breath — resolving the anxious "supposed-to-be-the-end" frame into something like acceptance. The production architecture: no big-build payoff, no synth detonation, just the same chord cycling for almost eight minutes, with Mahoney's pulse holding the floor — the Mahoney "I subtract" instinct at closer scale.

## The rest of the tracklist — "One Touch," "Pow Pow," "You Wanted a Hit," "Somebody's Calling Me"

The middle of the record breathes between its anchor tracks. **"One Touch"** is a churning dance-floor cut built around a single-note Wurlitzer-and-synth ostinato — all forward propulsion, no chorus payoff. **"Pow Pow"** is the spoken-word vamp, Murphy half-rapping over a sequenced beat that swings between menace and pratfall — the album's most divisive cut, closest cousin to early DFA mixtape culture. **"You Wanted a Hit"** is the meta-track: nine-plus minutes of Murphy explicitly addressing the label and audience pressure to deliver a single, structured as mood shifts rather than verse-chorus form. **"Somebody's Calling Me"** is the *Station to Station*-era Bowie ballad sequel — slow, narcotic, EMS Synthi A in the back, vocals deliberately blurred — the track that draws the clearest line back to the [Bowie *Station to Station* sessions](./bowie-station-to-station.md). The recurring structural move across this middle: cold opens, deliberately small first minutes, big payoff late.

## The signal chain at the Mansion vs. Plantain

Because Murphy was working in Rubin's residential studio rather than DFA's Plantain, the load-bearing signal-chain details that define [the Plantain sound](../artists/james-murphy-analog-synth-philosophy.md) — the UA preamp → DBX 165 → LA-2A drum chain, the AKG BX-10 spring reverb, the car-elevator echo chamber, the Oram BEQ 24 + Purple Audio master section — were *not in the room*. What was: the Mansion's house chain plus the gear Murphy and Russom shipped from New York. The credited synth rack includes the **EMS VCS3 Putney**, **Korg Poly Ensemble**, **Roland System** units, **Yamaha CS-60**, and **Moog** equipment. The CS-60 and EMS continue their familiar LCD roles (drum-source and joystick-pad voice — see [LCD Synth Sound Design](../artists/lcd-soundsystem-synth-sound-design.md)), but the *room* is bigger, more reverberant, more rock-tracking-oriented than Plantain's basement. ARLO should not assume Mansion = Plantain — the records sound different partly because the rooms differ in size and partly because Sardy's mix sensibility on four of the nine tracks is rockier than Murphy's own.

## The farewell tour and Madison Square Garden

The album cycle resolved at **Madison Square Garden on 2 April 2011** — a nearly four-hour sold-out show framed as LCD's farewell. The night was documented by Dylan Southern and Will Lovelace in the 2012 film **"Shut Up and Play the Hits,"** premiering at Sundance 22 January 2012 ([Wikipedia — Shut Up and Play the Hits](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shut_Up_and_Play_the_Hits)). The film cuts between concert footage, a Chuck Klosterman interview with Murphy, and the 48 hours around the show; members of Arcade Fire appear as backing singers on "North American Scum." MSG retroactively became the period on the sentence *This Is Happening* started — until the 2015 reunion announcement reopened the band as an active concern and produced *American Dream* (2017). ARLO should treat MSG / *Shut Up and Play the Hits* as the live-document bookend to the Mansion sessions.

## What the Mansion sessions model for an ARLO-style project

*This Is Happening* models a clean set of structural moves for a final-statement record: (1) **live where you record** — a residential studio collapses writing and tracking into one day; (2) **commit to the "this is the end" framing as a production discipline,** even if it later turns out to be wrong; (3) **bring your synth rack with you** — Murphy and Russom shipped the EMS, CS-60, and modular voices from New York rather than using the house gear; (4) **let one collaborator drift deliberately out of time** (Russom's "out of time synth thing" on "Drunk Girls") and chop the result back in; (5) **earn the drop with restraint** ("Dance Yrself Clean"'s three-minute cold open); (6) **build a closer that subtracts rather than adds** ("Home"); (7) **acknowledge the lifts publicly** (the "Heroes" cover at Coachella, the Velvets form of "Drunk Girls"). The aesthetic is *commitment under a deadline you set yourself.*

## Verification notes and caveats

A few honest hedges. (a) The "Drunk Girls" homage is consistently sourced to *White Light/White Heat*, not *Loaded* — community lore is split, but primary press names the noisier 1968 Velvets record, and that's the sound of the LCD track. (b) **The Mansion** is in Laurel Canyon, not the Hollywood Hills proper, and is distinct from the Houdini Estate at 2400 Laurel Canyon — Rubin's Mansion is at 2451. The "all-white mansion" descriptor in Fuse TV's recap refers to the interior, not the building's name. (c) **Eric Broucek did not engineer this record.** His DFA tenure was roughly 2003–2008; *This Is Happening* engineering credits belong to Thornley, Bjerk, and Gomez. ARLO should not propagate the seed's "verify Broucek continuation" framing — verification *failed*. (d) Dave Sardy's mix work on four tracks is a load-bearing credit that distinguishes this master from *Sound of Silver* and is sometimes underplayed in LCD coverage.
