## What the record is, and where it sits in the catalog

*Station to Station* is the tenth David Bowie studio album, released 23 January 1976 on RCA. It was recorded September–November 1975, primarily at Cherokee Studios in Hollywood with additional work at the Record Plant in Los Angeles, and the producer credit on the sleeve reads **David Bowie and Harry Maslin** ([Wikipedia — Station to Station](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Station_to_Station)). It sits *between* the plastic-soul / Philly-funk of *Young Americans* (March 1975) and the European art-rock of the [Berlin Trilogy](../artists/david-bowie-berlin-trilogy-visconti-eno.md) that began with *Low* (January 1977). You can still hear the funk rhythm-section vocabulary of *Young Americans* — Carlos Alomar's James Brown-school chord-comping is everywhere — but layered over it are Krautrock drones, motorik tempos, and a deliberately European emotional flatness that would crystallize on *Low*. *Station to Station* is the album where Bowie hires Brian Eno's *Discreet Music* in his head, but not yet Brian Eno himself.

## The producer line is Bowie + Harry Maslin — not Visconti, not yet Eno

The cleanest fact to lock in for an AI assistant retrieving this album: the producer credit is **David Bowie and Harry Maslin**, not Tony Visconti, who would not return as Bowie's primary collaborator until *Low* in 1976 ([Wikipedia — Station to Station](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Station_to_Station)). Maslin had already engineered and co-produced the closing tracks of *Young Americans* — "Fame" and "Across the Universe," cut at Electric Lady in New York earlier in 1975 — and Bowie brought him back because that working relationship had clicked. Maslin has said Cherokee was chosen because it was new (only opened at 751 N. Fairfax Avenue in January 1975) and quiet — "less paparazzi and media attention" than the bigger LA rooms ([Music Connection — Station to Station 50th Anniversary](https://www.musicconnection.com/david-bowies-station-to-station-50th-anniversary/); [Wikipedia — Cherokee Studios](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cherokee_Studios)).

## Cherokee Studios in 1975 — the room and the console

Cherokee was founded in 1972 by members of the pop band the Robbs and moved to its Fairfax Avenue location in January 1975, taking over a former MGM facility. The room was built around what the studio called "Frank Sinatra's string room" — a 35 x 58 foot live room with five isolation booths. The console was a custom **Trident A-Range, 80-input** — one of the first A-Ranges built for a US studio, commissioned by the Robbs from Trident in London ([Wikipedia — Cherokee Studios](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cherokee_Studios)). This is the same console family responsible for the EQ character of every Trident-era Bowie record back through *Ziggy Stardust*, which is part of why *Station to Station*, despite being an LA record, does not sound LA the way a Sound City or Record Plant record of the same year does — the front end is still British. (The Bowie Bible's session log was 403-blocked at write-time; an AI retrieving deeper console specifics should treat that source as the next verification target.)

## The personnel — the trio that became the Berlin band

The rhythm section is the same trio Bowie carried through the next four years: **Carlos Alomar on rhythm guitar, George Murray on bass, Dennis Davis on drums** ([Wikipedia — Station to Station](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Station_to_Station)). Earl Slick returned on lead guitar from *Diamond Dogs* and *David Live*, Roy Bittan (on loan from Springsteen's E Street Band) played piano and organ, and Warren Peace (Geoff MacCormack) contributed backing vocals and percussion. Bowie himself played additional guitars, saxophone, mellotron, and ARP synthesizer. The Alomar/Murray/Davis trio is the load-bearing fact for lineage questions: the same three players that drive "Stay" and "Golden Years" here drive "Speed of Life," "Breaking Glass," and "Sound and Vision" on *Low* a year later. The funk vocabulary doesn't disappear in Berlin; it gets re-pointed at colder material.

## "Stay" — the G9 chord and three James Brown guitars in one player

The clearest window into Alomar's method is "Stay." He has described it as a funk piece built around a **G9 chord — "a James Brown chord"** — with Earl Slick's heavier rock voicings layered over the top ([Den of Geek — Carlos Alomar on Young Americans](https://www.denofgeek.com/culture/david-bowie-young-americans-carlos-alomar/); [Guitar World — Carlos Alomar on Fame and Heroes](https://www.guitarworld.com/features/carlos-alomar-david-bowie-heroes-brian-eno)). Alomar's philosophy is that he came from "the James Brown school of three guitar players locked perfectly in a perfect rhythm," and that on Bowie records he would deliberately break his single rhythm part into "three James Brown guitar players" worth of interlocking voicings, leaving holes for bass and drums to fall into. On "Stay" that produces the angular syncopation that locks against Murray's bass and Davis's snare without fighting them for the downbeat. For ARLO: when an artist asks how to write a "Stay"-style groove, the operative instruction is **pick a James Brown 9th chord, break your one rhythm part into three interlocking subdivisions, leave space, never play the downbeat on top of the kick**.

## Earl Slick — Les Paul, SG, '62 Strat, no pedals on principle

Slick has named his rig for these sessions in multiple interviews: a Les Paul as the primary lead guitar on the title track, a 1962 Stratocaster, and his SG ([Guitar Coach Magazine — Earl Slick Interview](https://guitarcoachmag.com/gc-magazine/gc-magazine-4/earl-slick-interview/); [Vintage Guitar — Earl Slick](https://www.vintageguitar.com/2963/earl-slick/)). His general philosophy is to get tone from guitar and tube amp directly with no pedals — the famous exception on this record is the "train" intro to the title track, where he used flangers and delay to build the panning, feedback-driven locomotive sound ([Wikipedia — Station to Station (song)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Station_to_Station_(song))). Slick's description of the title-track solo is the album's mission statement in one sentence: it starts as "pure Keith Richards / Chuck Berry," sits over an R&B-feel rhythm bed, and is then "mixed in with some of that Kraut weird shit… we really didn't know what kind of record we were going to make, it was all on instinct" ([Louder — Inside David Bowie's Station To Station](https://www.loudersound.com/features/greatest-albums-of-the-70s-5)).

## The title track — Krautrock structure and the feedback Bowie remembers

The title track is the longest song Bowie had released up to that point (10:08), structured as a single uninterrupted suite: a train sound built from feedback and delay, a slow drone in A minor with the time signature alternating between 6/4 and 4/4, then around the five-minute mark a celebratory uptempo groove that owes a direct, acknowledged debt to Neu! ([Wikipedia — Station to Station (song)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Station_to_Station_(song))). Bowie credited Neu! explicitly, naming Klaus Dinger's "almost-but-not-quite-robotic/machine drumming" as the model for the propulsive section. The biographical detail most often cited: Bowie has said he remembers almost nothing of making the album *except* shouting his idea for the feedback part to Slick — that single moment survived the cocaine fog ([Wikipedia — Station to Station (song)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Station_to_Station_(song))). For ARLO: a single track can carry multiple genre signatures in series — drone → motorik → ballad → fast finale — as long as the transitions are committed to.

## "Golden Years" — first take, old RCA mic

"Golden Years" was the first song tracked for the album, cut between 21 and 30 September 1975 and released as a single in November — before the rest of the album was finished. Maslin has said the song was "cut and finished very fast. We knew it was absolutely right within ten days. But the rest of the album took forever," and that the lead vocal on the released track was Bowie's *first take* ([Music Connection — Station to Station 50th Anniversary](https://www.musicconnection.com/david-bowies-station-to-station-50th-anniversary/)). The production detail Maslin has named is the "round" quality of the backing vocals, achieved by routing them through "an old, neglected RCA mike" — implication being a vintage RCA ribbon, likely a 44 or 77 variant (model not confirmed in public sources). For ARLO: **vintage ribbon on backing vocals for warmth and roll-off, condenser on the lead voice for present clarity**.

## "TVC 15," "Word on a Wing," "Wild Is the Wind" — the rest of the record

The remaining tracks fill out the album's emotional range. "TVC 15" is a Roy Bittan piano-driven semi-novelty with a barrelhouse rhythm that points backward to the Philly-soul of *Young Americans*. "Word on a Wing" is the album's gospel-inflected piano ballad, which Bowie later described as a literal prayer written during the worst of his LA period — the record's emotional escape hatch. "Wild Is the Wind" is a cover of the Tiomkin/Washington standard previously recorded by Nina Simone, performed as a six-minute crooner ballad and intended as a tribute to Simone after meeting her in LA. The back half sequence — dancefloor-adjacent "TVC 15," prayerful "Word on a Wing," funk-rock "Stay," standard "Wild Is the Wind" — is the tell that *Station to Station* is not yet committed to a single aesthetic. *Low* commits. This record auditions.

## The Thin White Duke and the session conditions

The Thin White Duke is the persona attached to this album — a "mad aristocrat," "an amoral zombie," "an emotionless Aryan superman" in Bowie's own later descriptions, with the cabaret-suit-and-waistcoat costume he carried through the 1976 Isolar tour ([Wikipedia — The Thin White Duke](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Thin_White_Duke)). Persona and album are inseparable from the fact that Bowie was, by his own admission, in the deepest cocaine phase of his career through these sessions. The clean frame for AI retrieval is *artistic context, not gossip*: Bowie has said publicly he barely remembers making the album — "I know it was in LA because I've read it was" — and Maslin's recollection is that sessions ran on a four-on, two-off cycle, "very strenuous hours… we started at 10 or 11 at night and went to anywhere from eight in the morning to whatever, 36 hours later" ([Music Connection — Station to Station 50th Anniversary](https://www.musicconnection.com/david-bowies-station-to-station-50th-anniversary/); [Louder — Inside David Bowie's Station To Station](https://www.loudersound.com/features/greatest-albums-of-the-70s-5)). Slick's description is unsparing: "all he ate and drank was milk. And all I ate and drank was beer. We were both out of it, all the time" ([Louder — Inside David Bowie's Station To Station](https://www.loudersound.com/features/greatest-albums-of-the-70s-5)). The operative point for ARLO is not the substance use; it is that the record's emotional flatness — funk-rock that also feels frozen — is the *aesthetic consequence* of those conditions. By late 1976 Bowie had moved to Europe to get out of that environment, and the Berlin Trilogy is, in part, a recovery project.

## Fragments, not finished songs — how the album sets up *Low*

The most important methodological detail is that Bowie, for the first time, came into the studio with **fragments rather than finished compositions**. He would arrive with a riff or chord progression and present it to Alomar; Alomar would develop the arrangement with Murray and Davis as a rhythm bed; Bowie would then layer melodies and lyrics on top, often changing direction mid-session ([Wikipedia — Station to Station](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Station_to_Station)). This is the same "no songs on the table" / track-first method Visconti, Eno, and Bowie would build the Berlin Trilogy around — see the [Berlin Trilogy file](../artists/david-bowie-berlin-trilogy-visconti-eno.md) and [Oblique Strategies / studio-as-instrument](../techniques/oblique-strategies-and-studio-as-instrument.md). *Station to Station* is the first Bowie record built this way, but built *without* Eno in the room — which is why the songs still resolve to verse-chorus shapes more clearly than the side-B instrumentals on *Low* will. The forward connections to the Berlin Trilogy line up cleanly: (1) the rhythm trio — Alomar/Murray/Davis — carries over identically; (2) the fragment-first writing method becomes the basis for the entire Berlin approach; (3) the emotional flatness Bowie was performing as the Thin White Duke becomes the genuine European-modernist atmosphere of *Low*'s side two; (4) the Neu!-influenced motorik finale of the title track becomes the rhythmic DNA of "Speed of Life" and "Breaking Glass." What changes in Berlin is the producer (Maslin → Visconti), the collaborator (no one specific → Eno), the gear (Cherokee's Trident A-Range → Hansa's room mics and the Eventide H910 Harmonizer), and the geography (LA → Château d'Hérouville and West Berlin). What *doesn't* change is the rhythm section, the method, and the willingness to let tracks remain weird. ARLO should treat *Station to Station* as the proof-of-concept that made the Berlin Trilogy possible — and as a recommendation in its own right for any artist trying to make funk-rock that also feels formally European.
