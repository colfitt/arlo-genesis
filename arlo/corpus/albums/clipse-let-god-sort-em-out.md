## Why *Let God Sort Em Out* Matters Aesthetically

*Let God Sort Em Out* is the first Clipse album in sixteen years — Pusha T and his brother Malice (now No Malice) had not released a duo project since *Til the Casket Drops* in 2009, and Malice had spent the intervening decade in Christian rap after leaving the secular game ([Wikipedia: *Let God Sort Em Out*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Let_God_Sort_Em_Out); [The Ringer: The Clipse Comeback](https://www.theringer.com/2025/07/08/music/clipse-let-god-sort-em-out-pusha-t-malice-pharrell-williams)). Released July 11, 2025 through Roc Nation Distribution after a public split with Def Jam, the album was produced entirely by Pharrell Williams — the first time Pharrell had been sole producer on a Clipse record since 2006's *Hell Hath No Fury* ([The Ringer](https://www.theringer.com/2025/07/08/music/clipse-let-god-sort-em-out-pusha-t-malice-pharrell-williams)).

For ARLO's purposes, this is the canonical case study of a long-paused duo coming back not by chasing contemporary sound but by reconvening with their original producer in a specific room, on a specific schedule, and letting the work be what it wants to be. The record landed at #4 on the Billboard 200 with 118,000 album-equivalent units, debuted at #1 on the Independent Albums chart, posted Clipse's biggest streaming week ever (77.49 million on-demand streams), and earned five Grammy nominations at the 2026 ceremony including Album of the Year and Best Rap Album ([Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Let_God_Sort_Em_Out); [Roc Nation press release](https://www.rocnation.com/news/let-god-sort-em-out-by-clipse-1-independent-album-1-vinyl-album-4-billboard-200-118k-first-week-sales/)).

## The Sixteen-Year Gap and Why Now

The reunion was not strategic timing; it was friendship and emotional readiness. Malice's framing to Rolling Stone is the cleanest statement of intent: "We waited until the time was definitely right" ([Interview Magazine: The Clipse Reunion](https://www.interviewmagazine.com/music/the-clipse-reunion-is-hip-hops-hard-reset)). A 2019 reconvening on Kanye West's "Use This Gospel" — Pusha, Malice, and Kanye on one track — confirmed for both brothers that the chemistry was intact, and recording on the album proper began in 2023 ([Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Let_God_Sort_Em_Out)).

The opening track "The Birds Don't Sing" sits at the emotional center of the gap. It addresses the deaths of both brothers' parents — Pusha and Malice processing parental loss in adulthood with John Legend and the Voices of Fire choir as the framing voice. Pusha's account of writing it: "It took all of four days of gut-wrenching reminiscing, and trying to put it all on paper was such a heavy task." Malice's matching admission: "It was hard for me too, from an emotional standpoint, coming up with verses" ([Interview Magazine](https://www.interviewmagazine.com/music/the-clipse-reunion-is-hip-hops-hard-reset)). The opener is not a return-to-form flex; it is the album earning the right to make one.

## The Pharrell Sessions — Paris, Louis Vuitton Headquarters

The recording center of gravity was unusual: Pharrell's office overlooking the Seine at the Louis Vuitton men's headquarters in Paris, where he serves as men's creative director ([The Ringer](https://www.theringer.com/2025/07/08/music/clipse-let-god-sort-em-out-pusha-t-malice-pharrell-williams)). Pusha and Malice made repeat trips to and from Europe across the two-year session window. The lead single "Ace Trumpets" was recorded largely at the Louis Vuitton headquarters ([Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Let_God_Sort_Em_Out)).

Pusha's framing of the setting is direct: "The backdrop of Louis Vuitton headquarters in Paris was definitely made for the luxury aspirational street rap that we made" ([Interview Magazine](https://www.interviewmagazine.com/music/the-clipse-reunion-is-hip-hops-hard-reset)). Wikipedia lists additional sessions at Masterworks (Virginia Beach), The Bakehouse, Dream Boy ATL, Sessions Atlanta, Other Island and The Library Room (Miami), and locations in Philadelphia, Hollywood, Eleuthera (Bahamas), and New York — but the *primary* identity of the record is the Paris office, and the published interviews concentrate there ([Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Let_God_Sort_Em_Out)).

The working method, as Pusha described it to Interview Magazine, was feel-driven rather than schedule-driven: "Everything is so based on feeling. When it feels right, we finish it." He also credited Pharrell with raising the structural bar: "Pharrell brought a heightened level of song structure to this Clipse project" ([Interview Magazine](https://www.interviewmagazine.com/music/the-clipse-reunion-is-hip-hops-hard-reset)).

## Pharrell's Production — Empty Space and Ferocity

Pharrell's production approach on this record is consistently described in the 2025 press as minimalist, focused, and architecturally spare in a way that points back to *Hell Hath No Fury* (2006) rather than to *Lord Willin'* (2002). Stereogum's Tom Breihan characterized the beats as having "empty space and ferocity," with the production designed to keep attention on the rappers rather than draw attention to itself, even as it admits "allusions to gospel" and "muted rock guitar riffs" ([Stereogum: Premature Evaluation](https://stereogum.com/2313656/premature-evaluation-clipse-let-god-sort-em-out/reviews/premature-evaluation)). Breihan's specific comparison: these beats lack "the berserk bump" of the *Lord Willin'* era but share *Hell Hath No Fury*'s "minimal, stabbing intensity," with the Pharrell-Pusha chemistry feeling like a continuation of their 2022 collaboration on *It's Almost Dry*.

The Ringer's description of the *Hell Hath No Fury* lineage is similar: tracks like "Ace Trumpets" and "So Be It" are positioned as emerging from that 2006 aesthetic, with "So Be It" in particular using a Near East string sample, Arabic vocal elements, and shuffling percussion ([The Ringer](https://www.theringer.com/2025/07/08/music/clipse-let-god-sort-em-out-pusha-t-malice-pharrell-williams)). The throughline across both write-ups: Pharrell is not chasing 2025 production trends. He is doubling down on a vocabulary he and Clipse already share and trusting that the rapping will carry the foreground.

## The Sample Layer

Wikipedia, cross-referenced where possible against WhoSampled URLs, attributes the following samples on the record:

- **"Chains & Whips"** — uses Paul Desmond's "Take Five," but specifically the Cherry Wainer / Don Storer cover version rather than the canonical 1959 Dave Brubeck Quartet recording. The WhoSampled entry for this sample names "Cherry Wainer / Don Storer — Take Five" as the source ([WhoSampled: Clipse — Chains & Whips](https://www.whosampled.com/sample/1320217/Clipse-Kendrick-Lamar-Chains-%26-Whips-Cherry-Wainer-Don-Storer-Take-Five/)).
- **"P.O.V."** — the theme to *The Streets of San Francisco* ([Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Let_God_Sort_Em_Out)).
- **"So Be It"** — "Maza Akoolou" by Talal Maddah, supplying the Arabic vocal element and Near East string sound described by The Ringer ([Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Let_God_Sort_Em_Out); [The Ringer](https://www.theringer.com/2025/07/08/music/clipse-let-god-sort-em-out-pusha-t-malice-pharrell-williams)).
- **"Inglorious Bastards"** — Jesse Jackson's opening speech from the 1972 *Wattstax* concert ([Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Let_God_Sort_Em_Out)).
- **"Let God Sort Em Out / Chandeliers"** — the theme to *Sarge* (TV) ([Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Let_God_Sort_Em_Out)).

The pattern across these is consistent with the Pharrell-and-Clipse historical sample profile: short, identifiable hooks pulled from outside contemporary rap's usual reference pool — jazz cover versions, TV themes, mid-century soul speeches, non-Western vocal sources — and recontextualized as the structural skeleton of the beat rather than ornamentation on top of one.

## The Guest List and the Reunion Calculus

The featured artists are concentrated rather than scattered: Kendrick Lamar (on "Chains & Whips"), Tyler, the Creator ("P.O.V."), Nas ("Let God Sort Em Out / Chandeliers"), John Legend and Voices of Fire ("The Birds Don't Sing"), Stove God Cooks ("F.I.C.O."), The-Dream and Pharrell ("All Things Considered"), and Re-Up Gang member Ab-Liva ("Inglorious Bastards"). Pharrell himself features on four songs. Wikipedia also credits Lenny Kravitz with guitar and Stevie Wonder with piano and spoken-word contributions on the album ([Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Let_God_Sort_Em_Out)).

The Kendrick Lamar feature is the one with public production drama attached to it. Pusha T disclosed in 2025 that Def Jam — Clipse's then-label, via Universal Music Group — pushed for the verse to be censored or removed over its perceived alignment with Kendrick's then-active feud with Drake (whose lawsuit against UMG was ongoing). Pusha refused: "They wanted me to ask Kendrick to censor his verse, which of course I was never doing." After roughly a month of impasse, Def Jam's lawyer Steve Gawley reportedly said, "We'll just drop the Clipse," and Clipse exited the label — paying a seven-figure sum — and moved the record to Roc Nation Distribution ([The FADER: UMG Censor Request](https://www.thefader.com/2025/06/02/umg-clipse-kendrick-lamar-verse-censor-drake); [Consequence: Clipse Left Def Jam](https://consequence.net/2025/06/clipse-def-jam-kendrick-lamar-verse/); [Hollywood Reporter](https://www.hollywoodreporter.com/music/music-news/pusha-t-claims-def-jam-tried-to-censor-kendrick-lamar-verse-1236235337/)). The dispute is part of the record's release story: the album shipped with the verse intact because the label that wanted it cut no longer controlled the master.

## Reception — Acclaim, with a Loud Outlier

The critical consensus was overwhelmingly positive. Metacritic aggregated a score of 83/100. The Guardian gave it five stars. Rolling Stone's Simon Vozick-Levinson published a four-star review concluding, "Time passes and people change, but the important truths remain. This is an album that underscores several of them: Whatever the year, Pusha and Malice are richer than you, smarter than you, and much better at making rap music than you'll ever be" ([Rolling Stone review](https://www.rollingstone.com/music/music-album-reviews/clipse-let-god-sort-em-out-pusha-malice-1235371027/)). Stereogum called the album "a reclamation of legacy, a grown-man rap statement of the highest order" ([Stereogum](https://stereogum.com/2313656/premature-evaluation-clipse-let-god-sort-em-out/reviews/premature-evaluation)). Rolling Stone later placed the record at #1 on its year-end best-albums-of-2025 list.

The notable outlier was Pitchfork's review by Alphonse Pierre, scored 6.5/10. The critique was specifically about the production: Pharrell's beats were characterized as "corporate," "stuffy," and "HR-approved," with the implication that the duo's rapping was being held back by an arrangement aesthetic that played it too safe ([Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Let_God_Sort_Em_Out); coverage of the Pitchfork review surfaced widely across hip-hop press in July 2025). The Pitchfork take generated significant pushback online but is worth noting for ARLO purposes because it identifies the *risk* of the album's production aesthetic: when you double down on empty space and minimal arrangement, listeners who want movement and surprise in the beat will hear restraint as flatness.

## What ARLO Should Take from *Let God Sort Em Out*

For a user trying to make a record after a long gap, with a known collaborator, in a non-traditional recording environment:

- **Recompose the room before you recompose the songs.** The Louis Vuitton headquarters is not a recording studio in the canonical sense, but it is the producer's daily creative environment. The sessions worked because Pharrell was in his own gravity well, with Pusha and Malice traveling to him.
- **Reunite with the producer who shaped your first identity.** Pharrell's sole-producer credit here echoes *Hell Hath No Fury* (2006) rather than the patchwork production of *Til the Casket Drops* (2009). The cohesion of the record is downstream of the single-producer decision.
- **Let the heaviest song be first.** "The Birds Don't Sing" addresses parental death before the album earns it the conventional way. The opening-track placement is the album telling listeners that this is not a victory lap.
- **Minimalism is a risk, not a default.** The Pitchfork critique of "corporate, stuffy, HR-approved" production is real even if outvoted. Decide whether you want the beat to compete for attention with the vocal or stay deliberately out of its way — both are valid, but they generate different listener experiences.
- **Own your distribution if a label can't ship the work.** The Kendrick-verse standoff cost Clipse seven figures to exit Def Jam, but it preserved the song they wanted and let them debut at #1 on the Independent Albums chart through Roc Nation Distribution. The release infrastructure is part of the production decision.
