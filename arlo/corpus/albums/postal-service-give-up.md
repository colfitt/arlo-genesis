# The Postal Service — *Give Up* (2003)

**Artist:** The Postal Service (Jimmy Tamborello + Ben Gibbard; Jenny Lewis as touring/featured third member)
**Year:** 2003 (released February 19, 2003 on Sub Pop Records) ([Wikipedia — *Give Up*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Give_Up))
**Producer(s):** The Postal Service (Jimmy Tamborello and Ben Gibbard, self-produced) ([Wikipedia — *Give Up*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Give_Up))
**Engineering/Mix:** Jimmy Tamborello (primary tracking and arrangement, LA); Chris Walla (mix consultation / Death Cab–side engineering context — verify per-track credits)
**Featured Vocals:** Jenny Lewis (Rilo Kiley) on roughly half the album, lead on "Nothing Better"; Jen Wood (additional backing vocals)

## Why This Record Matters to the ARLO Stack

*Give Up* is the load-bearing reference for two distinct ARLO design conversations. It is the *workflow* example — a record built by two musicians in different cities, who barely knew each other, who shipped audio on physical CD-Rs through the mail and trusted that the asynchronous gap between them would clarify rather than confuse the work. And it is the *aesthetic* example — the album that took Tamborello's IDM-leaning laptop-electronica vocabulary and welded it to confessional indie-rock songwriting in a way that proved enormously durable. By 2013 the album had sold over 1.1 million copies in the U.S. and was certified platinum; Sub Pop counts it as the label's second best-selling release of all time, surpassed only by Nirvana's *Bleach* ([Wikipedia — *Give Up*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Give_Up)). Every subsequent "indie band makes an electronic record" project sits downstream of this template. For ARLO, *Give Up* exemplifies three patterns: (1) asynchronous remote collaboration as a creative constraint, not just a logistical compromise; (2) a single hardware workstation — the Kurzweil K2000RS — treated as a complete studio rather than one voice among many; and (3) indie-electronic crossover built around vulnerability rather than dance-floor energy.

## Origin — From "(This Is) The Dream of Evan and Chan" to a Full Album

The collaboration started two years before the album. Tamborello, recording as Dntel, asked Ben Gibbard (already known as Death Cab for Cutie's songwriter) to contribute vocals to "(This Is) The Dream of Evan and Chan" on Dntel's 2001 album *Life Is Full of Possibilities*. It was the proof of concept: an IDM/glitch instrumental under a fully-sung indie vocal, built by mail without the two meeting in person. Sub Pop A&R Tony Kiewel heard the result and offered the duo an album deal; the band name described the working method ([Wikipedia — The Postal Service](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Postal_Service); [Life of the Record — The Postal Service](https://lifeoftherecord.com/the-postal-service)). Gibbard later said the Postal Service tracks functioned as "nice breaks" in his concurrent writing for Death Cab's *Transatlanticism* — the two records are companion works of his 2002–2003 output, which the 2023–2024 20th-anniversary tour explicitly acknowledged by pairing them ([Life of the Record — The Postal Service](https://lifeoftherecord.com/the-postal-service)).

## The CD-R Mail-Collaboration Workflow

The mechanics were physical. Tamborello, in Los Angeles, produced an instrumental sketch — programmed beats, K2000-sourced synth lines, sampled string fragments, structural arrangement — and burned it to a CD-R. He mailed the disc to Gibbard in Seattle. Gibbard received the disc, listened on a portable Discman while walking his neighborhood, and let melody and lyric ideas emerge against Tamborello's already-arranged track. He recorded vocals at home ("Computerworld") and shipped the disc back to Tamborello for further iteration ([MusicRadar — Postal Service interview](https://www.musicradar.com/artists/bands/i-didnt-really-understand-how-any-of-it-worked-we-were-just-young-and-up-for-anything-the-postal-service-on-creating-indie-electronic-classic-give-up); [Life of the Record — The Postal Service](https://lifeoftherecord.com/the-postal-service)). Despite the band name, the actual carriers were FedEx and UPS — both for speed and to avoid losing master discs ([Wikipedia — *Give Up*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Give_Up)).

What matters for the ARLO stack is the *creative consequence*. Tamborello and Gibbard barely knew each other when they started, and the mail delay meant Tamborello had already committed an arrangement before Gibbard ever heard the track. As Tamborello later put it, "We didn't even know each other that well… we were just young and up for anything" — and that separation between arrangement and vocal/lyric decisions actually clarified each contributor's role ([MusicRadar — Postal Service interview](https://www.musicradar.com/artists/bands/i-didnt-really-understand-how-any-of-it-worked-we-were-just-young-and-up-for-anything-the-postal-service-on-creating-indie-electronic-classic-give-up)). The mail delay forced both parties to commit — Tamborello couldn't second-guess once a disc was in the mail, and Gibbard couldn't ask for an instrumental rewrite without restarting the cycle. Constraint as creative driver.

## The Kurzweil K2000 as a Whole Studio

The single most important gear fact about *Give Up* is that nearly the entire instrumental record runs through a **Kurzweil K2000RS** — the rack-mount version of Kurzweil's flagship sampler-synth workstation. The K2000RS provided the bell-tone leads, squelched and rounded bass voices, pad textures, and the chassis for Tamborello's sample manipulation. He controlled it via MIDI from a Macintosh Quadra sequencer and recorded final mixes onto a PowerBook G4 running Cubase 5 ([MusicRadar — Postal Service interview](https://www.musicradar.com/artists/bands/i-didnt-really-understand-how-any-of-it-worked-we-were-just-young-and-up-for-anything-the-postal-service-on-creating-indie-electronic-classic-give-up); [Life of the Record — The Postal Service](https://lifeoftherecord.com/the-postal-service)).

Supporting hardware included a **Clavia Nord Modular**, a **Korg microKORG**, a **Line 6 delay pedal** (used as a creative effect on drum-machine output), and an **Alesis MIDIverb** for reverb and modulation. The mix chassis was a **Mackie 1604** — Tamborello ran eight outputs from the K2000 and eight from the laptop into the Mackie and committed stereo mixes from there ([MusicRadar — Postal Service interview](https://www.musicradar.com/artists/bands/i-didnt-really-understand-how-any-of-it-worked-we-were-just-young-and-up-for-anything-the-postal-service-on-creating-indie-electronic-classic-give-up)).

What is striking from a 2026 vantage is what is *missing*: no bus compression, no side-chain ducking, no parallel-processing aux returns. Tamborello has been candid about this — "I didn't really understand how any of that worked." The absence of mix-bus polish is what gives the record its lo-fi-but-bright character. Drums are unducked and bright; vocals sit forward without being seated in a polished pop bed; the synths breathe. For ARLO this is the *opposite* pattern from a meticulously-engineered electronic record like *Discovery* or *Vespertine* — a producer who treated his sampler-synth as a complete environment and did not reach for the surrounding tools an experienced electronic-pop engineer would have used.

## Sampling Approach — Dollar-Bin Strings, Reversed and Pitched

The classical-string textures threaded through the record were not from a sample library and were not played live. Tamborello bought dollar-bin classical LPs, dropped the needle at random places on the record, sampled whatever played into the K2000, and then pitch-shifted, reversed, time-stretched, and re-keyed the result until it fit the track ([MusicRadar — Postal Service interview](https://www.musicradar.com/artists/bands/i-didnt-really-understand-how-any-of-it-worked-we-were-just-young-and-up-for-anything-the-postal-service-on-creating-indie-electronic-classic-give-up)). The blind-sampling method — taking whatever the random needle drop gave you and shaping it after the fact — is the same constraint posture as the OP-1 sketchpad workflow catalogued in `corpus/techniques/hardware-sampling-workflow.md`: the producer commits to a found source and lets the process author the texture, rather than searching a library for the "right" string sound.

The K2000's onboard sampling and DSP did the lifting. Drum patterns were assembled in the **Battery** soft-sampler plugin, then occasionally re-routed out through the Line 6 delay pedal to introduce rhythmic distortion and stutter, then time-corrected back into the grid before being committed to the mix ([MusicRadar — Postal Service interview](https://www.musicradar.com/artists/bands/i-didnt-really-understand-how-any-of-it-worked-we-were-just-young-and-up-for-anything-the-postal-service-on-creating-indie-electronic-classic-give-up)).

## "Such Great Heights" — The Bell Hook and the Squelched Drums

"Such Great Heights" did the cultural work. Gibbard said it "came together late in the recording process" and "came out of nowhere," describing the experience as "spiritual transcendence" and noting it was the first time he had written a fully positive love song ([Wikipedia — Such Great Heights](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Such_Great_Heights)).

The iconic hook — the bright, bubbling, music-box bell motif that opens the song and recurs throughout — is a K2000RS patch driven via MIDI, with the bell character coming from the K2000's onboard FM/sample voices and a delay pedal trailing each note ([Grokipedia — Such Great Heights](https://grokipedia.com/page/Such_Great_Heights)). Synth-replication culture has documented the patch extensively; Korg's "Patch of the Week" series rebuilt the "rounded, bubbling synth line" on a modern prologue, confirming the source patch is a relatively straightforward subtractive-plus-delay voice ([KORG USA — Patch of the Week 109](https://www.facebook.com/KORGusa/videos/802001873790572/)). The drums are the second signature element — a tight, squelched kick-snare-clap pattern, forward without compression-driven loudness. Jen Wood (not Jenny Lewis) provided the airy upper-register backing vocal on the studio recording.

The single peaked at #21 on Billboard's Hot 100 Singles Sales chart, spent eleven weeks on the chart, and was certified platinum ([Wikipedia — Such Great Heights](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Such_Great_Heights)). The original was licensed to Target, UPS, Kaiser Permanente, and Ask.com television advertisements.

## The Iron & Wine Cover and the *Garden State* Propagation

Sam Beam (Iron & Wine) recorded an acoustic cover of "Such Great Heights" — finger-picked nylon-string guitar, sotto-voce double-tracked vocal, no electronic elements — which was placed on the 2004 *Garden State* soundtrack and used in the film's most-quoted scene. The cover took on a life of its own; it was later licensed to M&M's television commercials and became, for a stretch of the mid-2000s, more culturally visible than the original ([Wikipedia — Such Great Heights](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Such_Great_Heights)).

The propagation pattern matters for ARLO: the original is a synth-pop track that established the song; the acoustic cover is what carried the song into film and advertising; the two versions coexist in cultural memory as different songs that happen to share lyrics and melody. This is the same propagation shape that the corpus catalogs around "Hallelujah" or "Hurt" — a song whose canonical version becomes contested through cover-mediated dissemination.

## Jenny Lewis — Backing Vocals and "Nothing Better"

Jenny Lewis of Rilo Kiley contributed backing vocals across roughly half the album and sings the female lead on "Nothing Better," which is structured as a sung dialogue between Gibbard and Lewis. Lewis recorded her parts over the course of several days in Tamborello's bedroom studio after being contacted by Gibbard ([Wikipedia — *Give Up*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Give_Up)). On the 2013 and 2023–2024 reunion tours she joined as the third on-stage member, doubling on keyboards and percussion in addition to vocals.

## The Other Anchor Tracks

**"The District Sleeps Alone Tonight"** opens with a slow four-on-the-floor kick under a foggy synth pad, situating the record in Washington, D.C. **"Sleeping In"** is the album's brightest track, with its most overt indie-pop chord progression and Lewis's clearest backing vocal. **"We Will Become Silhouettes"** uses a stop-start drum-machine pulse against bouncy synth bass and reads, in retrospect, like post-9/11 apocalypse-pop — the Shins later covered it. **"Brand New Colony"** builds from a single repeated synth motif into a cathartic "everything will change" chant-along outro that became a setlist closer on later tours. **"Natural Anthem"** closes the record with an ambient instrumental outro — Tamborello's least pop-leaning moment, with K2000 and Nord Modular drone-and-swell work for the final two minutes.

## Sub Pop, the Release, and the Sales Trajectory

Sub Pop released *Give Up* on February 19, 2003. It debuted modestly but had unusually long legs — driven by independent radio (KCRW in LA, KITS in San Francisco), picked up by KROQ-FM Los Angeles in January 2004 (a full year post-release), and permanently propelled by the *Garden State* cultural moment in the second half of 2004 ([Wikipedia — Such Great Heights](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Such_Great_Heights)). The album peaked at #45 on the Billboard 200 and was certified platinum. By 2013, U.S. sales exceeded 1.1 million units. Sub Pop frames *Give Up* as the label's second-best-selling album of all time, surpassed only by Nirvana's *Bleach* — a claim repeated across label communications and consistent with the certified-sales math ([Wikipedia — *Give Up*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Give_Up); [MusicRadar — Postal Service interview](https://www.musicradar.com/artists/bands/i-didnt-really-understand-how-any-of-it-worked-we-were-just-young-and-up-for-anything-the-postal-service-on-creating-indie-electronic-classic-give-up)).

## The U.S. Postal Service Cease-and-Desist and the Settlement

In August 2003, six months after the album's release and as it was gaining commercial traction, the United States Postal Service served the band with a cease-and-desist letter, asserting that the band's name caused "tarnishment and dilution" of the agency's trademark on the phrase "postal service" ([Far Out Magazine — Postal Service legal battle](https://faroutmagazine.co.uk/legal-battle-centre-the-postal-service/); [Wikipedia — The Postal Service](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Postal_Service)).

Rather than litigate, the band — primarily Gibbard, who handled the negotiation directly with USPS representatives — reached a settlement that took the form of a licensing agreement with terms more amicable than the C&D had suggested:

1. The band was permitted to continue using "The Postal Service" as their name.
2. The band agreed to *promote* the U.S. Postal Service in band communications.
3. The band agreed to perform at the U.S. Postal Service's National Executive Conference in Washington, D.C., in November 2004 — which they did.
4. Future copies of *Give Up* and any subsequent releases would include a notice acknowledging the U.S. Postal Service trademark.
5. The U.S. Postal Service was granted permission to monetize the band's music — specifically, to sell copies of their releases on the official USPS website. ([Far Out Magazine — Postal Service legal battle](https://faroutmagazine.co.uk/legal-battle-centre-the-postal-service/); [Medium / Knowledge Stew — The Band That Agreed to Promote the U.S. Postal Service](https://medium.com/knowledge-stew/the-band-that-agreed-to-promote-the-u-s-postal-service-cbccf723cc2a))

Tamborello later told Pitchfork that the affair initially seemed "depressing" but ended up being "kind of fun," noting that "everyone was sort-of in on the joke." Verification confidence on the settlement specifics is high; the band-performs-at-USPS-conference detail and the USPS-sells-band-records detail are corroborated by multiple independent sources.

## The Reunion Tours — 2013 and 2023–2024

The band reunited in 2013 for the album's tenth anniversary. Sub Pop issued a deluxe reissue with B-sides and two new tracks recorded with Jenny Lewis ("Turn Around," "A Tattered Line of String"). The 2013 tour ran from Coachella in April through a final show at Lollapalooza on August 5, 2013, which both Tamborello and Gibbard publicly called the band's last performance ([Wikipedia — The Postal Service](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Postal_Service)). It wasn't. In 2023, for the 20th anniversary of both *Give Up* and Death Cab's *Transatlanticism*, the Postal Service announced a co-headlining tour with Death Cab — each band performing its anniversary album in full, Gibbard pulling double duty as lead singer of both. The tour ran through 2024, with the final scheduled performance at HFStival in Washington, D.C., on September 21, 2024. Jenny Lewis again joined as the third member.

## What ARLO Should Take From *Give Up*

Four transferable patterns: (1) **asynchronous remote collaboration as creative posture, not just logistical compromise** — the mail delay forced commitment and clarified individual contributions; modern cloud-DAW or voice-memo exchanges sit on the same axis. (2) **A single hardware workstation can carry an entire record** — the K2000RS was not one voice among many, it was the studio; an aesthetics agent should recognize when a record's character reduces to one piece of gear plus a producer's vocabulary. (3) **Absence of mix polish can be the signature** — Tamborello's missing compression and side-chain ducking was inexperience, but it gave the record an openness subsequent "properly" engineered indie-electronic records lacked. (4) **The covers-and-licensing tail can outweigh the original release** — *Give Up* sold steadily for two years before *Garden State* / Iron & Wine turned it into a cultural artifact; the cover-and-soundtrack propagation graph deserves first-class treatment.
