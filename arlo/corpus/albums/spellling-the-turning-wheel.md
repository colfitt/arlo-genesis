## The Session Story In One Paragraph

*The Turning Wheel* is the record where Chrystia Cabral — an Oakland experimental-pop artist who had built her first two albums largely alone on a Roland Juno-106 — decided to translate her synth palette part-for-part into a 31-piece acoustic ensemble. She wrote and produced the entire record herself, but for the first time made full demos before tracking, then handed those demos to arrangers Ted Case (strings) and Nick Marchione (brass), and tracked each player one-on-one when COVID-19 collapsed the original plan of a live in-room session ([Atwood Magazine, *Spellling on The Turning Wheel*](https://atwoodmagazine.com/sttw-spellling-the-turning-wheel-music-interview-2021/); [Audiofemme](https://www.audiofemme.com/spellling-the-turning-wheel/)). The engineering anchor was John Finkbeiner at New Improved Recording in Oakland; Drew Vandenberg added production and mixed most of the record at Chase Park Transduction in Athens, GA; Adrian Morgan mastered at Timeless Mastering in Brooklyn ([Spellling Bandcamp](https://spellling.bandcamp.com/album/the-turning-wheel)). The result — released by Sacred Bones on 25 June 2021 — earned a 79/100 Metacritic and is the corpus's strongest example of how to make an orchestral art-pop record on a self-recorded budget without losing the auteur-bedroom voice.

## The "Bedroom-Orchestra" Premise

Cabral's first two records — *Pantheon of Me* (2017) and *Mazy Fly* (2019) — were made almost entirely on a Roland Juno-106, with sparse acoustic overdubs at Antiquity Records in Oakland ([Sacred Bones, *Mazy Fly*](https://www.sacredbonesrecords.com/products/sbr221-spellling-mazy-fly); [Bandcamp, *Mazy Fly*](https://spellling.bandcamp.com/album/mazy-fly)). *The Turning Wheel* keeps the authorship structure — Cabral writes, produces, arranges, conducts — but inverts the instrumentation: synth pads become string quartets, lead-synth motifs become French horn and trumpet, drum-machine pulses become congas, banjo, and live percussion. The decision means the ensemble never sounds like a studio orchestra hired by a producer — it sounds like one woman's synth patches grew acoustic bodies.

## The 31 Musicians (Verified)

The 31-musician headline is widely repeated in the press and verified against the Bandcamp credits ([Audiofemme](https://www.audiofemme.com/spellling-the-turning-wheel/); [Atwood Magazine](https://atwoodmagazine.com/sttw-spellling-the-turning-wheel-music-interview-2021/); [Spellling Bandcamp](https://spellling.bandcamp.com/album/the-turning-wheel)). The roster groups roughly as:

- **Strings:** ATLYS Quartet (Sabrina Tabby and Jinty McTavish on violin, Rita Andrade on viola, Genevieve Tabby on cello) and Del Sol String Quartet — two full quartets used as separate textural colours rather than doubled
- **Brass:** Calliope Brass Quintet — Rebecca Steinberg and Monica Benson on trumpet / flugelhorn, Erin Paul on French horn, Sara Mayo on trombone, Jennifer Hinkle on bass trombone
- **Woodwinds:** Carolyn Walter (bassoon, clarinet, bass clarinet); Lidia Rodriguez (baritone saxophone)
- **Harp:** Amy Ahn and Destiny Muhammad
- **Guitars / Bass:** Milo Jimenez (acoustic), Satcher Hsieh and Lisa Reed (electric); Giulio Xavier Cetto and Doug Stuart (bass)
- **Keys:** Javier Santiago and Donia Jarrar (piano)
- **Percussion:** Janak JP and Jacob Richards; Brijean Murphy on congas; MacGregor Munson on banjo
- **Vocals:** Ezra Teshome, Noor Al-Samarrai and Dharma Mooney (choir); Tenzin Choegyal (featured)

Cabral did not hire a contractor-style orchestra; she hired specific chamber ensembles whose internal sound was part of the colour palette. ATLYS and Del Sol read differently in the arrangements; Calliope Brass is a chamber quintet, not a section of session players. This is closer to how a film composer assembles a score than how a pop record traditionally hires strings.

## Demos-First — A New Workflow For Cabral

Cabral's stated workflow shift: *"I knew for the first time that I would need to make demos, because in the past, I just hit record and kept whatever came out"* ([Atwood Magazine](https://atwoodmagazine.com/sttw-spellling-the-turning-wheel-music-interview-2021/)). On *Mazy Fly* the Juno-106 take *was* the record. *The Turning Wheel* required every note to be notatable so Ted Case and Nick Marchione could chart parts players could read. The corpus pattern: when translating a bedroom synth record to live acoustic instruments, the demo isn't a sketch, it's the score. Same logic as the Björk / Vince Mendoza orchestral pass on *Vespertine* — auteur composes, arranger transcribes, ensemble interprets (see `corpus/albums/bjork-vespertine.md`).

## The Pandemic One-On-One Tracking

The original plan was an in-room ensemble session. COVID-19 forced a re-organisation that suited Cabral's temperament: *"it kind of played to my strengths where I could work with people more one on one instead of this huge orchestration of people in the same room together"* ([Audiofemme](https://www.audiofemme.com/spellling-the-turning-wheel/)). She also named the personal cost: *"Personally I'm a really introverted person, so this process took a lot of courage"* ([Audiofemme](https://www.audiofemme.com/spellling-the-turning-wheel/)), and *"It was a very drawn out process and one of the hardest things I've ever done"* ([Atwood Magazine](https://atwoodmagazine.com/sttw-spellling-the-turning-wheel-music-interview-2021/)). The corpus-relevant consequence is that the album is overdub-orchestral rather than live-ensemble-orchestral. Each player tracked individually against Cabral's demo click; the ensemble was assembled in the mix.

## The Engineering Triangle — Oakland, Athens, Brooklyn

Tracking happened at New Improved Recording in Oakland with John Finkbeiner — close to Cabral and to most of the Bay Area players ([Spellling Bandcamp](https://spellling.bandcamp.com/album/the-turning-wheel)). Drew Vandenberg added production and mixed at Chase Park Transduction in Athens, GA — a long-standing R.E.M.-orbit studio with a reputation for layered indie-pop mixing. "Always" and "Awaken" were mixed by Maryam Qudus, the San Francisco producer behind Spacemoth. Adrian Morgan mastered at Timeless Mastering in Brooklyn. The corpus point: a bedroom-orchestra record can be tracked in a modest local room and then sent to a higher-budget mix and master facility — the cost ladder is back-loaded into post-production rather than spread across an expensive tracking schedule.

## "Above" And "Below" — The Two-Half Architecture

The double-LP is split into two named halves. **Above** (Side A/B): "Little Deer," "Always," "Turning Wheel," "The Future," "Awaken," "Emperor with an Egg." **Below** (Side C/D): "Boys at School," "Legacy," "Queen of Wands," "Magic Act," "Revolution," "Sweet Talk" ([Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Turning_Wheel_(album)); [Spellling Bandcamp](https://spellling.bandcamp.com/album/the-turning-wheel)). "Above" tilts toward warm acoustic instrumentation — strings, harp, banjo, choir — and "Below" pulls the palette back toward the darker, more gothic textures of *Mazy Fly*, but voiced now through bassoon, bass clarinet, and bass trombone rather than synth. The structural decision matters because it lets the record honour Cabral's previous synth-darkwave aesthetic on the back half while using the front half to declare the new orchestral vocabulary.

## "Little Deer"

Lead single, opener of "Above," 5:39. The track was sparked by Frida Kahlo's painting *The Little Deer* and an actual encounter near UC Berkeley: *"I can clearly remember walking my dog near UC Berkeley… All I had was that intro and the idea of a deer"* ([Atwood Magazine](https://atwoodmagazine.com/sttw-spellling-the-turning-wheel-music-interview-2021/)). The arrangement opens with string quartet and harp, voice entering high and exposed; brass joins on the second pre-chorus; a soul-inflected groove (congas, electric bass) anchors the chorus. The track is the album's argument-statement: this is what a synth pop song sounds like when every part is acoustic.

## "Always"

"Above" side, 5:14, mixed by Maryam Qudus. Press coverage frames it as a "1950s prom" pastiche with warped edges ([Atwood Magazine](https://atwoodmagazine.com/sttw-spellling-the-turning-wheel-music-interview-2021/)) — slow waltz feel, doo-wop chord changes, but with Cabral's vocal floating in a register and reverb space that reads as ghost-orchestra rather than period revival. The decision to hand "Always" and "Awaken" to a different mixer (Qudus) means the two warmest songs on the record sit in a slightly different sonic room than the rest; the contrast is part of the record's emotional architecture.

## "Boys at School"

Opener of "Below," 7:28 — the longest track on the album and the second single. Cabral on the song's emotional core: she was *"stepping back into my younger self, my teenage self to voice my angst, desires and disillusionments,"* and the main piano motif *"was striking something really raw and both delicate and fierce… immediately transported me to the era of my youth, of this time when you are really beginning to confront the mirror of yourself to the outside world"* ([Circuit Sweet, *Boys at School* single announcement](https://circuitsweet.co.uk/2021/05/spellling-shares-new-single-boys-at-school-from-forthcoming-album-the-turning-wheel-out-june-25-on-sacred-bones/)). Structurally the song works as a chamber-pop overture — solo piano with strings, escalating into alt-R&B / theatrical-pop terrain ([Atwood Magazine](https://atwoodmagazine.com/sttw-spellling-the-turning-wheel-music-interview-2021/)). It is the closest thing on the record to a stage musical in seven minutes.

## "Magic Act"

"Below" side, 5:40. The arrangement leans hardest into the bedroom-orchestra trick: low brass and bassoon take the role a synth bass would have on *Mazy Fly*, with harpsichord-like keyboard figures and string ostinatos building underneath a theatrical vocal. The track is the record's strongest case for why the synth-to-acoustic translation isn't nostalgia or budget cosplay — the *colour* of a real bass clarinet doing what a Juno sub-oscillator would have done changes the affect of the song. Where a synth pad reads as atmosphere, the acoustic instrument reads as a *character*.

## "Sweet Talk"

Closer, 3:33. Pulls the palette back toward intimacy — solo piano and small chamber ensemble, choir entering late, no big brass. After the maximalism of "Revolution" and "Magic Act," "Sweet Talk" functions as a benediction: the orchestra steps back and the auteur-singer is left at a single instrument with one harmonic floor. The record ends not on a climax but on a hush, which is the opposite of the strategy *Mazy Fly* used (synth-driven dark crescendo). It is the architectural proof that Cabral wanted the orchestral record to feel held, not overwhelming.

## *Mazy Fly* → *The Turning Wheel* — The Leap

*Mazy Fly* (Sacred Bones, February 2019, Metacritic 84) was made primarily on a Roland Juno-106 with sparse acoustic overdubs at Antiquity Records in Oakland ([Sacred Bones, *Mazy Fly*](https://www.sacredbonesrecords.com/products/sbr221-spellling-mazy-fly); [Bandcamp, *Mazy Fly*](https://spellling.bandcamp.com/album/mazy-fly)). Its aesthetic is minimal-synth / darkwave with art-pop vocal phrasing — closer to early-'80s 4AD than chamber pop. *The Turning Wheel* shares Cabral's voice, songwriting habits, and Sacred Bones home, but inverts the instrumental ratio. Wikipedia frames the genre shift as *"fewer elements of dark wave and gothic and more of progressive pop compared to Mazy Fly"* ([Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Turning_Wheel_(album))). The corpus pattern: a coherent artistic identity can survive a complete instrumental re-skinning if the songwriting voice and producer-as-auteur stance hold steady.

## Sacred Bones And The Oakland Scene

Sacred Bones is the New York indie label whose roster includes Zola Jesus, Jenny Hval, Pharmakon, Marissa Nadler, Moon Duo, and the Hereditary / Midsommar soundtracks ([Sacred Bones, Spellling collection](https://www.sacredbonesrecords.com/collections/spellling)). The label's gothic / ritual / art-pop centre of gravity makes the *Mazy Fly* → *Turning Wheel* arc legible: Spellling is the artist who translated the label aesthetic into chamber-pop without abandoning it. On the local side, Oakland in the late 2010s hosted a dense experimental-pop and chamber-music ecosystem — Antiquity Records, New Improved Recording, Del Sol Quartet (Bay Area since the early 1990s), and a population of conservatory-trained players willing to work indie-pop sessions. Cabral didn't have to import an orchestra; she built one from her local scene plus ATLYS and Calliope Brass. A "bedroom-orchestra" record is more available in a city with real chamber-music infrastructure than the bedroom premise suggests.

## Critical Reception

Metacritic 79/100 across nine reviews ("generally favorable"); AnyDecentMusic? 7.5/10 ([Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Turning_Wheel_(album))). Notable scores: Pitchfork 7.5/10, Line of Best Fit 9/10, Louder Than War 4.5/5, NME 4/5, Under the Radar 7.5/10, AllMusic 3.5/5. The score is lower than *Mazy Fly*'s 84/100 but the cultural footprint is larger — *The Turning Wheel* is the record that put Spellling on year-end lists in outlets that hadn't covered *Mazy Fly*.

## What This Record Teaches The Corpus

- **A bedroom-synth auteur can scale to a 31-piece ensemble without surrendering authorship** — demo-as-score workflow plus dedicated arrangers (Case, Marchione) translating synth parts to notated charts.
- **Hire ensembles, not session contractors.** ATLYS and Del Sol as distinct quartet textures, Calliope Brass as a coherent quintet — internal character a hired-gun section would not provide.
- **Overdub-orchestral can sound ensemble-orchestral with the right mix discipline.** The demos hold the architectural plan; Vandenberg's mix collapses overdubs into shared rooms.
- **Geographic split of tracking and mixing is a cost ladder.** Local tracking (Oakland), higher-budget mix (Chase Park Transduction), dedicated mastering room (Timeless) — spend back-loaded into post.
- **"Two-half" record-side architecture lets you honour a prior aesthetic without being trapped by it.** "Above" declares the new vocabulary; "Below" honours *Mazy Fly* darkness in acoustic clothes.
- **Instrument *colour* changes more than instrument *role*.** A bass clarinet replacing a sub-bass synth changes the song's emotional weight, not just its timbre.
