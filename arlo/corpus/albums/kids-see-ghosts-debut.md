# Kids See Ghosts — *Kids See Ghosts* (2018)

**Scope:** album-anchored — the Kanye + Cudi joint project as the third release in the 2018 Wyoming Sessions seven-song run, the psych-rock-meets-Cudi-hum production playbook, and the mental-health framing

## Why *Kids See Ghosts* Matters Aesthetically

*Kids See Ghosts* is the third of five seven-song albums Kanye West executive-produced in the summer of 2018 out of his rented and then purchased ranch near Jackson Hole, Wyoming. Released June 8, 2018 through GOOD Music, Wicked Awesome and Def Jam, the record runs twenty-three minutes and fifty seconds across seven tracks and pairs West with Scott Mescudi — Kid Cudi — the artist he signed to GOOD Music in 2008 after first hearing the humming hook of "Day 'n' Nite" ([Wikipedia: *Kids See Ghosts*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kids_See_Ghosts_(album)); [Wikipedia: Kid Cudi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kid_Cudi)).

For ARLO the album is interesting on three fronts: it is the cleanest 2018 example of West's enforced seven-track form factor; it is the record where Mike Dean's role as synth-lead-and-mix-bus partner is most legible under two distinct lead vocalists; and it is a mental-health-themed project made by two collaborators hospitalized within twenty months of recording ([NPR review](https://www.npr.org/2018/06/12/619183944/the-strange-subdued-catharsis-of-kanye-and-cudis-kids-see-ghosts); [Okayplayer: Kid Cudi on the album](https://www.okayplayer.com/news/kid-cudi-kids-see-ghosts-saved-him-mental-health-issues.html)). The College Dropout-era Kanye context — Roc-A-Fella debut, soul-chop loops, school-as-frame conceit — is treated separately in [Kanye West — *The College Dropout* (2004)](./kanye-college-dropout.md).

## The Wyoming Sessions — Where This Album Sits

The Wyoming Sessions is the fan-coined name for the five-week streak of seven-track albums West executive-produced in May–June 2018 from a property he had bought outside Jackson Hole, originally listed as Monster Lake Ranch and renamed West Lake Ranch ([Variety: Inside Kanye's listening extravaganza](https://variety.com/2018/music/news/inside-kanye-wests-album-listening-extravaganza-in-wyoming-1202827523/); [Wikipedia: *Ye*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ye_(album))). The release order:

- **Pusha T — *Daytona*** (May 25, 2018) — seven tracks.
- **Kanye West — *ye*** (June 1, 2018) — seven tracks.
- **Kids See Ghosts — *Kids See Ghosts*** (June 8, 2018) — seven tracks.
- **Nas — *Nasir*** (June 15, 2018) — seven tracks.
- **Teyana Taylor — *K.T.S.E.*** (June 22, 2018) — eight tracks (the only outlier).

Tracking ran across late 2016 through June 2018; the final push happened on the West Lake Ranch property in the weeks before each release ([Wikipedia: *Kids See Ghosts*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kids_See_Ghosts_(album)); [Yeezy Mafia retrospective](https://yeezymafia.com/throwback-the-wyoming-sessions-yes-2018-weekly-album-run/)). Mike Dean has described the format as a deliberate target — at one point West had roughly ten songs cut for *ye*, threw the entire record out weeks before release, and re-cut a new seven-song version in about two weeks ([Complex: Mike Dean on restarting *ye*](https://www.complex.com/music/a/cmplxjoshua-espinoza/mike-dean-talks-kanye-west-discarding-entire-ye-album-restart)). That discipline — twenty-something minutes, no filler, one continuous mood — is the formal innovation of the run and the thing *Kids See Ghosts* most cleanly demonstrates.

The location matters as a production choice — studio-as-retreat: a remote room, a fixed roster of collaborators on premises, and listening sessions held outdoors at night rather than at industry events ([W Magazine: Jackson Hole](https://www.wmagazine.com/gallery/jackson-hole-kanye-west-photos); [Vice: I Feel Like Kanye When I'm in Wyoming](https://www.vice.com/en/article/kanye-west-new-album-listening-party-ye-wyoming/)).

## Producers, Engineers, and Personnel

Executive production is West and Cudi. The supporting roster on the credits is unusually deep for a twenty-four-minute record. Producers and co-producers across the seven tracks include Mike Dean, Plain Pat (Patrick Reynolds), Dot da Genius (Oladipo Omishore), Benny Blanco, André 3000, Andrew Dawson, Evan Mast (of Ratatat), Jeff Bhasker, Justin Vernon, Francis and the Lights, Cashmere Cat, Noah Goldstein, BoogzDaBeast, and others ([Wikipedia: *Kids See Ghosts*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kids_See_Ghosts_(album)); [The Fader: full album credits](https://www.thefader.com/2018/06/08/kids-see-ghost-album-creds)). Plain Pat and Dot da Genius are the original Cudi production architects from *A Kid Named Cudi* and *Man on the Moon: The End of Day* — their presence is how the record threads to Cudi's 2008–09 sonic identity ([Wikipedia: Kid Cudi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kid_Cudi)).

Mike Dean is the through-line. He co-produces on every Wyoming Sessions record, plays synth leads and analog pads, and mixes/masters the run on his own chain — Moog Voyagers, Roland Juno-106, Korg Triton, and an outboard mastering stack that aims at a loud, mid-forward master rather than a streaming-loudness-normalized one ([Tape Op: Mike Dean interview](https://tapeop.com/interviews/122/mike-dean); [MusicTech on Dean's setup](https://musictech.com/news/gear/mike-dean-analogue-synth-reverb-moog-oberheim-yamaha/); [Billboard on Dean and Kanye](https://www.billboard.com/music/features/mike-dean-producer-kanye-west-the-idol-synths-interview-1235433506/)). What is specific to *Kids See Ghosts* is that his synth leads are the connective tissue between Cudi's hummed vocal phrases and the chopped psych-rock samples that supply the rhythm tracks.

## "Feel the Love" — Track 1, Pusha T, and Kanye's Drum-Mouth Hook

The album opens with Cudi singing the title hook ("I can still feel the love") over plaintive piano, then breaks into roughly two minutes of Kanye West vocalizing machine-gun fire as a percussion line. Pusha T delivers the only conventional rap verse. The track is credited to West with co-production from Mike Dean and Benny Blanco, plus additional production from Plain Pat, Evan Mast, Justin Vernon, Francis and the Lights, Cashmere Cat, and Noah Goldstein — a long list of co-pros for a three-minute opener that is mostly one hook, one verse, and a vocalized rhythm ([Wikipedia: Feel the Love](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Feel_the_Love_(Kids_See_Ghosts_song)); [Revolt: Pusha T on his verse](https://www.revolt.tv/article/2018-06-20/95485/pusha-ts-verse-on-kids-see-ghosts-took-place-just-how-you-imagined)).

Pusha's vocal was tracked under ten hours before release — a small but telling fact about Wyoming workflow, where mixes were being finalized while features were being recorded ([Wikipedia: Feel the Love](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Feel_the_Love_(Kids_See_Ghosts_song))). The drum-mouth hook reframes the opener as a sound-design statement: the first thing you hear after Cudi's chorus is not a snare but West using his own voice as an automatic-rifle simulation, mic'd close and mixed up to the master.

## "Fire" and "4th Dimension" — The Sample-Heavy Middle

Track two ("Fire") and track three ("4th Dimension") sit back-to-back as the album's sample-driven peak. "4th Dimension" is the WhoSampled-confirmed flagship: it builds the entire instrumental around Louis Prima's 1936 holiday recording "What Will Santa Claus Say (When He Finds Everybody Swingin')," with Prima posthumously credited as a featured artist on the track ([WhoSampled: 4th Dimension / What Will Santa Claus Say](https://www.whosampled.com/sample/577149/Kids-See-Ghosts-4th-Dimension-Louis-Prima-What-Will-Santa-Claus-Say-(When-He-Finds-Everybody-Swingin%E2%80%99)/); [Wikipedia: 4th Dimension](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/4th_Dimension_(song))). Secondary sample interpolations on the same track include Shirley Ann Lee's "Someday," Master P / Fiend / Silkk the Shocker / Mia X / Mystikal's "Make Em Say Uhh!", and Pusha T's "Infrared" from *Daytona* a fortnight earlier ([WhoSampled track page for 4th Dimension](https://www.whosampled.com/Kids-See-Ghosts/4th-Dimension/)).

The Prima choice is the editorial signature: a 1930s swing record about Santa Claus, retimed into a hip-hop tempo, with a verse from André 3000 underneath. The unlikely source material plus the swing-feel ride pattern is the move — the same Kanye instinct that picked Aretha and Curtis Mayfield in 2004, applied fourteen years later to a holiday novelty. Procedural lesson: provenance does not have to be cool to make a cool sample; the further the source is from hip-hop's default crate, the more recognizable the chop becomes once it lands. "Fire," by contrast, is mostly in-room — guitar, drums, Cudi's lead, the title as chant — moving the album from sample-as-mood into sample-as-architecture without changing register.

## "Reborn" — Plain Pat / Dot da Genius / Evan Mast and the Cudi-Hum Mantra

"Reborn" is the emotional center of the album. The arrangement is a four-chord piano loop (D minor / A minor / G major / F major in most readings) under Cudi humming the title and West rapping a single sustained verse about anxiety and survival ([Wikipedia: Reborn](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reborn_(song)); [Tunebat: Reborn key/BPM](https://tunebat.com/Info/Reborn-KIDS-SEE-GHOSTS/4RVbK6cV0VqWdpCDcx3hiT)). Production credit goes to Kanye West and Dot da Genius with co-production from Plain Pat, Evan Mast, and Kid Cudi, with additional production from Benny Blanco ([WhoSampled: Reborn](https://www.whosampled.com/Kids-See-Ghosts/Reborn/); [The Fader credits](https://www.thefader.com/2018/06/08/kids-see-ghost-album-creds)).

The arrangement is intentionally small. The intro is a plinking piano figure and Cudi's wordless humming — the same humming-as-hook approach Cudi has used since the Sidekick demos that became "Day 'n' Nite" in 2008, a lineage that runs forward through Travis Scott, Juice WRLD, and Lil Peep ([Wikipedia: Day 'n' Nite](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Day_%27n%27_Nite); [Wikipedia: Kid Cudi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kid_Cudi)). On "Reborn" the hum is the song. The writing economy — four chords across roughly five minutes, hum standing in for chorus — is what makes the seven-song format feel earned rather than truncated.

## "Kids See Ghosts" — The Title Track, Yasiin Bey, and the Drum Loop

The title track features Yasiin Bey (Mos Def) and rides a chopped drum loop with a low-strung guitar riff under West's and Cudi's verses ([Wikipedia: *Kids See Ghosts*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kids_See_Ghosts_(album))). It is the album's tightest verse track — the only one where both leads rap in alternating sections without the structure folding into a hummed bridge — and Bey's coda functions as a benediction rather than a feature.

The duo-name conceit ("Kids See Ghosts") is also the project conceit: the mental-health framing of the record reads ghosts as the symptoms — paranoia, intrusive thoughts, the dissociative residue of breakdowns — and treats seeing them as a survivable rather than terminal state ([NPR review](https://www.npr.org/2018/06/12/619183944/the-strange-subdued-catharsis-of-kanye-and-cudis-kids-see-ghosts)).

## "Cudi Montage" — Kurt Cobain "Burn the Rain"

The closer samples Kurt Cobain's unreleased solo home recording "Burn the Rain," released on the 2015 *Montage of Heck: The Home Recordings* soundtrack to the Brett Morgen documentary about Cobain ([WhoSampled: Cudi Montage / Burn the Rain](https://www.whosampled.com/sample/577152/Kids-See-Ghosts-Cudi-Montage-Kurt-Cobain-Burn-the-Rain/); [The FADER: Kanye and Cudi sample Cobain](https://www.thefader.com/2018/06/08/kanye-west-cudi-kurt-cobain-kids-see-ghosts-sample-reborn); [Wikipedia: Cudi Montage](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cudi_Montage)). Cudi has said in a 2018 *Billboard* cover that of every sample on the record, the Cobain clearance was the one he was most anxious about, and that Courtney Love and Frances Bean Cobain were "really cool" about clearing it ([Okayplayer: Cudi Montage as Cobain tribute](https://www.okayplayer.com/originals/kids-see-ghosts-kid-cudi-kanye-kurt-cobain.html)). Production credit is Kid Cudi and Dot da Genius with co-production from Mike Dean and additional vocals from Mr Hudson.

The Cobain chop is dissonant — a chopped guitar strum used as the loop's anchor — and the contrast with West's gospel-leaning verse is the editorial move: the most damaged guitar texture on the record carrying the most resolution-leaning vocal performance.

## "Freeee (Ghost Town, Pt. 2)" — The Mr. Chop "Stark" Sample and Ty Dolla \$ign

The album's penultimate track is a direct sequel to "Ghost Town" from *ye*, released seven days earlier. "Freeee (Ghost Town, Pt. 2)" features Ty Dolla \$ign and is built around a chopped sample of "Stark" by Mr. Chop, including the source recording's electric guitar riff ([Wikipedia: Freeee (Ghost Town, Pt. 2)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Freeee_(Ghost_Town,_Pt._2))). The song reads as rap-rock with psychedelic detuning — Mike Dean's synth lead doubling the guitar riff, the drums leaned hard on the snare backbeat. Production credit is West and Cudi with co-production from Mike Dean, Jeff Bhasker, and BoogzDaBeast, plus additional production from Andrew Dawson, Andy C, and Russell "Love" Crews.

The cross-album sequel ("Ghost Town" → "Freeee (Ghost Town, Pt. 2)") is another small structural innovation of the Wyoming run: two seven-track albums released a week apart, sharing a sonic premise across the seam.

## Mike Dean's Role — Synth Lead, Mix Bus, Loud Master

Across all seven tracks the recurring credit is Mike Dean as co-producer, mixer, and synth player. The audible signature on *Kids See Ghosts* is the way the synth leads sit *above* the sampled rhythm tracks rather than under them — panned center, brought up to vocal level, so that on "Reborn" and the title track the synth is the second lead vocal as much as an instrument.

Dean has said publicly that the seven-song format was less a fixed plan than a moving target — *ye* was cut and re-cut weeks before release, and surrounding albums were finalized in similar last-minute pushes ([Complex: Dean on the *ye* restart](https://www.complex.com/music/a/cmplxjoshua-espinoza/mike-dean-talks-kanye-west-discarding-entire-ye-album-restart); [NME](https://www.nme.com/news/music/mike-dean-says-kanye-west-restarted-work-on-ye-just-weeks-before-release-3294771)). That deadline pressure is part of why the record sounds the way it does: short tracks because there was not time to lengthen them; loud masters because there was not time to second-guess the chain; one hummed hook per song because that was the fastest way to commit a vocal.

## The Mental-Health Framing

Kid Cudi entered a rehabilitation facility in late 2016 for depression and suicidal ideation, mid-rollout for his solo album *Passion, Pain & Demon Slayin'*. Kanye West was hospitalized for psychiatric observation in November 2016 after canceling the *Saint Pablo* tour ([NPR review](https://www.npr.org/2018/06/12/619183944/the-strange-subdued-catharsis-of-kanye-and-cudis-kids-see-ghosts); [Wikipedia: *Kids See Ghosts*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kids_See_Ghosts_(album))). The album's thematic content — anxiety, paranoia, suicidal ideation, recovery — is rooted in both events. Cudi has been quoted saying the project "saved" him at a point where he was uncertain whether he would keep making music ([Okayplayer: Cudi on the album](https://www.okayplayer.com/news/kid-cudi-kids-see-ghosts-saved-him-mental-health-issues.html)).

For ARLO the relevant observation is structural rather than autobiographical: the record's compression — seven songs, twenty-three minutes, hummed hooks, no skips, no extended runs — is itself a treatment plan. There is no room for a self-indulgent verse, because there is no second verse on most tracks. The form is doing therapeutic work that the lyrics describe.

## Critical Reception and Chart Performance

Metacritic aggregated 18 reviews to a score of 84/100, the highest of any Wyoming Sessions release ([Metacritic page](https://www.metacritic.com/music/kids-see-ghosts/kids-see-ghosts)). The album debuted at number two on the Billboard 200 and topped the Top R&B/Hip-Hop Albums chart, going gold in the US and silver in the UK ([Wikipedia: *Kids See Ghosts*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kids_See_Ghosts_(album))). Critical consensus across Pitchfork, NPR, *Spin*, and others foregrounded the chemistry between the two leads and the psychedelic production register, with NPR characterizing the record as "a performance of depression" that nonetheless ends in something like catharsis ([NPR](https://www.npr.org/2018/06/12/619183944/the-strange-subdued-catharsis-of-kanye-and-cudis-kids-see-ghosts); [Spin review](https://www.spin.com/2018/06/kanye-west-kid-cudi-kids-see-ghosts-review/)).

## What ARLO Should Take from *Kids See Ghosts*

For a user trying to make a record like this:

- **Pick a form factor that forces editing.** Seven tracks, twenty-three minutes. You do not need a second verse if you do not have one ready. A hum can be the chorus.
- **Use unlikely source material.** A 1936 Louis Prima holiday novelty becomes the bed of "4th Dimension" because no one else is digging there. Crate-distance is editorial leverage.
- **Layer two distinct lead vocalists.** West-as-rapper and Cudi-as-hum are different instruments. Alternating them eliminates the need for transitions a single voice would have to build.
- **Let the mix engineer play.** Mike Dean is credited as synth player and co-producer on every track, not just as mixer. The synth lead and the mix decision are the same decision.
- **Make deadline pressure part of the method.** The Wyoming Sessions ran on weekly drops with songs finished hours before release. The form's brevity is a function of that pressure, not a separate aesthetic choice.
