## What this file is — and what it isn't

This is the session-anchored record of how *Lincoln* (released September 25, 1988, on Bar/None / Restless) got made — who tracked what at Al Houghton's Dubway Studio in Times Square, how the drum-machine + accordion arrangements were sequenced on a Macintosh 512k running Performer, and how a body of material that had been audience-tested across the *Bring Me The Head Of Kenny Rogers* touring year became the band's second LP. It is not a TMBG career biography and not a Dial-A-Song service history; it is the second-album consolidation moment, when the two Johns made the jump from a Brooklyn answering-machine demo pipeline to a real record that would chart.

## The producer credit — Bill Krauss, the "third Giant," for the last time

The sleeve credits **Bill Krauss** as producer, and his role was larger than the word implies. Krauss had been with TMBG since 1983, when he met Flansburgh through a mutual Antioch College friend; he produced the 1985 demo tape, the 1986 self-titled debut, and *Lincoln*, and also ran live sound from a 1983 show at Dr. B's onward — TMBW summarizes his standing as "the third Giant" ([TMBW — Bill Krauss](https://tmbw.net/wiki/Bill_Krauss)). *Lincoln* was Krauss's last record with the band; he stepped back after release, citing a dislike of crowds as audiences grew. His technical legacy on *Lincoln* is specific: he put a computer in the room. Per Linnell, Krauss "kind of forced" the band onto MIDI sequencing, telling them "drum sequencing on the Macintosh was going to be the big change" between records ([TMBW — Lincoln](https://tmbw.net/wiki/Lincoln)). When ARLO is asked who produced *Lincoln*, the answer is: **Bill Krauss, sole credit, the final TMBG album in a five-year working relationship that began before the band had a label.**

## The studio — Dubway, Times Square, Al Houghton

All tracking and mixing happened at **Dubway Studio**, Al Houghton's room in the Music Building in Midtown Manhattan, founded in 1981 ([Wikipedia — Dubway Studios](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dubway_Studios)). The band had relocated to Dubway from Studio PASS during the final sessions of the 1986 debut and stayed for *Lincoln*, with Houghton engineering both. Sessions began as early as **October 1987**, after the *Bring Me The Head Of Kenny Rogers* tour, and ran through **May–June 1988**, finishing about four months before the September 25 release ([Wikipedia — Lincoln](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lincoln_(They_Might_Be_Giants_album)); [TMBW — Lincoln](https://tmbw.net/wiki/Lincoln)). The room was small; at one point during summer overdubs Krauss's Macintosh 512k overheated and smoked — Linnell described "an enormous column of smoke" — but the Performer sequences had been backed to floppies and no work was lost. *Lincoln* is the record where TMBG's workflow became computer-dependent enough that a Mac crash was a session crisis.

## The Dial-A-Song pipeline — what changed for the LP

Dial-A-Song had been running since November 1983, after Flansburgh's apartment was burglarized and Linnell broke his wrist in a bicycle accident, halting live work. Flansburgh adapted the format from Dial-A-Prayer and ran it from a Crazy Eddie answering machine housed inside a suitcase in his Bed-Stuy kitchen, updated nearly daily, eventually on the Brooklyn number 718-387-6962 after the 1984 area-code split ([TMBW — Dial-A-Song](https://tmbw.net/wiki/Dial-A-Song)). By 1987 the service was the band's primary songwriting feedback loop; demos for **"The World's Address"** and **"Snowball In Hell"** went out on Dial-A-Song before the LP, and **"Lie Still, Little Bottle,"** **"Pencil Rain,"** and **"Purple Toupee"** were road-tested live across the 1987 tour ([TMBW — Lincoln](https://tmbw.net/wiki/Lincoln)). Flansburgh framed the consolidation: "A lot of the material on the first album was just written for the record and hadn't been audience tested. The big difference with *Lincoln* is that we've done hundreds of shows since that first record." *Lincoln* is not a bedroom-tape compilation translated to studio fidelity — it is bedroom-tape sketches that survived a tour and a phone line before they were tracked.

## The instruments — Linnell's accordion, Flansburgh's guitar, everything else

The two-Johns instrument map on *Lincoln* is wider than the duo's reputation suggests. **John Linnell**: lead vocals, accordion, autoharp, clarinet, keyboard, saxophones, bass harmonica, banjo. **John Flansburgh**: lead vocals, electric and acoustic guitar, trumpet, melodica ([Wikipedia — Lincoln](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lincoln_(They_Might_Be_Giants_album))). The accordion is the album's signature timbre. Linnell picked it up in 1983 because a neighbor in his apartment building had one and TMBG needed something portable for a busking show on the Brooklyn Promenade; his framing is that he was already a keyboard player (organ and clavinet) and the accordion was a "more portable and accessible" keyboard with bellows-driven dynamic control closer to an organ than a piano ([TMBW — Accordion](https://tmbw.net/wiki/Accordion)). On *Lincoln* he favors the right-hand keyboard side and uses the bellows for swells rather than left-hand bass-button parts. The accordion functions as the band's primary harmonic instrument where a guitar band would put rhythm guitar — the lead hook of "Ana Ng," the verse pads of "Purple Toupee," the polka shuffle of "Cowtown."

## The drum machines — Yamaha RX, Alesis HR-16, the Chris Butler nudge

*Lincoln* was tracked almost entirely to programmed drums. Initial sequencing went to a **Yamaha RX-11 / RX-15** driven by **Performer** running on Krauss's **Apple Macintosh 512k**, with a MIDI box between them — TMBG's first computer-sequenced record ([TMBW — Lincoln](https://tmbw.net/wiki/Lincoln); [Wikipedia — Lincoln](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lincoln_(They_Might_Be_Giants_album))). Midway through production, Chris Butler (of the Waitresses) recommended the newly released **Alesis HR-16** and the band switched for much of the album. Flansburgh's framing: the HR-16 offered "varied and often smaller sounds" missing from previous machines, including a more authentic snare "ping" and papery brush-on-snare textures that suited the band's vaudeville-pop arrangements better than the bigger Yamaha kits. The HR-16 had launched in 1987, was affordable for indie acts, and gave TMBG a more acoustic-leaning sample library at exactly the moment they were graduating from cassette arrangements. **One track uses live drums** — Dr. Kenneth Nolan plays kit on the jazz-inflected "Lie Still, Little Bottle" — but the other seventeen are sequenced. The decision is not a financial workaround; it is the band's compositional voice.

## The other studio tools — Akai S1000, CZ-101, Fairlight

Beyond the drum machines, the *Lincoln* signal chain included an **Akai S1000 sampler** (used for sample-and-trigger work including the spoken-word material below), a **Casio CZ-101** or **Micromoog** for synth bass, and a **Fairlight CMI** for the scream effects on "Cowtown" ([TMBW — Lincoln](https://tmbw.net/wiki/Lincoln)). Keyboard parts were played live, not sequenced — the Macintosh handled drums and triggers, but accordion, organ, clavinet, and synth lines were performed in real time to tape. *Lincoln* is not a sequenced-everything record; the rhythm section is computerized and the harmonic/melodic layer is hand-played, sometimes with vintage acoustic instruments. That hybrid is the album's structural signature.

## "Ana Ng" — noise-gated guitar and the Vietnamese phone book

"Ana Ng" is the album's lead single (1988, #11 Billboard Modern Rock Tracks despite no U.S. commercial release) and the clearest single-song case study of the album's method ([Wikipedia — Ana Ng](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ana_Ng)). The title came from Linnell flipping through the NYC phone book, finding roughly four pages of the surname **Ng** — vowelless, common in Vietnamese and Cantonese, dense in NYC — and calling numbers; he reached a Dr. Ng's voicemail, which seeded the song. The lyric reframes the incident as an antipodal love song: the narrator imagines someone on the opposite side of the world, with the 1964 New York World's Fair as recurring reference. Flansburgh has elaborated that if "Ana Ng" lives in Vietnam, the narrator must be in Peru — Peru is Vietnam's geographic antipode.

The production trick worth keeping is the **noise-gated guitar**. Linnell wanted Flansburgh's rhythm guitar to sound "really robotic," and Krauss programmed a gate via the Macintosh that opens and closes in MIDI-sync with the drum sequence, slicing the sustained chord into a stuttering machine-pulse — a forerunner of the sidechain-gated-pad sound ubiquitous a decade later, executed in 1988 with a 512k Mac and a hardware gate. The bridge contains a spoken line from **Lisa Klapp**, recorded over the telephone. The Adam Bernstein video was shot at Ward's Island Fireman's Training Center; Pitchfork later flagged the visual non-sequitur of the video using a map of Romania despite the song's Vietnam/Peru geography.

## "Snowball in Hell" — the Paul J. Meyer sample

"Snowball in Hell" is TMBG's clearest case of building a song around a found audio object. The recurring spoken-word phrase is sampled from **Paul J. Meyer**'s 1961 motivational LP *Time Capital*, part of *How to Master Time Organization*, released by the Success Motivation Institute — Joe Anderson's voice asking Paul whether he is back on the "time is money" kick ([WhoSampled — Snowball in Hell](https://www.whosampled.com/sample/420148/They-Might-Be-Giants-Snowball-in-Hell-Paul-J.-Meyer-Time-Capital/)). The cassette had been a birthday gift to Flansburgh, repurposed as sample source — the Dial-A-Song-era TMBG aesthetic of treating thrift-store ephemera as compositional raw material, scaled up to LP fidelity via the Akai S1000. The demo had circulated on Dial-A-Song before the album cut.

## "Purple Toupee" — the unreasonable narrator and the 1960s

"Purple Toupee" is broad political satire told by a narrator who lived through the 1960s and now misremembers everything — civil-rights movements, assassinations, and cultural touchstones jumbled into a confidently wrong recitation. Linnell has described his use of the **unreasonable narrator** — articulate, certain, factually demolished — as a recurring writing device, and "Purple Toupee" is the canonical example ([TMBW — Purple Toupee](https://tmbw.net/wiki/Purple_Toupee)). The stated target is the late-1980s "sixties revival" Linnell perceived as a one-dimensional caricature; the joke is that the narrator IS the caricature. The arrangement leans on accordion, drum machine, and a strident Flansburgh guitar lick functioning as percussion. Released as the third single in July 1989.

## "They'll Need a Crane" — Gerry & the Pacemakers riff, Housemartins arrangement

Linnell has described the song as starting with "an old Gerry & the Pacemakers riff" — a Merseybeat melodic kernel — arranged via what Flansburgh has called a Housemartins influence, citing similarities to "Happy Hour" (1986) ([Wikipedia — They'll Need a Crane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/They'll_Need_a_Crane)). Linnell's method: "we think up a good title and then write the song round it" — the title-first technique that drives much of the *Lincoln* lyric body. The song is about the dissolution of a relationship between two characters ("Lad" and "Gal"), narrated with the same arms-length lightness the band applies to harder subjects across the record. Released as the second single in February 1989, and the song TMBG performed on their **network-television debut on *Late Night with David Letterman*, February 1, 1989**.

## The Ordinaires on "Kiss Me, Son of God"

The album's one obvious chamber-arranged moment is the closing **"Kiss Me, Son of God,"** with arrangement contributions from **The Ordinaires**, a nine-piece Manhattan ensemble — strings and brass scored over the band's existing track ([TMBW — Lincoln](https://tmbw.net/wiki/Lincoln)). Krauss had a working relationship with the Ordinaires and produced their material separately. It is the album's only large-ensemble texture, deliberately sequenced as the LP's exit.

## The label — Bar/None, Hoboken, and the Enigma deal

*Lincoln* was released on **Bar/None Records**, the New Jersey indie label founded by Hoboken record-store owner **Tom Prendergast** and partner **Glenn Morrow**. TMBG's 1986 debut had been Bar/None's launch release; *Lincoln* arrived with Bar/None distributed through **Enigma Records**, then the second-largest U.S. independent ([TMBW — Bar/None](https://tmbw.net/wiki/Bar/None)). Restless Records handled some U.S. distribution. The album peaked at **#89 on Billboard's Top Pop Albums chart** in 1989, spent 19 weeks on the chart, and by 1999 had sold over 200,000 copies — strong enough to bring TMBG to Elektra in 1990 for *Flood*. It also peaked at #116 on Australia's ARIA chart, the band's first international chart appearance.

## Critical reception — Christgau, Trouser Press, AllMusic

*Lincoln* arrived to predominantly positive reviews. AllMusic later gave it five stars; NME 8/10; *Q* 4/5; *Rolling Stone* 3/5, calling it "every bit as eccentric as its predecessor" ([Wikipedia — Lincoln](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lincoln_(They_Might_Be_Giants_album))). **Robert Christgau** in *The Village Voice* was characteristically mixed — calling the band "actively annoying" and the hooks "cleverness for cleverness's sake," while conceding "damned clever they are" and singling out "Purple Toupee" and "Ana Ng" ([Robert Christgau — Lincoln](http://robertchristgau.com/get_album.php?id=3923)). **Trouser Press** flagged the album's "baffling level of invention" alongside increased musical complexity and electricity over the debut ([Trouser Press](https://trouserpress.com/reviews/they-might-be-giants/)). Pitchfork later placed *Lincoln* at #78 on its 100 Best Albums of the 1980s — the canonization moment, given that in 1988 TMBG read as a novelty act to much of the press.

## The artwork — Brian Dewan shrine, Linnell accordion diagrams

The cover — a shrine assembled by **Brian Dewan**, photographed by **Carol Kitman** — and the CD/LP label graphics, drawn by **Linnell as accordion-instruction diagrams**, were designed by Flansburgh. Two separate cover photos were used for domestic and international releases. The accordion-diagram labels advertise the album's central instrument in a quasi-pedagogical register — the same wink-at-the-form tone the music itself operates in.

## Why this record matters to ARLO

*Lincoln* is the canonical example of three principles ARLO is built to support: (1) **a bedroom-tape pipeline can scale**, but only if demos are audience-tested before the studio — Dial-A-Song plus a tour year is what makes the second album a consolidation rather than a sophomore slump; (2) **drum-machine plus accordion is a complete arrangement vocabulary** — a sequencer and a harmonic instrument with dynamic control can replace a conventional rhythm section; (3) **the unreasonable narrator** is a generative songwriting device, holding the form (confident first-person testimony) constant while the content (factually demolished) does the comic and emotional work. The workflow — write a title, sketch on Dial-A-Song, road-test on tour, then track with sequenced drums and live-played accordion/guitar in a small Manhattan room — maps cleanly onto a DAW-plus-LLM workflow today.
