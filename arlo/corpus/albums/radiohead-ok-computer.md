# Radiohead — *OK Computer* (1997)

**Artist:** Radiohead (Thom Yorke, Jonny Greenwood, Ed O'Brien, Colin Greenwood, Phil Selway)
**Year:** 1997 (released 21 May 1997 in Japan, 16 June 1997 in the UK on Parlophone, 1 July 1997 in the US on Capitol) ([Wikipedia: *OK Computer*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/OK_Computer))
**Producers:** Radiohead and Nigel Godrich — Godrich's first full Radiohead production credit, having engineered *The Bends* (1995) under John Leckie ([Wikipedia: *OK Computer*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/OK_Computer); [Audio Media International — Deep Cuts: The Making of *OK Computer*](https://audiomediainternational.com/radiohead-ok-computer-producer/))
**Engineering Crew:** Godrich worked essentially solo. Per his own account: "Literally, it was just me working on the album. I didn't have an assistant; I didn't have any help." The total session crew was "the band and me and Peter 'Plank' [Clements] who was their roadie… the seven of us plus the cook and Mango, Jane's cat" ([Rolling Stone — *OK Computer*: An Oral History](https://www.rollingstone.com/music/music-features/radioheads-ok-computer-an-oral-history-196156/); [Audio Media International](https://audiomediainternational.com/radiohead-ok-computer-producer/))
**Studios:** Canned Applause (apple-storage shed near Didcot, Oxfordshire — July 1996); St. Catherine's Court (16th-century Tudor manor near Bath, Somerset, owned by actress Jane Seymour — September–October 1996, with overflow sessions into early 1997); strings tracked at Abbey Road Studios, London (January 1997); mixing at AIR and Mayfair Studios, London; mastering at Abbey Road. Album completed 6 March 1997 ([Wikipedia: *OK Computer*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/OK_Computer); [Audio Media International](https://audiomediainternational.com/radiohead-ok-computer-producer/))
**String Arrangement:** Jonny Greenwood (sixteen-violin section on "Climbing Up the Walls"; smaller string parts on "Exit Music (For a Film)" and elsewhere) — recorded at Abbey Road
**Scope:** album-anchored — the mansion-recording sessions that bridged Radiohead from guitar-rock (*The Bends*) toward post-rock-electronic (*Kid A*), and Godrich's transition from house engineer to lifelong band collaborator
**Source:** Deep-research synthesis (2026-05) — verify before treating as authoritative
**Aesthetic-stack relevance:** art-rock production, mansion-studio tracking, producer-as-collaborator origins, dense-but-clear arrangement, the late-90s tape-plus-Pro-Tools hybrid that would dominate the next decade
**Tags:** `radiohead`, `ok-computer`, `nigel-godrich`, `st-catherines-court`, `1997`, `art-rock`, `mansion-studio`

## Why *OK Computer* Matters Aesthetically

*OK Computer* is the record where Radiohead stopped being a guitar band and became a production project. *The Bends* (1995) had won the band critical respect within the alternative-rock framework; *OK Computer* dismantled that framework while still tracking, for the most part, live in a room. The album is the hinge between two eras: behind it sits the verse-chorus rock songcraft of *Pablo Honey* (1993) and *The Bends*; in front of it sits the textural, electronic, Pro-Tools-as-composition method of *Kid A* (2000) — covered in detail in [Radiohead — *Kid A*](./radiohead-kid-a.md). What *OK Computer* uniquely owns is the *bridge* — guitars and live drums still doing most of the structural work, but with Mellotron, sampled drum loops, musique concrète interludes, Penderecki-influenced string clusters, and Pro Tools editing already entering the vocabulary.

For ARLO's purposes, this is the canonical case study of a band leveraging an unconventional recording environment (a Tudor mansion, no studio clock, no label suits on site) to accelerate a stylistic shift the band could not have made inside a conventional commercial facility.

## The Canned Applause Phase — An Apple Shed Without a Toilet

Sessions began in July 1996 at Canned Applause — a converted apple-storage shed on a farm near Didcot, Oxfordshire, that the band used as their rehearsal and recording space ([Wikipedia: *OK Computer*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/OK_Computer)). Godrich's description is unsentimental: "essentially, a cork box without a toilet," located near farmland and a power station ([Audio Media International](https://audiomediainternational.com/radiohead-ok-computer-producer/)).

The band finished or nearly finished four songs there — "Electioneering," "No Surprises," "Subterranean Homesick Alien," and "The Tourist" ([Wikipedia: *OK Computer*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/OK_Computer)). But the shed proved fatal to the album as a whole: Yorke blamed its proximity to the band members' homes (everyone went home at night, momentum collapsed), while Jonny Greenwood blamed the lack of dining and bathroom facilities ([Wikipedia: *OK Computer*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/OK_Computer)). The decision to move was structural — a record made of self-contained day-jobs was not going to cohere.

## St. Catherine's Court — The Mansion as Instrument

Radiohead rented St. Catherine's Court, a 16th-century Tudor manor near Bath owned by actress Jane Seymour, for the second tranche of sessions in September–October 1996, with overflow work continuing into early 1997 ([Wikipedia: *OK Computer*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/OK_Computer); [nigelgodrichproducer.tumblr.com — OK Computer, 20 Years Later](https://nigelgodrichproducer.tumblr.com/post/162026485890/ok-computer-20-years-later)). The Cure had recorded *Wild Mood Swings* there in 1995, so a production-ready power supply and the precedent of a working session were in place ([nigelgodrichproducer.tumblr.com](https://nigelgodrichproducer.tumblr.com/post/162026485890/ok-computer-20-years-later)).

Godrich treated the building itself as part of the instrument list. He set the control room up in the library — "a lovely dead space" — and tracked the band in the manor's ballroom, with its wooden floors and paneling ([nigelgodrichproducer.tumblr.com](https://nigelgodrichproducer.tumblr.com/post/162026485890/ok-computer-20-years-later)). The nursery, full of soft toys, was used for vocals; the stone rooms and an orangery attached to the building were used for further vocal sessions ([nigelgodrichproducer.tumblr.com](https://nigelgodrichproducer.tumblr.com/post/162026485890/ok-computer-20-years-later)). The lead vocal on "Exit Music (For a Film)" was tracked partway up the Court's stone staircase to harvest the natural reverb ([Audio Media International](https://audiomediainternational.com/radiohead-ok-computer-producer/)).

Godrich's framing of the why: "Why would you go into a space where people had done the same thing a thousand times? It's like using a public toilet" ([Rolling Stone Oral History](https://www.rollingstone.com/music/music-features/radioheads-ok-computer-an-oral-history-196156/)). And on the creative consequence of room choice: "If I set you up in that corner here, you'll always remember this room and you'll be inspired by that plant or whatever" ([nigelgodrichproducer.tumblr.com](https://nigelgodrichproducer.tumblr.com/post/162026485890/ok-computer-20-years-later)). Colin Greenwood, more directly: "It was this incredibly beautiful, peaceful place before we ruined the silence with the music" ([Rolling Stone Oral History](https://www.rollingstone.com/music/music-features/radioheads-ok-computer-an-oral-history-196156/)).

For broader context on Godrich's repeated use of country houses across his career — the same logic later applied at Tottenham House on *In Rainbows* and at Batsford Park on *Kid A* — see [Nigel Godrich Engineering Method](../artists/nigel-godrich-engineering-method.md).

## The Engineer-Becomes-Producer Promotion

Godrich had been Radiohead's engineer, not producer, on *The Bends* sessions in 1995 — working under producer John Leckie at RAK Studios in London ([Wikipedia: Nigel Godrich](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nigel_Godrich)). For *OK Computer*, the band initially intended to self-produce and had brought Godrich in for equipment advice. He gradually evolved into co-producer over the course of the sessions ([Audio Media International](https://audiomediainternational.com/radiohead-ok-computer-producer/)). His own framing of the role: "take responsibility for the record… ensure they get the ideas across" ([Audio Media International](https://audiomediainternational.com/radiohead-ok-computer-producer/)).

This is the album that established a career-long relationship: Godrich has produced every Radiohead studio album since, and he was widely characterized within a decade as the band's "sixth member." For the full arc — including the Beck, McCartney, Atoms for Peace, and Smile work that branched out from this foundation — see [Nigel Godrich Engineering Method](../artists/nigel-godrich-engineering-method.md).

## The Rig — Tape, Two Mixing Desks, a Mac, and a Plate

The tracking rig at St. Catherine's Court was a 1990s analog-spine hybrid ([Audio Media International](https://audiomediainternational.com/radiohead-ok-computer-producer/); [The King of Gear — Nigel Godrich's Rig](https://thekingofgear.com/nigel)):

- **Tape machine:** Otari MTR-90II two-inch 24-track
- **Mixing desks:** an MTA Series 980 (vintage British design, rare even in 1997) as the primary console, with a Soundcraft Spirit 24 for extra channels
- **Plate reverb:** EMT 140
- **Pro Tools** for editing and final polish

Guitars: Yorke through a Fender Twin Reverb; Jonny Greenwood and Ed O'Brien on Vox AC30 for clean tones, with Greenwood's Fender 85 and O'Brien's Mesa/Boogie Dual Rectifier Trem-O-Verb for the overdrive work. Mics were Shure SM57s on cabinets. Colin Greenwood's bass: a sunburst Fender Precision through a Gallien-Krueger 800RB head and an Ampeg SVT 8×10. Keys/synths included a Novation Bass Station, a Korg Prophecy, an analog Moog, the Mellotron M400, and an obscure ZX Spectrum-based synth used for the "bubbling outro" on "Let Down." Drum sampling was via an Akai S3000 rack-mount. Vocal chain: a Neumann valve U47 or a Rode valve mic into Godrich's outboard ([Audio Media International](https://audiomediainternational.com/radiohead-ok-computer-producer/); [The King of Gear](https://thekingofgear.com/nigel)).

Godrich on the vocal treatment, which he kept restrained: "I haven't used much processing, just a bit of plate reverb, or a short delay… I'm always thinking, can you hear what he's saying, because his lyrics are so great" ([Audio Media International](https://audiomediainternational.com/radiohead-ok-computer-producer/)).

## Live-In-A-Room — Roughly 80% of the Album

The fundamental tracking posture at St. Catherine's Court was live performance with minimal isolation. Approximately 80% of the album was captured live, without instrument-level overdubs ([Wikipedia: *OK Computer*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/OK_Computer)). Ed O'Brien, blunt about it: "A lot of that record was recorded live without people recording overdubs… something special happens when you're playing live… you're looking at one another" ([Wikipedia: *OK Computer*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/OK_Computer); [Rolling Stone Oral History](https://www.rollingstone.com/music/music-features/radioheads-ok-computer-an-oral-history-196156/)).

Yorke's vocals were frequently kept from first takes — his stated belief was that subsequent takes would "sound really lame" ([Wikipedia: *OK Computer*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/OK_Computer)). This is the same don't-over-polish instinct that runs through Godrich's later philosophy: "vibe over fidelity, momentum over polish" — see [Nigel Godrich Engineering Method](../artists/nigel-godrich-engineering-method.md).

## "Paranoid Android" — The Four-Section Tape Stitch

"Paranoid Android" is the album's most architecturally ambitious track — a 6:23 multi-section composition the band openly likened to a collision of the Beatles' "Happiness Is a Warm Gun" and Queen's "Bohemian Rhapsody" ([Wikipedia: *OK Computer*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/OK_Computer)). It was originally drafted at roughly fourteen minutes and trimmed in editing to its final length.

Production-wise, the song's distinct sections were tracked months apart, which forced Godrich to "manually merge each element to just one piece of 24-track tape" — a tape-edit operation he stood back from once finished and said, in his own paraphrase, "You guys have no idea what I've just done" ([Audio Media International](https://audiomediainternational.com/radiohead-ok-computer-producer/); [Rolling Stone Oral History](https://www.rollingstone.com/music/music-features/radioheads-ok-computer-an-oral-history-196156/)). The result is a song that *sounds* through-composed but was assembled from non-contiguous performances — a method that prefigures the cut-and-paste workflow that would dominate *Kid A* three years later, though done here with razor blades and bounces rather than Pro Tools regions.

## "Karma Police" — The Live-Take Half and the Loop Half

"Karma Police" was initially tracked as a complete live performance, but Yorke was dissatisfied with the second half. Godrich's account: "We went out for a pint and he sort of complained about how he didn't like the second half" ([Audio Media International](https://audiomediainternational.com/radiohead-ok-computer-producer/)). The solution was to keep the live first half and rebuild the song from the middle section onward using samples and loops rather than band performance, with O'Brien's self-oscillating guitar — fed through an AMS DMX 15-80S digital delay — providing the chaotic outro texture ([Audio Media International](https://audiomediainternational.com/radiohead-ok-computer-producer/)).

The chord progression of the verses tips audibly to the Beatles' "Sexy Sadie" — a descending chromatic inner voice that arguably borrows the G-chord arpeggio shape from Lennon's *White Album* original ([Diffuser — Beatles vs. Radiohead](https://diffuser.fm/the-beatles-radiohead-song-parallels/); [Wikipedia: *OK Computer*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/OK_Computer)). The band has not made the borrowing a public talking point, but the resemblance is clear enough that Noel Gallagher publicly accused Radiohead of having "basically ripped off the Beatles" — a charge that probably says more about Gallagher than about Radiohead, but is worth noting as a contemporaneous data point.

## "Climbing Up the Walls" — Penderecki and the Sixteen Detuned Violins

The album's most overtly avant-garde arrangement decision is Jonny Greenwood's string writing for "Climbing Up the Walls." Greenwood scored the climactic string section for sixteen violins and instructed the players to play quarter-tones apart from each other, producing a dissonant white-noise cluster directly modeled on Krzysztof Penderecki's *Threnody to the Victims of Hiroshima* (1960) ([Diffuser — Radiohead Uses a String Section to Go "Climbing Up the Walls"](https://diffuser.fm/radiohead-climbing-up-the-walls/); [Wikipedia: Jonny Greenwood](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jonny_Greenwood)). The arrangement was a deliberate move away from "Eleanor Rigby"-style rock strings, which Greenwood had come to find cliché ([Diffuser](https://diffuser.fm/radiohead-climbing-up-the-walls/)). The strings were tracked at Abbey Road in January 1997.

This is the moment Jonny Greenwood's *arranger-composer* identity arrives on record. Within three years he would be writing for the Orchestra of St John's on *Kid A*'s "How to Disappear Completely" (see [Radiohead — *Kid A*](./radiohead-kid-a.md)); within a decade he would be writing soundtrack work for Paul Thomas Anderson. The Penderecki-derived cluster on "Climbing Up the Walls" is the first piece of evidence that Greenwood was going to be more than a guitarist.

## "No Surprises" — One Take, Glockenspiel, the Lullaby Aesthetic

"No Surprises" was, by the band's account, recorded in many versions but kept as the first take — every later attempt felt worse ([Wikipedia: No Surprises](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/No_Surprises)). The track is built on acoustic and electric guitar, glockenspiel (Jonny Greenwood's Premier model — see [The King of Gear — Jonny's Premier Glockenspiel](https://thekingofgear.com/post/163515773455/jonnys-premier-glockenspiel)), and vocal harmonies. The opening guitar figure is consciously modeled on the Beach Boys' "Wouldn't It Be Nice" — a twinkling, pacified surface deliberately set against lyrics that read, in plain English, as a depressive's quiet wish for self-erasure ([Ultimate Classic Rock — No Surprises](https://ultimateclassicrock.com/radiohead-no-surprises/); [Diffuser — No Surprises](https://diffuser.fm/radiohead-no-surprises/)).

The aesthetic — childlike timbre, low affect, lyrical despair — would become a Radiohead signature. There is no celeste on the recording; the bright top is glockenspiel. (Treat any "celeste on No Surprises" claim with skepticism; the well-documented instrument is the Premier glockenspiel.)

## "Subterranean Homesick Alien" — Rhodes and *Bitches Brew*

"Subterranean Homesick Alien" is the Fender Rhodes track on the record, and it is — per Yorke's own statement in a Q Magazine interview from October 1997 — the product of him listening to Miles Davis's *Bitches Brew* (1970) endlessly while driving ([Wikipedia: Subterranean Homesick Alien](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Subterranean_Homesick_Alien); [Diffuser — Subterranean Homesick Alien](https://diffuser.fm/radiohead-subterranean-homesick-alien/)). The track features Greenwood's guitar through effects pedals over Yorke on Fender Rhodes — a deliberate emulation of *Bitches Brew*'s wandering, weather-system atmosphere. The song was tracked at Canned Applause in early August 1996 and survived the move to St. Catherine's Court intact ([Wikipedia: Subterranean Homesick Alien](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Subterranean_Homesick_Alien)).

Yorke later noted that to play the song live the band would have to carry two Fender Rhodes — a small logistical point that incidentally signals how central the keyboard was to the track's identity.

## "Exit Music (For a Film)" — Mellotron Choir, Stone Stairs, Portishead-Adjacent Bass

"Exit Music (For a Film)" was commissioned for the closing credits of Baz Luhrmann's *Romeo + Juliet* (1996). It opens with Yorke's acoustic guitar and voice — the lead vocal tracked partway up the stone staircase at St. Catherine's Court to capture the natural reverb ([Audio Media International](https://audiomediainternational.com/radiohead-ok-computer-producer/)). A Mellotron M400 8-Voice Choir patch builds underneath the second half ([WhoSampled — Exit Music (For a Film) sample of Mellotron's 8 Voice Choir #1](https://www.whosampled.com/sample/827554/Radiohead-Exit-Music-(For-a-Film)-Mellotron-8-Voice-Choir-%231/)), and the climax brings in drums and a heavily distorted bass — an explicit attempt, per the band, at the Portishead trip-hop sound that was dominant in Bristol at the time ([Wikipedia: *OK Computer*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/OK_Computer)).

The Mellotron choir patch — sampled from a real choir in the early 1960s, replayed via tape strip — is the signature texture of the track, and shows up again on "Lucky" and "The Tourist" ([Planet Mellotron — Radiohead](https://www.planetmellotron.com/revr1.htm); [Wikipedia: *OK Computer*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/OK_Computer)).

## "Airbag" — DJ Shadow, the S3000, and the Pedal-Board Loop

"Airbag" opens the album with a sampled, looped drum part — Selway's playing chopped up via an Akai S3000 and edited on a Mac, then run back through Jonny Greenwood's guitar pedal board across multiple takes to give it "distorted bite, phasing and occasional wah" ([Audio Media International](https://audiomediainternational.com/radiohead-ok-computer-producer/); [Wikipedia: *OK Computer*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/OK_Computer)). The methodology is openly cribbed from DJ Shadow's *Endtroducing…* (1996), which had recalibrated the entire question of what a drum performance could be on a rock record.

For ARLO, this is the most direct sample-thinking-applied-to-live-drums moment on the album — and the technique it foreshadows on *Kid A*'s programmed-and-acoustic toggle ("15 Step" on *In Rainbows* eventually makes the same move).

## "Let Down" — The 3 AM Ballroom Take in 5/4

"Let Down" was tracked in the master ballroom at St. Catherine's Court at 3 AM ([Audio Media International](https://audiomediainternational.com/radiohead-ok-computer-producer/)). The track's signature texture is the interlocking arpeggios — Greenwood and O'Brien playing a "sparkling arpeggiated Fender Starcaster on Rickenbacker riff" in 5/4 against the rest of the band's 4/4 pulse, "to add a sense of floaty groundlessness" ([Audio Media International](https://audiomediainternational.com/radiohead-ok-computer-producer/)). The ZX Spectrum-based synth provides the bubbling outro texture. The Phil Spector "wall of sound" was an explicit reference for the layered guitar approach ([Wikipedia: *OK Computer*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/OK_Computer)).

The polyrhythmic-arpeggio-as-bedrock idea is what "Weird Fishes/Arpeggi" would refine ten years later on *In Rainbows* — see [Radiohead *In Rainbows* Production](../artists/radiohead-in-rainbows-production.md).

## "Fitter Happier" — Macintosh Fred and the Ten-Minute Lyric

"Fitter Happier" is the album's musique concrète interlude — synthesized speech (the Macintosh "Fred" voice from the SimpleText application) reciting a list of bourgeois life targets, over distant piano and ambient noise. Yorke wrote the lyric in ten minutes during a stretch of writer's block. His own explanation: "I was feeling incredible hysteria and panic, and it was so liberating to give the lyrics to this neutral-sounding computer" ([Audio Media International](https://audiomediainternational.com/radiohead-ok-computer-producer/)).

Aesthetically this is the track that most explicitly anticipates *Kid A*'s "computer voice" textures and Yorke's later willingness to treat his own voice as one instrument among many.

## "Lucky" — Already in the Can

"Lucky" had actually been recorded a year earlier than the rest of the album, in five hours with Godrich, for the *Help* charity compilation (1995) — making it the oldest piece of *OK Computer* material and an early signal of what the Godrich-Radiohead pairing could produce when the studio clock was off ([Wikipedia: *OK Computer*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/OK_Computer)). The three-piece guitar arrangement grew from O'Brien's high-pitched chiming intro figure, achieved by strumming above the guitar nut ([Wikipedia: *OK Computer*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/OK_Computer)). Mellotron choir provides the dramatic bed.

## Phil Selway's Drum Sound at St. Catherine's

Selway's drum sound on *OK Computer* — wide-room, woody, recognizably *in* the ballroom rather than close-miked into sterility — is one of the album's defining sonic signatures. The combination of a stone-and-wood manor, a small SM57-heavy mic budget, and Godrich's preference for capturing the room ambience rather than gating it produced a kit sound that reads as both warm and slightly haunted. Selway's parts also bear the marks of an emerging hybrid sensibility: on "Airbag" his playing exists only as raw material for sampling and re-shuffling; on "Let Down" he holds a steady 4/4 against the guitars' 5/4; on "No Surprises" he plays as restrained a part as any major drummer of the era ever printed. The drummer-as-grid identity that would solidify on *Kid A* and *In Rainbows* is already audible here.

## Mixing — AIR, Mayfair, and the "Half a Day per Mix" Rule

Mixing took place at AIR Studios and Mayfair Studios in London, with mastering at Abbey Road ([Audio Media International](https://audiomediainternational.com/radiohead-ok-computer-producer/)). Godrich's documented mixing-velocity rule on this record — completing mixes in roughly half a day each to keep them fresh — is the earliest documented appearance of a principle he later codified across his career: "do something fast enough and then keep going so you can't get bored with the thing you've just done" (see his 1997 Mix interview cited in [Nigel Godrich Engineering Method](../artists/nigel-godrich-engineering-method.md)).

## The Bridge to *Kid A* — What *OK Computer* Already Contains

Several elements that read as "new" on *Kid A* are already present on *OK Computer* in embryonic form:

- **Sampled drum loops as primary rhythm tracks** ("Airbag") — anticipates *Kid A*'s programmed beats.
- **Musique concrète interludes inside an album sequence** ("Fitter Happier") — anticipates *Kid A*'s "Treefingers" and the spoken-word fragments on *Amnesiac*.
- **Tape-stitched multi-section compositions** ("Paranoid Android") — anticipates the through-composed Pro Tools editing on *Kid A*.
- **Mellotron and analog-synth textures replacing rhythm-guitar parts** ("Exit Music," "Lucky") — anticipates the keyboard-and-electronics palette of *Kid A*.
- **Detuned-cluster string writing** ("Climbing Up the Walls") — anticipates Greenwood's Orchestra-of-St-John's writing on *Kid A*'s "How to Disappear Completely."

What is **not** yet on the record, despite the brief's hypothesis, is the **ondes Martenot**. Jonny Greenwood did not acquire his ondes until 1999, during the Paris (Guillaume Tell) sessions for *Kid A* — see [Radiohead — *Kid A*](./radiohead-kid-a.md) and [The King of Gear — Greenwood and the ondes Martenot](https://thekingofgear.com/post/59984869688/jonny-greenwood-and-the-ondes-martenot). The first Radiohead record to feature ondes Martenot is *Kid A*, not *OK Computer*. ARLO should treat any "ondes Martenot on *OK Computer*" claim as false.

## Production Takeaways for an ARLO-Era Aesthetic

For an artist trying to make a "bridge record" — keeping live-band identity while opening the door to electronic/textural composition — *OK Computer* offers a set of replicable principles:

1. **Move the sessions out of the band's home town.** Canned Applause failed precisely because everyone slept in their own beds. St. Catherine's Court worked because nobody had anywhere else to be.
2. **Treat the room as the reverb.** Stone stairwell, ballroom, library control room. No outboard reverb on the lead vocal of "Exit Music"; the staircase did the work.
3. **Track live, edit aggressively.** Approximately 80% live, then fix sections by tape-splicing months-apart performances onto a single 24-track ("Paranoid Android").
4. **Let the band fail at a song, then rebuild half of it from loops.** "Karma Police" is the case study — first half kept, second half reassembled from samples and self-oscillating delay.
5. **Bring a string section in once, do something unrepeatable.** Sixteen violins playing quarter-tones apart at Abbey Road on "Climbing Up the Walls" was a single afternoon's work that defined the album's avant-garde wing.
6. **Use one early-recorded track as the album's anchor.** "Lucky" predated the album proper by two years and helped define what Godrich-with-Radiohead could sound like under low pressure.
7. **Trust the first vocal take.** Yorke's belief that the next take would be "lame" is consistent across both *OK Computer* and the Godrich-produced records that follow.
