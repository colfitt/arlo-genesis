# System of a Down — *Toxicity* (2001)

**Artist:** System of a Down
**Year:** 2001 (released 4 September 2001 on American Recordings / Columbia)
**Producers:** Rick Rubin and Daron Malakian, with Serj Tankian credited as co-producer ([Wikipedia, *Toxicity*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Toxicity_(album)))
**Engineering / Studio Anchor:** Dave Schiffman, tracking at Cello Studios (Studio 2), 6000 Sunset Boulevard, Hollywood — the Rubin-favoured room of the period ([Mix, *System Of A Down*](https://www.mixonline.com/recording/system-down-372602); [Recording Studio Rockstars, RSR388 — Dave Schiffman](https://recordingstudiorockstars.com/rsr388-dave-schiffman-recording-with-rick-rubin-red-hot-chili-peppers-system-of-a-down-and-strumbellas/))

## The Session Story In One Paragraph

*Toxicity* was tracked March–July 2001 at Cello Studios in Hollywood, with Rick Rubin producing alongside guitarist and primary composer Daron Malakian, Serj Tankian credited as co-producer, and Dave Schiffman engineering — the Schiffman/Rubin pairing that had just finished Red Hot Chili Peppers' *Californication* ([Mix, *System Of A Down*](https://www.mixonline.com/recording/system-down-372602); [Recording Studio Rockstars, RSR388 — Dave Schiffman](https://recordingstudiorockstars.com/rsr388-dave-schiffman-recording-with-rick-rubin-red-hot-chili-peppers-system-of-a-down-and-strumbellas/)). The band cut roughly thirty-two songs and voted fourteen onto the album using an anonymous A/B/C grade system, six voters total — four members plus management — with the leftovers later issued as *Steal This Album!* in 2002 ([Variety, *Toxicity* oral history](https://variety.com/2021/music/news/system-of-a-down-toxicity-album-oral-history-1235054344/)). Andy Wallace mixed at The Enterprise in Burbank on an SSL G+. Released 4 September 2001, the record debuted at number one with 220,000 first-week copies and was sitting on top of the chart on 11 September — a timing coincidence that drove Clear Channel to issue its "lyrically questionable" memo and pull "Chop Suey!" from playlists ([Wikipedia, *Toxicity*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Toxicity_(album))). It went on to six-times platinum in the United States in 2022, on roughly 2.7 million domestic and twelve million worldwide units.

## Rick Rubin In The Mid-Period

*Toxicity* is a mid-period Rubin production — sequenced between Slipknot's *Iowa* (2001) and the deepening Johnny Cash *American Recordings* run, inside the longer arc from Rage Against the Machine's debut to System's own *Mezmerize*/*Hypnotize* (2005). Participants describe Rubin's stance in non-technical terms. Malakian: "He's not a musician, so he listens to the song outside of the musician's shoes." Dolmayan: "I could not imagine anyone else ever producing System of a Down" ([Variety, *Toxicity* oral history](https://variety.com/2021/music/news/system-of-a-down-toxicity-album-oral-history-1235054344/)). Rubin's own framing emphasised focusing arrangements, getting best performances, and creating a comfortable creative environment. The stated sonic goal was "really dry, in your face and unapologetic" — Schiffman has repeated that brief to interviewers since. The audible result is an album with little ambient depth at the rhythm-section layer and an unusually upfront vocal, resisting the cathedral-reverb metal aesthetic of the period.

## Daron Malakian As Primary Composer

The corpus point easiest to miss reading liner notes: Daron Malakian writes most of System of a Down. By his and the band's account he composes roughly 90–95% of the material — melodies, chord progressions, and most lead vocal lines — and Tankian is principally a lyricist and singer on the songs Malakian brings ([Wikipedia, Daron Malakian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Daron_Malakian)). On *Toxicity* he framed the goal as evolution toward bigger, more open songs with melodic choruses ("'Aerials,' 'Toxicity,' and 'ATWA' brought an evolution to our sound") and is credited as co-producer, not as guitarist alone — reflecting his practical authority in the room. Shavo Odadjian wrote the core riff of the title track and brought it in as "Version 7.0," a useful reminder that even with Malakian as primary author the writing was not singular.

## Cello Studios, Hollywood — The Room

A factual housekeeping note: Cello Studios was *not* a former Sunset Sound Factory. The Sound Factory was a separate Selma Avenue room acquired by Sunset Sound in 1981. Cello was the western half of the historic United Western Recorders complex at 6000 Sunset Boulevard, which Rick Adams purchased and rebranded in 1999. From 1999 to 2005 it hosted Blink-182, Rage Against the Machine, Tool, System of a Down, Weezer, Muse, Red Hot Chili Peppers, Audioslave, and the Mars Volta; Studio 2 became Rubin's preferred room. Doug Rogers bought the facility in 2006 and reopened it as EastWest Studios ([EastWest Studios history](https://www.eastweststudios.com/history/)). The band chose Cello in part because The Cramps' *A Date with Elvis* had been recorded there; Tankian described it as "an incredible sounding room."

## Tracking Chain — Drums, Bass, Guitars, Vocals

Dolmayan's kit (five toms plus RotoToms) was miked for transient detail: Sennheiser 421 and FET 47 on kick; Sennheiser 441 (bottom) plus SM57 and AKG C-60 (top) on snare; 421s on toms; AKG C-12 overheads; SM57s on ride ([Mix, *System Of A Down*](https://www.mixonline.com/recording/system-down-372602)). Odadjian's 1962 Gibson Firebird went to three cabinets on separate tracks — a vintage Ampeg head into an 8×10 (FET 47 and RE20, through LA-2As) for the main tone, a Trace Elliot into a 4×10 for midrange gnarl, and a Distressor-compressed DI for definition. Schiffman has described spending three days getting sounds and going through about fourteen basses, seven or eight heads, and five or six cabinets before settling — the dry-and-unapologetic brief is partly an artifact of how much pre-tracking sound-design happened. Malakian ran Marshall plus Mesa Boogie (Mesa for "teeth," Marshall for "body"), each cabinet miked with SM57 and SM87 through a pair of black-face 1176s; multiple guitar passes per part, on some sections up to twelve layers, with Malakian recording takes up to fifty times to find the right one. Tankian's vocals were captured to two principal mics by intensity — Shure SM7 for aggressive deliveries, AKG C-12 for operatic and delicate passages — a switching strategy that helps explain the breadth of timbre across "Chop Suey!," "Toxicity," "Aerials," and "Forest" without obvious post-EQ. A Kaoss Pad handled background-vocal texture. Primary tape was an Ampex ATR-24 with a Studer A800 brought in for overdubs and fast punching at high tempos; vocals and percussion tracked into Pro Tools and summed back into analog. Console was a Neve 8038.

## Serj Tankian's Vocal Approach

Tankian's vocal vocabulary on *Toxicity* is the public face of the record — across the album he moves between low spoken declamation, an operatic upper register, Armenian-folk-derived melodic ornament, scatting drawn from his work with Arto Tunçboyacıyan, and a hoarse growl. Rubin's framing is that Tankian "grew tremendously as a vocalist" during the 1998 debut's touring cycle and arrived willing to "try things nobody was doing" ([The Ringer, *Toxicity* oral history](https://www.theringer.com/2021/09/02/music/system-of-a-down-oral-history-toxicity)). The corpus point: the range is genre-bending, but the *placement* is genre-defying. Rubin and Schiffman keep the lead vocal dry and forward, sitting above an aggressive guitar bed rather than washed into it — closer to a Johnny Cash *American* recordings vocal placement than to a 2001 nu-metal one. That decision is what lets Tankian's most extreme moves read as character rather than as effect.

## "Chop Suey!" — The Lead Single

"Chop Suey!" was released 13 August 2001 and is the song most associated with the record. The original working title was "Suicide"; the label discouraged it for commercial-radio reasons and the band self-edited — Malakian has said the rename references 1940s mobster-movie diction and the idea of "chopping suicide in half." Tankian got stuck on the bridge lyric; the often-told story is that Rubin had him pull a book off a shelf at random and use what he found as a writing prompt, producing the church-allusion bridge ([Far Out, Rubin on "Chop Suey!"](https://faroutmagazine.co.uk/rick-rubin-system-of-a-down-chop-suey/)). Six days after release the album was at number one; a week later it was being pulled from Clear Channel rotation. The track's structure is the album's compositional thesis in miniature — thrash verse welded to sung melodic chorus welded to quiet harmonised bridge.

## "Toxicity," "Aerials," "Forest," "Psycho"

The title track originated as a Shavo Odadjian riff ("Version 7.0") and is the cleanest example of the album's verse-to-chorus dynamic budgeting — a near-spoken Tankian verse over a held bass pulse, opening to a double-tracked chorus with Malakian's harmony underneath. "Aerials" is the structural inverse of "Chop Suey!" — a mid-tempo near-ballad with Tankian's voice held operatic across the chorus. Rubin's documented arrangement note was to "play the main riff heavily" in the middle section; Malakian played sitar (his first time on the instrument) and Arto Tunçboyacıyan contributed body-percussion and the scat tag at the end ([Variety, *Toxicity* oral history](https://variety.com/2021/music/news/system-of-a-down-toxicity-album-oral-history-1235054344/)). "Aerials" hit number one on Mainstream Rock as the third single (June 2002) and earned a Grammy nomination. "Forest" is Malakian's compositional showcase — he wrote the music; lyrically the song reads as either ecological (Tankian has described the band's politics as in part "radical ecology") or religious-allegorical depending on the listener ([Songfacts, "Forest"](https://www.songfacts.com/facts/system-of-a-down/forest)) — and the clearest demonstration of how layered-guitar logic carries song shape, an arpeggiated clean figure stacked into a chorale-thick bed. The whizzing texture in "Psycho"'s introduction was produced by placing a vibrator next to the guitar pickup and varying its speed, a moving electromagnetic-field artefact at the pickup coils — a small detail that is a useful microcosm of the album's source-not-post production stance.

## The Voting System And Track Selection

The band tracked roughly thirty-two songs and used a six-voter anonymous A/B/C grading process (four members plus management) to pick the sequence; everything graded A made it on, with select B-graded tracks added to fill the runtime. Dolmayan fought unsuccessfully to include "Innervision," which ended up on *Steal This Album!* the following year. The structural corpus point: *Toxicity*'s tightness — fourteen songs in 44:07, very little filler — is partly a selection-process artefact, not just a writing-quality one. Curated-from-surplus is a recurring pattern in airtight records (see also Daughters' *You Won't Get What You Want*, where roughly a hundred Dropbox sketches were filtered to ten tracks).

Andy Wallace mixed at The Enterprise in Burbank on an SSL G+, which is the standard sonic explanation for why *Toxicity* sounds like a 2001 major-label rock record despite Rubin's "dry and unapologetic" tracking brief — Wallace's mix discipline gives the album commercial readability and chart-translatable punch while keeping the lead vocal forward. Eddy Schreyer mastered at Oasis Mastering. The result translates exceptionally well on small speakers: bass and kick tightly coupled, snare dry and present, vocal well above the bed at conversation volume.

## Armenian-American Politics And The 9/11 Coincidence

The cultural-political content of *Toxicity* is not subtext — it is most of the lyric. All four members are of Armenian descent and grandchildren of Armenian Genocide survivors; the album foregrounds U.S. prison policy ("Prison Song"), foreign policy and CIA covert action, addiction politics, ecological collapse, and police violence, with Genocide-recognition advocacy as the off-record framing the band has carried since ([Time, Tankian on the Armenian Genocide](https://time.com/3834792/serj-tankian-armenian-genocide/); [SyriacPress, Tankian on Armenian Genocide recognition](https://syriacpress.com/blog/2020/03/14/system-of-a-downs-serj-tankian-fights-for-recognition-of-the-armenian-assyrian-and-greek-genocide-of-1915/)). Released 4 September 2001, the album debuted at number one and was sitting on top of the chart on 11 September. Clear Channel — then the dominant U.S. terrestrial radio operator — circulated an internal advisory of "lyrically questionable" songs; "Chop Suey!" was on the list. Simultaneously Tankian posted an essay titled "Understanding Oil" criticising U.S. Middle East policy; Sony pulled it quickly under pressure and Tankian appeared on Howard Stern to defend it. The radio ban did not damage the record commercially — singles kept charting, "Aerials" hit number one Mainstream Rock the following year — a clean case study in how a major-label rock record collided with a national-trauma news cycle and survived because the songwriting was strong enough to weather airplay loss. *Toxicity* sits inside the same LA alt-metal moment as Rage Against the Machine — politicised, ethnically marked, Rubin-adjacent (Rubin produced RATM's *Renegades* in 2000), recording at the same Hollywood facility (Cello). In 2001 it was possible for a politicised alt-metal album to be a six-times-platinum chart-topper; that is genuinely period-specific.

## The Greg Fidelman Question

A correction to the brief: Greg Fidelman is *not* the engineer of record on *Toxicity*. Fidelman engineered System of a Down's self-titled 1998 debut and went on to a long Rubin-adjacent run with Slipknot, Metallica, Slayer, and Adele — he is correctly described as "Rubin's right hand" in the broader sense, but on *Toxicity* the chief engineer is Dave Schiffman, who came up the same way (Rubin's engineer through *Californication* and *Toxicity*) and is the load-bearing figure for this specific record. Credits list Greg Collins, Darren Mora, Al Sanderson, Ryan McCormick, and Jim Champagne in additional engineer / assistant roles ([Wikipedia, *Toxicity*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Toxicity_(album)); [Wikipedia, Greg Fidelman](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Greg_Fidelman)). Worth noting because conflating Fidelman and Schiffman is a common error in writing about Rubin's mid-period engineering team.

## What This Record Teaches The Corpus

- **Curated-from-surplus produces airtight records.** Thirty-two songs into fourteen via an anonymous voting system is a selection discipline that explains the album's lack of filler; the *Steal This Album!* sessions material is the leftover, not a separate writing cycle.
- **Dry-and-forward vocal placement can hold extreme vocal range.** Tankian's operatic-to-growl-to-scat span works because Rubin and Schiffman do not bury him in reverb. Vocal dominance is again a placement decision, not a volume one.
- **Layered guitar can be the harmonic floor.** Malakian's stacked-takes approach lets the rhythm guitar carry chord-and-counter-melody together; the bass is freed to sit on a single pulse for long stretches as a result.
- **Political content can be a commercial asset when the songwriting carries it.** *Toxicity* is the case study against the "politics dampens chart performance" assumption for major-label rock — six-times platinum on lyric content that names CIA action and Armenian Genocide history.
- **A producer's role is mostly arrangement and confidence.** Rubin's documented interventions on *Toxicity* are small in technical scope and large in compositional effect — the "play the main riff heavily" note on "Aerials," the random-book lyric prompt on "Chop Suey!". Useful corpus reminder against gear-fetishised producer models.
