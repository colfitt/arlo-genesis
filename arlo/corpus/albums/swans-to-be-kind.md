## The Session Story In One Paragraph

*To Be Kind* is Swans' third record after Michael Gira reformed the band in 2010, and the second of a loose trilogy with *The Seer* (2012) and *The Glowing Man* (2016) made by the same reunion-era six-piece. Gira took the band to Sonic Ranch, a residential studio complex on a 1,700-acre pecan orchard in Tornillo, Texas, where rehearsals began in early October 2013 and tracking followed almost immediately; mixing finished in Dallas in mid-December 2013 ([Young God Records, *To Be Kind*](https://younggodrecords.com/products/to-be-kind)). John Congleton — by then best known for his production work with St. Vincent, John Grant, Erykah Badu, and Angel Olsen — engineered the record start to finish. Gira holds the sole producer credit. Much of the material had been road-tested on the 2012–13 *Seer*-cycle tours, so the band came in able to track long pieces as live takes, which Gira then "butchered" with hundreds of overdubs at Congleton's Elmwood Studios in Dallas ([Chaos Control, Gira interview on *To Be Kind*](https://chaoscontrol.com/michael-gira-of-swans-2014/)). The project was funded in advance by Swans fans via a handmade limited 2xCD live album, *Not Here / Not Now*, sold through Young God — a direct-to-fan model Gira had been refining since the band's 2010 return. Released in May 2014, the record peaked at #37 on the Billboard 200 and #38 in the UK — the highest commercial position any Swans studio album had achieved — and earned a Metacritic of 88, including a 9.2 from Pitchfork ([Wikipedia, *To Be Kind*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/To_Be_Kind)).

## The Reunion-Era Lineup As Composing Body

The 2010 reformation of Swans was not a reunion in the classic sense — Gira deliberately reframed it as a continuation of the project under that name with a substantially new band, distinct from the 1982–1997 personnel. By *To Be Kind* the lineup had stabilised: Norman Westberg (the only other player with deep 1980s/90s Swans history) on electric and acoustic guitar; Christoph Hahn on lap-steel guitars; Phil Puleo (ex–Cop Shoot Cop) and Thor Harris (long-time Gira collaborator from Angels of Light) splitting drums and broader percussion duties; Christopher Pravdica on bass; Gira fronting and playing guitar. Bill Rieflin — a Ministry/REM/King Crimson veteran — is credited as "Honorary Swan Forever" and plays additional drums, piano, bass, guitar, synths and keys ([Swans Bandcamp](https://swans.bandcamp.com/album/to-be-kind)). The arrangement consequence is structural: with two full drummers in the band and Thor Harris's tuned-bell, vibraphone and wind-instrument kit on top, the rhythm section can function as a small percussion orchestra rather than as a rock backline. Many of the climax sections on *To Be Kind* — the closing minutes of "Bring the Sun / Toussaint L'Ouverture," the long crests of "She Loves Us" and "Kirsten Supine" — derive their density from layered percussion rather than from guitar saturation. The two-drummer ensemble is also why the live recordings translated to tape with so little additional production: the rhythm bed was already over-determined before any overdubs arrived.

## John Congleton's Role — Engineer, Not Producer

The credit precision matters for this file. Gira is the sole producer of record; Congleton's credits are recording engineer, mixing engineer, and additional piano ([Wikipedia, *To Be Kind*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/To_Be_Kind); [Young God Records, *To Be Kind*](https://younggodrecords.com/products/to-be-kind)). This is a meaningful framing inside Congleton's broader career. By 2013 he had production credits on St. Vincent's self-titled record, Erykah Badu's *New Amerykah Part Two*, John Grant's *Pale Green Ghosts*, and a wide spread of art-pop and indie-rock projects. On Swans he is functioning in the more invisible role — capturing a band Gira is shaping, mixing what Gira has decided. Gira has been blunt in interviews about treating the studio as an instrument and the engineer's job as collaborating with that intent rather than imposing a producer's editorial frame ([Chaos Control, Gira interview](https://chaoscontrol.com/michael-gira-of-swans-2014/)). The St. Vincent connection — Annie Clark's vocal appearances on four of the record's ten tracks — runs through Congleton, who had been her producer on three consecutive records by the time *To Be Kind* was tracked. The pipeline is: Congleton brings the room (Sonic Ranch, then his Elmwood Studios in Dallas), the engineering hands, and the access to vocal guests; Gira brings the band, the songs, the production vision, and the post-tracking overdub layering.

## Sonic Ranch As Setting

Sonic Ranch was founded in 1989 by Tony Rancich on inherited family land — a 1,700-acre pecan orchard in Tornillo, Texas, bordering the Rio Grande and the Mexican frontier, about 40 miles southeast of El Paso ([Wikipedia, *Sonic Ranch*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sonic_Ranch); [Sonic Ranch official site](https://www.sonicranch.com/)). The site grew into six recording rooms scattered across the compound with residential lodging built into and around a 1930s twelve-room Spanish-hacienda main house; meals are cooked on site, laundry handled, and artists are expected to stay through the duration of the session. By the early 2010s Sonic Ranch had become a destination room for projects that wanted long uninterrupted residencies — At The Drive-In's *Relationship of Command*, several Mars Volta records, Yeasayer, Conor Oberst, and many Latin-music projects had tracked there. For Swans the consequence is operational: a band of six players plus guests and engineer can move in together, rehearse and track over a contiguous block of weeks without the daily fragmentation of a city studio. Gira's preference for long-form takes and band-as-organism tracking depends on having the whole group present and warm; Sonic Ranch's residential architecture is the precondition for that workflow. The vintage console and microphone collection (two Neve consoles, a Studer A827 tape machine, Neumann U47s) is the secondary draw; the primary draw is the residency model.

## Live Ensemble Tracking And The Overdub Layer

Gira has described the *To Be Kind* tracking method directly: basic tracks were cut live as a band, and then "in the case of these songs there are hundreds of overdubs," with the studio treated as "a tool, an instrument in and of itself" ([Chaos Control, Gira interview](https://chaoscontrol.com/michael-gira-of-swans-2014/)). The first half of that sentence is the discipline; the second half is the maximalism. The live takes preserve the breathing, drift, and dynamic conversation of the touring band — much of the material had already lived for a year on the road during the *Seer* tours. The overdub layer then thickens the takes with additional percussion, woodwinds, brass, strings, guest vocals, and the harmonic density that defines the record's saturated mid-range. This is a different production logic from the more familiar "tracked-in-isolation, comp-the-best-bits" indie-rock workflow Congleton was running on his St. Vincent and Angel Olsen records at the same time. The base layer is committed to performance; the production happens above it rather than instead of it. The corpus lesson is that maximalism and live-ensemble tracking are not mutually exclusive — a 30-minute piece can be both a real band performance and a heavily overdubbed studio construction, with the overdubs reading as scaffolding on top of an already-load-bearing performance rather than as the structural source of the piece.

## "Bring The Sun / Toussaint L'Ouverture" — The 34-Minute Centrepiece

Track four is the album's centrepiece and its longest piece: a 34:05 two-part composition that begins as an extended modal drone and chant ("Bring the Sun") and resolves into the second half ("Toussaint L'Ouverture"), an invocation of the Haitian revolutionary leader who led the slave revolt that founded Haiti in the 1790s. Gira has framed Haiti as "a fulcrum for everything horrible and good" — the site of both colonial extraction and the first successful slave revolution in modern history — and described the second half of the piece as arising from his reading a biography of L'Ouverture during the writing of the record ([Songtell paraphrase of Gira interview](https://www.songtell.com/swans/bring-the-sun-toussaint-l-ouverture)). The piece functions as the album's structural pivot — it sits at the end of disc one, exhausts the band over more than half an hour, and then disc two opens with the comparatively song-shaped "Some Things We Do" and the seventeen-minute "She Loves Us." The composition is also the clearest demonstration of the reunion-era Swans's ability to sustain a single harmonic and rhythmic field for tens of minutes without losing dynamic shape — climax-and-collapse architecture stretched to the length of a symphonic movement.

## "Just A Little Boy (For Chester Burnett)" — Howlin' Wolf As Subject

The second track, 12:39 long, is dedicated to Chester Burnett — Howlin' Wolf — and reads as Gira's most overt blues-form invocation on the record. The arrangement leans on Pravdica's bass figure and the two-drum kit, with Hahn's lap steel playing the harmonic-decoration role a slide guitar would in a Wolf or Hubert Sumlin track. The choice to title-dedicate to Burnett is in line with Gira's long-running framing of Swans as an inheritor of the American gothic-blues tradition more than of the no-wave or industrial scenes the band is usually filed under. The corpus relevance is the songwriting move: a 12-minute piece can carry a blues-form harmonic centre while operating at sustained ensemble volume — the form is preserved in the bass figure and the call-and-response logic, even when the surface sounds nothing like a delta or Chicago blues record.

## "Some Things We Do" — Strings As Counterweight

"Some Things We Do" (5:09) is the album's shortest piece and its quietest. Julia Kent's string arrangement is the dominant instrumental colour, with a vocal duet between Gira and Little Annie (Annie Anxiety Bandez, a long-time Gira collaborator) carrying the song. Its position on the running order — directly after the 34-minute "Bring the Sun / Toussaint L'Ouverture" — is functional. The sequencing logic across both discs alternates extended high-density pieces with comparatively contained ones, so the listener gets reset points between climaxes. "Some Things We Do" is the most pronounced reset on the record. The corpus lesson is about dynamic budgeting at album scale: on a 121-minute record, a single shorter piece can carry the listener's recovery and rehearmonisation between two longer pieces without breaking the album's voice.

## Annie Clark / St. Vincent As Vocal Sideman

Clark contributed background vocals to four tracks — "Screen Shot," "Bring the Sun," "Kirsten Supine," and "Nathalie Neal" ([Wikipedia, *To Be Kind*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/To_Be_Kind)). She is functioning in the sideman role here, layered into background harmony stacks rather than featured as a lead voice. The pipeline through Congleton is direct: he had just produced her *Strange Mercy* and the self-titled *St. Vincent* record around the same period. The corpus lesson is about how a player-as-featured-voice economy operates around an engineer-producer node — Congleton's roster of artists overlapped session-by-session in this period (Clark, John Grant, Angel Olsen, Swans), and Annie Clark's presence on *To Be Kind* is one of the more visible artefacts of that network. Cold Specks (Ladan Hussein) added background vocals on "Bring the Sun" via the same network ([Wikipedia, *To Be Kind*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/To_Be_Kind)).

## The Fan-Funded Production Model

The Young God liner notes describe the funding directly: "The recordings and entire process of this album were generously and perhaps vaingloriously funded by Swans supporters through our auspices at younggodrecords.com via the release of a special, handmade 2xCD live album entitled Not Here / Not Now" ([Swans Bandcamp](https://swans.bandcamp.com/album/to-be-kind); [Young God Records, *To Be Kind*](https://younggodrecords.com/products/to-be-kind)). The mechanism is worth naming because it is structurally upstream of the record's sound. *Not Here / Not Now* was a hand-assembled limited live document — Gira signed and numbered copies in his own apartment for weeks — sold direct to fans at a high unit price. The proceeds covered the cost of the *To Be Kind* sessions, including the residential stay at Sonic Ranch, Congleton's engineering time, and the extensive overdub period at Elmwood. The corpus point is that the album's scale (six-piece band, residential studio, hundreds of overdubs, six-week tracking-and-mixing block) is downstream of a funding model that does not require advance recoupment from a label. Mute and Young God released the record but did not finance it. That financial independence is the precondition for the artistic choices — a label-financed project would have been under pressure to constrain runtime, eliminate the 34-minute centrepiece, or compress the residential session into something smaller.

## The Shift From *The Seer* (2012)

*The Seer* (2012) was Swans's first reunion-era statement at this scale — a 119-minute double album with its own 32-minute title track, recorded at Marcata Recording in New York with engineer Kevin McMahon. *To Be Kind* is the immediate successor and is arguably the more song-shaped, riff-anchored record of the two. *The Seer* leaned more on extended drone, sustained harmonium and string passages, and longer formless build sections; *To Be Kind* tracks more like a band record, with discrete riffs and pulse signatures driving most of the long-form pieces. The relocation from Marcata to Sonic Ranch and Elmwood Studios is part of that shift — the residential setting at Tornillo allowed a more sustained live-ensemble tracking block than the Marcata setup had supported, and Congleton's mixing aesthetic tends toward clarity-of-source even in dense arrangements. The corpus lesson is about how the same artist can run two adjacent 120-minute records with different load-bearing strategies: drone-anchored on *The Seer*, ensemble-pulse-anchored on *To Be Kind*.

## Reception And Chart Position

*To Be Kind* peaked at #37 on the Billboard 200 and #38 on the UK Albums Chart — the first time any Swans studio record cracked the top 40 in either market ([Wikipedia, *To Be Kind*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/To_Be_Kind)). It additionally hit #5 US Independent Albums, #7 US Top Alternative Albums, and #10 US Top Rock Albums, and charted in Austria, Belgium (Flanders), Germany, and the Netherlands. The Metacritic score is 88 across 35 reviews; Pitchfork awarded 9.2, NME 9, Spin 9, The Needle Drop (Anthony Fantano) 10, AllMusic 4/5, A.V. Club A−. The lone notable dissent came from The Guardian, which described the album as "uncompromising to the point of overindulgence." Year-end placement was broad across publications. The deluxe edition included a live DVD of the band's 2013 Primavera Sound performance in Barcelona. The corpus point is about how scale and accessibility relate inversely on this record's reception arc — the longest, densest, least-formatted Swans studio album to date was also the one that achieved the highest commercial chart position, because by 2014 the audience for that scale of work had grown alongside the band's reformation cycle.

## What This Record Teaches The Corpus

- **A residential studio enables live-ensemble tracking at length.** The choice of Sonic Ranch is not décor — it is the operational precondition for tracking 17-to-34-minute pieces as live ensemble takes. A six-piece band with two drummers and guest players cannot run that workflow out of a city studio billed by the day.
- **"Producer" and "engineer" credits are not interchangeable.** Gira produced; Congleton engineered and mixed. The distinction matters for how a notable engineer-producer like Congleton appears across the *To Be Kind*–era catalogue in two different roles depending on the project.
- **Live-ensemble base layer + hundreds of overdubs is a viable maximalist workflow.** The two halves coexist. The band performance is captured first; the studio-as-instrument layer is added second. The overdubs do not replace the performance — they thicken it.
- **A two-drummer rhythm section can carry climax architecture by itself.** Many of the album's loudest passages derive their density from layered percussion (kit drums + Thor Harris's tuned-bell and wind kit) rather than from guitar walls.
- **Fan-funded direct-to-supporter releases can finance label-scale records.** Selling a limited handmade live album direct through Young God paid for the *To Be Kind* sessions outright. The financial independence enabled the runtime and the studio choice.
- **Historical-figure subject matter can be a structural anchor for an extended composition.** Toussaint L'Ouverture is not décor — the second half of "Bring the Sun" uses the historical reference as a way to give a 34-minute piece narrative direction without a verse/chorus form.
