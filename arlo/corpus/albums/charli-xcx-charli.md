# Charli XCX — *Charli* (2019)

**Artist:** Charli XCX (Charlotte Aitchison)
**Year:** 2019 (released 13 September 2019 on Asylum / Atlantic Records UK)
**Producers:** A. G. Cook (executive producer and dominant track-level producer), with Lotus IV, Nömak, Dylan Brady, umru, Stargate, Andrew Watt, Oscar Holter, Patrik Berger, Baseck and others — verify per-track credits via Wikipedia and liner notes ([Wikipedia, *Charli* (album)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Charli_(album)); [Stereogum tracklist announcement](https://www.stereogum.com/2047594/charli-xcx-new-album-tracklist-blame-it-on-your-love-video/news/))

## The Session Story In One Paragraph

*Charli* is the album where Charli XCX's experimental-pop instincts and her major-label contract reach an uneasy truce, with A. G. Cook positioned as both executive producer and tactical buffer between the artist and Atlantic Records. The record was originally meant to continue the mixtape numbering of *Number 1 Angel* (2017) and *Pop 2* (2017) but was rebooted as a full studio album after a long development-hell period that included a shelved earlier draft titled *XCX World* ([Pop 2 mixtape — Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pop_2_(mixtape))). Cook joined as creative director in mid-2016 — by Charli's own framing, "really to act as a buffer between Charli and Atlantic Records," who were pushing a more conservative marketing posture ([L'Officiel USA, A.G. Cook interview](https://www.lofficielusa.com/music/ag-cook-pc-music-exclusive-interview-hyperpop-charli-xcx)). Sessions ran 2017–2019 across Los Angeles, London, Stockholm, Berlin and New York with a rotating producer pool. The feature list is conspicuously the longest of Charli's career to that point — 13 guest credits across 15 tracks — and the artifact reads as a deliberate proof-of-concept that PC Music's bubblegum-bass / hyperpop vocabulary could carry an Atlantic-budget commercial release.

## The Atlantic Records Tension — Production Context

*Charli* cannot be understood without the label fight that produced it. Charli has talked openly about her "turbulent" relationship with Atlantic from the *Vroom Vroom* EP (2016) onward — half-hearted promotion, repeated delays, a cancelled *XCX World* album, and label executives pushing for more "radio-friendly" material ([NME, Charli on Atlantic relationship](https://www.nme.com/news/music/charli-xcx-says-her-relationship-with-label-atlantic-used-to-be-turbulent-but-is-in-a-good-place-now-3900446); [Varsity, *The rise and CRASH of Charli XCX*](https://www.varsity.co.uk/music/23550)). Cook's strategic move as executive producer was to "incentivize her to double down on it and only release music she actually liked" ([L'Officiel USA](https://www.lofficielusa.com/music/ag-cook-pc-music-exclusive-interview-hyperpop-charli-xcx)). The most direct artifact of the tension lives in "Gone": the chorus contains a conspicuous absent rhyme, and Charli later confirmed the original line — replaced for release — was an explicit complaint about her label ([Varsity, *The rise and CRASH of Charli XCX*](https://www.varsity.co.uk/music/23550)). For the corpus, the lesson is structural rather than gossipy: the production aesthetic of *Charli* is the negotiated compromise between a PC Music creative core and an Atlantic Records commercial demand, and the seams of that negotiation are audible in the track-list shape (mainstream features framing experimental cuts) rather than in the timbres.

## A. G. Cook As Executive Producer — Continuity From *Pop 2*

A. G. Cook had executive-produced *Pop 2* (December 2017), which Pitchfork's Meaghan Garvey called "the best full-length work of both Charli and PC Music's respective careers" ([Pop 2 mixtape — Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pop_2_(mixtape))). *Charli* extends that working relationship into the major-label studio context. Cook is credited on the majority of tracks and named the album itself ([Wikipedia, *Charli* (album)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Charli_(album))). His production signature across the record is the same one he would later codify on *Brat*: maximal-but-disciplined synth choices, aggressive Auto-Tune as a foreground texture, blown-out kick patterns sitting alongside chiptune-bright lead lines, and a willingness to let songs end abruptly or jump-cut between sections rather than smoothing transitions. The cross-link for ARLO's purposes is `corpus/artists/charli-xcx-brat-production.md` — almost every production-philosophy claim made about *Brat* has its earlier draft in *Charli*'s production decisions, just spread across a larger producer pool and softened by Atlantic-friendly hook structures.

## The Hyperpop Lineage — Cook, SOPHIE, And The PC Music Vocabulary

PC Music, the record label and art collective Cook founded in 2013, is the institutional origin of what the press would later call hyperpop ([Wikipedia, A. G. Cook](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/A._G._Cook); [Hyperpop — Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hyperpop)). The vocabulary — extreme Auto-Tune, pitched-up vocals, blown-out maximal synths, exaggerated bubblegum melodies built on industrial-bright timbres — was developed in parallel with SOPHIE, whose own pioneering production work is widely cited as the other founding pole of the style ([Garfield Messenger, *SOPHIE & the Legacy of Hyperpop*](https://www.garfieldmessenger.org/6576/articles/ae/sophie-the-legacy-of-hyperpop/); [Rolling Stone, *PC Music Are for Real*](https://www.rollingstone.com/music/music-news/pc-music-are-for-real-a-g-cook-and-sophie-talk-twisted-pop-58119/)). Cook's own framing of the difference: "Sophie was trying to make stuff very synthetically like in a lab, while [I was] seeing how much personality he could incorporate" — synthetic precision versus personality-first synthesis. *Charli* is the album where that vocabulary stops being underground-coded and gets pushed through a mainstream label's release apparatus. The corpus-level cross-reference is `corpus/techniques/hyperpop-sound-design.md`; *Charli* is the canonical commercial-deployment case study for that technique file.

## Auto-Tune As Foreground Instrument

Auto-Tune across *Charli* is not pitch-correction — it is a featured timbre. Cook's working approach is to use the plugin to transform vocal performances into "integral elements of the overall sound design," crafting vocal textures that "teeter between raw emotion and polished surrealism" ([L'Officiel USA](https://www.lofficielusa.com/music/ag-cook-pc-music-exclusive-interview-hyperpop-charli-xcx); [Internet Tattoo, *Plugins Behind A.G. Cook's Hyperpop Sound*](https://www.internettattoo.com/blog/ag-cook-hyperpop-plugin-svst)). The most explicit demonstration is "Click," where Vulture described the heavy Auto-Tune treatment as a "mindfuck" ([Wikipedia, *Click*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Click_(Charli_XCX_song))). Charli's leads, the feature vocals, and even ad-libs are routinely processed with the retune speed pushed to the maximum-snap setting that produces the characteristic robotic-glide signature. The corpus cross-link is `corpus/techniques/auto-tune-as-creative-tool.md`; *Charli* is one of the highest-profile albums to use the plugin as a *foreground identity choice* rather than a corrective utility.

## "Gone" — The Album's Production Centrepiece

"Gone" with Christine and the Queens is the most-discussed production on the record and the clearest synthesis of the album's ambitions. The song was tracked at Lotus Library in Stockholm in early 2019; Charli completed an initial version, wasn't satisfied, recorded ten more tracks before returning to it, and then sent the file to Héloïse Letissier (Christine and the Queens), who delivered her chorus suggestion within roughly fifteen minutes ([Wikipedia, *Gone*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gone_(Charli_XCX_and_Christine_and_the_Queens_song))). Production credits go to A. G. Cook, Lotus IV, and Nömak, with additional production by Baseck; writing credits add Noonie Bao to the producer pool ([Hipstrumentals, *Gone* instrumental credits](https://hipstrumentals.com/2019/08/charli-xcx-christine-and-the-queens-gone-instrumental-prod-by-no%CC%88mak-lotus-iv-a-g-cook/)). The track was completed across three studios: Lotus Library (Stockholm), Lotus Lounge (Los Angeles), and Vincent Ave (Los Angeles). Sonically it blends progressive pop, electropop, industrial percussion, and what reviewers called "kinetic, bilingual funk-pop" — '80s drum textures meeting '90s synth choices with the bassy stutters and crackles of Cook's signature filter-distortion vocabulary ([Wikipedia, *Gone*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gone_(Charli_XCX_and_Christine_and_the_Queens_song))). For ARLO's purposes, "Gone" is the most useful single-track reference on *Charli* because the production foregrounds the album's defining gesture — distorted, brittle synth-bass under a hugely emotional dual lead vocal that is itself Auto-Tune-processed but used for expressive lift rather than pure novelty.

## "1999" — The Outlier Production

"1999" with Troye Sivan is the album's most commercially streamlined cut and the production outlier in the track list. It was written at Max Martin's Los Angeles compound with Swedish writer-producer Oscar Holter and Swedish singer-songwriter Noonie Bao; Holter produced, with additional writing from Brett "Leland" McLaughlin ([Wikipedia, *1999*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1999_(Charli_XCX_and_Troye_Sivan_song))). The hook-of-record — "I just wanna go back, sing 'Hit me baby one more time'" — was Max Martin's own suggestion, a self-referencing edit to a chorus line he himself had produced on Britney Spears in 1999. The track sits stylistically closer to MGK-era major-label pop than to Cook's PC Music aesthetic, and its position as the album's lead single (October 2018, eleven months before the album release) is itself a tell about the label-versus-artist tug. Production-wise, *Charli* is best understood as a record that contains "1999" as the Atlantic-coded gravity well and surrounds it with PC Music-coded production choices that pull the album back toward the experimental center.

## "Click" — Hyperpop In Commercial Frame

"Click" with Kim Petras and Tommy Cash is the album's hyperpop manifesto in commercial-track form. It was produced by Dylan Brady, umru, and A. G. Cook ([Wikipedia, *Click*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Click_(Charli_XCX_song))). Brady is half of the duo 100 gecs, whose debut *1000 gecs* dropped four months earlier in May 2019 and is the other 2019 album-of-record for the hyperpop conversation; his presence on "Click" is the explicit handshake between PC Music's older lineage and the next-wave 100 gecs camp. umru is a New York producer adjacent to the PC Music scene; the song began tracking at his apartment in early 2019, with Charli writing nights with him before moving the file to Los Angeles and sending stems out to Petras and Cash for verse contributions ([Wikipedia, *Click*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Click_(Charli_XCX_song))). The production stacks blown-out kicks, glitched and pitched vocal chops, and a final-section detonation that smashes through the song's earlier section — the kind of structural rupture that would become a *Brat* signature five years later.

## "Shake It" — Posse Cut In PC Music Drag

"Shake It" features Big Freedia, CupcakKe, Brooke Candy and Pabllo Vittar, and is the album's posse cut — four queer/rap collaborators across one chaotic bounce-coded production. The track is the clearest example of *Charli*'s sequencing philosophy of pairing a long, dense feature-list against a single high-intensity production. Big Freedia carries the New Orleans bounce vocabulary; CupcakKe and Brooke Candy operate in different but adjacent dirty-rap registers; Pabllo Vittar pulls the production toward Latin-pop and Brazilian drag-pop. Sonically the production is built around blown-out 808s, looped vocal stabs, and the kind of all-caps maximalism that PC Music had been threading through the underground for years. The track functions structurally as the album's release-valve party song — placed at position 13 of 15, after the more emotional middle stretch.

## "Warm," "Cross You Out," "Blame It On Your Love" — The Atlantic-Friendly Middle

"Warm" with HAIM, "Cross You Out" with Sky Ferreira, and "Blame It On Your Love" with Lizzo form a triangulated bid for adult-contemporary pop radio in the middle of the record. "Blame It On Your Love" was a release-version reworking of "Track 10," a *Pop 2* track that had been a fan favourite in its experimental form. The "Blame It On Your Love" version smooths the original's harsh edges into something label-trackable while keeping the song's hook intact — the trade-off being precisely what Charli has talked about as the Atlantic compromise. "Warm" with HAIM and "Cross You Out" with Sky Ferreira are tonally and sonically the closest the album gets to conventional indie-adjacent pop production; their placement back-to-back early in the record (positions 3 and 6) is a deliberate accessibility move before the more experimental back half opens up.

## "Next Level Charli," "Official," "2099" — The Bookended PC Music Identity

The album opens and closes on the most PC Music-coded productions on the record. "Next Level Charli" opens with a chiptune-bright synth-fanfare and a self-referencing chorus that effectively re-introduces the artist in her new sonic identity. "Official" is the album's quietest moment — a near-ballad that strips back the maximalism to demonstrate that the project can hold an emotional fixed point. "2099" — the closing track with Troye Sivan — is the explicit thesis statement: a maximalist hyperpop near-instrumental finale that explicitly answers "1999" earlier in the tracklist by inverting its nostalgia toward an imagined future. The structural decision to bookend the record with these PC Music productions while burying "1999" at position 4 is itself a curatorial argument about which version of Charli the album is asking the listener to remember.

## "February 2017" — The Confessional Centrepiece

"February 2017" with Clairo and Yaeji is the album's emotional confession and the most-praised track among critics for its lyrical specificity. The title refers to a specific period in Charli's life and the song reads as autobiographical reckoning rather than persona-pop. Yaeji's understated production-influenced vocal delivery and Clairo's intimate folk-pop sensibility pull the song's production toward bedroom-pop rather than maximalist hyperpop. The track is the structural argument that *Charli* can house both ends of the contemporary indie-pop spectrum within a single forty-five-minute statement.

## Critical Reception — The Pre-*Brat* Consensus

*Charli* received a Metacritic score of 80/100 across 22 reviews ([Wikipedia, *Charli* (album)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Charli_(album)); [Metacritic, *Charli*](https://www.metacritic.com/music/charli/charli-xcx)). Variety called it "a mature, fully realized and downright visionary album" that is "innovative and adventurous but not off-puttingly weird" and praised her aim of "pushing pop as far as it can go" ([Variety review](https://variety.com/2019/music/reviews/album-review-charli-xcx-charli-1203334771/)). NME's Hannah Mylrea called it "Bold, brash and brilliant … Charli XCX at her most genuine"; Clash gave it 9/10. Commercially the record debuted at UK #14 and US Billboard 200 #42 — respectable but not the major-label crossover Atlantic had pushed for. The pre-*Brat* critical consensus framed *Charli* as the artistic peak of her feature-album phase; the post-*Brat* re-reading frames it as the document where every production tool she would later weaponise on *Brat* gets its first commercial deployment.

## What Came Next — *how i'm feeling now* (2020)

Eight months after *Charli* released, COVID-19 lockdowns trapped Charli at home and she made *how i'm feeling now* — a six-week DIY album produced collaboratively with fans over Zoom and text, released 15 May 2020 ([Wikipedia, *how i'm feeling now*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/How_I'm_Feeling_Now)). The arc from *Pop 2* (2017) to *Charli* (2019) to *how i'm feeling now* (2020) traces a single creative through-line — Cook's executive-producer relationship is preserved, the PC Music vocabulary deepens, and the feature-album shape of *Charli* contracts back into a self-contained intimate statement. *Brat* (2024) is the eventual rave-pop crystallisation of every production decision tested across this three-album sequence. The corpus-relevant point: *Charli* is the hinge album. Before it, Charli is an underground-coded artist with major-label scaffolding; after it, every subsequent record builds on what *Charli* proved viable.

## What *Charli* Demonstrates For ARLO

*Charli* is the most useful case study in the corpus for what a major-label compromise looks like when the compromise is *won by the artist*. The record contains conventional pop concessions (the "1999" lead single, the HAIM and Sky Ferreira features, the "Blame It On Your Love" smoothing) and also contains some of the most uncompromising hyperpop production released through a major label that decade ("Click," "Shake It," "Next Level Charli," "2099"). The production lessons travel: (1) Auto-Tune as a foreground textural identity rather than a corrective tool; (2) feature-list curation as a way to widen sonic vocabulary inside one record without losing artist identity; (3) executive-producer-as-buffer between artist and label as a working pattern; (4) sequencing as a tool to negotiate between commercial and experimental impulses within a single forty-five-minute statement. Every one of these lessons gets re-applied, in tighter form, on *Brat*.
