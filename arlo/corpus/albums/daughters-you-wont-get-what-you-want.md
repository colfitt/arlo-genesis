## The Session Story In One Paragraph

*You Won't Get What You Want* is the album Daughters made eight years after dissolving and five years after quietly reuniting in 2013 — the gap is the structural fact of the record. Marshall got sober during the time away and was initially wary of returning to road life; the reunion only happened after Andy Low at Robotic Empire put Marshall and Sadler in a room together, and the conversation about new music took roughly five minutes to start ([Consequence, Marshall interview](https://consequence.net/2018/11/daughters-alexis-marshall-interview-2018/)). The band, geographically dispersed by then, traded sketches in a shared Dropbox — Sadler has described roughly a hundred instrumental ideas accumulated before the studio dates ([Motif RI, Nick Sadler interview](https://motifri.com/komdeci19/)). Tracking and mixing took place at Machines with Magnets in Pawtucket, Rhode Island, with Seth Manchester engineering and co-producing alongside Sadler ([Wikipedia, *You Won't Get What You Want*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/You_Won%27t_Get_What_You_Want)). Released on Ipecac on 26 October 2018, the album ended the year on a long list of best-of-2018 features — The Needle Drop number one, Sputnikmusic number two, Rolling Stone's number-two metal album, The Quietus at thirteen — and locked in an 87 Metacritic average ([Wikipedia, *You Won't Get What You Want*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/You_Won%27t_Get_What_You_Want)).

## The Hiatus As Compositional Frame

Daughters dissolved in 2009 after a tour Marshall has described as physically and personally unsustainable; the self-titled third album appeared in 2010 with the band already broken up. The 2013 reunion did not produce music quickly — Marshall has described his post-sobriety relationship to touring as making the road suddenly seem like a scary place ([Consequence, Marshall interview](https://consequence.net/2018/11/daughters-alexis-marshall-interview-2018/)). The relevant corpus point is that the gap is audible in the record. Earlier Daughters albums lean on the speed-and-chaos vocabulary of grindcore-adjacent noise; *YWGWYW* is paced, composed, and slow-building. PopMatters' framing captures the shift cleanly — the band "leave behind the frenetic energy of their past practices and instead create an intense, sludge-oid structure filled with noise rock allusions" ([PopMatters, After an Eight-Year Hiatus Daughters Reach Their Peak](https://www.popmatters.com/daughters-you-wont-get-what-2613663305.html)). Hiatus did not soften the band; it rearranged the time signature of the dread.

## The Dropbox Demo Pipeline

The band lived in different cities by 2018, so instrumental sketches were shared remotely. Sadler developed guitar and drum-machine ideas alone, then dropped them into a shared Dropbox folder; the group selected and reworked the strongest ideas together once in the same room ([Motif RI, Nick Sadler interview](https://motifri.com/komdeci19/); [Consequence, Marshall interview](https://consequence.net/2018/11/daughters-alexis-marshall-interview-2018/)). The relevant corpus lesson is that the album's compositional clarity is not in spite of the asynchronous workflow but partly because of it: ideas had to survive on a hard drive, played back cold, to earn a slot. That is closer to an album-as-curatorial-act process than to a live-room jam culture, and it explains why a noise record has unusually little filler.

## Machines With Magnets — The Studio As Co-Author

Machines with Magnets is the Pawtucket, Rhode Island studio operated by Seth Manchester. The facility is hybrid analog/digital and has the institutional character of an experimental music house rather than a commercial rock studio. Manchester's stated philosophy emphasises community and collaborative relationship over technical credentials — "It's about building communities … most of the people I work with, especially these days, are my friends" — and a willingness to push gear into failure: "I'm always interested in pushing pieces of equipment and pushing space, in really fucked up ways, I'm trying to always overdrive all my gears to see how it sounds" ([New Noise, Producer Spotlight — Seth Manchester](https://newnoisemagazine.com/interviews/producer-spotlight-interview-with-seth-manchester-of-machines-with-magnets-studio/)). Sadler echoes the experience from the band side: "they're not trying to force anything on you, and will let you try out your weird ideas" ([Motif RI, Nick Sadler interview](https://motifri.com/komdeci19/)). The same room and the same engineer became the production home of Lingua Ignota across multiple records, and the cross-pollination is audible — both projects share a willingness to track raw, imperfect performances at high gain in a space that treats overdriven equipment as content. (See `corpus/albums/lingua-ignota-caligula.md` and `corpus/albums/lingua-ignota-sinner-get-ready.md` when present.)

## Alexis Marshall's Vocal — Spoken, Howled, Processed

Marshall's vocal is the most-cited element of the record and the clearest break from earlier Daughters. The performance vocabulary is closer to spoken word, declamation, and ragged shout than to screamed-through-overdrive grindcore vocal. He has been explicit about escaping the genre default — pure screaming bored him because there was no change in tone or pitch ([The Quietus, Alexis Marshall interviewed](https://thequietus.com/articles/25754-alexis-marshall-daughters-interview)). He cites David Yow (Jesus Lizard) and Nick Cave as energetic-rather-than-technical reference points and frames his own approach as narrative rather than confessional: "I'm not necessarily trying to convey my feelings, but rather to tell a story" ([Consequence, Marshall interview](https://consequence.net/2018/11/daughters-alexis-marshall-interview-2018/)). The studio treatment matters as much as the delivery — vocals are close-miced and dry, with selective heavy distortion, plate-style reverb tails, and pitched delay/feedback bleeding into the bed. Rolling Stone's Kory Grow described the result as "commanding, half-spoken vocal performances" deployed across "industrial dance" and slow-build textures ([Rolling Stone review](https://www.rollingstone.com/music/music-album-reviews/review-daughters-you-wont-get-what-you-want-747987/)). The corpus point: the vocal is a *character* in the mix, not an emotion-channel — phrasing, repetition and processing are deployed like film-dialogue craft.

## Nicholas Sadler's Guitar — Technique As Anti-Technique

Sadler's guitar language is closer to feedback choreography than to riff playing. His own description of his development: "in a way, not having any technique became the technique," with the style built from "simple things like sliding quickly up and down the frets" ([Motif RI, Nick Sadler interview](https://motifri.com/komdeci19/); [guitarguitar, Nick Sadler interview](https://www.guitarguitar.co.uk/news/140513/)). Across the record the guitar functions as a noise generator producing pitched feedback, broken-glass high-frequency transients, scraped harmonics, and motoric single-note ostinati rather than power chords. Sadler has also said the texture is not principally a pedal-chain trick — it is mostly about how songs are structured, meant to be perceived, and whether they are produced or not produced ([guitarguitar, Sadler interview](https://www.guitarguitar.co.uk/news/140513/)). The fingertip damage he has described from this style — periodic friction injury, kept in check with Fast Fret and lemon oil — is a practical clue to the playing intensity.

## Jon Syverson's Drums — Industrial-Mechanical Patterns

Syverson's drumming is the engine of the album's industrial flavour. Patterns lean on repeating tom and floor-tom figures, mechanical kick patterns, and ride/cymbal punctuation rather than the spasmodic blastbeat language of earlier Daughters. He has described his evolution as a refusal to repeat himself ([Modern Drummer, Jonathan Syverson feature](https://www.moderndrummer.com/2010/03/jonathan-syverson/)) and called the band "noise rock" as the easiest single-word fit ([Music Existence, Syverson interview](https://musicexistence.com/blog/2019/02/24/jon-syverson-of-daughters-talks-returning-as-a-band-skronk-and-advice-to-young-bands/)). The Pitchfork descriptor is the most quoted shorthand for the result — "martial drums cranked to incapacitating volumes" ([Wikipedia, *You Won't Get What You Want*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/You_Won%27t_Get_What_You_Want)). Manchester's overdrive-everything stance is part of why the kit sounds the way it does — drum mics pushed into colour rather than kept clean.

## Samuel Walker's Bass — Floor And Pulse

Samuel M. Walker's bass is the structural floor that lets the guitar abandon harmony. On tracks like "City Song" and "The Reason They Hate Me" the bass holds a single repeating figure or pulse for long stretches while guitar and vocal handle event-level variation. The role is closer to a synth-bass arpeggiator than to a rock bassist's groove function — bass plus drums together form the machine layer the rest of the record reacts against.

## "City Song" — The Opener As Threshold

"City Song" opens the record with a long, slow-burning build. The arrangement is exemplary of the album's general construction: a single repeating bass-and-drum cell sets a tempo and stays put; guitar enters as feedback texture and stays out of conventional riff territory; Marshall's vocal arrives as low spoken declamation and accumulates intensity through repetition rather than escalation in pitch. The track is the album's tonal mission statement — the patience is what reads as new compared to the earlier self-titled record.

## "Long Road, No Turns" — Captivity And Drift

"Long Road, No Turns" was the third single, written by Sadler and Marshall. Rolling Stone's Grow described the arrangement as instrumental elements that "dissolve around the vocal performance" while Marshall navigates themes of captivity ([Rolling Stone review](https://www.rollingstone.com/music/music-album-reviews/review-daughters-you-wont-get-what-you-want-747987/)). The arrangement uses dynamic *retreat* — backing parts fading out under the vocal — rather than the louder-equals-more default.

## "Satan In The Wait" — Minimalist Centrepiece

"Satan in the Wait" was the lead single (July 2018) and the first new Daughters music in eight years. Sadler has cited it as a track he is especially proud of for its restraint — the recording prioritises space and repetition while avoiding instrument overload, an approach he ties to film-score logic and to admiration for sparse arrangements ([Motif RI, Nick Sadler interview](https://motifri.com/komdeci19/)). At over six minutes the track is closer to a slow-build post-rock structure than a noise-rock song; the climactic guitar event arrives late and reads as relief because of how much patience has preceded it.

## "The Reason They Hate Me" — Industrial Dance

"The Reason They Hate Me" was the second single and the most rhythmically immediate piece on the album. Rolling Stone's Grow described it as "an industrial dance song" with heavy rhythmic emphasis ([Rolling Stone review](https://www.rollingstone.com/music/music-album-reviews/review-daughters-you-wont-get-what-you-want-747987/)). Marshall has framed the lyric as "a complaint, an expostulation, a critique on the abuse of critique" — i.e. the song is reflexive, turning its own contempt outward at the practice of contempt ([Wikipedia, *You Won't Get What You Want*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/You_Won%27t_Get_What_You_Want)). Production-wise the song demonstrates the album's most overt machine-pulse — bass and kick locked, vocal forward and dry, guitar reserved for high-frequency punctuation rather than harmonic content.

## "Ocean Song" — The Seven-Minute Narrative

"Ocean Song" is the track Marshall has most readily called out as one of the band's best, and as the model for an aspirational version of the band — "I would be happy if we made a whole record of just 7-minute-long stories" ([Consequence, Marshall interview](https://consequence.net/2018/11/daughters-alexis-marshall-interview-2018/)). It is a narrative piece in the David Yow / Nick Cave lineage Marshall cites — a story-vocal carried across a sustained instrumental field. The arrangement gives the vocal narrative the structural priority that the harmonic ambiguity of the guitar would otherwise erode.

## "Guest House" — The Closer As Battery

"Guest House" closes the record. Rolling Stone described layered percussion building under a vocal about access and invitation ([Rolling Stone review](https://www.rollingstone.com/music/music-album-reviews/review-daughters-you-wont-get-what-you-want-747987/)). The track functions as a release valve for the album's accumulated dread — the most cathartic instrumental outburst on the record, sequenced last. The corpus point is structural: an album that mostly substitutes patience for catharsis can earn one large catharsis at the end, and *YWGWYW* uses that budget on "Guest House."

## Mix And Production Logic

Manchester's mix logic rewards a few specific listening tests. Vocals stay dry and forward in the bed — even when distortion or pitch-shifted delay is on the lead, the dry signal sits in front of those effects rather than being washed away by them. Drum tones are pushed into saturation at the source — Manchester's stated practice of overdriving "all my gears" reads as kit transients with grain rather than the polite-and-spacious modern rock drum sound. The low end is held principally by bass and kick — there is no synth-sub layer doing the work for them — which means the record has weight without subterranean range, and translates well on cheap speakers. Heba Kadry's mastering preserves the dynamic differential the arrangements set up; loud passages are loud, quiet passages are genuinely quiet, and the album does not loudness-war itself flat.

## Reception And The 2018 Noise-Rock Moment

Critical reception was near-uniform acclaim. Metacritic's 87 average reflected universal acclaim across eleven reviews. Pitchfork's writeup framed the appeal as a paradox — "Daughters' accessibility is directly proportional to their uncompromising compositional choices — hypnotic dissonance, martial drums cranked to incapacitating volumes, scathing vocal repetition, all rendered through impossibly vivid production" ([Wikipedia, *You Won't Get What You Want*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/You_Won%27t_Get_What_You_Want)). Year-end placements included The Needle Drop number one, Sputnikmusic number two, Rolling Stone's number-two metal album, The Quietus at thirteen, and over fifteen other best-of-2018 features. The cultural moment matters — the album landed in a year when noise rock and industrial-leaning heavy music re-entered mainstream critical conversation, and it became a reference for the next several years of the style. It is also Daughters' final studio release to date.

## What This Record Teaches The Corpus

- **Patience is a heaviness multiplier.** Daughters earn the violence of the loud passages by spending most of the record refusing to deploy them. The corpus point is dynamic-range budgeting at the album scale, not the track scale.
- **Asynchronous demoing produces unusually-filtered records.** A hundred Dropbox sketches winnowed slowly to ten album tracks is closer to record-as-curatorial-act than to record-as-jam-document; the lack of filler is the audible signature.
- **Studio-as-co-author is real when the engineer is consistent.** Machines with Magnets and Seth Manchester are part of the band's sound on this record in the same way Conny Plank or Steve Albini are part of the records they engineered; the overdriven-source aesthetic is a Manchester signature audible across his catalogue (see Lingua Ignota cross-references when present).
- **A spoken-word vocal can be the loudest thing in a noise-rock mix.** Marshall's dry-and-forward approach demonstrates that vocal dominance is a *placement* decision; volume and processing on the supporting elements can stay aggressive without burying the lead.
- **Feedback and friction can carry a guitar part for an entire album.** Sadler's anti-technique guitar shows that pitched-feedback and high-frequency texture can substitute for riff and chord vocabulary if drums and bass hold the harmonic floor.
