## Why *To Pimp a Butterfly* Matters Aesthetically

*To Pimp a Butterfly* is the record where a major-label rapper, already at the commercial peak after *good kid, m.A.A.d city* (2012), traded radio-friendly West Coast hip-hop for a sprawling jazz-funk-spoken-word treatise tracked largely with live musicians. Released March 15, 2015 on Top Dawg / Aftermath / Interscope, the album debuted at number one in the US and UK, won the Grammy for Best Rap Album, and "Alright" became the de facto soundtrack of the Black Lives Matter movement ([Wikipedia: *To Pimp a Butterfly*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/To_Pimp_a_Butterfly); [Wikipedia: Alright](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alright_(Kendrick_Lamar_song))).

For ARLO, *TPAB* is the canonical case study of a hip-hop record built from a live-jazz ensemble rather than samples — and of an executive-producer-led collective (Sounwave as in-house TDE anchor) curating outside-producer contributions without losing tonal coherence.

## The Three-Year Timeline and the South Africa Trip

Sessions ran November 2012 through February 2015 across five studios: Chalice Recording (Hollywood), Downtown Studios (New York), House Studios (Washington, D.C.), Notifi Studios (St. Louis), and No Excuses Studios (Santa Monica) ([Wikipedia: *TPAB*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/To_Pimp_a_Butterfly); [Sound on Sound: Inside Track](https://www.soundonsound.com/techniques/inside-track-kendrick-lamars-pimp-butterfly)).

A 2014 South Africa visit, including Mandela's Robben Island cell, was the pivot: Lamar scrapped "two or three albums' worth of material" and refocused on Black American identity and institutional racism ([Wikipedia: *TPAB*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/To_Pimp_a_Butterfly)). Sounwave: "Sitting in [Nelson] Mandela's house in Johannesburg, we said, 'This is the sound, this is the message.'" ([Billboard: Sounwave interview](https://www.billboard.com/articles/business/9334666/sounwave-kendrick-lamar-to-pimp-a-butterfly-interview)). The cultural climate — Trayvon Martin (2012), Ferguson (August 2014), Eric Garner, Tamir Rice, Sandra Bland — is not background but text. "The Blacker the Berry" was written shortly after Trayvon Martin's death and released February 9, 2015 as a direct response to backlash over Lamar's Ferguson remarks ([Wikipedia: The Blacker the Berry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Blacker_the_Berry_(Kendrick_Lamar_song))).

## The Studio — Dr. Dre's No Excuses, the SSL 4000 G+, and "MixedByAli"

Most of *TPAB* was tracked and mixed at Dr. Dre's No Excuses Studio in Santa Monica — nicknamed "Tom-Tom" — built around the last Solid State Logic 4000 G+ console SSL produced (1991), previously used on *The Chronic* and *The Slim Shady LP* ([Sound on Sound: Inside Track](https://www.soundonsound.com/techniques/inside-track-kendrick-lamars-pimp-butterfly)). Engineer/mixer Derek "MixedByAli" Ali, Lamar's right-hand engineer at TDE: "you can't get that analogue sound in the box, period."

**Anti-hallucination note:** the brief that initiated this seed referenced "No I.D.'s personal studio." That attribution does not appear in the primary literature; No I.D. is not credited on *TPAB*. The central tracking-and-mix room was Dr. Dre's No Excuses. Executive producers were Dr. Dre and Anthony "Top Dawg" Tiffith ([Wikipedia: *TPAB*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/To_Pimp_a_Butterfly)).

**Vocal chain:** Stephen Paul-modified Telefunken U47 (alternate: U67) → Neve 1073 preamp → Tube-Tech CL1B compressor. Tracking plug-ins: Waves Renaissance Compressor, Metaflanger, De-Esser, SSL Channel, S1 Imager, Avid Air Chorus. **Mix bus and printing:** SSL stereo compressor + GML 8200 EQ → Ampex ATR102 half-inch tape via Lavry Gold A-D, in pursuit of the "vintage '70s sound" Kendrick had requested from the start. Ali spent roughly 80 percent of mix time on a single Auratone in mono ([Sound on Sound](https://www.soundonsound.com/techniques/inside-track-kendrick-lamars-pimp-butterfly)).

## Sounwave as Production Anchor

Mark "Sounwave" Spears, TDE's in-house producer since the label's beginnings, has production credit on nine of the album's sixteen tracks and operated as de-facto A&R: vetting outside collaborators, keeping continuity across the multi-year writing arc, gatekeeping script and beat versions ([SPIN: Sounwave interview](https://www.spin.com/2015/04/sounwave-interview-kendrick-lamar-to-pimp-a-butterfly/); [Wikipedia: *TPAB*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/To_Pimp_a_Butterfly)). The role is hip-hop's closest analogue to the Godrich-with-Radiohead producer-as-curator pattern — see [Nigel Godrich Engineering Method](../artists/nigel-godrich-engineering-method.md). The Digi+Phonics collective (Sounwave, Tae Beast, Willie B, Dave Free) supplied the in-house spine; outside producers — Flying Lotus, Pharrell, Boi-1da, Knxwledge, Rahki, LoveDragon (Terrace Martin + Josef Leimberg), Whoarei — supplied specific tracks that Sounwave integrated.

## "Wesley's Theory" — Flying Lotus, Thundercat, George Clinton, Boris Gardiner

The opener originated on the Yeezus Tour bus: Flying Lotus played Kendrick a beat he had been holding for his Captain Murphy alter ego, and Lamar took it ([Medium: Oral History of *TPAB*](https://medium.com/cuepoint/the-oral-history-of-kendrick-lamar-s-to-pimp-a-butterfly-622f725c3fde)). Production credits: Flying Lotus, Sounwave, Thundercat, Flippa (Ronald Colson). The track opens with a sample of Boris Gardiner's "Every Nigger Is a Star" (1974) ([WhoSampled: *TPAB* sample list](https://www.whosampled.com/album/Kendrick-Lamar/To-Pimp-A-Butterfly/)). George Clinton sings the chorus — Lamar flew to find him "in the woods" somewhere in the South. Thundercat plays bass and voices the "White American Powers That Be" verse in his characteristic tenor-into-falsetto. Josef Leimberg provides trumpet across the record. The drum mix consumed three eighteen-hour days alone, with thirteen post-approval bounces ([Sound on Sound](https://www.soundonsound.com/techniques/inside-track-kendrick-lamars-pimp-butterfly)).

## "For Free? (Interlude)" — Terrace Martin's Live Jazz Combo

The track that most clearly announces the jazz-hip-hop hybrid is the spoken-word "For Free? (Interlude)," arranged by Terrace Martin for a live jazz band tracked in a single energised session. Ali's mic plot: RCA 77 (overhead left), Neumann U48 (overhead right), RCA 44 in front of kit, AKG C24 room split between drums and sax, Neumann M49 close on sax, Neumann U48 on bass, AKG 414EB with C12 capsules on piano — all direct to SSL with mono compression. Kendrick's vocal was tracked through stacked Electro-Voice RE20 and 666 mics for a "warm, almost distorted sound" ([Sound on Sound](https://www.soundonsound.com/techniques/inside-track-kendrick-lamars-pimp-butterfly)).

## "King Kunta" — Sounwave's Pivot, Thundercat on One String

Sounwave's first version was "the jazziest thing ever with pretty flutes." Kendrick asked him to "make it nasty," referencing a DJ Quik record. Sounwave swapped in heavier drums, simplified the harmonic bed, and brought in Thundercat for bass ([SPIN: Sounwave interview](https://www.spin.com/2015/04/sounwave-interview-kendrick-lamar-to-pimp-a-butterfly/); [Wikipedia: King Kunta](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/King_Kunta)). Thundercat played the entire bassline on a single string. Production: Terrace Martin, Michael Kuhle, Sounwave. The song interpolates Michael Jackson's "Smooth Criminal" (1987) and Mausberg's DJ Quik-produced "Get Nekkid" (2000), with elements of James Brown's "The Payback" (1974) and Ahmad's "We Want the Funk" (1994) ([Wikipedia: King Kunta](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/King_Kunta)).

## "u," "Alright," "The Blacker the Berry" — The Mid-Album Trio

Three middle-of-album tracks each illustrate a different producer-curation pattern Sounwave used to keep the record coherent.

**"u"** was the hardest song for Kendrick to perform. The first section is built on a beat by Sacramento producer Whoarei with Taz Arnold (additional production: Sounwave). Kamasi Washington's tenor sax is the dominant live-jazz voice across both halves. The second half is the famous hotel-bathroom-mirror breakdown — Kendrick's vocal layered against Washington and Terrace Martin's alto interplay ([Okayplayer: Kamasi Washington on *TPAB*](https://www.okayplayer.com/watch-kamasi-washington-jam-w/-terrace-martin-discuss-to-pimp-a-butterfly/602646)).

**"Alright"** almost did not make the album. Pharrell Williams produced the instrumental in 2014, originally for Fabolous. Sounwave discovered the beat and brought it to Kendrick, who could not reconcile it with the rest of *TPAB*'s jazz/funk palette. Sounwave and Terrace Martin reworked the drums; Lamar then approved it ([Wikipedia: Alright](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alright_(Kendrick_Lamar_song))). Cultural afterlife: youth-led BLM protests adopted the chorus as a chant; the song won Best Rap Performance and Best Rap Song at the 58th Grammys.

**"The Blacker the Berry"** — Boi-1da supplied the hard percussive drum bed; KOZ and Terrace Martin filled out the production. Written shortly after Trayvon Martin's death and released as the album's second single on February 9, 2015. The closing jazz coda features Robert Glasper on keys, Thundercat on bass, and Lalah Hathaway on vocals — a "soulful" resolution to a deliberately abrasive verse landscape. Title nods to Wallace Thurman's 1929 novel and a lyric on Tupac's "Keep Ya Head Up" ([Wikipedia: The Blacker the Berry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Blacker_the_Berry_(Kendrick_Lamar_song))).

## "These Walls" — 90-Track Mix, "Underwater" Bass Doubler

"These Walls" — Terrace Martin / Sounwave, featuring Bilal, Anna Wise, Thundercat — is the track Ali used to walk *Sound on Sound* through his mix process. Roughly 90 tracks. Drum processing: SSL desk EQ, API 550 on snare, Neve 2254 parallel compression, SPL Transient Designer, Pultec EQP-1A. Kendrick's lead vocal: Waves Renaissance Compressor (manual/opto, threshold ~-18 dB), SSL channel strip, Waves S1 Imager to "open up" the raspy take, EMT 250 reverb, Empirical Labs Distressor; outboard Neve 2254 and Fairchild 670 (Fairchild in parallel on guest vocals). On Thundercat's bass: Waves Doubler for an "underwater" effect plus S1 Imager and Pultec EQ. Stereo bus: SSL compression into GML 8200 EQ — same chain across the record ([Sound on Sound: Inside Track](https://www.soundonsound.com/techniques/inside-track-kendrick-lamars-pimp-butterfly)).

## "Hood Politics," "Momma," "How Much a Dollar Cost" — The Sample-Forward Cluster

Three back-half tracks lean back into hip-hop's sample architecture. **"Hood Politics"** — Tae Beast / Sounwave / Thundercat — is built primarily on Sufjan Stevens's "All for Myself" from *The Age of Adz* (2010), with interpolated references to Notorious B.I.G.'s "Notorious Thugs" and Jay-Z's "Imaginary Player" ([WhoSampled: Hood Politics / All for Myself](https://www.whosampled.com/sample/339926/Kendrick-Lamar-Hood-Politics-Sufjan-Stevens-All-for-Myself/); [NME: Kendrick samples Sufjan](https://www.nme.com/news/music/kendrick-lamar-86-1214484)). For how that source bed was engineered, see [Sufjan Stevens Age of Adz Production](../artists/sufjan-stevens-age-of-adz-production.md). **"Momma"** — Knxwledge and Taz Arnold — is built around a chopped sample of Knxwledge's own beat "So[rt]." with Lalah Hathaway backing vocals ([WhoSampled: Momma / So[rt].](https://www.whosampled.com/sample/339937/Kendrick-Lamar-Momma-Knxwledge-So%5Brt%5D./)). **"How Much a Dollar Cost"** (featuring James Fauntleroy and Ronald Isley) was produced by LoveDragon — the duo of Josef Leimberg and Terrace Martin. The piano figure has been widely noted to resemble Radiohead's "Pyramid Song" (2001); Radiohead are not credited in the liner notes ([Wikipedia: How Much a Dollar Cost](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/How_Much_a_Dollar_Cost)).

## "i" — Rahki, Live-Replay of "That Lady"

The lead single "i" was produced by Los Angeles producer Rahki (who also produced "Institutionalized"). Critically, the Isley Brothers' "That Lady" is not sampled from the master: Rahki flew the Isleys in, Ronald Isley re-sang parts, and the band re-recorded the rhythm bed live — the entire arrangement is a replay ([Wikipedia: i (Kendrick Lamar song)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/I_(Kendrick_Lamar_song)); [Fader: Rahki on producing "i"](https://www.thefader.com/2014/10/16/beat-construction-rahki)). The album version is structurally distinct from the single: it cuts off mid-song into a live-crowd argument and a freestyle, turning a celebratory standalone into a contested moment inside the album's larger arc. Drum miking for the live "That Lady" session: AKG D112 inside kick, Neumann 47FET outside, Shure SM57 top/bottom on snare, Sennheiser 421 on toms, Neumann KM84 on ride, Shure SM81 on hi-hat, Neumann 87 overheads, room mics AKG C24, Telefunken ELAM 251, Neumann TLM170 ([Sound on Sound](https://www.soundonsound.com/techniques/inside-track-kendrick-lamars-pimp-butterfly)).

## "Mortal Man" — The 1994 Tupac Interview and the Unfurling Poem

The closing track ends with a simulated conversation between Lamar and Tupac Shakur. The Tupac audio is from a previously unreleased November 1994 interview between Shakur and Mats Nileskär, host of Swedish radio show *P3 Soul*, recorded roughly two weeks before the Quad Studios shooting in New York. Lamar met Nileskär in Germany in 2014 and was given the tape; sampling was approved by Tupac's stepbrother Mopreme and mother Afeni Shakur ([Wikipedia: Mortal Man](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mortal_Man); [Hollywood Reporter: Tupac interview on *TPAB*](https://www.hollywoodreporter.com/news/general-news/listen-rare-tupac-interview-featured-781697/)).

The closing conversation is also where the album's unfurling-poem structure resolves: each track has added a line to a spoken-word verse Kendrick has been reciting in interludes throughout, until on "Mortal Man" he reads the completed poem to Tupac and asks for a response. That device, plus Sounwave's curatorial integration into a single sonic palette (SSL G+, MixedByAli vocabulary, ATR102 print), is what allows *TPAB* to swing from Pharrell's pop ("Alright") to Boi-1da's drum-heavy minor key ("Blacker the Berry") to Knxwledge's lo-fi ("Momma") to Flying Lotus's electro-funk ("Wesley's Theory") to a live jazz quintet ("For Free?") without feeling like a compilation.

## What ARLO Should Take from *To Pimp a Butterfly*

For a user trying to make a record like this:

- **Have one production anchor curate many outside producers.** Sounwave's nine-of-sixteen production credit plus his A&R/gatekeeper role is the structural lesson — coherence at the curator layer, not at the beat-maker layer.
- **Live musicians over samples, where possible — but keep the samples that earn their keep.** *TPAB* is mostly live-tracked, but Boris Gardiner ("Wesley's Theory"), Sufjan Stevens ("Hood Politics"), and Knxwledge ("Momma") are doing irreplaceable work.
- **Re-record samples live when budget and access allow.** The Isley Brothers replay on "i" is the model — same source music, new performance, full clearance, fewer legal headaches.
- **Build a narrative spine outside the beats.** The spoken-word poem that completes on "Mortal Man" gives the album a structural reason to move across genres.
- **Track everything to tape at the end.** ATR102 print via Lavry Gold A-D, after a Pro-Tools-and-SSL chain, is a workable hybrid template.
