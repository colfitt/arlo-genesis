## The Album-as-Artifact — Why *What's Going On* Sits Apart in the Motown Catalogue

*What's Going On* is the record retrieved when someone wants "the moment Motown stopped being a hit factory and let an artist make an album." It is the first LP on which Marvin Gaye is credited as sole producer, the first Motown record built end-to-end on a single artist's political concept, and [the first Motown album to credit the in-house session players (the Funk Brothers) by name on the sleeve](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/What%27s_Going_On_(Marvin_Gaye_album)). Its technical fingerprints — an accidental warm-up sax, double-tracked lead vocals printed in stereo by mistake, a bass take played from the floor of the studio — are the foreground of its mythology rather than buried in footnotes. For ARLO: *What's Going On* is the canonical reference for "self-produced soul concept album, Detroit 1970–71, Funk Brothers rhythm section, jazz-leaning arrangement, multi-tracked overlapping lead vocals."

## Producer Lineage — Gaye Self-Producing After the Motown Assembly Line

Berry Gordy's Motown of the 1960s ran on what its own staff called the assembly line: songs assigned by [Holland–Dozier–Holland, Norman Whitfield, or Smokey Robinson](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/What%27s_Going_On_(Marvin_Gaye_album)), tracked by the Funk Brothers in the Hitsville basement on a tight rhythm template, vocals dropped on top by whichever Motown star fit the song, mixes signed off by Quality Control. Gaye had been inside that system since 1961. The shift on *What's Going On* — provoked by the 1970 death of his duet partner Tammi Terrell, his brother Frankie's return from Vietnam, and riot-scarred Detroit — was that Gaye took the producer chair himself. Per [Sound on Sound](https://www.soundonsound.com/techniques/classic-tracks-marvin-gaye-whats-going), the album "was the first of Marvin Gaye's albums to afford him credit as sole producer." That credit is the technical fact behind the looser feel: no in-house producer enforcing a four-on-the-floor template, and arrangement decisions (chord-chart bass parts, jazz horns, layered conversational vocals) read as an artist's choices rather than a label's.

## Berry Gordy's Resistance — "The Worst Record I Ever Heard"

The most-told story about *What's Going On* is its near-non-release. Gordy reportedly called the title single ["the worst record I've heard in my life"](https://www.nolazine.com/post/berry-gordy-initially-rejected-marvin-gaye-s-what-s-going-on-calling-it-the-worst-record-he-d-e) when Gaye first played it for him, citing its jazz-inflected sound, its political content, and its perceived risk to Motown's crossover formula. Gaye, by this account, refused to record anything else for the label until the single was put out. The end-run came from Motown's own sales department: [vice president Barney Ales pressed 100,000 copies of the single and released it on 17 January 1971 without Gordy's authorisation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/What%27s_Going_On_(song)). When the record sold through almost immediately and dominated R&B radio, Gordy gave Gaye thirty days to finish a full album of his own production. The arc — rejection, sales-department guerrilla release, capitulation, full-album greenlight — is well-attested across multiple secondary sources but most colourfully retold in [Ben Edmonds' *What's Going On? Marvin Gaye and the Last Days of the Motown Sound*](https://archive.org/details/whatsgoingonmarv0000edmo) (Mojo Books, 2001), which remains the deepest book-length treatment of the sessions. For retrieval: when a user asks "the album the label tried to kill," this is one of the canonical cases.

## The Three Studios — Hitsville Studio A, Golden World Studio B, The Sound Factory

The album was tracked across more rooms than any Motown record had reasonably required up to that point. Per [Sound on Sound's reconstruction with engineer Ken Sands](https://www.soundonsound.com/techniques/classic-tracks-marvin-gaye-whats-going), rhythm sections went down at **Hitsville USA Studio A** (2648 West Grand Boulevard, original Motown basement, live room nicknamed "The Snake Pit" — wooden floor, horizontal acoustic treatment, dropped ceiling, dead-tight sound) and **Golden World Studio B** (3246 West Davison, a former competitor studio Motown absorbed in 1966, retaining its Neumann solid-state console and Scully 8-track). **United Sound Studios** in Detroit handled additional rhythm tracking. Strings, brass, percussion overdubs, and lead vocals were largely cut in Studio B by Sands; some additional work was logged at **The Sound Factory** in West Hollywood. Final stereo mixing happened at the **Motown Center** tenth-floor mix room on an Electrodyne console feeding paired Altec 604E monitors — the last major Motown release Sands mixed there before the company moved to LA.

## Tape, Console, Mics — The Hitsville Signal Path Circa 1970

Studio A's 1970 control room ran a two-console configuration: a [six-input mono desk in the centre, an eight-channel stereo desk on the left, Langevin faders assignable to L/C/R, 16-channel monitoring wing](https://www.soundonsound.com/techniques/classic-tracks-marvin-gaye-whats-going). The recording path had no channel EQ — colour came from mic choice, room sound, and the [Motown echo chambers in the attic spaces at 2648 and 2652 West Grand](https://www.soundonsound.com/techniques/classic-tracks-marvin-gaye-whats-going), wet-plaster-walled rooms with shellacked floors and Shure SM57s catching reflections, piped back via equalised telephone lines. Tape was a two-inch Ampex MM1000 16-track, Motown's standard since a 1968 upgrade from the in-house Ampex-based 8-track chief engineer Mike McLean had built. Lead vocals were tracked through a Neumann U87 — McLean had converted both studios from tube to solid-state mics — with Neumann U67s still in use on piano and snare and an RCA DX77 ribbon on bass drum. Headline: solid-state front end into 16-track tape, with character coming from the attic chambers and the room rather than the console.

## The Multi-Tracked Vocal Aesthetic — A Stereo-Comparison Mistake

The most-imitated technical move on the record — Gaye's overlapping double lead, one voice riding melody, a second voice riffing conversationally over the top — was a happy accident of vocal comping. Per Ken Sands in Sound on Sound, ["We recorded two lead vocals that Marvin wanted to compare with one another"](https://www.soundonsound.com/techniques/classic-tracks-marvin-gaye-whats-going). Rather than bouncing them to separate mono tapes for A/B, Sands made one stereo tape — rhythm track centred, take one hard left, take two hard right. When Gaye played it back with both vocals coming at him at once, he heard a sound nobody had been making. Both takes stayed in the mix. The choice became Gaye's signature for the rest of his Motown career (*Let's Get It On*, *I Want You*) and the structural model for layered-soul-vocal records that followed; on *What's Going On* it is still raw — two distinct passes coexisting because the comparison rig made them coexist.

## "What's Going On" — The Title Track's Accidents

The single is built on three documented accidents that together define the record's aesthetic.

**The Eli Fontaine sax intro.** Alto saxophonist [Eli Fontaine warmed up while the tape was rolling and played the now-canonical four-bar phrase that opens the song](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/What%27s_Going_On_(song)). When he told Gaye he was ready to do a real take, Gaye told him to go home: ["You goof off exquisitely. Thank you."](https://www.soundonsound.com/techniques/classic-tracks-marvin-gaye-whats-going) The intro on the released record is the warm-up.

**The Lem Barney / Mel Farr party intro.** Gaye invited [Detroit Lions defensive back Lem Barney and running back Mel Farr](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/What%27s_Going_On_(song)) — close friends, regular pickup-basketball partners — into the studio, along with co-writer Obie Benson of the Four Tops, to record the greeting-and-chatter bed under Fontaine's sax. The voices saying "Hey man, what's happening" and "Solid, baby" are not actors; they are Gaye's actual social circle, captured live.

**James Jamerson on the floor.** The bass line on the title track — chromatic, ornamented, more a melodic counter-line than a root-five anchor — is canonically attributed to [James Jamerson](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/James_Jamerson). The story, told most colourfully by Motown pianist [Joe Hunter to *The Guardian* and repeated widely](https://www.guitarworld.com/features/marvin-gaye-whats-going-on), is that Gaye found Jamerson in a Detroit club, brought him back to Hitsville drunk, and Jamerson — unable to sit on his usual stool — lay on his back on the studio floor and played the part in one take while someone held the chart over him. Arranger David Van De Pitte's account in Sound on Sound corroborates the floor performance. Verification confidence: medium-high. Two named participants (Hunter, Van De Pitte) corroborate it, and it is consistent with Jamerson's documented working habits, but it is not in Jamerson's own first-person testimony and is not explicitly framed this way in Allan Slutsky's *Standing in the Shadows of Motown* (which this researcher did not directly consult). Treat as well-attested oral history rather than a recording-log fact.

## The Funk Brothers as a Credited Band

The album's personnel — the first Motown record to put the studio musicians on the cover — established the canonical Funk Brothers lineup as it played in 1970–71:

- **James Jamerson** — Fender Precision bass on the title track and most of side one
- **Bob Babbitt** — bass on ["Inner City Blues (Make Me Wanna Holler)" and "Mercy Mercy Me (The Ecology)"](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inner_City_Blues_(Make_Me_Wanna_Holler)), Jamerson's younger counterpart and the band's other regular bassist
- **Chet Forest** — drums on the title track; Sands described him as "more of a swing big-band drummer" than Motown's usual Richard "Pistol" Allen, and Gaye specifically wanted that looser feel
- **Joe Messina** — electric guitar
- **Robert White** — acoustic guitar
- **Eddie Willis** — guitar (on multiple album tracks)
- **Earl Van Dyke** — keyboards / bandleader
- **Jack Ashford** — tambourine, vibes, marimba, percussion (the Funk Brothers' percussion-colour utility player)
- **Eddie "Bongo" Brown** — bongos, congas
- **Eli Fontaine** — alto saxophone (title track intro)
- **Wild Bill Moore** — tenor sax solo on ["Mercy Mercy Me (The Ecology)"](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mercy_Mercy_Me_(The_Ecology))

Plus Detroit Symphony Orchestra members on strings (conducted by David Van De Pitte) and trumpeter Johnny Trudell on horns. Gaye himself played piano on multiple tracks and contributed what the credits call "box drum" — a percussive woodblock-or-cardboard-box hit, [drenched in studio echo, especially audible on "Mercy Mercy Me"](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mercy_Mercy_Me_(The_Ecology)).

## David Van De Pitte's String and Brass Arrangements

The orchestral language of the album — woody flutes, low strings sliding under the groove, brass stabs that feel like jazz horn-section writing rather than Motown sweetening — is the work of arranger and conductor [David Van De Pitte](https://www.soundonsound.com/techniques/classic-tracks-marvin-gaye-whats-going), with Paul Riser arranging strings on "Mercy Mercy Me". Van De Pitte wrote the bass charts Jamerson read from on the studio floor, which is partly why the title-track bass is more linear and through-composed than the room-improvised Jamerson lines of earlier Motown hits. Strings were tracked at Golden World Studio B with Sands engineering. Robert Christgau famously [dismissed the arrangements as "the lowest kind of movie-background dreck"](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/What%27s_Going_On_(Marvin_Gaye_album)) — useful counter-citation for any query that frames the orchestration as universally beloved. For ARLO: when a user asks for "jazz-leaning string arrangement on a soul record, 1971," Van De Pitte is the canonical reference.

## Per-Track Notes

- **"What's Going On"** — Fontaine sax intro, Barney/Farr party chatter, Jamerson floor bass, Forest drums, Gaye double-vocal stereo split. Released as single 17 January 1971.
- **"What's Happening Brother"** — Written for Gaye's brother Frankie returning from Vietnam. Continuous fade-in from the title track on the LP master, one of the earliest examples in soul of treating an album side as a through-composed suite.
- **"Flyin' High (In the Friendly Sky)"** — Heroin allegory; Funk Brothers rhythm section with prominent vibes from Ashford.
- **"Save the Children"** — Spoken-word interjections layered over sung lead; the multi-tracked-vocal aesthetic at its most overt.
- **"God Is Love"** — Brief gospel-leaning track, originally cut as the B-side of the title single.
- **"Mercy Mercy Me (The Ecology)"** — Babbitt bass, Wild Bill Moore tenor sax solo, [Gaye's "box drum" struck with a rubber mallet and drowned in chamber reverb](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mercy_Mercy_Me_(The_Ecology)), Paul Riser strings, Andantes (Motown's veteran backing trio) on background vocals. Second single; reached number 4 on the Hot 100.
- **"Right On"** — Longest track on the LP (over seven minutes), Latin-percussion-driven groove, flute and saxophone solos riding a long modal vamp.
- **"Wholy Holy"** — Gospel-influenced, strings and harp foregrounded.
- **"Inner City Blues (Make Me Wanna Holler)"** — [Babbitt bass as the song's melodic hook](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inner_City_Blues_(Make_Me_Wanna_Holler)), Gaye on piano, fades into a reprise of the title track to close the album in a circular arc. Third single; number 9 on the Hot 100.

## Sequencing — The First Soul Concept Album as a Continuous Suite

The LP is sequenced as a continuous emotional arc rather than a sequence of standalone singles: party chatter and sax fade into "What's Happening Brother" without a true stop, "Flyin' High" bleeds into "Save the Children," and "Inner City Blues" reprises a fragment of the title track to close the loop. This is one of the earliest mainstream Black American records to treat the album side as a through-composed unit rather than a singles container — a posture later picked up by Stevie Wonder's classic-period albums and the Curtis Mayfield concept records running in parallel. The closest white-rock analogue is the *Abbey Road* B-side medley two years earlier; where the Beatles edit was a studio-assembly stitch, *What's Going On* is a continuous emotional throughline with cross-fades.

## Anti-Hallucination Notes — What Is and Isn't Verified

- The Berry Gordy "worst record I ever heard" attribution is well-attested but exists in slightly variant wordings across sources ("worst record I've heard in my life," "worst thing I ever heard," etc.). Treat the *gist* as solid and the exact wording as variable. Verification confidence: high on the gist, medium on exact phrasing.
- The Jamerson "playing from the floor" story is corroborated by two named participants (Joe Hunter and David Van De Pitte) but is not directly sourced to Jamerson himself in the materials consulted here. Allan Slutsky's *Standing in the Shadows of Motown* would be the next verification step. Verification confidence: medium-high; well-attested oral history, not a recording-log fact.
- The single's release date is most commonly given as **17 January 1971** (per the song's Wikipedia page) but **20 January 1971** also appears in some accounts. Treat 17 January as the more commonly cited date.
- Recording dates: title track sessions ran 1 June – 21 September 1970, with the remainder of the album cut in a roughly 30-day window between 17 March and 5 May 1971 after Gordy's greenlight.
- "United Sound Studios" appears in the studios list alongside Hitsville and Golden World; The Sound Factory in West Hollywood appears in Wikipedia but Ken Sands' Sound on Sound interview implies most actual tracking happened in Detroit. Treat LA involvement as a question to verify before any user-facing technical claim.
- The "Funk Brothers" name had not yet been coined as the canonical collective brand in 1971; the album credits the individual players. Re-grouping them as "the Funk Brothers" is partly retroactive (and partly the work of Allan Slutsky's later book and the 2002 documentary of the same name).
