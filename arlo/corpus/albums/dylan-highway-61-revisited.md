# Bob Dylan — *Highway 61 Revisited* (1965)

*Highway 61 Revisited* is the record where Dylan finishes the turn that *Bringing It All Back Home* had only started four months earlier. The previous album had been split down the middle — electric side one, acoustic side two. *Highway 61* keeps only the closing acoustic ballad as a vestige of the old form; everything else is a band record. It is Dylan's first fully electric album, cut at Columbia Studio A in Midtown Manhattan during two short blocks of summer 1965, under two different producers, with a cast of session players who had mostly never met him before they walked into the room.

## Context: Four Months From Folk to Rock

*Bringing It All Back Home* came out in March 1965 with Tom Wilson producing — half acoustic, half electric, the band assembled by Wilson and built around the kind of overdub experiment he had been running on Dylan tracks since late 1964 (most famously trying out a Fats-Domino-style backing on the unreleased electric "House of the Rising Sun," directly inspired by the Animals' hit). By June 1965 Dylan was back at Columbia Studio A to start the follow-up. Between the two recording blocks for *Highway 61* he played the Newport Folk Festival on July 25, 1965 — the notorious electric set with Mike Bloomfield, Al Kooper, Sam Lay, and Jerome Arnold that split the folk audience down the middle. He reconvened in the studio four days later. The album was released August 30, 1965.

## The Wilson-to-Johnston Handoff

The most consequential production fact on this record is that it has two producers, and the swap happens between sessions, not between sides.

- **Tom Wilson** produced the **June 15–16, 1965** sessions. Those are the sessions that yielded "Like a Rolling Stone." Wilson was the producer of record on every Dylan studio album going back to *The Freewheelin' Bob Dylan*'s later sessions; he had pushed Dylan toward the electric overdub experiments of *Bringing It All Back Home*.
- **Bob Johnston** produced the **July 29 – August 4, 1965** sessions, which produced the rest of the album. Johnston, a Columbia staff producer based partly in Nashville, was new to Dylan and would go on to produce the next five Dylan albums in a row, including *Blonde on Blonde*, *John Wesley Harding*, *Nashville Skyline*, *Self Portrait*, and *New Morning*.

The exact reason for the swap has never been settled. Dylan, asked directly, said only, "I looked up one day, and Bob was there." Bob Johnston pointed to friction between Wilson and Dylan's manager, Albert Grossman. Mike Bloomfield said Dylan hadn't been happy with Wilson's band work. The result, for retrieval purposes, is a clean per-track split: **only "Like a Rolling Stone" was produced by Tom Wilson on the released album. Everything else was produced by Bob Johnston.**

A separate note: this is *not* the Hammond era. John Hammond, Dylan's discoverer, had been out of the producer chair since the *Freewheelin'* sessions in 1962–63. He is sometimes confused into this story; he was nowhere near *Highway 61*.

## Studio and Crew

Columbia Studio A on Seventh Avenue in Manhattan was Columbia's flagship Midtown room — a tall, live space the label used for everything from Sinatra dates to Miles Davis sessions. The sessions ran fast, with the band largely playing live to tape and Dylan dictating tempo and feel from the floor rather than from the control room. Bloomfield functioned as de facto musical director once tracks were rolling.

Core personnel across the album:

- **Mike Bloomfield** — electric guitar (from the Paul Butterfield Blues Band)
- **Al Kooper** — organ (and the occasional electric guitar and piano)
- **Paul Griffin** — piano and organ
- **Bobby Gregg** — drums (most tracks)
- **Sam Lay** — drums on "Highway 61 Revisited" (also Butterfield band)
- **Joe Macho Jr.** — bass on "Like a Rolling Stone"; **Russ Savakus** briefly tried, then replaced by **Harvey Brooks** for the bulk of the July/August sessions
- **Charlie McCoy** — acoustic guitar overdub on "Desolation Row"
- **Frank Owens** — piano on the June sessions
- **Bruce Langhorne** — tambourine

## "Like a Rolling Stone" — The Wilson Session

The single canonical session on this record is June 15–16, 1965, in Studio A.

On **June 15**, Dylan ran the song down in **3/4 waltz time** with himself at the piano. Five takes were attempted. None worked. The released versions of these early waltz attempts later surfaced on *The Bootleg Series Vol. 1–3* in 1991.

On **June 16**, the band reconvened and the song was rebuilt in 4/4. Dylan moved to electric guitar. Bloomfield was on his blonde 1963 Fender Telecaster — the same instrument he would carry to Newport six weeks later and play on the Butterfield Blues Band's debut LP that September. Paul Griffin was originally seated at the organ.

The Al Kooper organ part is the single most discussed accidental contribution on a 1960s rock record, and the details are well documented in Kooper's memoir *Backstage Passes and Backstabbing Bastards*. The shape of the story, as Kooper has told it consistently over six decades:

- Kooper was a 21-year-old session guitarist who had been invited by Tom Wilson as a hang, not a player. He set up to play guitar but realized, watching Bloomfield warm up, that he was outclassed.
- Paul Griffin moved from the organ bench to piano partway through the setup, leaving the Hammond seat open.
- Kooper told Wilson he had an organ part. Wilson said something to the effect of "you don't even play organ" but, distracted by something else, never actually said no. Kooper treated the absence of "no" as permission and slid onto the bench.
- Kooper could barely hear the organ over the band, didn't know the changes, and consequently played his chords roughly an eighth note *behind* the rest of the band — chasing what he heard rather than leading it. The famously delayed, suspended-feeling organ entries are an artifact of him waiting to confirm each chord before committing.
- On playback in the control room, Dylan asked Wilson to bring the organ up in the mix. Wilson protested that Kooper "wasn't an organ player." Dylan insisted. The fader went up. The take with the loud organ became the master.

The released master is the fourth complete take from June 16 (there were two rehearsals and four takes to a usable performance, then eleven more attempts that failed to top it). Length: 6 minutes 13 seconds — at the time, the longest 45 rpm single ever released by a major label. It reached #2 on the Billboard Hot 100, behind only "Help!" by the Beatles.

## The Johnston Sessions: July 29 – August 4

When the band reconvened on **July 29**, Wilson was gone and Johnston was in the chair. Joe Macho was out; Russ Savakus tried bass briefly but didn't fit; Kooper recommended his friend Harvey Brooks, who came in cold and held the chair for the rest of the album.

That first Johnston night produced three masters: **"Tombstone Blues"** (Bloomfield's most aggressive showcase on the album), **"It Takes a Lot to Laugh, It Takes a Train to Cry"**, and the single-only **"Positively 4th Street."** At the end of that long night, with drummer Bobby Gregg already gone home, Dylan attempted a first pass at "Desolation Row" with only Kooper on electric guitar and Brooks on bass — a stark, drummerless version later released on *The Bootleg Series*.

Subsequent sessions through August 4 yielded **"From a Buick 6,"** **"Ballad of a Thin Man"** (one false start, one take), **"Queen Jane Approximately"** (seven takes), **"Highway 61 Revisited"** (the title track), **"Just Like Tom Thumb's Blues"** (sixteen takes), and the final, full-band-then-stripped-down version of **"Desolation Row."**

## Bloomfield, "No B.B. King," and the Country Lean

Bloomfield had auditioned for Dylan (through John Hammond) back in early 1964 as an acoustic player and not been called back. By mid-1965 he was the lead guitarist of the Butterfield Blues Band — one of the loudest, fastest, most blues-fluent white guitarists in America. Dylan brought him up to Woodstock before the June 15 session and, according to Bloomfield's own later interviews, instructed him specifically *against* playing blues clichés on the record: "I don't want any of that B.B. King stuff." Dylan wanted more *country* in the lead voice — chiming, ringing intervals rather than bent string-vibrato microtonal blues phrasing. The Bloomfield part on "Like a Rolling Stone," with its falling treble fills and chordal embellishments rather than bent solos, is the audible result. On "Tombstone Blues" Dylan let him cut loose; the rest of the album he reins him in.

## The Title Track and Kooper's Police Whistle

"Highway 61 Revisited" the song is a roadhouse stomp built on Sam Lay's drums (Bobby Gregg sits this one out — Lay is the Butterfield drummer Dylan had used at Newport four days before the session). The piercing siren that punctuates the verses is credited on the original sleeve simply as "Police Car" with the part attributed to Dylan.

The story behind that whistle, told repeatedly by Kooper: he wore an Acme-style police siren whistle on a chain around his neck as a running joke — he would blow it in the control room or at parties, mostly drug-related humor. During the title-track take he liked how it sounded against the rhythm, took it off his neck, hung it around Dylan's neck, and told him to play it instead of the harmonica. Dylan did. The credit on the sleeve preserves the joke.

## "Desolation Row" and Charlie McCoy

The 11-minute closing track is the album's lone acoustic survivor — and the seed of everything that follows in Dylan's catalogue. Bob Johnston flew in **Charlie McCoy**, a 24-year-old Nashville session guitarist and harmonica player Johnston had been working with at Columbia's Nashville studio, and brought him to Studio A on **August 4** to overdub a second acoustic guitar against Dylan's vocal and acoustic. McCoy improvised the entire flamenco-tinged countermelody and fills on a single pass. The recorded version that became the master is Dylan acoustic, Harvey Brooks on bass, and McCoy overdubbing acoustic guitar — no drums, no organ, no Bloomfield.

This session matters historically beyond the song itself: Johnston used McCoy's performance to demonstrate to Dylan how fast and how musically Nashville session players could work. The next year, Johnston moved Dylan's *Blonde on Blonde* sessions out of Studio A and down to Columbia's Nashville studio, with McCoy and the rest of the Nashville Cats as the band. That move — and the "thin wild mercury sound" Dylan later associated with those Nashville sessions — is a story that belongs to the *next* album, not this one.

## Release, Reception, and Position in the Catalogue

*Highway 61 Revisited* came out on August 30, 1965 on Columbia. It reached #3 on the Billboard 200, #4 on the UK Albums Chart, was certified Gold in 1967 and Platinum in 1997, and entered the Grammy Hall of Fame in 2002. Rolling Stone's "500 Greatest Albums of All Time" placed it at #4 in 2003 and #18 in the 2020 revision.

In Dylan's own catalogue, the record is the hinge. *Bringing It All Back Home* (March 1965) had broken the folk taboo against electric instruments; *Highway 61* committed to the band format outright, with poetry-as-rock-lyric scaled up to the eleven-minute "Desolation Row." *Blonde on Blonde* the following year would push the same approach further south to Nashville. Together they form the trilogy that turned the singer-songwriter mode into a viable rock idiom and made the rock LP a legitimate vehicle for long-form lyric writing.
