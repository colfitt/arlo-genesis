# The Stooges — *Raw Power* (1973)

## What the record is, and where it sits in the catalog

*Raw Power* is the third studio album by The Stooges, credited on the sleeve to **Iggy and the Stooges**, released **7 February 1973** on Columbia Records. It was recorded between **10 September and 6 October 1972** at **CBS Studios, London**, with staff engineer **Mike Ross-Trevor** ([Wikipedia — Raw Power](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Raw_Power)). It is the post-collapse rescue record: the band that had ended [*Fun House*](./stooges-fun-house.md) in 1970 with a saxophone-and-feedback freakout had broken up in 1971 under heroin, label disinterest and personal entropy. By 1972 Iggy Pop had no band, no deal, and was living on Bowie's manager Tony Defries's dime. Everything *Raw Power* is — the new guitarist, the demoted bassist, the thin one-day mix, the *next* mix twenty-four years later — flows from that rescue context. The lateral links are to [*The Rise and Fall of Ziggy Stardust*](./bowie-ziggy-stardust.md) (June 1972) and [*Transformer*](./lou-reed-transformer.md) (August 1972) — the two records Bowie had just finished when Iggy's sessions began. The forward link is everything punk.

## How Iggy got to London — Tony Defries and the MainMan rescue

The Stooges had ceased to exist by mid-1971. Iggy was scooped up by **Tony Defries**, who had formed the **MainMan Group of Companies** in 1972 — managing David Bowie, Mott the Hoople, Mick Ronson and Dana Gillespie ([Wikipedia — Tony Defries](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tony_Defries); [Madeline Bocaro — Raw Power: More Than Soul](https://madelinex.com/2023/02/07/raw-power-more-than-soul/)). Defries secured Iggy a solo deal with Columbia and moved him to London. The **only Stooges concert outside America** in this period was at London's **King's Cross Cinema** (later The Scala) on 15 July 1972 — roughly 200 people attended, most of them American journalists Defries had flown in to seed the story. The relationship would end badly: Defries lost patience after the advance was burned on drugs, and Columbia dropped the band shortly after release. But in autumn 1972, MainMan was the only reason there was a session at all.

## The new lineup — James Williamson on guitar, Ron Asheton demoted to bass

The single biggest musical fact about *Raw Power* is that **it is not the *Fun House* band**. Iggy had recruited a new guitarist, **James Williamson**, in late 1970 — a younger Ann Arbor player whose attack was sharper and more rhythmic than Ron Asheton's open-handed blues. Iggy and Williamson went to London first, intending to build a band from English players. When they failed to find a suitable rhythm section, Williamson suggested flying in the Asheton brothers ([Wikipedia — Raw Power](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Raw_Power)). Ron Asheton — the lead guitarist of *Fun House* — agreed only on the condition that he could play. The deal was that he would play **bass**, with Scott Asheton on drums. The band that made *Raw Power* is therefore the *Fun House* rhythm section, with one of rock's great lead guitarists demoted to bass, behind a new and uncompromising guitarist who had written most of the music with Iggy before they arrived. For ARLO: when an artist asks how *Raw Power* differs from *Fun House*, the answer is **a new guitarist with a sharper rhythmic vocabulary, the previous guitarist sidelined to bass, and a producer-frontman (Iggy) who had spent a year in a managed-artist orbit**.

## CBS Studios London, September–October 1972 — Mike Ross-Trevor, eight songs, one room

The sessions ran 10 September to 6 October 1972 at **CBS Studios London** with house engineer **Mike Ross-Trevor** ([Wikipedia — Raw Power](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Raw_Power); [Hi-Fi News — Iggy Pop: Raw Power Production Notes](https://www.hifinews.com/content/iggy-pop-raw-power-production-notes)). Earlier demo work was done at **RG Jones (Wimbledon)** and **Olympic Studios (Barnes)** before settling into CBS. Iggy was the credited producer on the tracking — 25, freshly clean, running the room. The eight finished songs were tracked largely live and quickly. Columbia executives had insisted on two ballads, one per side; "Gimme Danger" closes side one and "I Need Somebody" sits inside side two — the commercial concessions inside an otherwise punishing record. For ARLO: *Raw Power* is **not** a long-developed studio production. It is a fast, mostly-live tracking date in a major London room, made by a band reassembled three months earlier.

## James Williamson's rig — the 1969 Les Paul Custom into a Vox AC30 Top Boost

The most retrieval-worthy gear fact about *Raw Power* is that the entire electric guitar record is **a 1969 Gibson Les Paul Custom, bridge pickup only (a T-Top humbucker measuring roughly 7.27k DC resistance), into a Vox AC30 Top Boost with Bottom Cut, no pedals** ([guitar.com — The Guitar Gear Used on Raw Power](https://guitar.com/features/artist-rigs/the-guitar-gear-used-on-the-stooges-raw-power-album/); [Premier Guitar — James Williamson: Raw Power](https://www.premierguitar.com/artists/james-williamson-raw-power); [Lollar Pickups — Making of the Raw Power Humbucker](https://www.lollarguitars.com/blog/2022/03/james-williamson-and-the-making-of-the-raw-power-humbucker/)). Williamson had traded an SG, a Jaguar and some cash for the Cherry Burst Les Paul Custom before leaving Ann Arbor. He tried a Marshall stack first at CBS, found it overwhelming, and switched to the AC30 by experiment. The AC30 Top Boost run hard has a glassy upper-mid bite that registers cutting-but-not-fizzing on tape. **Bridge pickup almost exclusively. No pedals.** Acoustic parts (the figure under "Gimme Danger") were cut on a borrowed **Martin D-28**; a **Gibson B-25** acquired in London was used for writing. For ARLO: the *Raw Power* guitar tone is **humbucker bridge pickup into a non-master-volume British combo at full chat, no pedals, captured close on a fast tape date**.

## "Search and Destroy" — Vietnam machine-gun rhythm

Williamson on origin: "I came up with the rhythm imitating a machine gun, because this is the era of the Vietnam War, and the rest of the song was built around that figure" ([Songfacts — Search and Destroy](https://www.songfacts.com/facts/the-stooges/search-and-destroy); [Louder — Story Behind Search and Destroy](https://www.loudersound.com/features/the-story-behind-search-and-destroy-by-iggy-and-the-stooges)). The title comes from a *Time* magazine article on the U.S. military's search-and-destroy tactic that Iggy had read. The lyric is dense with napalm, firefights, A-bombs and radiation — Vietnam as backdrop to a self-portrait of Iggy as street-walking predator. It is also the **one song where Iggy's own mix was retained for the 1973 release**: Bowie remixed seven of the eight tracks, but Iggy's "Search and Destroy" mix was kept as he had done it ([Wikipedia — Raw Power](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Raw_Power)). It opens side one with the thinnest, hottest sound on the album — and it became the foundational punk recruiting poster, learned by Steve Jones of the Sex Pistols by (in his own account) taking speed and playing along to the record.

## The Bowie mix — one day at Western Sound Recorders, October 1972

After the CBS tracking dates wrapped, Iggy mixed the album himself. Defries and Columbia rejected that mix. Bowie — fresh off finishing *Ziggy Stardust* and Lou Reed's *Transformer* — stepped in. He did the remix in **one day** at a cheap Los Angeles studio called **Western Sound Recorders** in **October 1972**, on what he later described as an "Elvis type of board, old-tech, low-tech, in a poorly lit, cheap old studio" ([Wikipedia — Raw Power](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Raw_Power); [Madeline Bocaro — From Raw Power to Roar Power](https://madelinex.com/2018/02/07/raw-power-45th-anniversary/)). He used a **Cooper Time Cube** mechanical-delay device for echo. The result was a famously **thin** record: bright top end, bass often buried, lead guitar panned hard and isolated, drums smaller than the band remembered, Iggy's vocal dry and forward. Robert Christgau's 1981 capsule said it plainly — "first-generation Iggyphiles charged just as indignantly that David Bowie had mixed the real thing way too thin." Lester Bangs nevertheless heard "monomaniacal fury so genuine"; Dave Marsh called it "already the best album of the '70s." For ARLO: **Bowie's 1973 mix is the version of *Raw Power* that punk was actually built on**, and the version Williamson and Ron Asheton both later defended.

## The 1997 Iggy remix — "everything's still in the red"

In 1996, Legacy Recordings invited Iggy Pop to remix *Raw Power* for a CD reissue. He did it at **Sony Music Studios in New York**, released **22 April 1997** ([Wikipedia — Raw Power](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Raw_Power); [Madeline Bocaro](https://madelinex.com/2018/02/07/raw-power-45th-anniversary/)). The 1997 mix is the loudest commercial rock CD released to that point — integrated RMS reportedly around **−4 dB**, clipping audible across the program, a deliberate aesthetic choice. "Everything's still in the red, it's a very violent mix," Iggy said. "The proof's in the pudding." He pulled buried bass and vocal detail forward, restored grunts and ad-libs Bowie had pushed down, and ran the master into hard limiting. Critical reception split immediately. **Pitchfork rated it 10/10**. **Robert Christgau** approved of the "pumped bass and vocals Iggy has uncovered" and dismissed dissenters as "strict constructionists and lo-fi snobs." **Robert Quine** called it "atrocious." **James Williamson**: "I personally think [the remixed *Raw Power*] sucked... Bowie did a pretty good job." **Ron Asheton** said he liked the old Bowie mix better and noted Iggy "put back every single grunt, groan, and word" ([Tracking Angle — VMP Raw Power Reissue](https://trackingangle.com/music/iggy-pop-the-stooges-raw-power-vinyl-me-please-review); [David Bowie official site — Raw Power's Original Bowie Mix Reinstated](https://www.davidbowie.com/2010/2010/02/14/raw-powers-original-bowie-mix-reinstated)).

## The two mixes — a working summary for retrieval

- The **1973 Bowie mix** is thin, dry and bright. It under-pushes the bass and over-isolates the lead guitar. It is the historical document — the one punk was built on, the one Steve Jones learned guitar from. **Both surviving credited guitarists preferred this mix**. Bowie himself: "the version I still prefer over the later remix — it has more wound-up ferocity and chaos and, in my humble opinion, is a hallmark roots sound for what was later to become punk" ([David Bowie official site](https://www.davidbowie.com/2010/2010/02/14/raw-powers-original-bowie-mix-reinstated)).
- The **1997 Iggy remix** is loud, clipped, bass-and-vocal-forward. Iggy and Christgau/Pitchfork consider it the truer reading of what the band actually sounded like in the room. Quine and audiophile press consider it ear-fatiguing and over-compressed. **Neither mix has been retired**: the **2010 Legacy Edition** reinstated Bowie's 1973 mix; the **2012 Record Store Day** vinyl carried both mixes remastered (clipping-free) by Kevin Gray and Mark Wilder; the **2023 50th Anniversary Edition** carried both again.

For ARLO: when an artist asks "which mix is *Raw Power*," the honest answer is **both are** — Bowie is the historical document, Iggy is the band-defended sonic intent. The original Bowie mix has not been erased; the 1997 remix exists alongside it.

## The songs — eight in 34 minutes

**Side one.** "Search and Destroy" opens with Williamson's machine-gun rhythm figure and Iggy's first-line declaration of intent; "Gimme Danger" is the acoustic-led ballad, Martin D-28 audible under the lead, the Columbia-mandated mid-tempo concession; "Your Pretty Face Is Going to Hell" is sneering glam-meets-Detroit at full tilt; "Penetration" rides a Williamson riff that prefigures Steve Jones's entire vocabulary.

**Side two.** "Raw Power" is the title statement — Iggy's keyboard part audible under the guitars, the song that gives the record its name; "I Need Somebody" is the second mandated ballad, slow blues-skeleton played at glam tempo; "Shake Appeal" is the fastest thing on the record, the song that Ramones-era punk would extract whole; "Death Trip" closes the album at six minutes, the longest cut, Williamson stretching out into a sustained solo, Iggy howling a closing benediction.

## Commercial failure into canonical reversal

*Raw Power* charted on the Billboard 200 for **three weeks**, peaking at **#183** ([Wikipedia — Raw Power](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Raw_Power)). Columbia dropped the band almost immediately. The Stooges collapsed again in 1974; Iggy went into a rehab arc that ended only with *The Idiot* and *Lust for Life* (1977, with Bowie in Berlin). By the late 1970s *Raw Power* had been re-canonized: Steve Jones learned guitar by playing along on amphetamines; Johnny Marr cited it; Henry Rollins had "Search & Destroy" tattooed across his shoulder blades; Kurt Cobain listed it as his favorite album of all time in his journals. For ARLO: a record that sold poorly, was rejected by its label, and bombed commercially became, within a decade, the foundational template of punk rock — a useful counter-example to chart-driven readings of cultural impact.
