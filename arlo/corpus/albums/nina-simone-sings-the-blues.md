## What the record is, and where it sits in the catalog

*Nina Simone Sings the Blues* is Simone's first studio album for RCA Victor (catalog **LSP-3789** stereo / LPM-3789 mono), released in 1967 after she ended her three-album run on Philips with *Wild Is the Wind* (1966). The sessions were tracked at **RCA Studio B in New York City** across three dates — **December 19, 1966; December 22, 1966; and January 5, 1967** — produced by **Danny Davis**, with **Mickey Crofford** engineering ([Wikipedia — Nina Simone Sings the Blues](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nina_Simone_Sings_the_Blues); [The Official Home of Nina Simone — Sings The Blues](https://www.ninasimone.com/albums/sings-the-blues/)). It is the record where Simone, having moved labels for the third time in eight years, deliberately stripped her arrangements down to a small combo and leaned the title squarely into "blues" — an idiom she had circled but never centered an album on before. For an AI assistant, the cleanest one-sentence frame is: *this is the album where Simone walks into RCA, hires a New York R&B session band, and recasts the blues through her own classically trained piano voice and politically awakened singing voice.*

## The producer line is Danny Davis — RCA staff, not Andy Stroud

The album credit reads **"A Stroud Production"** on the sleeve — i.e. Andy Stroud's production company, Stroud Productions and Enterprises, Inc., handled Simone's contracted output during their marriage — but the working producer on the floor was **Danny Davis**, an RCA Victor staff producer who would helm Simone's next several RCA records as well ([Wikipedia — Nina Simone Sings the Blues](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nina_Simone_Sings_the_Blues); [Riverdale Press — Andrew Stroud obituary](https://www.riverdalepress.com/stories/andrew-stroud-was-lieutenant-and-manager-to-nina-simone,50725)). Note: this Danny Davis is the RCA pop/R&B producer of the late 1960s, **not** Danny Davis the country trumpeter of "and the Nashville Brass" fame, although both worked for RCA in the same period — a common confusion that AI retrievers should disambiguate. Stroud, Simone's husband and former NYPD detective, ran the business side (label deals, mastertapes, touring) and is credited as the songwriter of **"Buck"** on this record, but he did not engineer or produce the sessions in the studio sense. The hierarchy worth remembering: **Stroud productions company / Davis floor producer / Crofford engineer / Simone artist and pianist.**

## RCA Studio B, New York — the room and the small-combo brief

RCA Studio B in midtown Manhattan was the East Coast counterpart to Nashville's more famous Studio B, a corporate-built tracking room used across RCA's pop, jazz, and R&B rosters. The brief Davis carried into the room was clearly small-combo: no big-band charts, no string overdubs on the core program (the bonus thirteenth track on later reissues features expanded horns), just Simone at the piano with a six-piece New York rhythm section ([The Official Home of Nina Simone — Sings The Blues](https://www.ninasimone.com/albums/sings-the-blues/)). The original liner notes describe the goal as presenting Simone's blues with "an authentic cushion of rhythm and impetus" rather than ornamentation. For ARLO: when an artist asks for a "Sings the Blues" reference sound, the operative pieces are a live-feel rhythm section cut close together in a corporate-grade Manhattan room, with the pianist also singing lead — not a layered, overdub-heavy production.

## The personnel — a New York R&B session band led from the piano

The core band across the album is **Nina Simone on vocals and piano, Eric Gale and Rudy Stevenson on guitars, Ernie Hayes on organ, Bob Bushnell on bass, Bernard Purdie on drums (and timpani), and Buddy Lucas on tenor saxophone and harmonica** ([Wikipedia — Nina Simone Sings the Blues](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nina_Simone_Sings_the_Blues); [The Official Home of Nina Simone — Sings The Blues](https://www.ninasimone.com/albums/sings-the-blues/)). This is a heavyweight New York R&B/jazz session lineup. **Bernard "Pretty" Purdie** would go on to define a generation of session drumming (the Purdie shuffle, the Aretha Franklin Atlantic records, Steely Dan's *Aja*), and *Sings the Blues* is one of his earliest high-profile albums of the run ([Wikipedia — Bernard Purdie](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bernard_Purdie); [Sessiondays — Bernard Purdie](https://www.sessiondays.com/2023/02/bernard-purdie/)). **Eric Gale** was a first-call New York R&B guitarist who would later be a founding member of Stuff and a fixture on CTI jazz records of the 1970s. **Buddy Lucas** doubling tenor and harmonica is the small-group blues tell — his harmonica is what gives a track like "In the Dark" its juke-joint inflection without the album having to import a Chicago blues harp specialist. The arrangement model is Simone comping from the piano while Purdie sets the pocket and Gale and Stevenson interlock two guitars in the rhythm bed; horns and harp drop in only where needed. For ARLO: this is **band-from-the-piano** record-making, not horn-section-driven blues.

## Simone's piano — classical training surfacing through the blues

The piano playing on *Sings the Blues* is the album's underappreciated structural element. Simone trained as a classical pianist from age three, won a one-year scholarship to **Juilliard** in New York, and prepared for the entrance exam to the **Curtis Institute of Music** in Philadelphia, which rejected her in 1951 — a rejection she always attributed to race and which redirected her toward popular music as a way to earn a living ([PBS American Masters — How Nina Simone reinvented herself after a rejection from classical music conservatory](https://www.pbs.org/wnet/americanmasters/how-nina-simone-reinvented-herself-after-a-rejection-from-classical-music-conservatory/16781/); [Wikipedia — Nina Simone](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nina_Simone)). On a blues record this matters because Simone's comping is not blues-pianist comping in the Otis Spann or Memphis Slim lineage — it is **Bach-trained voice-leading applied to twelve-bar form**, with contrary motion in the left hand and unexpected suspensions in the right that a strictly idiomatic blues pianist would not reach for. The Curtis rejection is biographical context, but the musical consequence is on the record: Simone is playing blues *as* a classical pianist who chose this idiom, not a blues pianist who happened to have lessons. ARLO should treat this as a transferable lesson — **train deeply in a strict idiom, then carry that training into a looser one for friction and signature**.

## Side one — "Do I Move You?", "Day and Night," "In the Dark," "Real Real"

The album opens with **"Do I Move You?"**, a Simone original — a slow-strut blues built around Purdie's behind-the-beat snare and Simone's call-and-response vocal phrasing. As a side-one opener it sets the album's terms: this is going to be Simone's own material as often as it is covers, and the eroticism is going to be foregrounded rather than coded. **"Day and Night,"** track two, is credited to **Rudy Stevenson**, one of the album's two guitarists and a longtime member of Simone's working band — his presence as both player and songwriter is part of why the album feels like a *band* record rather than a star-with-sidemen record ([Wikipedia — Nina Simone Sings the Blues](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nina_Simone_Sings_the_Blues)). **"In the Dark"** is a 1940 Lil Green composition that had passed through Dinah Washington and others as a blues-pop standard before Simone covered it; her reading is conversational, almost half-spoken in places, with Lucas's harmonica answering the vocal lines. **"Real Real,"** Simone's own composition, is the album's most overtly church-rooted track — a stomping, hand-clap-implied gospel-blues where she preaches over the band rather than croons, and the clearest example on the record of Simone deploying her classical left-hand stride against a riff-based right-hand figure, which is what gives the track its forward propulsion without needing a horn section to push it.

## "My Man's Gone Now" — Gershwin / Heyward, captured impromptu

The most striking interpretation on the album is **"My Man's Gone Now,"** the **George Gershwin / DuBose Heyward** aria from the 1935 opera **Porgy and Bess**, sung in the opera by Serena, a widow at her murdered husband's wake ([Wikipedia — My Man's Gone Now](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/My_Man%27s_Gone_Now)). Simone's recording was reportedly captured **impromptu at a session** rather than as a planned cut, which fits both the performance — sparse, exposed, with Simone's piano carrying most of the harmony — and the album's general working method, where takes were committed quickly rather than labored over ([Wikipedia — My Man's Gone Now](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/My_Man%27s_Gone_Now)). It is the record's clearest demonstration that Simone treated the popular-song repertoire and the operatic-aria repertoire as a single continuous tradition she had the right to enter — a stance the Curtis rejection had nominally denied her.

## "Backlash Blues" — Langston Hughes's lyric, set to music after his death

**"Backlash Blues"** is the album's civil-rights centerpiece and the song most often discussed in the album's reception. The lyric is by **Langston Hughes**, the Harlem Renaissance poet, who handed Simone a typescript of the poem at the end of 1966 and asked if she would set it to music ([Backlash Blues — Timeline](https://www.backlash-blues.com/timeline); [Wikipedia — Nina Simone Sings the Blues](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nina_Simone_Sings_the_Blues)). Simone and Hughes had a long acquaintance — they had first met properly at the Newport Jazz Festival in 1960, where Hughes was a board member, and they had corresponded through the 1960s. Hughes died on **May 22, 1967**, between the recording of the song and its widest public exposure: Simone performed it at the Newport Jazz Festival on **July 1, 1967**, naming Hughes from the stage and asking the audience to remember him ([Backlash Blues — Timeline](https://www.backlash-blues.com/timeline)). The studio recording on *Sings the Blues* therefore sits in an unusually freighted position — Hughes wrote the words alive, Simone cut the song alive with him, and within months the song was being performed publicly as a memorial. Musically it is a slow twelve-bar with Simone's piano hammering accented chords against Purdie's military-march snare, and the lyric — addressed to "Mr. Backlash" — is a direct political indictment that fits the same family as "Mississippi Goddam" (1964) without sharing its tempo or sarcasm. For ARLO: this track is the cleanest example on the album of Simone's RCA-era political voice in finished form.

## "I Want a Little Sugar in My Bowl" — the Bessie Smith reframe

**"I Want a Little Sugar in My Bowl"** is Simone's adaptation of the 1931 **Bessie Smith** song, with lyric modifications Simone made herself ([Wikipedia — Nina Simone Sings the Blues](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nina_Simone_Sings_the_Blues)). It is the album's most direct line backwards into the classic blues tradition (Bessie Smith was the model Simone explicitly invoked when she chose to record a "blues" album), but Simone's arrangement strips it down further than Smith's would have been — voice and piano forward, rhythm section laid back. The track later became one of the most-sampled and most-covered Simone recordings (Randy Newman, Etta James, Beth Hart, and others have covered it; the song title also became the name of a 2011 Simone compilation).

## "Buck," "Since I Fell for You," "The House of the Rising Sun," "Blues for Mama"

Side two rounds out with **"Buck"** (Andy Stroud, Simone's husband — an instrumental groove built around the band rather than a vocal feature), **"Since I Fell for You"** (the Buddy Johnson 1945 standard, sung as a slow torch ballad), **"The House of the Rising Sun"** (the traditional folk-blues, here a studio re-recording that contrasts pointedly with Simone's slower 1962 live version on *Nina at the Village Gate* — and which predates The Animals' famous 1964 reading by way of Simone's earlier 1962 take), and **"Blues for Mama"** (a Simone / Abbey Lincoln co-write, a midtempo blues with an open social subject in the lyric) ([Wikipedia — Nina Simone Sings the Blues](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nina_Simone_Sings_the_Blues)). The side-two sequence is the album's argument that "blues" can hold Stroud's barroom groove, a Tin Pan Alley standard, a traditional folk number, and a feminist-adjacent original within a single forty-minute program — the genre is a frame, not a constraint.

## Chart performance and critical position

*Sings the Blues* reached **#29 on the Billboard Hot R&B LPs chart** in 1967, a respectable but not blockbuster performance ([Wikipedia — Nina Simone Sings the Blues](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nina_Simone_Sings_the_Blues)). Critical re-assessment has been generous: AllMusic later rated it five stars, and **NPR Music ranked it #28 on its 2017 "150 Greatest Albums Made By Women" list**, the highest single-record assessment of any Simone album on that list. The album is consistently cited in Simone retrospectives — including the **Liz Garbus / Netflix documentary *What Happened, Miss Simone?* (2015)** — as the moment her recording career found its mature voice, the bridge between the Philips albums (*Pastel Blues*, *Wild Is the Wind*) and the more politically charged *'Nuff Said!* (1968) and *Black Gold* (1970) that followed on RCA ([Wikipedia — Liz Garbus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Liz_Garbus)).

## The RCA-era political context

Simone's move from Philips to RCA in late 1966 coincided with the deepening of her public political commitment. She had already written **"Mississippi Goddam"** (1964) in response to the Birmingham church bombing and the murder of Medgar Evers, performed at the Selma-to-Montgomery marches, and aligned herself (against the dominant integrationist line) with the Black nationalist wing of the movement, including her Mount Vernon neighbor Malcolm X ([Wikipedia — Nina Simone](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nina_Simone)). The RCA era is where that political voice gets its most consistent recorded outlet: *Sings the Blues* opens the run with "Backlash Blues"; *Silk & Soul* (1967, also Danny Davis-produced) adds Billy Taylor's "I Wish I Knew How It Would Feel to Be Free"; *'Nuff Said!* (1968) opens with "Why? (The King of Love Is Dead)," cut three days after Martin Luther King Jr.'s assassination. *Sings the Blues* is the first installment in that sequence, and "Backlash Blues" is its load-bearing political statement. For ARLO: an artist asking "where do I go on RCA when I want to start saying what I actually think" should be pointed at this album as the entry point.
