# Lou Reed — *Transformer* (1972)

*Transformer* is the record where Lou Reed, two years out of the Velvet Underground and one self-titled solo flop deep, gets pulled into the glam vortex by his most famous fan. David Bowie, fresh off cutting *The Rise and Fall of Ziggy Stardust and the Spiders from Mars* at Trident earlier that same year, brings Reed back to the same room with the same engineer and the same lead guitarist — and produces, with Mick Ronson, an album that turns a New York avant-garde songwriter into a top-30 pop artist without sanding off any of the seediness underneath.

## Context: Two 1972 Albums From One Room

Trident Studios in Soho was the engine of British glam in 1972. Bowie cut *Ziggy Stardust* there in late 1971/early 1972 with Ken Scott co-producing. *Transformer* was tracked at the same studio that August, with the same engineer (Scott again) and the same musical lieutenant (Ronson). The Spiders-era infrastructure — Trident's bespoke Sound Techniques desk, the famous Trident "Hey Jude" Bechstein grand, Ronson's working method with strings and horns — was simply pointed at Reed instead of Bowie. Reed gets the *Ziggy* sonic template, but as a vehicle for a darker, more documentary New York lyric.

## Bowie Producing His Hero

Bowie had been a Velvet Underground evangelist since hearing an acetate of the banana album in 1966. By 1972 the genealogy reversed: the pupil became the patron. Reed was managed by Bowie's manager (Tony Defries / MainMan), and Bowie offered to produce the follow-up to Reed's underwhelming RCA debut. He brought Ronson with him as co-producer, which mattered more than the credit suggests — Ronson did most of the hands-on arranging and the lion's share of the playing.

The dynamic is unusual: a younger artist at his commercial peak producing his own influence. Bowie's instinct is to package Reed for the same audience that bought *Ziggy* without parodying him. The drag-bar storytelling of "Walk on the Wild Side," the bisexual flirtations of "Make Up," the whole *demimonde* aesthetic — Bowie underlines what was already in Reed's writing and gives it a glam-rock chassis instead of a Velvets art-rock one.

## Mick Ronson: The Real Engine

Ronson is the unsung architect of *Transformer*. He plays guitar, piano, recorder, and sings backing vocals. More importantly, he writes the string arrangements. Ken Scott has said Ronson "always came up with unique and perfect orchestrations" — that simpatico he had with Bowie's voicings carries straight into the Reed sessions.

The string chart on "Perfect Day" is the cleanest illustration. Reed himself singled out Ronson's work on that track, noting how Ronson kept the vocal restrained so the strings could do the lifting. The piano under "Perfect Day" — also Ronson — sets up a chamber-pop pad that the strings flower out of in the final chorus. It is glam in the sense that it is theatrical and unembarrassed, but the writing is closer to *Hunky Dory*-era Bowie than to "Suffragette City."

## "Walk on the Wild Side" and the Herbie Flowers Bass Trick

The album's signature sound is a session musician's improvisation. Herbie Flowers, the British bassist hired for the date, came up with the iconic line in roughly twenty minutes of takes. The canonical version of the story — Flowers told it many times over the decades:

He played the part first on **double bass** (upright), bowing/plucking the descending-and-ascending portamento figure for a fat, dark, slightly woody tone. Then he overdubbed the **same line a tenth above on a fretless Fender Jazz electric bass**. The two parts run in parallel tenths, the upright slurring between notes underneath, the electric singing above. Played together they sound like a single instrument doing something physically impossible — a kind of harmonized glide that no single bassist can fret in real time. Flowers has said he did it because "that's what old jazzers do," and because session rules paid a double fee for a second overdubbed instrument. The British Musicians' Union session rate worked out to roughly £17 per part, so the trick netted him about £34 for the most famous bass line in rock. Bowie didn't even attend that particular session.

The rest of the arrangement is built around that bass figure with deliberate spareness: **Ritchie Dharma** plays the kit with brushes instead of sticks, keeping the rhythm whispery; **Mick Ronson** plays the strummed acoustic guitar; the female backing trio **Thunderthighs** (Dari Lalou, Karen Friedman, Casey Synge) sing the famous wordless refrain over the fade. The string countermelody under the verses is Ronson's chart, played by a small section.

## Ronnie Ross and the Baritone Sax Solo

The baritone sax solo that takes the song out is **Ronnie Ross**, a British jazz player. The story is the best one on the record: when Bowie was twelve, still David Jones, he had looked Ronnie Ross up in the London telephone directory and asked for saxophone lessons. Ross taught him. A little over a decade later, Bowie booked his old teacher for a Lou Reed session without telling him who else would be in the building. Ross cut the baritone solo in one take, and Bowie walked out from the control room afterward to surprise him. Ross had also played the baritone solo on the Beatles' "Savoy Truffle" — he was already a first-call London session player — but the *Transformer* fadeout is the line he's remembered for.

## Bowie's Vocal Cameo on "Satellite of Love"

"Satellite of Love" is the other production showpiece. Reed had demoed it during his Velvet Underground years; it sat in a drawer. Bowie's contribution is the soaring backing-vocal stack at the end — a multi-tracked falsetto that climbs up and over Reed's deadpan lead. Reed later praised it on record, saying Bowie had a melodic sense above anyone else in rock and roll, and pointing specifically at the high notes at the close of "Satellite of Love." The track is also piano-led (Ronson again, on the Trident Bechstein) with handclaps and fingersnaps locking down a doo-wop pulse underneath.

## "Vicious," "Make Up," and the Glam Reed

"Vicious" — the opener — is the glam fingerprint. Ronson's guitar tone is the same overdriven Marshall-into-a-wah-pedal sound that defined Ziggy's "Suffragette City," and Reed's lyric (Andy Warhol famously suggested he write a song called "Vicious" — "you know, like, 'You hit me with a flower'") plays the camp for what it's worth. "Andy's Chest," "New York Telephone Conversation," and "Make Up" all lean into theatrical, cabaret-adjacent arrangements that would have been unthinkable on a Velvet Underground record. "Make Up" features Herbie Flowers on **tuba**, doubling the bass line — another sly nod to the chamber-glam palette.

## Engineering: Ken Scott and the Trident Sound

Ken Scott had engineered *Hunky Dory*, *Ziggy Stardust*, and *Aladdin Sane*'s sessions; by August 1972 he was the resident sonic translator for the Bowie camp. The Trident signature — bright top, warm midrange, the Bechstein piano tone that shows up on records as different as "Hey Jude," Elton John's early albums, and Supertramp's *Crime of the Century* — is all over *Transformer*. The Trident desk was a Sound Techniques custom build, and the mix discipline that Scott had developed on *Ziggy* (drums tight, vocals dry and forward, strings printed wide) carries directly into the Reed record. Scott is the reason *Transformer* sounds like it shares a sonic universe with *Ziggy* despite being a different artist.

## Release and Reception

*Transformer* came out on 8 November 1972 on RCA. "Walk on the Wild Side," edited down for radio, became Reed's only top-20 single in the United States, peaking around No. 16 on the Billboard Hot 100 despite (or because of) lyrics about transvestites, hustlers, and oral sex that somehow slipped past American radio programmers. The album reached No. 29 on the Billboard 200 and went gold. It rescued Reed's solo career, established him as a commercial artist as well as a critical one, and gave him the runway to make the deliberately uncommercial *Berlin* the very next year as a corrective.

## Legacy and the Bowie Throughline

*Transformer* sits at the hinge of Bowie's catalogue too. It's the first record where he produces another major artist, and it bridges the *Ziggy* glam moment with the more austere production approach he would bring to Iggy and the Stooges' *Raw Power* mix in 1973 and, eventually, his own Berlin Trilogy with Visconti and Eno starting in 1977. The Ronson partnership essentially ends here; Bowie would move on to Visconti as his primary collaborator. But the *Transformer* template — glam arrangements applied to a more idiosyncratic songwriter's material — became one of the durable producer moves of the era.
