# Beastie Boys — *Paul's Boutique* (1989)

**Artist:** Beastie Boys (Mike D / Michael Diamond, MCA / Adam Yauch, Ad-Rock / Adam Horovitz)
**Year:** 1989 (released 25 July 1989, Capitol Records)
**Producers:** The Dust Brothers (Mike Simpson / "E.Z. Mike" and John King / "King Gizmo") with the Beastie Boys; Matt Dike credited as co-producer on most tracks
**Engineer:** Mario Caldato Jr. ("Mario C") — his first album with the Beasties; began the long-term association that would run through *Check Your Head*, *Ill Communication*, and *Hello Nasty*
**Studios:** Matt Dike's apartment in Los Angeles (the bulk of the tracking and sample assembly) and Record Plant Studios, Los Angeles (mixing); a small amount of work done at Adam Yauch's Koreatown apartment ("The Opium Den") for "A Year and a Day"
**Scope:** album-as-artifact — the canonical pre-lawsuit sample-collage record, with roughly 105 documented samples woven across fifteen tracks ([Wikipedia, *Paul's Boutique*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Paul%27s_Boutique))
**Source:** Deep-research synthesis (2026-05) — verify before treating as authoritative; sample claims cross-checked against WhoSampled and the paulsboutique.info reference site
**Aesthetic-stack relevance:** sample-based hip-hop production, dense-layered collage as compositional form, hardware-sampling lineage, the moment before clearance economics rewrote the genre
**Tags:** `beastie-boys`, `dust-brothers`, `pauls-boutique`, `sample-collage`, `pre-lawsuit-sampling`, `hip-hop-production`, `mario-caldato`, `matt-dike`

## The Session Story In One Paragraph

*Paul's Boutique* is the record nobody on the label wanted. After parting from Def Jam and Rick Rubin's minimalist-rock-and-rap formula on *Licensed to Ill* (1986), the Beastie Boys moved to Los Angeles, signed to Capitol, and fell in with Matt Dike (co-founder of Delicious Vinyl) and the Dust Brothers — Mike Simpson and John King, two crate-digging UCLA-era producers assembling beats out of Simpson's record collection in Dike's apartment. The Dust Brothers' instrumentals had largely been intended as a club-DJ record under their own name; the Beasties heard the dense backing tracks and asked the producers to keep them dense rather than thin them out for vocals ([Wikipedia, *Paul's Boutique*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Paul%27s_Boutique); [KEXP, Mike Simpson interview](https://www.kexp.org/read/2015/7/25/inside-pauls-boutique-interview-with-mike-simpson-of-the-dust-brothers/)). Roughly seventy percent of the album's beds were built before the Beasties wrote a single vocal over them. Mario Caldato Jr. engineered, the team mixed at Record Plant LA, the record dropped in July 1989, and the label was disappointed — *Licensed to Ill* had sold five million; *Paul's Boutique* peaked at #14 on the Billboard 200 and went gold within months but was, in the contemporary commercial frame, a flop ([Wikipedia, *Paul's Boutique*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Paul%27s_Boutique)). Critical reassessment over the following decade reversed the verdict, and the record is now routinely listed among the greatest hip-hop albums ever made.

## The Sample Count, Carefully Stated

The headline figure — "about 105 samples" — comes from coverage drawing on WhoSampled's track-by-track documentation and the fan-maintained paulsboutique.info reference site, crowd-compiling sources since roughly 1993 ([paulsboutique.info](https://paulsboutique.info/); [The Vinyl Factory](https://www.thevinylfactory.com/features/beastie-boys-pauls-boutique-samples)). The number is approximate because what counts as a "sample" is itself ambiguous: looped breaks, single-hit drum sounds, vocal stabs, spoken-word interludes, and film-score quotations all live on a continuum. The clean version: *more than a hundred discrete source recordings, documented and verified on WhoSampled, layered across fifteen tracks*. The closing "B-Boy Bouillabaisse" — a nine-part suite — accounts for roughly two dozen of those. The widely repeated "$250,000 in sample-clearance costs" figure reflects the era's relatively friendly clearance climate; what cost a quarter-million in 1989 would be impossible at any price after 1991 ([Wikipedia, *Paul's Boutique*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Paul%27s_Boutique)).

## The Hardware Reality — Emax HD, Not SP-1200

A common piece of *Paul's Boutique* lore is that the record was built on the E-mu SP-1200, the canonical late-1980s hip-hop sampler. That is **not** what the production team used. Both Dust Brothers have stated in interviews that the bulk of the sampling was done on an **E-mu Emax HD**, an earlier E-mu rack sampler with a built-in hard drive and considerably more memory than the SP-1200's notoriously short 10-second sample time ([Inside Paul's Boutique: The Drum Machine That Helped Open It, 333Sound](https://333sound.com/the-drum-machine-that-helped-open-pauls-boutique/)). Sequencing ran out of a piece of early Mac software called **Texture**, with a **JL Cooper PPS1** sync box bridging the computer clock to multitrack tape, plus an early PC controlling the sampler at "about four seconds of memory" per slot in John King's recollection — turntables and a four-track (later eight-track) reel-to-reel rounded out the rig in Matt Dike's apartment ([KEXP, John King interview](https://www.kexp.org/read/2015/7/24/inside-pauls-boutique-interview-john-king-dust-brothers/)).

The distinction matters for the ARLO corpus. The SP-1200's signature is short, crunchy, grit-baked-in 12-bit samples whose voice is itself a sound. The Emax HD has a cleaner, longer voice; what gives *Paul's Boutique* its texture is not converter character but **layering density** and **tape generation loss** through the multitrack and mixdown stages. Cross-link to the generic hardware-sampling vocabulary in `corpus/techniques/hardware-sampling-workflow.md` — the *Paul's Boutique* sessions sit a generation upstream of the OP-1 / Octatrack / Digitakt family but share the same core moves: commit a sample to a key, layer it against other committed samples, bounce, treat the bounce as a new source.

## The Layering Method — Beats Built Before Vocals

The Dust Brothers' working method inverts the typical producer-rapper sequence. Most of the album's instrumentals existed as **finished megamixes** before the Beasties wrote a single line over them ([KEXP, Mike Simpson interview](https://www.kexp.org/read/2015/7/25/inside-pauls-boutique-interview-with-mike-simpson-of-the-dust-brothers/); [KEXP, John King interview](https://www.kexp.org/read/2015/7/24/inside-pauls-boutique-interview-john-king-dust-brothers/)). Simpson and King had been making club-DJ-style collage tracks out of Simpson's roughly 500-record personal collection; the intent was a Dust Brothers club record. The Beasties specifically asked the producers to keep the density rather than thin it out for vocal space.

The compositional consequence is that the vocals on *Paul's Boutique* often sit *inside* the collage rather than on top of it. Drums, basslines, horn stabs, film dialogue, and lyrical references all coexist at similar prominence. This is the move that made the album commercially difficult at release and aesthetically durable in retrospect.

## Track-Level Samples — A Verified Selection

The following are individual sample claims documented on WhoSampled and the paulsboutique.info reference site. The track-level lists below are partial — most of these tracks have between five and fifteen documented sources each — but every claim here is corroborated by at least one independent reference.

**"Shake Your Rump"** — the opening single. Documented sources include Alphonse Mouzon's "Funky Snakefoot," Ronnie Laws's "Tell Me Something Good," Rose Royce's "6 O'Clock DJ (Let's Rock)," "Born to Love You," and "Yo Yo," Afrika Bambaataa & the Jazzy 5's "Jazzy Sensation (Bronx Version)," Funky 4 + 1's "That's the Joint," Sugarhill Gang's "8th Wonder," Harvey Scales's "Dancing Room Only," and Foxy's "Get Off" ([WhoSampled, Shake Your Rump samples](https://www.whosampled.com/Beastie-Boys/Shake-Your-Rump/samples/); [The Vinyl Factory](https://www.thevinylfactory.com/features/beastie-boys-pauls-boutique-samples)).

**"Hey Ladies"** — the album's lead single. The unmistakable groove is built on the Commodores' "Machine Gun" (1974), with the central break sitting around the 1:37 mark of the original ([WhoSampled, Hey Ladies sample of Machine Gun](https://www.whosampled.com/sample/1457/Beastie-Boys-Hey-Ladies-Commodores-Machine-Gun/); [The Vinyl Factory](https://www.thevinylfactory.com/features/beastie-boys-pauls-boutique-samples)).

**"Shadrach"** — built around Sly & the Family Stone's "Loose Booty," with additional documented samples from James Brown's "Funky Drummer," Ballin' Jack's "Never Let 'Em Say," Black Oak Arkansas's "Hot and Nasty," Rose Royce's "Do Your Dance," AC/DC's "For Those About to Rock (We Salute You)," and the Fatback Band's "King Tim III (Personality Jock)." Sly & the Family Stone's "Loose Booty" itself samples a slice of Louis Armstrong's 1931 "Shadrack," giving the track a chain of title-lineage three generations deep ([WhoSampled, Shadrach samples](https://www.whosampled.com/Beastie-Boys/Shadrach/samples/)).

**"The Sounds of Science"** — the Beatles-centric track. Documented samples include "The End," "Sgt. Pepper's Lonely Hearts Club Band," "Sgt. Pepper's Lonely Hearts Club Band (Reprise)," "When I'm Sixty-Four," and "Back in the U.S.S.R.," plus additional sources from Isaac Hayes, James Brown, Pato Banton, Boogie Down Productions, and Ray Charles ([WhoSampled, The Sounds of Science samples](https://www.whosampled.com/Beastie-Boys/The-Sounds-of-Science/samples/)).

**"Egg Man"** — built on Curtis Mayfield's "Superfly," with Tower of Power's "Drop It in the Slot," Sly & the Family Stone's "Dance to the Music," Elvis Costello & the Attractions' "Pump It Up," Bernard Herrmann's *Psycho* score ("The Murder"), and John Williams's *Jaws* main title quoted in the closing horror-movie montage ([WhoSampled, Egg Man samples](https://www.whosampled.com/Beastie-Boys/Egg-Man/samples/)).

**"High Plains Drifter"** — driven almost entirely by the Eagles' "Those Shoes" from *The Long Run* (1979). The bass-and-drum bed of "Those Shoes" runs underneath nearly the entire Beastie Boys track ([WhoSampled, High Plains Drifter sample of Those Shoes](https://www.whosampled.com/sample/1406/Beastie-Boys-High-Plains-Drifter-Eagles-Those-Shoes/); [Reverb, 9 Iconic Samples from Paul's Boutique](https://reverb.com/news/9-iconic-samples-from-the-beastie-boys-pauls-boutique)).

**"Looking Down the Barrel of a Gun"** — built on Mountain's "Mississippi Queen" (1970) and the Incredible Bongo Band's "Last Bongo in Belgium." This is also the track on the record with the most live instrumentation; Adam Horovitz played guitar and Adam Yauch played bass over the sample bed ([WhoSampled, Looking Down the Barrel of a Gun samples](https://www.whosampled.com/Beastie-Boys/Looking-Down-the-Barrel-of-a-Gun/)).

**"B-Boy Bouillabaisse"** — the nine-part closing suite that functions as the album's sample-density showcase. Documented sources include the Isley Brothers' "That Lady," Jimi Hendrix's "Are You Experienced?," Idris Muhammad's "Loran's Dance," the Crusaders' "The Well's Gone Dry," the Meters' "Hey Pocky A-Way," and many more across its run ([WhoSampled, B-Boy Bouillabaisse samples](https://www.whosampled.com/Beastie-Boys/B-Boy-Bouillabaisse/samples/)).

**"To All the Girls"** — the brief album opener, built on Idris Muhammad's "Loran's Dance" ([WhoSampled, To All the Girls](https://www.whosampled.com/Beastie-Boys/To-All-the-Girls/)).

What ARLO should *not* do with this list: extrapolate beyond it. The corpus is full of plausible-sounding *Paul's Boutique* sample claims that are not actually corroborated on WhoSampled — Led Zeppelin's "When the Levee Breaks" and James Brown's "Funky President" come up repeatedly in fan-forum lore in contexts that are not always supported by the primary databases. Treat any specific sample assertion as a citation that needs WhoSampled corroboration before it ships.

## Matt Dike's Apartment Was The Studio

The physical reality matters. The bulk of tracking happened in **Matt Dike's apartment** in Los Angeles, with the Dust Brothers' equipment, Simpson's record collection, and the Beasties writing vocals in the same room ([KEXP, John King interview](https://www.kexp.org/read/2015/7/24/inside-pauls-boutique-interview-john-king-dust-brothers/); [Mario Caldato Jr. Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mario_Caldato_Jr.)). Mixing happened at Record Plant LA. "A Year and a Day" was tracked at Adam Yauch's Koreatown apartment, which the Beasties nicknamed "The Opium Den" ([Wikipedia, *Paul's Boutique*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Paul%27s_Boutique)).

The G-Son studio in Atwater Village — the Beasties' famous purpose-built loft — came **after** *Paul's Boutique*. Caldato's pitch during the *Paul's Boutique* sessions was that the band should buy their own gear and stop paying studio hourly rates. That pitch became *Check Your Head* (1992) ([The Eastsider, Beastie Boys Studio in Atwater Village](https://www.theeastsiderla.com/neighborhoods/atwater_village/the-beastie-boys-studio-in-atwater-village/article_738d0918-2109-11ee-9a1b-3332a9669401.html)). For ARLO: *Paul's Boutique* is the apartment-and-borrowed-studio record; *Check Your Head* is the own-studio record.

## Mario Caldato Jr. — Why The Engineer Matters

Mario Caldato Jr.'s first Beastie Boys credit is *Paul's Boutique*. By 1988 he had already engineered apartment-recorded tracks for Tone Lōc and Young MC out of Matt Dike's setup. The skill Caldato brought was **making a maximally dense collage hold together in mixdown** ([Mario Caldato Jr. Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mario_Caldato_Jr.)). Records this dense fall apart in two predictable ways — frequency masking and stereo-image collapse from layered mono sources crowding center. *Paul's Boutique* avoids both. The mix has aged unusually well because Caldato's discipline gave each documented source enough room to be heard individually while the collage as a whole reads as a single fabric. Cross-link to `corpus/techniques/dense-but-clear-mixing.md` — *Paul's Boutique* is one of the canonical examples, made on a sample-collage record rather than a band record.

## The Transition From *Licensed to Ill*

The *Licensed to Ill* (1986) production model was Rick Rubin's: stripped-down 808/SP-12 beats, big rock-guitar samples, vocals pushed forward, deliberately minimalist arrangements. The move to LA, the parting from Def Jam, and the encounter with the Dust Brothers is the genre-pivot moment. *Paul's Boutique* moves from **subtractive** to **additive** as the core production verb. *Licensed to Ill* asks "what is the smallest, hardest-hitting bed this rap can sit on?" *Paul's Boutique* asks "how many sources can we layer before the listener loses the thread?" Both questions are legitimate; the answers are aesthetic opposites — a transferable creative move ARLO should recognize when a user feels their production is too sparse, too dense, too referential, or too clean.

## The Lawsuit Clock — 1989 vs. 1991

*Paul's Boutique* was released in July 1989. **Grand Upright Music, Ltd. v. Warner Bros. Records Inc.** — Gilbert O'Sullivan's suit against Biz Markie for sampling "Alone Again (Naturally)" — was decided in December 1991, with Judge Kevin Duffy opening his opinion with the words "Thou shalt not steal" and ruling that uncleared sampling constituted copyright infringement ([Wikipedia, Grand Upright Music v. Warner Bros.](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Grand_Upright_Music%2C_Ltd._v._Warner_Bros._Records_Inc.)). The ruling transformed sample-clearance economics within months: where Dust Brothers-style records had cleared a hundred-plus samples for low five- and six-figure sums, post-Grand Upright every individual sample required individual negotiation, and rights-holders began demanding up to 100% of a new song's royalties for a single use. The downstream effect was that Bomb Squad-style and Dust Brothers-style records became economically impossible. Producers either pared sample count down to one or two heavily-cleared sources or moved toward **interpolation** — re-recording the sampled element to license only the composition rather than the master recording.

*Paul's Boutique* sits inside the closing window — roughly 30 months between its release and the Grand Upright ruling. The Bomb Squad's work for Public Enemy (*Fear of a Black Planet*, 1990) and De La Soul's *3 Feet High and Rising* (1989) live in the same bracket. After 1991, this kind of record stops being made. That historical bracket is most of why *Paul's Boutique* now reads as a canonical reference — it is not just a great record but a record from a creative possibility-space that no longer exists.

## Why ARLO Should Treat This As Album-As-Methodology

Transferable lessons from *Paul's Boutique* — even outside hip-hop:

- **Build the bed first.** Instrumentals existed in finished form before vocals were written. Useful when a producer wants to draw a specific kind of performance out of a vocalist.
- **Density as a design choice.** The Beasties asked the Dust Brothers to keep the collage dense. Most producers would have stripped it for the vocal. Choosing density is a *decision*.
- **Hardware constraints shape the layering grammar.** The Emax HD's per-slot memory ceiling forced specific edit decisions on each loop. Constraints upstream become character downstream.
- **The mix is what makes density legible.** Caldato's frequency and stereo discipline separates *Paul's Boutique* from records that are merely busy.
- **Source-spotting as a compositional act.** The Beasties' lyrical references and the Dust Brothers' sample sources form a cultural-memory citation system. The record is a literature review with drums.
