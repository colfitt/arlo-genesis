## What this file is — and what it isn't

This file documents how *Sound of Silver* (released March 12, 2007 in the UK and March 20, 2007 in the US, jointly on DFA and Capitol) got made — specifically the 2006 tracking and early-2007 mix that produced "Get Innocuous!," "North American Scum," "Someone Great," "All My Friends," "Us v Them," "Watch the Tapes," the title track, and "New York, I Love You But You're Bringing Me Down" ([Wikipedia — Sound of Silver](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sound_of_Silver)). It is *not* a general Murphy-philosophy file (see [James Murphy's Analog-Synth-as-Character Philosophy](../artists/james-murphy-analog-synth-philosophy.md)), not the synth-vocabulary file (see [LCD Soundsystem Synth Sound Design Vocabulary](../artists/lcd-soundsystem-synth-sound-design.md)), not the live-rig file (see [LCD Soundsystem's Live Electronic-Rock Hybrid Rig](../artists/lcd-soundsystem-live-electronic-rig.md)), and not a Mahoney drumming primer (see [Pat Mahoney's Dance-Punk Drumming](../artists/pat-mahoney-dance-punk-drumming.md)). Cross-link those for the broader vocabulary; this file's job is the session-specific story.

## The producer credit and where the album was tracked

The album's producer credit on the sleeve is **"The DFA"** — the production entity comprising James Murphy and Tim Goldsworthy that had been running DFA Records since 2001. The recording locations on the LP are **Long View Farm Studios in North Brookfield, Massachusetts** and **DFA Studios in New York City** ([Wikipedia — Sound of Silver](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sound_of_Silver); [Discogs — Sound of Silver](https://www.discogs.com/release/2635428-LCD-Soundsystem-Sound-Of-Silver)). One disambiguation worth carrying into every retrieval: **DFA Studios and Plantain Studios are the same room.** Plantain is the in-house nickname used in the studio's own write-ups and in the gear-rundown literature; "DFA Studios" is the name that appears on the *Sound of Silver* credit. The artist files in this corpus call it Plantain; the sleeve calls it DFA. They are not separate facilities. Mixing was credited to **James Murphy and Dave Sardy**; mastering was **Geoff Pesche** at Abbey Road; **Eric Broucek** appears as engineering assistant and contributor of claps and shouts, with additional engineering credits to Matthew Thornley and Ian Neil ([Wikipedia — Sound of Silver](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sound_of_Silver)).

## Long View Farm — the room that wasn't Plantain

Long View Farm matters as a session location because it was a deliberate departure from the DFA basement on West 13th. Founded in 1974 by **Gilbert Scott Markle**, a Clark University philosophy professor turned audiophile, it was a converted dairy estate in rural Worcester County, Massachusetts — famous as the room where the Rolling Stones spent six weeks rehearsing *Tattoo You* and the 1981 tour in late summer 1981, where Stevie Wonder threw the *Songs in the Key of Life* listening party in 1976, and where the J. Geils Band tracked their EMI run including *Freeze Frame* ([Wikipedia — Long View Farm Studios](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Long_View_Farm_Studios); [Music Museum of New England — Long View Farm Studios](https://www.mmone.org/long-view-farm-studios/)). LCD had already used Long View on the 2005 self-titled debut, and the band returned in 2006 to track the bulk of *Sound of Silver* there. The reason was acoustic: Murphy had felt the debut sounded too "woody" — too much natural barn-board reflection — and wanted to do something corrective to the room itself ([Rolling Stone — 10 Things You Didn't Know About *Sound of Silver*](https://www.rollingstone.com/feature/lcd-soundsystems-sound-of-silver-10-things-you-didnt-know-110495/)).

## The aluminum-foil-and-silver-fabric studio

The corrective move is the most-cited *Sound of Silver* anecdote and the source of the album's title. Murphy and the team covered the Long View tracking room in **silver fabric and aluminum foil**, partly as an acoustic intervention (foil and fabric are bright, reflective at high frequencies, and aggressive — the opposite of a wood-paneled barn) and partly as an aesthetic talisman. Murphy told FACT magazine in late 2006 that he made the studio silver as a constant reminder of *"shiny music"* — citing **T. Rex, Heaven 17, Human League, Hawkwind's "Silver Machine," and Chrome** as the references, calling Chrome "the ultimate silver band" ([FACT Magazine — Interview: LCD Soundsystem](https://www.factmag.com/2009/01/01/interview-lcd-soundsystem/2/); [Rolling Stone](https://www.rollingstone.com/feature/lcd-soundsystems-sound-of-silver-10-things-you-didnt-know-110495/)). One scrap of the original silver fabric travelled with him to Rick Rubin's Mansion three years later for *This Is Happening* — a literal piece of *Sound of Silver*'s walls embedded in the next album's room ([Wikipedia — Sound of Silver](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sound_of_Silver)). The title itself layered three meanings, per Murphy: his father's line that having a child is "a permanent silver medal" (so this second album is structurally the silver-medal record after the debut); the literal sound of the silvered room; and the glam-and-chrome lineage ([Rolling Stone](https://www.rollingstone.com/feature/lcd-soundsystems-sound-of-silver-10-things-you-didnt-know-110495/)).

## The personnel — five names, but mostly one

The credited band on *Sound of Silver* is the live lineup that had cohered after the 2005 debut: **James Murphy** (vocals, drums, percussion, bass, programming, keyboards, guitar), **Pat Mahoney** (drums, percussion, vocals), **Tyler Pope** (guitars, bass), and **Nancy Whang** (vocals). Additional musicians on the sleeve: **Eric Broucek** (claps, vocals), **Marcus Lambkin** (claps), **Morgan Wiley** (piano), **Justin Chearno** (guitar), and a string section — **Jane Scarpantoni** (cello), **Lorenza Ponce** and **Amy Kimball** (violin), **David Gold** (viola) ([Wikipedia — Sound of Silver](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sound_of_Silver)). But the working method was less democratic than the credit list suggests. Multiple retrospectives confirm Murphy's pattern: even when band members tracked parts, he frequently re-recorded those parts himself, preferring his own performance to the "watered down" feel he attributed to playing-by-committee ([Rolling Stone](https://www.rollingstone.com/feature/lcd-soundsystems-sound-of-silver-10-things-you-didnt-know-110495/)). On the album credit Murphy plays drums on his own; **Pat Mahoney**'s acoustic kit drives "Sound of Silver" and several other tracks, and the live band's groove physics (see [Pat Mahoney's Dance-Punk Drumming](../artists/pat-mahoney-dance-punk-drumming.md)) informed the songs even when Murphy was the one capturing them. Tyler Pope — bassist of **!!!** and a fluent dance-punk player — is on guitar on tracks 1, 6, and 9 and bass on tracks 3, 6, and 9 ([TIDAL Magazine — Tyler Pope on his favorite LCD tracks](https://tidal.com/magazine/article/tyler-pope-chooses-his-favorite-lcd-soundsystem-tracks/1-38851)). Pope has said the title-track bass is "one of the funnest LCD basslines to play."

## "Get Innocuous!" — the CS-60-as-drum-machine opener

The album opens with what is by now the most-studied piece of LCD synth-as-percussion work, and it's track-one for a reason: the entire rhythm bed of "Get Innocuous!" is constructed not from a sampled drum kit but from **Yamaha CS-60** patches built into kicks and snares, then resampled and sequenced so they could be quantized and pitched ([XLR8R — LCD Soundsystem In The Studio](https://xlr8r.com/features/lcd-soundsystem-in-the-studio/)). The full sound-design vocabulary for this technique sits in [LCD Soundsystem Synth Sound Design Vocabulary](../artists/lcd-soundsystem-synth-sound-design.md); the session-specific point here is that "Get Innocuous!" is where this method first arrives in finished form on an LCD record. The bassline runs through a **Korg SQ-10** modular sequencer driving (per Murphy's own recollection) a **Moog Taurus II** — programmed into the 12-step rows of the SQ-10 and then gated, with Murphy playing drums live on top. Murphy told XLR8R the song's first build is "a beat that starts everything," and that beat is literally a synth pretending to be a kit.

## "Someone Great" — the *45:33* graft

The album's emotional pivot point has a documented backstory that other tracks don't. In summer 2006, Nike commissioned LCD to produce **a 45-minute continuous mix for the Nike+ Original Run series**, marketed as a workout soundtrack. The piece — *45:33* — was tracked between July and September 2006 at DFA Studios in NYC, with Eric Broucek and The DFA co-producing, and released digitally on October 17, 2006 ([Wikipedia — 45:33](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/45:33)). Murphy later admitted to the Guardian that the jogging-soundtrack framing was a cover story: he wanted to make a long-form record in the lineage of Manuel Göttsching's *E2-E4* (1984) and used the Nike commission as the financing mechanism to do it. **Track 3 of *45:33* — an instrumental section — is the music that became "Someone Great" on *Sound of Silver* four months later** ([Wikipedia — 45:33](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/45:33); [Wikipedia — Sound of Silver](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sound_of_Silver)). Murphy added vocals and arranged the section into a song form for the album. *Sound of Silver* is dedicated "to the memory of Dr. George Kamen (1942–2006)," a Bulgarian-born group-therapy pioneer in New York City who was widely understood to be Murphy's longtime therapist; secondary sources have long speculated that "Someone Great" memorializes Kamen, but Murphy has consistently refused to confirm or deny when asked, and at least one writer claiming proximity to Murphy has insisted the song is actually about someone else still living ([Hazlitt — Nine Short Essays About "Someone Great"](https://hazlitt.net/feature/nine-short-essays-about-someone-great-lcd-soundsystem)). The sonic spine of the track is the EMS Synthi A "joystick chord" technique documented in [LCD Soundsystem Synth Sound Design Vocabulary](../artists/lcd-soundsystem-synth-sound-design.md); Nancy Whang's harmonics sit against Murphy's vocal in the chorus.

## The *45:33* breakthrough — Nike funded the second half

Murphy has been explicit that *45:33* was the creative unlock for the rest of the album. He told Mojo that he was in a depressive spiral during the first half of recording — *"wanted to kill myself"* is the language he used about the emotional state in the studio — and that the Nike piece *"calmed me down"* and made *"the second half of the record just a breeze"* ([Rolling Stone](https://www.rollingstone.com/feature/lcd-soundsystems-sound-of-silver-10-things-you-didnt-know-110495/)). The reason is partly creative — *45:33* required him to *not* write three-minute pop forms for a stretch, which is a constraint reversal that re-opened the songwriting — and partly economic: the Nike commission paid for studio time. Tracks born partly out of the *45:33* working sessions and energy include not just "Someone Great" but the longer, build-driven middle of the album.

## "All My Friends" — the relentless piano, the Reich/Joy Division/Russell triangulation

The most-discussed *Sound of Silver* song is structurally the simplest. It rides a **single repeating piano figure in A major** for seven minutes and forty-two seconds, layering drums, bass, and synths on top without ever changing the piano's pattern. The song was written by **Pat Mahoney, James Murphy, and Tyler Pope** — a rare three-way credit for an LCD song ([Wikipedia — All My Friends (LCD Soundsystem song)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/All_My_Friends_(LCD_Soundsystem_song))). The compositional reference points are three: **Joy Division's "Transmission"** (1979), **Steve Reich's *Music for 18 Musicians*** (1976), and **Arthur Russell's Dinosaur L "Go Bang"** (1982). Murphy told Mojo in January 2008: *"I was purposefully trying to chase a feeling I got from Joy Division's 'Transmission,' which starts off so gentle, and becomes so fucking overwhelming"* — by the time "Transmission" reaches its repeated-imperative climax, Murphy said, *"your head's exploding"* ([Songfacts — All My Friends](https://www.songfacts.com/facts/lcd-soundsystem/all-my-friends)). The interlocking-piano texture of the intro — slightly out-of-phase, hypnotic, with the listener's ear locking and unlocking on different cells — points directly at Reich's *Music for 18 Musicians*, where four interlocking piano patterns build a rhythmicized wall by playing the same buildup a beat out of phase with each other. The "Go Bang" reference is the simplest of the three: Russell's track contains the line about wanting to see all my friends at once, and Murphy has called that lyric *"the simplest thing I could imagine, and it's fucking beautiful."* The lyric Murphy borrows from Pink Floyd's "Set the Controls for the Heart of the Sun" (1968) is also a documented graft. The piano was overdubbed by **Morgan Wiley** per the album credits; the relentlessness of the figure is the production decision — refusing to vary the piano part across nearly eight minutes, letting tension build through what's added around it rather than through changes to the spine.

## "Three days under the piano with a coat over my head"

The most-cited anecdote about Murphy's emotional state during the *Sound of Silver* sessions has him **lying under the piano for three days at Long View Farm with a coat over his head, telling people not to talk to him** ([Classic Album Sundays — The Story of LCD Soundsystem 'Sound of Silver'](https://classicalbumsundays.com/album-of-the-month-lcd-soundsystem-sound-of-silver/)). The story is corroborated by Murphy's own framing in the Mojo interview about wanting to kill himself in the first half of the record. It's worth carrying into any retrieval as a *production posture* fact, not a comic one — the album's emotional palette (the elegy of "Someone Great," the mortality-and-friends arc of "All My Friends," the title-track's wistfulness about a youth that wasn't actually that great) is downstream of a producer working from inside a depressive episode while wearing the engineer hat at the same time. The vocal-day rule documented in [James Murphy's Analog-Synth-as-Character Philosophy](../artists/james-murphy-analog-synth-philosophy.md) — write the lyric in the morning, track it that day, never neutral, never deferred — is what kept the work moving despite the posture.

## "North American Scum" — the last song written, written for the live show

"North American Scum" was tracked last and written backward from a stage-band requirement. Murphy told FACT: *"I needed a song that I felt would be good to play live. It was the last song written for the album. I wanted something we could rely on if the show wasn't going very well. All I had was the title, 'North American Scum'. Immediately I knew exactly what it meant"* ([FACT Magazine — Interview: LCD Soundsystem](https://www.factmag.com/2009/01/01/interview-lcd-soundsystem/2/)). He clarified the sentiment was not anti-American: *"It didn't mean something anti-American, which I don't feel. I take for granted that Americans are insecure and embarrassed."* The arrangement — Whang's keyboards, Mahoney's four-on-the-floor, a synth-rock hook designed to detonate in a 2,000-capacity room — is built around the live performance more than around the headphones experience. It became, predictably, the single that broke the record commercially.

## "Sound of Silver" (the title track), "Us v Them," "Watch the Tapes," "Time to Get Away"

The other four tracks each illuminate a piece of the album's design. **"Time to Get Away"** is the second track, sequenced right after "Get Innocuous!"; the lyric is widely read as Murphy's pissed-off side-eye at a music-industry figure. **"Us v Them"** runs eight minutes and gets progressively more intense across that span, building Mahoney's groove against Whang/Murphy synth stacks in the LCD long-form architecture that this album largely invented. **"Watch the Tapes"** is the closest thing on the record to a straight Ramones-velocity punk song dressed in dance clothes — fast, short by *Sound of Silver* standards, an arc through obsessive clubbing rather than nostalgia. **The title track** ("Sound of Silver") sits between "Someone Great" and "All My Friends" and is the album's most direct krautrock homage — a motorik-adjacent pulse, lyrics about being a teenager that pull back when you remember what being a teenager actually felt like, Tyler Pope's bass running the spine ([Stereogum — Sound of Silver Turns 10](https://stereogum.com/1928726/sound-of-silver-turns-10/reviews/the-anniversary); [TIDAL Magazine — Tyler Pope](https://tidal.com/magazine/article/tyler-pope-chooses-his-favorite-lcd-soundsystem-tracks/1-38851)).

## "New York, I Love You But You're Bringing Me Down"

The closer is a deliberate genre swerve. Where the rest of the album runs on synths, sequencers, and four-on-the-floor, "New York, I Love You But You're Bringing Me Down" opens as a piano ballad with Murphy front-and-center on vocals, builds into a band performance over five-and-a-half minutes, and ends the record on guitar rather than synthesizer. The lyrical content is gentrification and decline framed as a love song to a city that's no longer the city Murphy moved to. Sonically the choice to close on a ballad is the album's last reframe — the silver-fabric room has spent forty minutes inside synthetic dance music and then exits through a piano. Per the credits, the string quartet — Scarpantoni, Ponce, Kimball, Gold — is on this track ([Wikipedia — Sound of Silver](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sound_of_Silver)).

## Analog into Pro Tools — the workflow at this specific moment

The technical signature of *Sound of Silver* is not "analog vs digital" — Murphy is on record as preferring analog sound sources but tracking and mixing in Pro Tools, and Mark Pytlik's Pitchfork review (9.2, Best New Music; the album was named the publication's second-best of 2007) made the production legible to readers: *"Murphy's real legacy to dance music is his production sense; he's an analog obsessive with a general aversion to software, and *Sound of Silver* sounds deep, spacious, and full-blooded, far removed from the compressed, trebly, and overmastered paradigm that's gripped electronic music."* The chain that produces that quality is the Plantain/DFA-house signal flow documented in [James Murphy's Analog-Synth-as-Character Philosophy](../artists/james-murphy-analog-synth-philosophy.md): drums into a Universal Audio preamp, into a DBX 165 VCA compressor, into an LA-2A optical compressor, with the AKG BX-10 spring reverb across the bus, and tape used selectively for harmonic distortion rather than as the primary recording medium. The synths are the EMS Synthi, the CS-60, the SH-101 and the System 100m. The room is silvered. The Pro Tools session collects all of it. The mix — co-credited to Murphy and Dave Sardy and mastered by Geoff Pesche at Abbey Road — is what gives the album its commercial-radio loudness without the brittleness Murphy reads as failure mode.

## What changed between the 2005 debut and 2007

*Sound of Silver* is the record where LCD Soundsystem stops being James Murphy's bedroom project that toured with a band, and becomes a band that records together. The debut had been assembled largely solo with Murphy playing most parts; *Sound of Silver* arrives after eighteen months of touring with Mahoney, Whang, Pope, and Phil Mossman, and the songs are written with the touring band's groove physics in mind. Even when Murphy still re-records parts himself in the studio, the *shape* of the songs — the long instrumental builds, the willingness to ride a pulse for seven minutes, the architectural restraint that lets a piano figure carry "All My Friends" without ornamentation — comes from the band's stage practice. Pat Mahoney's *"hypnotic, not dumb"* philosophy (see [Pat Mahoney's Dance-Punk Drumming](../artists/pat-mahoney-dance-punk-drumming.md)) is what makes the long forms work. The no-click-track live architecture (see [LCD Soundsystem's Live Electronic-Rock Hybrid Rig](../artists/lcd-soundsystem-live-electronic-rig.md)) is what gave Murphy permission to write songs that don't have to slot into a sequencer's grid. The result is the first LCD record where the album form, not the single, is the unit of expression.

## Reception and what the album set in motion

*Sound of Silver* received an 86/100 Metacritic average across 41 reviews, was nominated for the Grammy for Best Electronic/Dance Album at the 50th Grammys, and was named Pitchfork's #2 album of 2007 ([Wikipedia — Sound of Silver](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sound_of_Silver)). It debuted at #46 on the Billboard 200 — Murphy had publicly aimed for the top 40 and was disappointed by the chart position, though the album would later be re-canonized in retrospect as one of the decade's most important records ([Rolling Stone](https://www.rollingstone.com/feature/lcd-soundsystems-sound-of-silver-10-things-you-didnt-know-110495/)). The forward lineage runs straight into [LCD Soundsystem — *This Is Happening*](./lcd-this-is-happening.md) (2010), recorded at Rick Rubin's Mansion in Los Angeles with a piece of the *Sound of Silver* silver fabric literally carried into the new room.
