## The Session Story In One Paragraph

*Hard Normal Daddy* is the record where Tom Jenkinson stopped chopping breaks like a programmer and started chopping them like a bass player who knew where the swing should land. It was his second album as Squarepusher, his first proper Warp full-length after the 1996 Rephlex debut *Feed Me Weird Things*, made in the same home setup with broadly the same gear — a used Akai S950 sampler, a Boss DR660 drum machine, a mixer, a DAT recorder, and his own electric bass — that he had bought with student-loan money while studying at Chelsea College of Art and Design ([Sound On Sound, Squarepusher](https://www.soundonsound.com/people/squarepusher); [Bandcamp Daily, *Feed Me Weird Things* turns 25](https://daily.bandcamp.com/features/squarepushers-feed-me-weird-things-interview)). The conceptual move was to push away from the explicitly jazzy debut toward "a more soundtrack-type of sound," with Jenkinson listening to early Lalo Schifrin and Herbie Hancock's *Death Wish* ([Squarepusher Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Squarepusher)). The technical brief was equally specific: program drums so well you could not hear where the live-feel ended and the machine began. Twelve tracks, sixty-two minutes, no vocals, no guests. NME and *The Wire* named it a 1997 album of the year; Pitchfork later placed it at #24 on its IDM-of-all-time list ([Wikipedia, *Hard Normal Daddy*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hard_Normal_Daddy)).

## Where This Record Sits — Post-AI Warp

Warp's *Artificial Intelligence* series ran 1992–1994 and ended before Jenkinson signed to the label. The original AI catalog — Aphex Twin's *Surfing on Sine Waves*, Autechre's *Incunabula*, Black Dog's *Bytes*, B12's *Electro-Soma*, Speedy J's *Ginger*, FUSE's *Dimension Intrusion* — codified "electronic listening music," the post-rave home-listening posture Warp co-founder Steve Beckett used to position the label ([Wikipedia, *Artificial Intelligence* series](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Artificial_Intelligence_(series))). Squarepusher was the second-wave inheritor: Aphex Twin caught him at the George Robey pub in Finsbury Park, ran *Feed Me Weird Things* on his own Rephlex imprint in June 1996, and Warp signed Jenkinson directly in December 1995 ([Squarepusher Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Squarepusher)). *Hard Normal Daddy* is therefore the first Warp-proper Squarepusher full-length and one of the records that pivoted the Warp aesthetic from AI-era headphones-techno toward rhythmically aggressive, instrumentally fluent late-'90s IDM — alongside Aphex's *Richard D. James Album* (1996) and just before Boards of Canada's *Music Has the Right to Children* (1998).

## The "Program Like A Player" Brief

The conceptual through-line is Jenkinson's stated intent on *Hard Normal Daddy*: programming "indistinguishable" from playing — in his own framing, an attempt to "program like a player, make it have that swing, with a bionic aspect" ([Tape Op #89, Squarepusher](https://tapeop.com/interviews/89/squarepusher)). This is the inverse of the typical IDM brief of the period, where the *point* was that the drums could not have been played. Jenkinson came up the other way: he played bass in a Metallica-influenced thrash band at twelve and was a working drummer before he was a programmer ([Squarepusher Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Squarepusher)). The Amen-break velocity programming on this record is the artifact of someone who knows how a real drummer would actually swing the second sixteenth off the kick.

## The Studio — Akai S950, DR660, DAT, A Mixer And A Bass

The documented gear for the *Feed Me Weird Things* / *Hard Normal Daddy* period is small and consistent. Jenkinson has said he took out a large student loan and bought a used **Akai S950 sampler**, **two drum machines**, **a mixer** and **a DAT recorder**; these played the integral roles on the 1996 debut and stayed in place for the 1997 follow-up ([Sound On Sound, Squarepusher](https://www.soundonsound.com/people/squarepusher)). The named drum machine of the era is the **Boss DR660** — "a bit of a non-event in the history of drum machines," in Jenkinson's own phrasing — which he also used as a MIDI sequencer to drive the S950 on the *Big Loada* tracks recorded around the same time ([Sound On Sound, Squarepusher](https://www.soundonsound.com/people/squarepusher)). Tape was the **Fostex M80** quarter-inch eight-track in the early '90s, replaced by a **Tascam MSR16** half-inch later in the decade; for *Hard Normal Daddy* either is plausible and neither is explicitly attributed. **The source brief's Atari ST / Cubase workflow assumption could not be verified in any source examined and is treated as unconfirmed.**

The bass guitar question deserves caution. Jenkinson is now associated with a stable of instruments — a Music Man Sabre, a Westone Thunder II fretless, a Rickenbacker 4001, Zoot and Warwick six-strings — but the specific bass tracked on *Hard Normal Daddy* is not pinned down in any single source. The Music Man Sabre is the one Jenkinson has named as "the best four-string I had" in a 2006 *Guardian* interview, the closest evidence to the 1996–97 era; the Westone fretless is a long-standing studio fixture. **The source brief's "Fender Jazz Bass" presumption is not corroborated by any source examined here and is omitted.**

## Sampler Workflow — Amen, Think, Funky Drummer, Chopped By Hand

The album lives or dies on its breakbeat chopping. Per-track source attribution is not exhaustively documented, but the technique is unambiguous: classic funk/soul breakbeats — the Amen break (The Winstons' "Amen, Brother"), Clyde Stubblefield's Funky Drummer, the Think break (Lyn Collins) — chopped into single hits and re-sequenced inside the S950, triggered from the DR660. On "Vic Acid" the Amen break is "shredded to within an inch of its life," with Jenkinson soloing with the chops the way a drummer solos with a kit ([George's Music Blog, *Hard Normal Daddy* review](https://georgetown5000.com/2025/09/26/squarepusher-hard-normal-daddy-1997-album-review/)). The S950 had enough sample-time at full bandwidth to load a full break, slice it, and re-trigger it with velocity-scaled hits — the corpus entry on hardware-sampling workflow is the technical sibling, and this is its virtuoso ceiling.

## "Coopers World" — The Mission Statement Opener

The opener is the album's calmest and most explicitly fusion-coded track. The pulse is mid-tempo funk rather than drill-'n'-bass; the harmony moves through jazz-chord extensions; the lead line is a clean electric bass with the pick-attack and voicing vocabulary Jaco Pastorius and Stanley Clarke made standard in '70s fusion. Douglas Wolk's phrase is the canonical one — Jenkinson sublimates the beats to "tunes like 'Coopers World' that approximate an early-'70s sort of funk-jazz hybrid" ([Wikipedia, *Hard Normal Daddy*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hard_Normal_Daddy)). The track functions as a thesis: an IDM record where the bass player is the lead voice and the drum machine knows how to swing.

## "Beep Street" — Chopped Snare, Jazz Chords, Layered Cello

"Beep Street" is the most-streamed track on the record. It opens with a "chopped snare" pattern that announces a jungle / drum-and-bass direction, then transitions into "jazz chords and layers of what sound like processed cello playing off against piano notes" ([George's Music Blog, *Hard Normal Daddy* review](https://georgetown5000.com/2025/09/26/squarepusher-hard-normal-daddy-1997-album-review/)). The drum sound is the canonical example of the album's velocity discipline — a Tape Op writer notes the drums "came from a box" but that "the programming is otherworldly and milks every drop of emotion from the velocity accents" ([Tape Op #89, Squarepusher bonus](https://tapeop.com/interviews/89/squarepusher-bonus)). The interviewer admits in print that he forgot to ask which drum machine the box actually was; Jenkinson's documented gear points at the DR660.

## "Vic Acid" — The Amen Solo

"Vic Acid" is the album's centrepiece for breakbeat virtuosity. A "twisted acid bassline" — almost certainly a Roland TB-303 or clone driven from the DR660 — snarls beneath an Amen break chopped, retriggered and stutter-edited into something that operates more like a drum solo than a sample loop ([George's Music Blog, *Hard Normal Daddy* review](https://georgetown5000.com/2025/09/26/squarepusher-hard-normal-daddy-1997-album-review/)). This is the track that established the "drill 'n' bass" pejorative — programmed drum performance taken to the point where it is virtuosity rather than groove.

## "Papalon" / "E8 Boogie" — The Soundtrack Pair

These long paired tracks are where the "soundtrack" brief Jenkinson named — Schifrin, *Death Wish*, 1970s police-procedural themes — is most legible. Both have distinct narrative arcs across their runtimes; "E8 Boogie" in particular foregrounds "insanely funky bass playing" ([George's Music Blog, *Hard Normal Daddy* review](https://georgetown5000.com/2025/09/26/squarepusher-hard-normal-daddy-1997-album-review/)). The genre logic is not jungle-with-bass-on-top — it is fusion-soundtrack with chopped breakbeats *in place of* the studio drummer the original 1970s scores would have used.

## "Rustic Raver" — The 303 Sketch

"Rustic Raver" is the album's most explicitly acid-techno-coded moment. A "burbling 303 bass synth does most of the work" against beats that may be original programmed kit hits rather than sampled breaks, with a "hypnotic melodic riff" sketching out "delightfully psychedelic freeform character" — a direction Jenkinson pushed further on the *Big Loada* EP later in 1997 ([George's Music Blog, *Hard Normal Daddy* review](https://georgetown5000.com/2025/09/26/squarepusher-hard-normal-daddy-1997-album-review/)).

## "Chin Hippy" — Proto-Breakcore

"Chin Hippy" is the album's most aggressive track and the clearest pointer to where Squarepusher would go on *Music Is Rotted One Note* and *Go Plastic* — "the most hectic and abrasive possible execution of breakbeat" with "barely controlled insanity" ([George's Music Blog, *Hard Normal Daddy* review](https://georgetown5000.com/2025/09/26/squarepusher-hard-normal-daddy-1997-album-review/)). It is proto-breakcore in everything but name — the genre tag did not exist in 1997 — and one of the foundational reference points for the school of producers (Venetian Snares, Aaron Funk, Otto von Schirach) who built the actual breakcore scene over the next half-decade.

## "Male Pill Part 13" And The Album's Critical Position

"Male Pill Part 13" pairs the moody synth pad palette of "Beep Street" with electric piano and electric guitar trading the lead over chopped breaks and "insane bass playing" — the closest the record gets to a straight fusion blowing-session structure ([George's Music Blog, *Hard Normal Daddy* review](https://georgetown5000.com/2025/09/26/squarepusher-hard-normal-daddy-1997-album-review/)). "Fat Controller," "Anirog D9," "Rat / P's + Q's" and "Rebus" complete the twelve-track sequence with shorter etudes in the same vocabulary. Chart performance was modest (UK #115) but the critical position is fixed: NME and *The Wire* placed it in their 1997 albums of the year; AllMusic's Sean Cooper called it "substantially cleaner and more thought out than previous releases" from Spymania and Rephlex; Stylus's Todd Burns called it Jenkinson's "masterpiece" in 2003 ([Wikipedia, *Hard Normal Daddy*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hard_Normal_Daddy)).

## The Jazz-Chord Vocabulary Under The Breaks

What separates *Hard Normal Daddy* from contemporaneous drum-and-bass is the harmonic vocabulary under the rhythm tracks. Jenkinson's bass voicings move through extended and altered jazz chords — minor sevenths, ninth and eleventh extensions, modal shifts — the kind of surface that requires a player fluent in the Pastorius / Stanley Clarke / Weather Report idiom. Jenkinson has confirmed the Pastorius influence directly: "still battling with the influence of (Jaco) Pastorius" ([Squarepusher Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Squarepusher)). The corpus point: the harmonic floor on this album is fusion's, not techno's. Strip the chopped breakbeats out and what remains is closer to *Heavy Weather* than to anything else on Warp in 1997.

## What This Record Teaches The Corpus

- **Programmed drums sound human when the programmer is a drummer.** Velocity-accent discipline at the level of *Hard Normal Daddy* is not a plugin setting — it is the artifact of someone who has played the part for years.
- **A small hardware rig outperforms a large software one when the operator is fluent.** S950, DR660, mixer, DAT, bass — shorter than most bedroom producers' plugin folders, more sonically distinctive than almost anything in its era.
- **Sampling is performance, not retrieval.** The Amen break on "Vic Acid" is an instrument to solo on rather than a loop to drop.
- **Live bass and chopped breakbeats are not incompatible.** The bass is tight enough to function as part of the drum-machine grid; the drums are loose enough to function as part of the bass groove.
- **Soundtrack-instrumental sits inside IDM if you let it.** The fusion-soundtrack frame absorbs the drill-'n'-bass chops without flattening them.
