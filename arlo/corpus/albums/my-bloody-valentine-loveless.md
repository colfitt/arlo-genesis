# My Bloody Valentine — *Loveless* (1991)

**Artist:** My Bloody Valentine
**Year:** 1991 (released 4 November 1991, Creation Records)
**Producer:** Kevin Shields (with Colm Ó Cíosóig contributing programming and percussion design)
**Engineers (verified):** Alan Moulder, Anjali Dutt, Harold Burgon, Dick Meaney, Darren Allison, Nick Robbins, Guy Fixsen
**Studios:** Nineteen rooms over roughly two and a half years (Feb 1989 – Sep 1991); primary anchors were Blackwing (Southwark), The Elephant (Wapping), Protocol (Holloway), Britannia Row, Falconer (Kentish Town) and The Church (Crouch End, where the final mix landed)
**Scope:** album-as-artifact — the canonical reference for shoegaze production, the "glide guitar" technique, reverse-reverb as a compositional element, and sampler-as-drum-bus workflow

## The Session Story In One Paragraph

*Loveless* is the kind of record that bankrupts a label and reorders a genre. Creation Records' Alan McGee initially budgeted five days. The sessions ran more than two years across nineteen studios with a rotating cast of engineers — most of whom Shields treated as building-supplied and untrusted, leaning almost entirely on Alan Moulder and later Anjali Dutt. The widely repeated £250,000 budget figure comes from a *Melody Maker* report; Shields has publicly disputed it, arguing actual studio spend was closer to £160,000 and the rest was three years of living expenses; Creation's Dick Green has said the real number was *higher* than reported, and his hair allegedly went grey from the pressure ([Wikipedia, Loveless](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Loveless_(album))). The artifact that came out the other end is dense, hot, monaural-feeling, vocally submerged, and built around a guitar technique that effectively does not exist outside of one player.

## The Studio List Is Not A Studio List — It's A Workflow

The "19 studios" figure is correct, but it misleads if it suggests Shields was chasing rooms for their character. He was chasing capacity. Sessions began at Blackwing in Southwark in February 1989, moved to The Elephant in Wapping for eight largely unproductive weeks in autumn 1989, and only settled into real momentum at Protocol in Holloway from May 1990 onward. Vocals were tracked at Britannia Row. Alan Moulder did mixing at Falconer in Kentish Town. Dick Meaney and Darren Allison mixed the final masters at The Church in Crouch End in autumn 1991 — the nineteenth room ([Wikipedia, Loveless](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Loveless_(album))). The lesson is *not* that great records require nineteen studios. It's that when a record's production lives inside one person's head, rooms and engineers become disposable infrastructure. The continuity is Shields and the tape.

## "Glide Guitar" — What It Actually Is

Glide guitar is the engine of the *Loveless* sound. The mechanics are physical and specific:

- A **Fender Jazzmaster** (Shields' canonical) or **Fender Jaguar** with the tremolo system loosened so the arm has almost no resistance, often **wrapped with tape so the arm does not fully seat into the body** ([gearnews.com, Loveless gear](https://www.gearnews.com/my-bloody-valentine-loveless-30-years-on-the-gear-and-how-to-glide/); [Stringjoy, Guitars of Kevin Shields](https://stringjoy.com/guitars-of-kevin-shields/))
- The arm held in the palm during strumming, with the bridge "pulled back as far as possible" so notes bend audibly *in time with the strum* rather than as a one-shot vibrato ([Stringjoy, Guitars of Kevin Shields](https://stringjoy.com/guitars-of-kevin-shields/))
- A guitar tuned somewhere non-standard, with the tremolo arm set "kind of unusually high"; Shields has said the first song he ever recorded with the technique was "Thorn" from the *You Made Me Realise* EP, and the second was "Slow" two hours later ([Stringjoy, Guitars of Kevin Shields](https://stringjoy.com/guitars-of-kevin-shields/))
- Often paired with a **Marshall Shredmaster**, dual **Boss PN-2 tremolos**, dual **Boss GE-7 graphic EQs**, a **Digitech Whammy**, a **Digitech 8-Second Sampler**, Jim Dunlop fuzz, and Roger Mayer Octave/Active fuzz ([gearnews.com, Loveless gear](https://www.gearnews.com/my-bloody-valentine-loveless-30-years-on-the-gear-and-how-to-glide/))
- Sometimes routed through a custom hybrid guitar — a Strat neck on a Jaguar body with EMG pickups — for a midrangier voicing on specific overdubs ([Tape Op, Kevin Shields Interview](https://tapeop.com/interviews/26/my-bloody-valentine-2))

The point is that "glide" is not a pedal and not a setting. It is a *physical instrument modification* combined with a *playing motion* whose output is then post-processed. Without that motion, none of the post-processing reads correctly. The corpus should treat this as analogous to a slide-guitar technique (see `corpus/techniques/open-tunings-and-slide-guitar.md`) — it is a complete instrumental dialect, not an effect.

## Reverse Reverb As The Whole Aesthetic

The other half of the *Loveless* signature lives in the outboard rack. Shields named his "secret weapon" in interviews repeatedly: the reverse-reverb program on a **Yamaha SPX90** and an **Alesis Midiverb II** ([Tape Op, Kevin Shields Interview](https://tapeop.com/interviews/26/my-bloody-valentine-2)). The Yamaha SPX90 in particular gets credit as the unit most responsible for the album's "breathing inward" quality.

Used the way Shields uses it, reverse reverb is not a tail effect. It is run at **100% wet**, with the dry signal removed, so what reaches the mix is only the reversed envelope of the played note ([Far Out Magazine, How to play guitar like Kevin Shields](https://faroutmagazine.co.uk/how-to-play-guitar-like-kevin-shields-my-bloody-valentine/)). The attack of the pick becomes a swell *into* the note; the note then chops off when the strum stops. This forces a specific performance compensation: the player has to play *ahead of the beat* because the audible peak of the sound arrives after the pick hits the strings. Combined with glide guitar's continuous-pitch motion, the result is a sound that has no clean transients, no clean pitch centers, and no stable rhythmic grid — yet still grooves. That is the *Loveless* trick.

Shields' stated "big no-no's" for the record were no echo, no chorus, no flanger, no panning, and crucially **no normal reverb tail**. The one effect permitted was reverse reverb ([Tape Op, Kevin Shields Interview](https://tapeop.com/interviews/26/my-bloody-valentine-2)). This single-effect discipline is what holds the record together sonically across 22 months and 19 rooms — the same processor stamp is on everything.

## Sampler-As-Drum-Bus — Colm Ó Cíosóig and the Akai S1000

Colm Ó Cíosóig was physically unable to track most of the album live; chronic illness and a documented breakdown during the early sessions ruled out conventional drum tracking ([Wikipedia, Loveless](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Loveless_(album))). Shields' response was structural rather than apologetic. He had Ó Cíosóig play individual hits, fills, and short patterns, captured them to DAT, and triggered them back through an **Akai S1000** sampler ([gearnews.com, Loveless gear](https://www.gearnews.com/my-bloody-valentine-loveless-30-years-on-the-gear-and-how-to-glide/)). On at least one track ("Only Shallow") drum patterns were programmed in **Steinberg Pro24 on an Atari ST** triggering an **Akai S900** ([Sound on Sound, Only Shallow](https://www.soundonsound.com/techniques/classic-tracks-my-bloody-valentine-only-shallow)). Ó Cíosóig played live drums on at least three songs after recovering — "Only Shallow," "Come in Alone," and overdubbed toms on "Loomer" — and entirely composed and programmed the closing track "Touched" ([Wikipedia, Loveless](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Loveless_(album))).

Shields has credited hip-hop — Public Enemy specifically — as the model for treating the drum kit as a *sample bank* rather than a performance ([Wikipedia, Loveless](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Loveless_(album))). The aesthetic implication is large: *Loveless*'s rhythmic feel is closer to a hip-hop record's relationship to live drums than to a rock record's. The kit is an instrument that has been recorded once and is now being played by a different musician. This connects directly to the corpus' sampling philosophy — see `corpus/sampling/` and `corpus/techniques/hardware-sampling-workflow.md`.

## "Only Shallow" — The Microcosm

The opening track is the most-documented session on the album and is worth treating as a worked example. For the signature squalling riff, Shields set up a **'60s Burman amp** and a **Fender Showman** *facing each other* with a single mic between them, both amps running tremolo at different rates. He layered four such tremolo passes, then **sampled the layered result and played it back reversed, an octave higher** ([Sound on Sound, Only Shallow](https://www.soundonsound.com/techniques/classic-tracks-my-bloody-valentine-only-shallow)). Vocals (tracked at Britannia Row) were performed in complete unedited takes with **no compression, no reverb, no delay**, then layered roughly ten deep — one take provided clarity, the others a supporting bed ([Sound on Sound, Only Shallow](https://www.soundonsound.com/techniques/classic-tracks-my-bloody-valentine-only-shallow)). Engineers on the song included Harold Burgon (Elephant) and Alan Moulder (Falconer mixing). The final master holds only three main guitar tracks plus a guide for the final verse ([Sound on Sound, Only Shallow](https://www.soundonsound.com/techniques/classic-tracks-my-bloody-valentine-only-shallow)).

The contradiction is the lesson. The track sounds like fifty guitar parts. It is three. The density comes from glide-guitar's continuous pitch motion and from the reverse-reverb envelope, not from overdub count. Shields has been emphatic on this point: the production is *much simpler than people assume*, with few overdubs, ordinary equipment, and simple effect chains. The complexity is in the *playing*, the *instrument prep*, and the *single effect stamp*.

## The Live-Playability Rule (Carefully Stated)

The pithy version of *Loveless*'s production philosophy — that the record was made to be unplayable live — is widely paraphrased but should be reported carefully. What Shields *has* said on record is that song structures were kept deliberately simple precisely so the production could "get away with a lot more when you mess around with the contents" ([Wikipedia, Loveless](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Loveless_(album))). What is *demonstrably true* from the technique inventory above is that several specific moves — the reversed-and-octave-shifted sampled tremolo layer in "Only Shallow," the sampled-drum bus, the 100% wet reverse-reverb stamp — cannot be reproduced by four humans on a stage without samplers and pre-rendered backing. The live MBV apparatus from 1991 onward has therefore depended on sample triggering and the famous "holocaust section" noise-wall — a different artifact than the studio record. Treat "you wouldn't be able to play this live" as a *consequence* of the production approach rather than a stated design rule.

## The Obsessive Overdub-And-Revise Method

Shields' working method on *Loveless* was iterative and re-recording-heavy rather than additive. Vocals on individual songs were re-tracked 10–18 times, and the eventual layered comp blended multiple full takes into a single voice rather than presenting any one performance ([Wikipedia, Loveless](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Loveless_(album))). Guitar parts were tracked, rejected, retracked. Engineers cycled because Shields trusted few of them with critical decisions — by his own account, Alan Moulder and later Anjali Dutt were the only engineers given real authority; others "came with the studio" ([Wikipedia, Loveless](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Loveless_(album))). This is a useful corrective to the romantic version of the *Loveless* story: it was not a marathon of constant new ideas. It was a marathon of *the same idea being revised against itself* until Shields stopped recognizing the bad takes.

## The Mix Room Cost (Financial And Creative)

The financial story is well-documented and worth keeping accurate. *Melody Maker*'s £250,000 figure is the widely repeated number; Shields has put the actual studio spend at roughly £160,000, with the remainder representing three years of band living expenses; Creation's Dick Green has said the *Melody Maker* figure was underreported by roughly £20,000 ([Wikipedia, Loveless](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Loveless_(album))). The point isn't the exact figure. The point is that the record put Creation Records into a corner so tight that Alan McGee dropped the band shortly after release, and the label's financial recovery was eventually subsidized by Sony's stake in Oasis. The mix-room marathons at The Church in Crouch End — with Dick Meaney and Darren Allison shepherding final masters — are part of music-business folklore precisely because nobody had spent that long on a guitar-band record before, and because the result *justified itself sonically* in a way that almost no other comparably-expensive 1990s album did.

## What ARLO Can Pull From This Record

A short, practical list of *Loveless*-derived moves that are reusable without copying the surface aesthetic:

- **One-effect discipline.** Pick a single processor and let it stamp the entire record. Shields chose reverse reverb; the corollary is that you can pick literally anything, but you commit to it on every source. See `corpus/techniques/oblique-strategies-and-studio-as-instrument.md` for the constraint-as-aesthetic principle.
- **Physical instrument modification as composition.** The glide guitar is a mechanical alteration, not a setting. The analog for a producer working in a different idiom is to ask what *physical* prep — capo, retuning, prepared piano, broken cymbal — would let a part exist that otherwise wouldn't.
- **Sampler-as-drum-bus.** When a player cannot or should not perform a part live, capture the smallest possible component (a single hit, a single bar) and trigger it. The aesthetic of *Loveless* drums is not "programmed drums" — it is "live drums played by a sampler."
- **Few tracks, intense parts.** Three guitars sound like fifty when the playing technique and the effect stamp are doing the heavy lifting. The corpus' density principle applies here at the *part* level, not the *layer count* level.
- **Trust no engineer you cannot trust.** Shields' personnel discipline is extreme but defensible: a record this committed to one sound needs the one person who hears that sound to be in the chair, full stop.
