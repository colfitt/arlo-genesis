## What the record is, and where it sits in the catalog

*Fun House* is the second Stooges studio album, recorded **11–25 May 1970 at Elektra Sound Recorders in Los Angeles** and released on Elektra on 7 July 1970 ([Wikipedia — Fun House](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fun_House_(The_Stooges_album)); [Ultimate Classic Rock — How the Stooges Created a Pre-Punk Milestone](https://ultimateclassicrock.com/stooges-fun-house/)). It sits between the John Cale-produced debut *The Stooges* (1969) and the Bowie-mixed, Williamson-driven *Raw Power* (1973). The lineup is the original Ann Arbor Stooges — **Iggy Pop (vocals), Ron Asheton (guitar), Dave Alexander (bass), Scott Asheton (drums)** — plus Detroit saxophonist **Steve Mackay** added two days before the band left for LA, who plays on three tracks ([Wikipedia — Steve Mackay](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Steve_Mackay)). James Williamson is not on this record; Dave Alexander is still alive and in the band; Iggy's worst drug-era hadn't fully arrived. *Fun House* is the document of the original four-piece operating at full live power with one outside element (Mackay) bolted on.

## The producer line is Don Gallucci — the "Louie Louie" keyboardist turned Elektra house producer

The producer is **Don Gallucci** — at age 14 the keyboardist on The Kingsmen's "Louie Louie," later leader of Don and the Goodtimes, and by 1970 the in-house producer at Elektra ([Perfect Sound Forever — Don Gallucci, Producer of Stooges' Funhouse](https://www.furious.com/perfect/dongallucci.html); [Wikipedia — Fun House](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fun_House_(The_Stooges_album))). Elektra president Jac Holzman handed him the assignment after the debut album's John Cale productions had failed to capture the band's live force. Gallucci's first reaction after seeing the band live was skeptical — he told Holzman they were an "interesting group, but I don't think you can get this feeling on tape" ([Perfect Sound Forever — Don Gallucci](https://www.furious.com/perfect/dongallucci.html)). Holzman had already booked the studio. Gallucci's solution to the "can't get it on tape" problem became the method that defines the record.

## The Elektra Sound Recorders setup — tear the studio apart and put the band on stage

The first day, the band tried a conventional setup with baffles between amps and headphones for the singer. It didn't work. Gallucci's response was to strip the room: **"He went about taking out the baffles, curtains, and fancy rugs. The idea was to make them comfortable and turn the studio into a stage"** ([Perfect Sound Forever — Don Gallucci](https://www.furious.com/perfect/dongallucci.html)). The band set up in one open room exactly as they would onstage, with no isolation between sources. Iggy was given a handheld mic and a small P.A. for vocal foldback instead of headphones, so he could move around as he did live ([Mix Online — Inside The Complete Fun House Sessions](https://www.mixonline.com/recording/rocking-studio-stooges-inside-complete-fun-house-sessions-372032)). The bleed was severe enough that bass amplifier vibrations rattled the snare drum on the recordings ([Wikipedia — Fun House](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fun_House_(The_Stooges_album))). Ron Asheton's account: **"We couldn't play unless it was loud — our limited ability demanded it. The recording was pretty quick: we did a track a day"** ([Wikipedia — Fun House](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fun_House_(The_Stooges_album))). For ARLO: when a band's sound depends on physical loudness, mutual bleed, and singer mobility, the engineering instruction is **abandon isolation, P.A. foldback over headphones, hand the vocalist a dynamic and let them roam, accept the bleed as part of the timbre**.

## The engineer — Brian Ross-Myring, the gentleman of the old school

Sessions were engineered by **Brian Ross-Myring**, a middle-aged Elektra staff engineer whose prior credit was reportedly a Barbra Streisand session. Gallucci has described him as coming **"from the old school, when recording was an elegant gentleman's business"**, with the philosophy that **"a guitar should sound like a guitar and a sax like a sax"** ([Mix Online — Inside The Complete Fun House Sessions](https://www.mixonline.com/recording/rocking-studio-stooges-inside-complete-fun-house-sessions-372032)). The juxtaposition is the methodological detail: a polite, no-coloration tracking engineer pointing 1-inch tape at a band tearing the room apart. The format was **1-inch, 8-track 3M multitrack**, with takes captured complete including final vocals — which is why the Rhino Handmade *Complete Fun House Sessions* box set could later release over 100 full alternate takes ([Mix Online — Inside The Complete Fun House Sessions](https://www.mixonline.com/recording/rocking-studio-stooges-inside-complete-fun-house-sessions-372032)). For ARLO: a hot, chaotic-sounding record can be cut by a conservative engineer if the setup itself does the aesthetic work.

## Take culture — 28 takes of "Loose," a track a day

Gallucci's plan was to **record around a dozen complete takes of one song each day, then pick the keeper later** ([Perfect Sound Forever — Don Gallucci](https://www.furious.com/perfect/dongallucci.html)). The actual numbers were higher: 28 takes of "Loose," roughly a dozen each of "Dirt" and "T.V. Eye," 15 of "Down on the Street" ([Mix Online — Inside The Complete Fun House Sessions](https://www.mixonline.com/recording/rocking-studio-stooges-inside-complete-fun-house-sessions-372032); [Rolling Stone — Henry Rollins on Fun House](https://www.rollingstone.com/music/music-features/henry-rollins-interview-stooges-fun-house-1022661/)). Henry Rollins's frame for this is the most useful one for explaining it to an artist: **"They're taking snapshots of the song... like they were taking a piece of marble, making the statue, but they didn't get the nose right. So the next take is another block"** ([Rolling Stone — Henry Rollins on Fun House](https://www.rollingstone.com/music/music-features/henry-rollins-interview-stooges-fun-house-1022661/)). Every take was a complete performance with vocals — no comping across takes, no overdub-fixing the lead. The keeper is the one performance where the whole room got the song right. For ARLO: when a song's identity is a *feel* the band locks into rather than a part you can punch in, **the production method is many full-band takes, vocals included, and pick the one that breathes**.

## Side one is the live set

The first side — **"Down on the Street," "Loose," "T.V. Eye," "Dirt"** — runs in the exact order the Stooges were playing those songs onstage in spring 1970 ([Wikipedia — Fun House](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fun_House_(The_Stooges_album))). The band had wanted "Loose" as the opener; Elektra pushed "Down on the Street" out front as the more commercial song. Henry Rollins's description of "Down on the Street" is the clearest account of what side one accomplishes: **"a perfect song. Played perfectly,"** built on Scott Asheton's drum pocket and Ron Asheton's wah-driven riff, cut in roughly an hour of session time ([Rolling Stone — Henry Rollins on Fun House](https://www.rollingstone.com/music/music-features/henry-rollins-interview-stooges-fun-house-1022661/)). The four-song side functions as a studio document of the band's live set: same songs, same order, same energy, plus the studio-only fidelity of being able to hear what Dave Alexander's bass is actually doing under Ron Asheton's wah.

## Side two is the comedown — Steve Mackay enters

The second side — **"1970," "Fun House," "L.A. Blues"** — is where Steve Mackay's tenor saxophone enters. Mackay had played with the Detroit avant-rock outfit **Carnal Kitchen** and had met Iggy at art school in Ann Arbor; Iggy formally added him to the band two days before they flew to LA after he'd sat in on Stooges shows ([Wikipedia — Steve Mackay](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Steve_Mackay)). The band's stated motivation was hearing saxophone on The Doors records and believing they could push it further ([Mix Online — Inside The Complete Fun House Sessions](https://www.mixonline.com/recording/rocking-studio-stooges-inside-complete-fun-house-sessions-372032)). Mackay plays on the back-half three songs only. Rollins's account of what Mackay does to the record: **"Anything Steve is on, it gives it the kind of crazy grease, like where the head comes open and all the crazy comes out... greasy lightning"** that makes "the whole thing goes bonkers" ([Rolling Stone — Henry Rollins on Fun House](https://www.rollingstone.com/music/music-features/henry-rollins-interview-stooges-fun-house-1022661/)). Mackay would be fired by Iggy in October 1970, five months after he joined, and would not return to the Stooges until the 2003 Coachella reunion ([Wikipedia — Steve Mackay](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Steve_Mackay)). The three songs he plays on are his only Stooges-era studio recordings.

## "L.A. Blues" — the free-jazz finale

The closing track is "L.A. Blues," roughly five minutes of unstructured noise: Mackay's saxophone skronking over disoriented dual drumming, Ron Asheton's guitar pushed into feedback, Iggy howling ([Wikipedia — Fun House](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fun_House_(The_Stooges_album))). It is the band committing fully to the free-jazz / fire-music vocabulary Mackay brought with him from Carnal Kitchen — Pharoah Sanders and Albert Ayler logic applied to a rock band's instrumentation. Critically, it is the only completely unstructured piece on the record; everything else is a song with a riff and a form. Sequencing it last makes the album read as a controlled descent: live-set rock on side one, looser blues-jam structures (the title track, "1970") in the middle of side two, then total dissolution at the end. For ARLO: the long-form rock-band-into-noise structure that hardcore, no wave, and noise rock would all later build on is *here*, on a 1970 major-label release, with a saxophonist as the catalyst.

## Iggy and Howlin' Wolf — the vocal model

Iggy Pop has cited **Howlin' Wolf** as the central influence on his vocal approach on *Fun House* ([Wikipedia — Fun House](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fun_House_(The_Stooges_album))). The model is the Chess-era Wolf: a vocal that is half-sung, half-shouted, harmonically loose but rhythmically dead-on, willing to break into pure howl when the music demands it. The performance was captured live with the band — no studio booth, no headphones, handheld dynamic mic, P.A. monitoring. The pop-screen-less mic and the room ambience are part of why the vocals sit *inside* the band rather than on top of it; the vocal is bleeding into the drum overheads and the drum overheads are bleeding into the vocal mic, and the whole thing is one acoustic event. For ARLO: when the goal is a vocal performance that feels physically *inside* a band, the engineering choice is **track in the room with the band, handheld dynamic, accept bleed, no isolation**.

## Elektra context — Danny Fields, the $5,000 contract, the commercial failure

The Stooges were signed to Elektra in September 1968 by **Danny Fields**, then Elektra's house publicist, who'd flown to Detroit on a WFMU tip to see MC5 and ended up offering contracts to both bands after the Stooges opened at the Grande Ballroom ([Wikipedia — Danny Fields](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Danny_Fields)). The contracts were unequal: MC5 got $20,000, the Stooges $5,000. *Fun House* — like the debut — sold poorly and did not chart in the Billboard 200 ([Ultimate Classic Rock — How the Stooges Created a Pre-Punk Milestone](https://ultimateclassicrock.com/stooges-fun-house/)). Elektra dropped the band in 1971. Iggy's heroin use deepened, Dave Alexander was fired, the original lineup collapsed; *Raw Power* would not appear until 1973 with Williamson on guitar, Ron Asheton on bass, Columbia, and Bowie attached. *Fun House*'s commercial failure is part of its meaning — a record made with no concession to a hit, which is precisely why punk, hardcore, and noise bands could later treat it as an unmonetized origin document. The reversal from 1970 flop to canonical proto-punk milestone took roughly a decade.

## Gallucci's "don't mess with it" philosophy

The producer's posture once the live-room setup was in place was minimal intervention. Gallucci on the band's own self-knowledge: **"I don't know. I'm not sure they knew. But they knew when it felt right, when the sound in the room felt right"** ([Perfect Sound Forever — Don Gallucci](https://www.furious.com/perfect/dongallucci.html)). His instruction to himself was **"don't mess with it"** — no overdubs of additional chords, no sweetening, no doubling guitars to thicken them ([Perfect Sound Forever — Don Gallucci](https://www.furious.com/perfect/dongallucci.html)). The keeper take is what's on the record. The arrangements are what the band played live. For ARLO: in the lineage of producers who got out of the way (Steve Albini, Owen Morris on certain records, Steve Lillywhite on *Boy*-era U2 in some respects), Don Gallucci on *Fun House* is an early canonical example. The instruction is **set the room, pick the gear, run tape, get out of the way, pick the best take, do not embellish**.

## What *Fun House* sets up — and what *Raw Power* breaks from

*Fun House* establishes a method the Stooges then abandoned. *Raw Power* (1973) is a different proposition: James Williamson's faster, more virtuosic guitar replaces Ron Asheton's wah-and-riff vocabulary; Iggy produces himself with David Bowie mixing; the studio is CBS in London; there is no sax and no free-jazz finale. The continuity is Iggy's voice and Scott Asheton's drumming. See [`stooges-raw-power.md`](./stooges-raw-power.md) for the 1973 Bowie-mix controversy. The cleanest frame for ARLO: ***Fun House* is the original Stooges; *Raw Power* is the Williamson Stooges.** *Fun House*'s lineage runs forward through punk (Sex Pistols, Damned), hardcore (Black Flag, Rollins), Detroit garage, no wave (James Chance with sax), and noise rock. *Raw Power*'s runs through glam-punk and faster late-70s punk.
