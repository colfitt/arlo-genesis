# Bob Dylan — *The Times They Are A-Changin'* (1964)

*The Times They Are A-Changin'* is the third Bob Dylan album and the last one he would make entirely on acoustic guitar and harmonica before the pivot to rock began with *Another Side of Bob Dylan* later in 1964 and accelerated with the electric half of *Bringing It All Back Home* in March 1965. It is the album where the topical-song role he had grown into during the *Freewheelin'* period is taken as far as it will go: a focused ten-song acoustic set whose center of gravity is not love or surrealism but the news. Two of its songs — "Only a Pawn in Their Game" and "The Lonesome Death of Hattie Carroll" — are tied to specific 1963 killings, written close enough to the events to function almost as journalism set to a borrowed melody. It is also the first complete Dylan album produced by **Tom Wilson** rather than John Hammond, and that detail matters more than the credit line suggests.

## Context: The End of Acoustic Dylan

*The Freewheelin' Bob Dylan* had come out in May 1963 and made Dylan a national figure on the folk circuit. Across that summer he played Newport (July 26), premiered "Only a Pawn in Their Game" at a voter-registration rally in Greenwood, Mississippi (July 6, at Pete Seeger's request), and performed at the March on Washington (August 28). The third-album sessions bracketed those events: three August dates and three October dates at Columbia Studio A. The album shipped in February 1964.

By that February, Dylan was already drafting *Another Side of Bob Dylan*, which he cut in a single all-night session in June. *Bringing It All Back Home* — electric side A, acoustic side B — followed in January 1965 with Tom Wilson again producing. *Times* is the closing bracket of a phase, not the middle of one: the last all-acoustic Dylan studio album until 1992's *Good as I Been to You*.

## The Hammond-to-Wilson Transition

The producer change is the most-misremembered detail of this period. John Hammond — the Columbia A&R legend who had signed Dylan in 1961 — produced the debut and most of *Freewheelin'*. By spring 1963, Dylan's manager Albert Grossman was openly hostile to Hammond, partly over the contract Hammond had signed Dylan to as a minor without parental consent, partly over Grossman's general territoriality. Columbia paired Dylan with **Tom Wilson**, a young African-American jazz producer the label had hired in 1963. Wilson took over for the final *Freewheelin'* session on April 24, 1963, cutting five new songs to replace withdrawn tracks. For *The Times They Are A-Changin'*, **Wilson produced every session**. Hammond is not on this record.

Wilson is consistently undersold in Dylan histories. Out of Harvard, he had founded Transition Records in 1956 (recording Sun Ra and Cecil Taylor before either had broad jazz reputations), and joined Columbia as a staff producer — one of the first African-Americans in that role at a major label. His better-known later work — overdubbing electric band onto "The Sound of Silence" without asking Simon & Garfunkel, producing the first Velvet Underground & Nico tracks, producing the early Mothers of Invention — postdates this album. Dylan himself, asked later if Wilson had given him "the rock sound," answered: "He did to a certain extent. That is true. He did. He had a sound in mind."

For *Times*, that sound was deliberately spare. Dylan plays acoustic guitar, blows harp in a rack, and sings. No overdubs, no harmony vocals, no bass, no second guitar. Wilson's contribution here is editorial more than sonic: pacing the sessions, picking takes, leaving Dylan alone when leaving him alone was the right call. The "thin wild mercury sound" — Dylan's famous self-description — belongs to *Blonde on Blonde* (1966), Nashville, Bob Johnston. It is not this record. *Times* is the opposite aesthetic: bone, no broth.

## Sessions at Columbia Studio A

All six sessions ran at Columbia Studio A, 799 Seventh Avenue, New York City — the same midtown room where Miles Davis cut *Kind of Blue* and where Dylan had recorded the previous two albums. The session log is unusually clean to summarize:

- **August 6, 1963** — Eight songs attempted; only "North Country Blues" yielded a usable master.
- **August 7, 1963** — Master takes for "Ballad of Hollis Brown," "With God on Our Side," "Only a Pawn in Their Game," "Boots of Spanish Leather."
- **August 12, 1963** — Nothing usable for the album (some takes later surface on bootlegs).
- **October 23, 1963** — Master takes for "The Lonesome Death of Hattie Carroll" and "When the Ship Comes In."
- **October 24, 1963** — Master takes for "The Times They Are a-Changin'" and "One Too Many Mornings."
- **October 31, 1963** — Single-song session: "Restless Farewell," the closer.

Between the August and October blocks, Dylan played Newport, performed at the March on Washington, and absorbed an unflattering *Newsweek* profile (early November on the stands but the reporting happened in October) that accused him of inventing his backstory and lifting "Blowin' in the Wind" from a New Jersey high school student. "Restless Farewell" — written and cut in a single session on October 31 — is in part the answer to that piece.

## The Title Track

"The Times They Are a-Changin'" is the rare Dylan song he has talked about as a deliberate construction. He told Cameron Crowe (for the *Biograph* liner notes) that he set out to write "a big song, with short concise verses that piled up on each other in a hypnotic way" — an anthem that could function as a group declaration rather than personal complaint. The melodic skeleton comes out of the Irish/Scots "Come All Ye" ballad tradition; commentators have also linked the move to Dylan's earlier "Paths of Victory," itself built on a Carter Family tune. The closing couplet rephrases Mark 10:31. The song is in 3/4 and rides on a chord pattern simple enough that any folk-club audience could pick it up by the second verse, which was the point.

## "Only a Pawn in Their Game" and Medgar Evers

Medgar Evers, NAACP field secretary in Mississippi, was shot in his driveway in Jackson on June 12, 1963 by Byron De La Beckwith. Dylan wrote "Only a Pawn in Their Game" within weeks. He premiered it on July 6 at a voter-registration rally in Greenwood, Mississippi — Pete Seeger had asked him to come down and play — and again at Newport later that month. He recorded it August 7, the first complete take serving as the master. At the March on Washington on August 28, the day Zantzinger was sentenced, Dylan performed it from the podium between Baez sets and King's "I Have a Dream" speech. He received only scattered applause; the song's argument — that the trigger man is a pawn of the politicians and the wealthy who teach poor whites to fear Black neighbors — was not what every marcher wanted to hear. Activist Bernice Johnson Reagon later credited it as "the very first song that showed the poor white was as victimized by discrimination as the poor black."

## "The Lonesome Death of Hattie Carroll"

Hattie Carroll was a 51-year-old African-American barmaid at the Emerson Hotel in Baltimore. On February 9, 1963, at a white-tie Spinsters' Ball, a 24-year-old tobacco-farm heir named William Devereux "Billy" Zantzinger — drunk, in tails — struck her with a toy cane after she was slow bringing a drink. She collapsed within minutes and died eight hours later at Mercy Hospital of a brain hemorrhage. Charged with murder, Zantzinger was convicted by a three-judge panel on the reduced count of manslaughter on August 28, 1963 — the same day as the March on Washington — and sentenced to six months in county jail, the start deferred so he could harvest his tobacco crop.

Dylan wrote the song almost immediately and recorded it on October 23, 1963 in two complete takes, adding the harmonica break only on the second. The melody is borrowed from "Mary Hamilton" (Child #173). The structural trick — three verses of escalating detail, each closed with the same chorus telling the listener to weep but not yet — would not have worked at a faster tempo. Dylan plays it almost mournfully slow.

## The Other Topical Songs

"With God on Our Side" was first performed at New York's Town Hall on April 12, 1963. Its melodic shape closely follows Dominic Behan's "The Patriot Game" (itself derived from "The Merry Month of May"); Behan publicly accused Dylan of plagiarism and Dylan didn't respond. The lyric is a verse-by-verse historical indictment — every American war from the Indian campaigns through Vietnam justified by the assumption of divine sanction, ending with the question of whether Judas Iscariot also had God on his side.

"When the Ship Comes In" was written, per Clinton Heylin, in a hotel room "in a fit of pique" after a desk clerk refused to admit Dylan until Joan Baez vouched for him. The Pirate Jenny / Brecht *Threepenny Opera* template is unmistakable — the avenging ship arriving for the deserving — and Dylan and Baez sang it together at the March on Washington before "Only a Pawn." Cut October 23.

"Ballad of Hollis Brown" — an older song attempted for *Freewheelin'* and held back — tells of a South Dakota farmer who shoots his wife, five children, and himself rather than watch them starve. The melody is a single drone-modal figure repeated through twelve verses; the only motion is the harmonica answer.

"North Country Blues," the lone keeper from August 6, is Dylan's portrait of the dying iron-range mining towns of his northern Minnesota childhood (he grew up in Hibbing), sung in the first person from a miner's widow's point of view — the first time Dylan wrote a full narrative from a woman's perspective.

## The Two Love Songs (Such As They Are)

"One Too Many Mornings" (October 24) is the album's most delicate track — a quiet, exhausted song about leaving a lover at three a.m., with a vocal line that lifts and falls across one of the simplest arpeggio figures on the record. Dylan later electrified it for the 1966 world tour with the Hawks; the studio version is hushed.

"Boots of Spanish Leather" inverts the "Girl from the North Country" structure — two lovers separated, letters going back and forth — and uses the same melodic shape Dylan had picked up from Martin Carthy's arrangement of "Scarborough Fair" during his late-1962 England trip. Drafted in Italy in early 1963 while Dylan was looking for Suze Rotolo. Salon's Bill Wyman called it "an abstract classic and one of the purest, most confounding folk songs of the time."

## "Restless Farewell" and the *Newsweek* Wound

The final session, October 31, was given over entirely to "Restless Farewell." A *Newsweek* profile by Andrea Svedberg, published in early November but known to Dylan and Grossman by late October, alleged that Dylan had concocted his hobo backstory (he had — his parents were middle-class Hibbing Jews named Zimmerman) and floated a false rumor that he had bought "Blowin' in the Wind" from a New Jersey teenager named Lorre Wyatt. Dylan's response was to lift the melody of the traditional Irish-Scots drinking song "The Parting Glass," write a series of valedictory verses about putting all of it behind him, and close the album with it. The closer is the one moment on *Times* where the protest singer turns the camera back on himself.

## Liner Notes and Cover Photograph

The original LP arrived with one of Dylan's first major prose poems on the back: "11 Outlined Epitaphs," a free-verse autobiography running through Hibbing as "a dyin' town," Woody Guthrie as the idol he was outgrowing, his ambivalence about the folk-revival audience, and an oblique answer to the *Newsweek* piece. The text reads in places like a draft of *Tarantula*. This is the first time a Dylan album functions as a literary object as well as a record.

The cover photograph is by **Barry Feinstein**. Dylan, age 22, is shot in tight black-and-white, head down, looking severe — Feinstein later said the resemblance to a Woody Guthrie portrait was deliberate. The session ran roughly ten minutes at a penthouse apartment belonging to photographer John Cort; Dylan picked the final frame himself off the contact sheet, which was unusual for the period. Feinstein became, with Daniel Kramer and Jerry Schatzberg, one of Dylan's three signature mid-decade photographers. (Don Hunstein had shot the iconic *Freewheelin'* cover; he is not on this record.)

## Release, Charts, Reception

Columbia released the album on February 10, 1964. It peaked at No. 20 on the Billboard 200 and, in 1965, at No. 4 in the UK. It eventually went gold in both territories. The contemporary reception was warm rather than ecstatic — some reviewers found it short on the humor and musical range of *Freewheelin'* — and a current of grumbling that the album was relentlessly grim runs through the back catalogue of period write-ups. NPR's Tim Riley would later call it "'Masters of War' stretched out into a concept album." That is fair, and it is also the point: *Times* is Dylan committing fully to the role the press had assigned him, in order — as the very next album would make explicit — to walk away from it.

## Legacy and the Pivot That Follows

Within six months of release, Dylan had cut *Another Side of Bob Dylan* (June 9, 1964, in one all-night session, again with Wilson). In January 1965 he and Wilson tracked *Bringing It All Back Home* — electric side A, acoustic side B. Wilson would then produce the first session of *Highway 61 Revisited*, including "Like a Rolling Stone," before being replaced by Bob Johnston in mid-1965. By the time you reach Newport 1965 and the boos, the acoustic protest period that *Times* defines is already over.

But the album itself does not feel transitional from inside. It is exactly the record it set out to be — ten songs, one guitar, one harmonica, one voice — and remains the cleanest single statement of mid-century American protest folk on a major label.
