## What the record is, and where it sits in the catalog

*Rain Dogs* is Tom Waits's ninth studio album, released 30 September 1985 on Island Records — the second installment of his "Island trilogy" alongside *Swordfishtrombones* (1983) and *Franks Wild Years* (1987), the three-record arc on which Waits abandoned the boozy lounge-piano persona of his 1970s Asylum catalog and rebuilt himself as a junk-orchestra ringmaster ([Wikipedia — Rain Dogs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rain_Dogs); [uDiscover — Tom Waits' Island Trilogy](https://www.udiscovermusic.com/stories/swordfishtrombones-rain-dogs-franks-wild-years-tom-waits-feature/)). It dispelled any doubts that the *Swordfishtrombones* turn was a one-off. The producer credit is **Tom Waits alone**, though his wife Kathleen Brennan is, by every account, the silent conceptual collaborator who made the whole period possible ([Wikipedia — Kathleen Brennan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kathleen_Brennan)). The album runs 19 tracks across 53:46 and is widely treated as the most stylistically eclectic of the three — saxophonist Arno Hecht called it "Tom Waits's equivalent of *Sgt. Pepper's*" ([uDiscover — Tom Waits in the Studio: The Island Albums](https://www.udiscovermusic.com/stories/tom-waits-in-the-studio-island-years-feature/)).

## The studio question — RCA Studio A on 6th Avenue, mixed at RPM and Quadrasonic

The cleanest single fact: *Rain Dogs* was **tracked at the old RCA Studio A on 6th Avenue in Midtown Manhattan**, and **mixed at RPM Studio and Quadrasonic Studio** in New York ([albumlinernotes.com — Rain Dogs liner notes](http://albumlinernotes.com/Rain_Dogs.html)). Marc Ribot has described the tracking room as "a big, old studio that doesn't exist anymore — the old RCA Studio A on 6th Avenue in New York, Midtown" ([Guitar World — Marc Ribot on Recording Tom Waits Classics](https://www.guitarworld.com/features/marc-ribot-connection)). The engineer was **Robert Musso** (who also played banjo on "Gun Street Girl"); assistant engineer Dennis Ferrante at RCA; mix-room assistants Jeff Lippay at RPM (who mixed "Downtown Train" separately) and Tom Gonzales at Quadrasonic; mastering by Howie Weinberg at Masterdisk ([albumlinernotes.com — Rain Dogs liner notes](http://albumlinernotes.com/Rain_Dogs.html)). For ARLO's retrieval: do *not* answer "recorded at RPM Studios" full stop — RPM was the mix room. The big, dead, gone Midtown room is where the noises got made.

## Songwriting in a Washington Street basement, with field recordings

Waits composed most of the record over a two-month stretch in fall 1984 from a basement room at Washington and Horatio Streets in Lower Manhattan, walking the neighborhood with a cassette recorder to capture street sound as a pre-production mood board ([Wikipedia — Rain Dogs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rain_Dogs)). The album's title concept — "rain dogs" as people the city has unmoored, who can no longer find their way home because the rain has washed out their scent trail — is geographically literal: Brennan and Waits had just relocated from Los Angeles to Manhattan, and the change of city is audible. The Asylum records were Los Angeles; *Rain Dogs* is the dock, the tenement, the after-hours bar in a port town.

## The Brennan partnership — Captain Beefheart, "Hang Down Your Head," and the dare

Every credible account of Waits's mid-period reinvention puts Kathleen Brennan at the center of it. Brennan, a script analyst Waits met on *One from the Heart* in 1980 and married soon after, introduced him to Captain Beefheart's *Trout Mask Replica* and gave him, per multiple sources, the "dare" to produce *Swordfishtrombones* himself rather than continue with longtime producer Bones Howe ([Wikipedia — Kathleen Brennan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kathleen_Brennan); [uDiscover — Island Albums feature](https://www.udiscovermusic.com/stories/tom-waits-in-the-studio-island-years-feature/)). On *Rain Dogs* she takes her first formal co-writing credit, on **"Hang Down Your Head"**, the album's southern-soul-inflected ballad; the album is dedicated "to Kathleen" ([albumlinernotes.com — Rain Dogs liner notes](http://albumlinernotes.com/Rain_Dogs.html)). By the early 1990s she would be Waits's full co-writer and co-producer of record. For ARLO: the *Rain Dogs* model for inviting a creative partner deeper is **start with one shared credit on the most emotionally exposed song, not the most experimental one**.

## Marc Ribot's first Waits session — the ESP knockoff Tele and the hollowbody

*Rain Dogs* introduced **Marc Ribot** to Tom Waits; the partnership has continued across nearly every Waits record since. Ribot arrived with what he has bluntly described as "a knockoff, fake thing" made by ESP in Japan: "It was awful but also beautiful. Poverty accounted for many of my choices back then" ([Guitar World — Marc Ribot on Recording Tom Waits Classics](https://www.guitarworld.com/features/marc-ribot-connection)). Waits demoed songs on his own beat-up hollowbody, with Ribot recalling: "He had this ratty old hollow body, and he would spell out the grooves. It wasn't a mechanical kind of recording at all" ([Wikipedia — Rain Dogs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rain_Dogs)). The methodological detail to extract: there were **no written charts** — Waits would play a song through, set a rhythm and a vibe, and the band would build parts on the spot ([uDiscover — Island Albums feature](https://www.udiscovermusic.com/stories/tom-waits-in-the-studio-island-years-feature/)). Ribot's own approach was character-driven — "Tom thinks of the guitar as a character in a play, you know? It's the voice of one of the characters in a story he's writing" — and governed by the destructive mantra "How can I destroy it?", with cheap pedals plugged straight into amps and true-bypass routing chosen so no signal-path color smoothed his angularity away ([Guitar World — Marc Ribot on Recording Tom Waits Classics](https://www.guitarworld.com/features/marc-ribot-connection)). The single most-cited verbal direction Ribot received: **"Play it like a midget's bar mitzvah"** — gestural, scenic, anti-technical, which is how Waits drove the whole room.

## The rhythm section — Larry Taylor and Stephen Hodges, summoned from California

The load-bearing rhythm section is **Larry "The Mole" Taylor** on upright and electric bass and **Stephen Hodges** on drums, both flown out from California — both had played on *Swordfishtrombones* and both became fixtures in Waits's touring band ([Rolling Stone — Larry Taylor Obituary](https://www.rollingstone.com/music/music-news/canned-heat-bassist-larry-taylor-obituary-874523/); [Big Ego Records — Stephen Hodges artist page](https://www.bigegorecords.com/stephen-hodges)). Taylor came out of Canned Heat's classic 1967 lineup (Monterey Pop, Woodstock) and brought a deep blues-and-jug-band feel to the upright that anchors "Singapore" and "Cemetery Polka" against the chaos above. Hodges is the more methodologically specific case: Waits asked him to play the kit **avoiding cymbals**, leaning instead on toms, marimbas, parade drums, and whatever hittable surface was in arm's reach. Hodges's own framing of the directive — "Tom always wanted orchestra accuracy with back-alley blues — it had to be loose, and had to be accurate" — is a portable production aesthetic ([Big Ego Records — Stephen Hodges artist page](https://www.bigegorecords.com/stephen-hodges)). Greg Cohen and Tony Garnier handle additional bass on some tracks (Cohen on the upright for "Gun Street Girl"); William Schimmel plays accordion across the polka and tango cuts ([albumlinernotes.com — Rain Dogs liner notes](http://albumlinernotes.com/Rain_Dogs.html)).

## Junkyard percussion — Michael Blair's traveling collection

The "junkyard orchestra" descriptor that follows the Island records around is concretely the work of percussionist **Michael Blair**, recommended into the sessions by engineer Robert Musso. Blair brought an extraordinary palette: tack drums from China, temple bowls from Japan, wood blocks from Thailand, roughly forty cymbals, car parts, kitchen appliances, parade drums, marimba, gong, bowed saw, and metal percussion, shakers, tambourines, and bells from around the world ([uDiscover — Island Albums feature](https://www.udiscovermusic.com/stories/tom-waits-in-the-studio-island-years-feature/)). The specific *Rain Dogs* anecdotes: on **"Singapore"**, Blair built the drum sound by striking a discarded chest of drawers with a hammer; on **"Jockey Full of Bourbon"**, the percussion bed was a barstool and a wooden two-by-four ([Rock and Roll Globe — Rain Dogs at 40](https://rockandrollglobe.com/rock/such-a-crumbling-beauty-tom-waits-rain-dogs-turns-40/)). Blair's summary of the philosophy: "Let's have a band of people be painters and sculptors telling these stories, rather than 'I've got a good jazz band'" ([uDiscover — Island Albums feature](https://www.udiscovermusic.com/stories/tom-waits-in-the-studio-island-years-feature/)). On **"Clap Hands"**, the interlocking marimba rhythm was constructed by overdubbing two separate marimba parts — Bobby Previte first, then Blair on top. For ARLO: **before you reach for the kit, walk the room and decide which non-instrument has the right grain**.

## Waits's own sound-hunting philosophy

Waits's canonical statement of his sound-sourcing philosophy from the *Rain Dogs* press cycle: **"If I want a sound, I usually feel better if I've chased it and killed it, skinned it and cooked it"** ([Wikipedia — Rain Dogs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rain_Dogs)). The corollary, given in interviews of the period: he sometimes preferred hitting a bathroom door with a two-by-four to using a snare, because the door had the sound he wanted. The methodological generalization: in this aesthetic, the studio is not a place to record a drum kit; it is a place to *find* the percussion instrument that already exists in the room, even if it is a piece of furniture — the opposite of a sample-pack workflow, closer to musique concrète routed through a song format. For a contemporary application of the same philosophy, see [Big Thief's nomadic recording approach](../artists/big-thief-nomadic-recording.md).

## The guest list — Keith Richards, Robert Quine, Chris Spedding, John Lurie

The guest-musician list is unusually deep and stylistically wide. **Keith Richards** plays guitar on "Big Black Mariah," "Union Square," and "Blind Love" (with backing vocals on the last), bringing a Stones-school rhythm feel; **Robert Quine** (Richard Hell & the Voidoids, Lou Reed) plays guitar on "Blind Love"; **Chris Spedding** plays alongside Ribot on "Singapore"; the Uptown Horns (Bob Funk, "Hollywood" Paul Litteral, Arno Hecht, Crispin Cioe) plus **Ralph Carney** on reeds and **John Lurie** on additional saxophone fill out the horns ([Wikipedia — Rain Dogs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rain_Dogs); [albumlinernotes.com — Rain Dogs liner notes](http://albumlinernotes.com/Rain_Dogs.html)). The combinatorial logic: each song on *Rain Dogs* is essentially **a different band per track**, hand-picked for the genre signature Waits wanted, with the Taylor/Hodges/Blair core providing continuity. That is the structural reason the album feels like a revue rather than a band record.

## Track-level — "Singapore" through "Anywhere I Lay My Head"

**"Singapore"** opens the record with a Mitteleuropean oompah march read by critics as Waits's most explicit Brecht-Weill homage — over a careening 2-step, Waits debuts the *Sprechgesang* speaking-into-singing delivery he would reuse for the rest of his career, percussion bed built from Blair's hammer-on-furniture work ([The Quietus — Rain Dogs at 40](https://thequietus.com/opinion-and-essays/anniversary/tom-waits-rain-dogs-anniversary-review/)). **"Jockey Full of Bourbon"** — the lead single — is Ribot's Cubano-noir showcase: a percussive mambo with the guitar foregrounded and the vocal almost a secondary character, phrasing shifting between staccato/angular and intentionally slurred ([Wikipedia — Jockey Full of Bourbon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jockey_Full_of_Bourbon)). **"Clap Hands"** is the marimba-and-gong track. **"Cemetery Polka"** is a minor-key, accordion-led polka portrait of a fictional dysfunctional family. **"Hang Down Your Head"**, the Brennan co-write, is the album's southern-soul ballad — Ribot's solo on it has since been transcribed and taught widely as a study piece ([Eric Haugen Guitar — Hang Down Your Head Solo Transcription](https://www.erichaugenguitar.com/products/tom-waits-hang-down-your-head-solo)). **"Time"** is the slow, accordion-tinged waltz that critics consistently pick out as the emotional anchor of the otherwise jagged sequence. **"Downtown Train"** is the most conventional song on the record — a rock ballad later covered to top-3 commercial success by Rod Stewart (Trevor Horn production) in 1989 — and the one track Jeff Lippay mixed separately at RPM ([albumlinernotes.com — Rain Dogs liner notes](http://albumlinernotes.com/Rain_Dogs.html); [uDiscover — Tom Waits' Island Trilogy](https://www.udiscovermusic.com/stories/swordfishtrombones-rain-dogs-franks-wild-years-tom-waits-feature/)). Track 19, "Anywhere I Lay My Head," is the closing brass-band benediction.

## Genre-collage as structural commitment

The methodological generalization to extract: *Rain Dogs* commits to **genre-per-song rather than genre-per-record** as a structural principle. A polka follows a mambo follows a ballad follows a Weillian cabaret march follows a southern-soul lament, and the through-line is not stylistic continuity but the consistency of Waits's character voicing and the rhythm-section core. The Brecht-Weill influence is the deepest stylistic thread — the German cabaret pulse of "Singapore," "Cemetery Polka," and "Tango Till They're Sore" sits over the same blues and parade-march substrate that informs "Big Black Mariah" and "Union Square" ([The Quietus — Rain Dogs at 40](https://thequietus.com/opinion-and-essays/anniversary/tom-waits-rain-dogs-anniversary-review/); [Rock and Roll Globe — Rain Dogs at 40](https://rockandrollglobe.com/rock/such-a-crumbling-beauty-tom-waits-rain-dogs-turns-40/)). For ARLO: when an artist is worried that their album draft sounds "too scattered," *Rain Dogs* is the proof-of-concept that **a 19-song record can change genre every track and still feel like a single artifact**, provided the character voice and rhythm-section identity hold.

## Position in the Island trilogy — what *Rain Dogs* sets up

*Franks Wild Years* (1987) keeps the Ribot / Blair / Taylor / Hodges core and the genre-collage method but threads them through a single narrative — songs written for a stage play about the character Frank O'Brien from *Swordfishtrombones*' "Frank's Wild Years." *Rain Dogs* is the middle panel where the band identity, the percussion philosophy, and the Brennan partnership are all in place but the conceptual frame is loose — 19 separate character studies rather than one extended one. If an artist is at the "I have a sound but no narrative arc" stage, *Rain Dogs* is the model. If they have an arc, *Franks Wild Years* is the next step.

## Anti-hallucination notes

The Waits production folklore is famously elaborate, and several widely-repeated stories — "broken piano recorded in a bathroom," various *Mule Variations*-era pasture recordings, chair-and-trash-can sessions — belong to *later* records (1990s onward) and not to *Rain Dogs*. The verified *Rain Dogs*-specific objects are: a chest of drawers hit with a hammer ("Singapore"), a barstool and a two-by-four ("Jockey Full of Bourbon"), Blair's roughly forty cymbals plus the international collection, and Ribot's ESP knockoff Telecaster. The studio facts: tracked at the old RCA Studio A on 6th Avenue, mixed at RPM and Quadrasonic, engineered by Robert Musso, mastered by Howie Weinberg. The producer credit is Tom Waits alone. Anything more elaborate — broken pianos in bathrooms, specific microphone models on specific vocals — should be checked against album-specific sourcing before being asserted.
