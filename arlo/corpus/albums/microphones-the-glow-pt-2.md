# The Microphones — *The Glow, Pt. 2* (2001)

**Artist:** The Microphones (Phil Elverum)
**Year:** 2001 (released September 11, 2001 on K Records and P.W. Elverum & Sun, Ltd.) ([Wikipedia, *The Glow Pt. 2*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Glow_Pt._2))
**Producer / Engineer / Performer:** Phil Elverum (primary; nearly all instruments, all production)
**Studio:** Dub Narcotic Studio, Olympia, Washington — Calvin Johnson's K Records facility in the former Olympia Knitting Mills building ([Tape Op, Calvin Johnson](https://tapeop.com/interviews/32/calvin-johnson/read/); [Tape Op, Phil Elvrum](https://tapeop.com/interviews/32/phil-elvrum))
**Recording Dates:** May 23, 2000 – March 23, 2001 — roughly ten months of long sessions, often early-morning and alone ([Wikipedia, *The Glow Pt. 2*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Glow_Pt._2))

## The Session Story In One Paragraph

*The Glow, Pt. 2* is the album where a 22-year-old Phil Elverum, given a key to Calvin Johnson's Dub Narcotic Studio, decided a one-person home-studio record could be as physically immersive as a commercial release — provided every artifact the tape gave you became compositional material. Elverum recorded between May 23, 2000 and March 23, 2001 in the former Olympia Knitting Mills building, working long sessions alone, composing as he tracked, layering dozens of voices on an Otari 8-track and an MCI 16-track running in parallel and bouncing aggressively to free up channels ([Wikipedia, *The Glow Pt. 2*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Glow_Pt._2); [Tape Op, Phil Elvrum](https://tapeop.com/interviews/32/phil-elvrum)). He treated tape saturation, mic clipping, and cassette-deck distortion not as accidents to fix in mastering but as the actual sound of the record. The result is a 66-minute song cycle that moves from near-silence room tone to blown-out percussive crush within a single song — the highest-water-mark album of his Microphones era, and the canonical reference for "Elverum production" before the Mount Eerie reinvention.

## Dub Narcotic Studio — The Room And Its Conditions

Dub Narcotic Studio occupies a roughly 110-by-45-foot open floor in Olympia's former Olympia Knitting Mills building, with 11.5-foot ceilings, no internal columns, and windows on three sides ([Tape Op, Calvin Johnson](https://tapeop.com/interviews/32/calvin-johnson/read/)). Calvin Johnson — Beat Happening singer, K Records founder — built it out in 1993 in a basement room and moved the operation into the larger Knitting Mills space later in the decade, adding the 2-inch 16-track in the upgrade. The signal-flow philosophy is the opposite of the isolation-booth tradition: no separated tracking rooms, ambient leakage welcome, and signal bypasses the mixing board on the way to tape (preamp directly into the deck; the board is for monitoring and mixdown). The studio is also the K Records office and warehouse — a workplace, not a destination facility.

Phil Elverum was given a key in September 1997: "Calvin just gave me a key to this room. Like, 'Do you want to record in my studio all the time?'" ([Tape Op, Phil Elvrum](https://tapeop.com/interviews/32/phil-elvrum)). For *The Glow, Pt. 2* he lived next door at the Track House, the Olympia musicians' communal house, and could monitor the studio from his roof. The "studio" was not a booking — it was a room he had continuous access to, which is the precondition for the album's slow, layered, ten-month build.

## The Tape Stack — Otari 8-Track And MCI 16-Track In Parallel

The technical core of *The Glow, Pt. 2* is two analog tape machines running in concert: an Otari 8-track on half-inch tape (Elverum's original Anacortes workflow rig) and a 16-track MCI on 2-inch tape (the Dub Narcotic house deck) ([Culturefly, Dissecting Phil Elverum](https://culturefly.co.uk/dissecting-phil-elverum-part-i-introduction-the-microphones/); [Wikipedia, *The Glow Pt. 2*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Glow_Pt._2)). Elverum had recorded on 8-track for two years before moving south and described his initial reaction to the upgrade as suspicion: "I was so used to recording on 8-track... When the 16-track came, I was like, 'That is so ridiculous, who needs 16 tracks?'" ([Tape Op, Phil Elvrum](https://tapeop.com/interviews/32/phil-elvrum)). Once he adapted, the 16-track became a Wall-of-Sound machine: "I do a lot of bouncing on the 16-track. Do like 12 guitars or pianos and bounce it down to get the Wall of Sound, one instrument at a time."

This bouncing workflow is structurally important. The dense vertical stacks on the title track and "I Want Wind to Blow" are not the work of a board with unlimited returns — they are the artifact of repeatedly mixing layered passes down to two tracks and freeing up the rest of the tape to add more. Each bounce generation imparts more tape saturation and haze, so the final element sits against an accumulated noise floor that is partly composition, partly bounce-chain artifact. This is what a "wall of sound" sounds like when the route is tape generation loss rather than digital emulation.

## Lo-Fi As A Compositional Decision

Calling *The Glow, Pt. 2* "lo-fi" obscures what is actually going on. The record is recorded with high-quality microphones (the Neumann U-67 is on Elverum's rig, alongside Sennheiser MKH 405 on drums) into well-maintained tape machines in a large room with good acoustics ([Tape Op, Phil Elvrum](https://tapeop.com/interviews/32/phil-elvrum); [Wikipedia, *The Glow Pt. 2*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Glow_Pt._2)). The lo-fi character is not an equipment limitation — it is an authored decision applied on top of capable infrastructure. Elverum's stated principle: "One of my favorite techniques is to just turn the mic up really loud. It opens up a whole new world of sound possibilities" ([Consequence, *The Glow Pt. 2* at 20](https://consequence.net/2021/09/the-microphones-the-glow-pt-2-anniversary-phil-elverum-interview/)). The Sennheiser MKH 405 on drums was driven into compression and clipping deliberately; vocals are mixed deliberately low; the cassette-deck-as-distortion-box trick (more on this below) is wired in by choice, not because no guitar amp was available.

His broader stance: "The idea of perfection never appealed to me; it seemed boring" ([Consequence, *The Glow Pt. 2* at 20](https://consequence.net/2021/09/the-microphones-the-glow-pt-2-anniversary-phil-elverum-interview/)). The lo-fi aesthetic here is a producer who could have made a clean record and chose not to, because the artifacts were carrying meaning he wanted to keep. The record is the cleanest available counter-example to the assumption that "lo-fi" implies "amateur" — it is *more* edited than most contemporaneous indie rock, the editing just runs toward grit rather than away from it.

## The Cassette-Deck Distortion Trick

The signature guitar distortion on *The Glow, Pt. 2* — the gritty, narrow-band, almost AM-radio crush — comes from a specific signal-path hack: Elverum ran his guitar into the microphone input of a thrift-store cassette deck, then took the headphone-jack output of that deck and fed it into a guitar amp ([Wikipedia, *The Glow Pt. 2*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Glow_Pt._2); [Culturefly, Dissecting Phil Elverum](https://culturefly.co.uk/dissecting-phil-elverum-part-i-introduction-the-microphones/)). The cassette deck's mic preamp clips immediately under guitar-level signal, its tape-bias circuit colors the result, and the headphone output is hot enough to overdrive the amp — a free two-stage distortion box made from a $5 piece of consumer electronics. The guitar riff on "I Want Wind to Blow" has the unmistakable thin-and-crushed cassette-mic-preamp character, and so do several other guitar parts across the album. The takeaway: distortion is not one effect, it is a family of timbres, and home-studio producers have free options once they think in terms of signal-path stages rather than pedal choices.

## Dynamic Range As Subject Matter

The most-cited quality of *The Glow, Pt. 2* is its dynamic range. The album moves "from barely audible recordings of room tone to noisy blown-out passages filled with crashing percussion" ([Stereogum, *Glow Pt. 2* Turns 20](https://stereogum.com/2160063/the-microphones-the-glow-pt-2-turns-20/reviews/the-anniversary)). On "I Want Wind to Blow," near-whispered vocal and piano give way to the clipped-percussion blast that drives the central riff. On "The Moon," whispered vocal sits over close-miked acoustic guitar with effectively no compression. The title track holds the line "my blood flows harshly" as a sustained vocal for fourteen seconds against an arrangement that swells and falls beneath it ([Wikipedia, *The Glow Pt. 2*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Glow_Pt._2)).

This is *the inverse* of the headphones-intimacy stance of *Vespertine*. *Vespertine* keeps a low consistent loudness ceiling and lets the listener lean in. *The Glow, Pt. 2* keeps broad dynamic range as the actual content — the whisper-to-roar movement is the song. Volume set for the quiet sections produces blown-out roar in the loud ones; volume set for the roar loses the whispers entirely. Elverum mixes vocals deliberately low, "perhaps intentionally encouraging listener engagement" ([Stereogum, *Glow Pt. 2* Turns 20](https://stereogum.com/2160063/the-microphones-the-glow-pt-2-turns-20/reviews/the-anniversary)). The record demands physical participation: volume-knob work, not passive playback.

## "I Want Wind to Blow" — The Opener As Method Demonstration

Recorded on January 1, 2001, the opener is the most-studied production object in the record because Elverum walked through its construction on *Song Exploder* ([Song Exploder, The Microphones](https://songexploder.net/the-microphones)). Piano is recorded at distance from the microphone to capture the natural room sound of the Knitting Mills space, rather than close-miked for clarity. Guitar parts are designed to sonically resemble water — achieved by miking both the amp and the strings simultaneously and balancing the proximity character ([Wikipedia, *The Glow Pt. 2*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Glow_Pt._2)). Two physical stereo systems were used to create panned stereophonic effects, with layered guitar tracks bouncing aggressively left-to-right ([Consequence, *The Glow Pt. 2* at 20](https://consequence.net/2021/09/the-microphones-the-glow-pt-2-anniversary-phil-elverum-interview/)). Harmonium and güiro sit underneath. The crushed-clipping percussion on the central riff is the Sennheiser MKH 405 driven into clip-as-feature. The song is in microcosm the album's whole method: dynamic extremes, panned-guitar density, percussion-as-distortion, and a foreground vocal that requires the listener to lean in.

## Per-Track Production Notes

- **"The Glow Pt. 2"** — Title track; the fourteen-second sustained "my blood flows harshly" vocal hold is the marquee performance, set against an arrangement that swells beneath it. ([Wikipedia, *The Glow Pt. 2*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Glow_Pt._2))
- **"The Moon"** — Acoustic-guitar chord figure under whispered vocal; nearly all of the album's quiet-pole sonic identity is concentrated here.
- **"Headless Horseman"** — One of the record's compressed-narrative moments; built around an acoustic figure with intrusion of louder percussive elements.
- **"I'll Not Contain You"** — Operates on the call-and-response panning logic used elsewhere on the record; vocals and instruments answer each other across hard-left / hard-right channels.
- **"Map"** — Opens and closes on a foghorn motif that leads into distorted organs and a heavy kick drum, the clearest single example of how Elverum used sound objects (not just instruments) as song-structural elements. ([Wikipedia, *The Glow Pt. 2*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Glow_Pt._2))
- **"I Want to Be Cold"** — Texture-driven; minimal harmonic motion, much of the song is the recording itself foregrounded.
- **"The Mansion"** — Closing-region track in the song cycle; an example of the album's late-passage compositional sprawl.
- **"Instrumental"** (first version) — Built around finger-snaps with a strict call-and-response panning structure that pre-figures the more aggressive stereo work on the opener. ([Wikipedia, *The Glow Pt. 2*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Glow_Pt._2))
- **"I Felt My Size"** — Atmospheric noise opens into chaotic strummed guitar that resolves into synth, piano, and double-time drums; one of the record's clearest examples of within-song genre-mutation.

## Tape Artifacts As Authored Content

Elverum's posture toward tape misbehaviour is non-corrective. He described a passage where "those tracks were kind of not playing back correctly, but I liked how it sounded" and kept the resulting shudder as the final mix ([Consequence, *The Glow Pt. 2* at 20](https://consequence.net/2021/09/the-microphones-the-glow-pt-2-anniversary-phil-elverum-interview/)). His mic-placement philosophy is similarly off-axis: "Try putting the mic somewhere else, besides where it's supposed to go, instead of putting it in front of the thing" ([Tape Op, Phil Elvrum](https://tapeop.com/interviews/32/phil-elvrum)). Multi-tracked piano layers reach the point where instrument identity dissolves: "Layering four tracks of that gives you a crazy wash of tone, it doesn't sound like a piano anymore... it sounds like nothing, a totally new instrument." The discipline here is *not knowing what something will sound like* until it is on tape, and treating what comes back as the primary material rather than an attempt at a pre-imagined target.

## The Olympia / K Records Network

*The Glow, Pt. 2* is also a network artifact. The credited engineers — Karl Blau, Khaela Maricich, Kyle Field, Jacob Navarro, Eddy Blau, Dave Matthies, Nate Ashley, Jenn-Kliese — are not session pros; they are the Olympia and Anacortes circle who passed in and out of the room during the ten-month build ([Wikipedia, *The Glow Pt. 2*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Glow_Pt._2)). Karl Blau, Elverum's collaborator in the trio D+ and his musical brother since Anacortes record-store after-hours sessions, is credited as "Chamberlin Rhythmate 40" on the album — Elverum's joke-attribution of the drum machine to Blau ([Karl Blau bio](https://www.karlblau.com/about); [Wikipedia, *The Glow Pt. 2*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Glow_Pt._2)). Calvin Johnson provided not just the room but the institutional cover of K Records' DIY-ideology, the no-mixing-board-on-the-way-to-tape philosophy, and the assumption that ambient noise belongs in the recording ([Tape Op, Calvin Johnson](https://tapeop.com/interviews/32/calvin-johnson/read/)). Mirah was present throughout the sessions as a musical and personal collaborator. The record is therefore not a solo bedroom-record in the strict sense — it is a one-person production sitting inside a deeply communal scene whose values (analog, no isolation, ambient leakage welcome, ideology over polish) the record fully embodies.

## The Glow As Motif

"The glow" is a recurring lyric and conceptual motif across Elverum's mid-period Microphones work. The previous album, *It Was Hot, We Stayed in the Water* (2000), contains a song titled "The Glow" that functions in part as an ode to Karl Blau ([Karl Blau bio](https://www.karlblau.com/about)). *The Glow, Pt. 2* is therefore not "The Glow" as a standalone reference but the continuation and expansion of a personal-cosmology vocabulary already in flight. For the corpus, the meaningful production point is that Elverum used a recurring motif across albums as a compositional tether — the song cycle on *Pt. 2* is meant to be heard against the *Pt. 1* it is naming.

## The Production Lineage Into Mount Eerie

Elverum recorded *Mount Eerie* (2003) — the final Microphones-name album — in the same Dub Narcotic Studio between November 2001 and June 2002, on what was effectively a 15-track 16-track (one channel was bad), with even more aggressive bouncing for samba-percussion passages running to 50+ layered tracks ([Grokipedia, Mount Eerie](https://grokipedia.com/page/Mount_Eerie); [Wikipedia, Phil Elverum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phil_Elverum)). After *Mount Eerie* he retired the Microphones name, adopted Mount Eerie as the project, founded P.W. Elverum & Sun in 2004, and moved production back to Anacortes. *The Glow, Pt. 2* sits at a specific career hinge: the highest realisation of the Dub Narcotic / Olympia / K Records phase, and the template that the post-2003 Mount Eerie project would mostly *strip back* — later Mount Eerie records favour exposed acoustic guitar and direct vocal over the wall-of-distortion approach. The Elverum production lineage is a fork: *The Glow, Pt. 2* defines the maximalist tape-aesthetic pole; the post-2016 Mount Eerie records (after Geneviève Castrée's death) define the stripped-down pole. *The Glow, Pt. 2* is the canonical reference for the first.

## What This Record Teaches The Corpus

- **Lo-fi is a sound-design choice, not an equipment limitation.** Good microphones, a good room, a good tape machine, and authored grit on top is more useful than bad gear and accidental grit.
- **Tape generation loss is a compositional element.** The audible haze around the layered piano and guitar stacks is the residue of repeated bouncing; if you want it digitally you have to model it on purpose.
- **A small consumer device can be a signature distortion source.** Thrift-store cassette decks, voice recorders, and answering machines all have characteristic clip behaviour — they are free, expressive, and unrepeatable.
- **Dynamic range can be the song.** *The Glow, Pt. 2* is the canonical counter-example to the loudness-war assumption that consistent loudness is a virtue. Volume-knob participation is here treated as part of the listening experience.
- **Album-long motif vocabulary rewards repeat listening.** Reusing "the glow" across two records turns the song cycle into a connected text rather than an isolated artefact.
- **A given studio's philosophy travels into the records made in it.** Dub Narcotic's no-isolation, signal-path-bypasses-the-board, ambient-leakage-welcome posture is recognisable in every K-era record made there. Choose the room as carefully as the gear.
