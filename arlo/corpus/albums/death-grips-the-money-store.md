## The Session Story In One Paragraph

*The Money Store* is the album that announced Death Grips as a major-label band without sounding like one. The trio — vocalist Stefan Burnett (MC Ride), drummer-and-co-producer Zach Hill, and producer-engineer Andy "Flatlander" Morin — formed in Sacramento in 2010 and self-released the *Exmilitary* mixtape in 2011 ([Wikipedia, *Death Grips*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Death_Grips)). They signed to Epic Records in February 2012 after a Sony-building meeting with L.A. Reid, with much of *The Money Store* already complete; the band framed the deal as Epic understanding to leave the work alone and offering "unlimited possibilities and total freedom" ([Exclaim!, Death Grips Discuss Their Major Label Deal](https://exclaim.ca/music/article/death_grips_discuss_their_major_label_deal_for_money_store)). The record was issued on 24 April 2012, with a Coachella cassette drop on 20 April, a Record Store Day vinyl on 21 April, and a YouTube leak ten days before official release ([Wikipedia, *The Money Store*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Money_Store)). Critical reception was near-unanimous acclaim — Pitchfork's Jayson Greene gave it Best New Music with an 8.7, AllMusic gave it five stars, Anthony Fantano awarded the first "10" of his reviewing career, and year-end lists placed it as high as number one at The Skinny and number two at Time Out London ([Wikipedia, *The Money Store*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Money_Store); [Sputnikmusic review summary](https://www.sputnikmusic.com/review/61949/Death-Grips-The-Money-Store/)). Within six months the band would self-leak *No Love Deep Web*, get dropped by Epic, and become the canonical case study for what a major-label experimental act could and could not do.

## The Trio And Their Roles

Death Grips on *The Money Store* is a strictly three-person operation: Burnett writes and performs all vocals; Hill plays drums and shares production with Morin; Morin handles engineering and the bulk of the electronic work ([Wikipedia, *Death Grips*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Death_Grips)). Hill is the bridge between the experimental-rock and hip-hop worlds — longtime drummer of the math-rock duo Hella, a Sacramento native born in 1979, with a late-90s Bay Area scene reputation for highly technical, chaotic playing ([Wikipedia, *Zach Hill*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zach_Hill)). Morin's public profile is much thinner by design — his most-quoted on-the-record line is "We get to play really loud" — but his "unorthodox style of production and sampling" is consistently named as one of the two defining sonic signatures of the band, alongside Hill's drumming ([Death Grips Wiki, Andy Morin](https://deathgrips.fandom.com/wiki/Andy_Morin)).

## Zach Hill's Drum-As-Noise Approach

Hill's drumming is the single most-cited element of the Death Grips sound, and the reason critics could not file the band as straight hip-hop. The kit is deliberately minimal — a three-piece of kick, snare, and floor tom played with a single pedal, augmented by a stainless-steel three-bell ride and a stainless-steel snare custom-made by Gregg Keplinger ([Modern Drummer, Zach Hill's Death Grips Drum Cam](https://www.moderndrummer.com/2012/03/zach-hills-death-grips-drum-cam/)). Hill described the method on "Lost Boys" as building a live drum take into the album track and then improvising over himself when performing — a layering strategy that lets recorded and live drums occupy slightly different rhythmic positions in real time. He kept time on monitor speakers rather than in-ears, treating playback as part of the kit rather than as a click ([Modern Drummer, Zach Hill's Death Grips Drum Cam](https://www.moderndrummer.com/2012/03/zach-hills-death-grips-drum-cam/)). The result is drum patterns that read as live performances even when studio-stacked — "frequently syncopated" figures "altered through studio processing, contributing to an irregular rhythmic structure" ([Wikipedia, *The Money Store*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Money_Store)). The Hella drummer's free-jazz-into-punk vocabulary moves directly into hip-hop production logic with no smoothing of the seams.

## Flatlander, Sampling, And Musique Concrète

Morin's production half of the band is built around what Hill described as a musique-concrète attitude to source material. In Hill's words to The Skinny: "we often make music out of material that other people wouldn't think to use. Like for instance we might take a sample from YouTube and then build a whole song around it with these more hi-fi instruments, and then erase the sample from YouTube so it's not even in the track. There's a lot of recycling and destruction that happens in the making of our music" ([The Skinny, Death Grips interview](https://www.theskinny.co.uk/music/interviews/death-grips-theres-a-lot-of-recycling-and-destruction-in-the-making-of-our-music)). Reported sample sources span the surreal-functional — tennis grunts from the Williams sisters and Vancouver SkyTrain audio on "System Blower," tracks from *Music from Saharan Cellphones*, Casio's *Sounds Effect 88* library on "The Fever (Aye Aye)" and "Hustle Bones," and self-sampling of the band's *Exmilitary*-era live material on "Lost Boys" and "Hacker" ([Wikipedia, *The Money Store*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Money_Store); [Boilerrhapsody, Album Breakdown — The Money Store](https://boilerrhapsody.com/2020/01/14/album-breakdown-death-grips-the-money-store/)). The major-label move is audible as constraint, not softening — the band has been explicit that compared to *Exmilitary*, the new album features "a very minimal amount of sampling" because Epic required clearances ([Exclaim!, Death Grips Discuss Their Major Label Deal](https://exclaim.ca/music/article/death_grips_discuss_their_major_label_deal_for_money_store)).

## MC Ride's Vocal — Instrument Of Abrasion

Burnett does not rap in any straightforward technical sense. The performance is closer to declamation and shout-as-instrument — what The A.V. Club called "another instrument of abrasion as he hollers in a voice so tattered and blown-out it must physically pain him" ([Wikipedia, *MC Ride*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/MC_Ride)). The vocal palette moves from rapid-fire syllabic flows on "Hacker" to guttural screams on "Blackjack" and percussive shouted hooks on "Get Got" and "The Fever (Aye Aye)." Production-side the signal is consistently overdriven, multi-tracked, doubled with pitch-shifted backing yells, and printed loud enough in the bed that it functions as a lead percussion line as much as a vocal melody. The reference microphone the band has named publicly is the Shure Beta 58A, referenced inside the lyric of "Fuck That" ([Wikipedia, *MC Ride*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/MC_Ride)). The corpus point: the vocal is processed and placed as a noise source first, a rap second.

## "Get Got" — Opening Statement

"Get Got" is the album's two-minute-fifty-two-second opening track and the canonical Death Grips sound design. The arrangement layers a Saharan-cellphones-derived rhythmic sample under distorted synth stabs, dry-and-bright kick patterns, and Burnett's chanted hook ([Boilerrhapsody, Album Breakdown — The Money Store](https://boilerrhapsody.com/2020/01/14/album-breakdown-death-grips-the-money-store/)). The mix logic the rest of the record will follow is set here: vocal forward and overdriven, drums printed with grain rather than polish, synth bass and synth lead aggressive enough to read as guitar substitutes, and an absence of conventional sub-bass — the low-end weight comes from the kick and synth-bass fundamental rather than a separate sub layer. "Get Got" was released as a free download on 27 February 2012, nearly two months before the album ([Wikipedia, *The Money Store*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Money_Store)).

## "The Fever (Aye Aye)" And "Hustle Bones" — Casio As Texture Source

Two of the album's catchiest tracks share an unlikely production root — Casio Computer Co., Ltd.'s *Sounds Effect 88* sample library ([Boilerrhapsody, Album Breakdown — The Money Store](https://boilerrhapsody.com/2020/01/14/album-breakdown-death-grips-the-money-store/)). "The Fever (Aye Aye)" builds an entire track around treated synth-effect material with conventional drum kit pulled back; "Hustle Bones" lays a Rodney O and Joe Cooley sample under the verse and uses the Casio library for the high-frequency hook punctuation. The corpus lesson is the willingness to treat a stock effects library as a primary instrument — the same compositional move the Bomb Squad made with discount funk records on *It Takes a Nation of Millions* (see `corpus/albums/public-enemy-it-takes-a-nation.md`), translated into 2012 industrial-rap terms.

## "Lost Boys" — Self-Sampling And The Live-In-The-Studio Loop

"Lost Boys" exemplifies Death Grips' self-referential sampling practice. The track samples "Fyrd Up" from the band's own *Live from Death Valley* tape — recycling their earlier mixtape-era material as raw input for the studio album ([Boilerrhapsody, Album Breakdown — The Money Store](https://boilerrhapsody.com/2020/01/14/album-breakdown-death-grips-the-money-store/)). Hill described the drum recording method as one where the album take started as a live drum performance, with the live show subsequently played over the recorded take — Hill improvising against himself in real time ([Modern Drummer, Zach Hill's Death Grips Drum Cam](https://www.moderndrummer.com/2012/03/zach-hills-death-grips-drum-cam/)). The track also carries Hill's stated punk-rock reference: he cited the Wipers as the inspiration for the song's rhythmic feel.

## "I've Seen Footage" — The Catchy One

"I've Seen Footage" is the album's most-cited pop crossover and the moment the band's danceable side comes fully forward. The bass-and-drum pattern leans hard into a 1980s electro-rap groove. The widely-circulated observation that the track samples or interpolates Salt-N-Pepa's "Push It" (1987) needs careful framing — WhoSampled does not list "Push It" as an officially confirmed sample, and most circulating evidence is the strong groove resemblance plus a popular mashup ([The Witzard on the resemblance](http://thewitzard.blogspot.com/2012/04/shes-so-heavy-death-grips-ive-seen.html); [WhoSampled, I've Seen Footage](https://www.whosampled.com/Death-Grips/I've-Seen-Footage/)). The defensible corpus framing is that the track is built on the same electro-rap drum-machine vocabulary as "Push It" — same era, same feel, same step — rather than asserting a literal sample lift. Pitchfork singled out the track as Best New Music on its own merits ([Wikipedia, *The Money Store*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Money_Store)).

## "Hacker" — The Closer And The Three-Tier Sample Strategy

"Hacker" closes the record at four minutes thirty-six seconds — the album's longest cut. Reported sample sources are a three-tier breakdown: an obscure Blue Devils YouTube source called "The Ditty," the band's own *Live from Death Valley* track "Poser Killer," and a reportedly-used twist of M83's "Midnight City" from *Hurry Up, We're Dreaming* — though the M83 connection is community-reported on WhoSampled and not formally licensed ([Boilerrhapsody, Album Breakdown — The Money Store](https://boilerrhapsody.com/2020/01/14/album-breakdown-death-grips-the-money-store/); [WhoSampled, Hacker](https://www.whosampled.com/Death-Grips/Hacker/)). The track's structure is the album's clearest demonstration of the band's accessibility-via-density strategy — a conventional verse-chorus-verse skeleton inside a fully experimental texture, with Burnett's fastest sustained flow on the record.

## The Major-Label Conditions And The Release Mechanics

The Epic deal was the single most-discussed business fact about *The Money Store* at release. Death Grips signed in February 2012 under the recommendation of then-executive VP of marketing Angelica Cob-Baehler ([Wikipedia, *The Money Store*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Money_Store)). The band's own 2012 framing was that Epic "truly believed in what we were doing" and offered creative freedom; Hill told The Skinny that the executives recognised they "couldn't understand" the music creatively and so chose not to interfere ([Exclaim!, Death Grips Discuss Their Major Label Deal](https://exclaim.ca/music/article/death_grips_discuss_their_major_label_deal_for_money_store); [The Skinny, Death Grips interview](https://www.theskinny.co.uk/music/interviews/death-grips-theres-a-lot-of-recycling-and-destruction-in-the-making-of-our-music)). The major-label-without-consent narrative attaches to the *follow-up* — *No Love Deep Web* — not to *The Money Store* itself. Verification timeline: Death Grips announced on 30 September 2012 that Epic was delaying *No Love Deep Web* until "next year sometime"; the band self-released the record on 1 October 2012 via their own website without label approval, posted Epic's cease-and-desist emails on Facebook on 31 October, and were dropped by Epic in November 2012 ([Wikipedia, *No Love Deep Web*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/No_Love_Deep_Web)). *The Money Store* itself came out with Epic's full cooperation, but the rollout already anticipated the guerrilla future — official release 24 April, Coachella cassette 20 April, Record Store Day vinyl 21 April, full YouTube leak ten days early on 14 April ([Wikipedia, *The Money Store*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Money_Store)).

## Mix And Production Logic

The mix rewards a few specific listening tests. Vocals stay forward and overdriven — Burnett's signal is consistently the loudest single element, with distortion baked into the print. Drums are pushed into saturation at the source: Hill's transients have grain and crackle, not modern rock-drum polish, which lines up with descriptions of "synths recorded with lots of crackle and hiss and glitches" alongside the drum tracks ([The Skinny, Death Grips interview](https://www.theskinny.co.uk/music/interviews/death-grips-theres-a-lot-of-recycling-and-destruction-in-the-making-of-our-music)). The low end is built from kick plus synth-bass rather than from a separate sub layer; there is no R&B-style 808 sub on most tracks, which is part of why the album does not behave like its hip-hop contemporaries on club PAs. Hill's stated stance — building songs out of failures, working "backwards by starting with pieces of something that we have purposely destroyed" — is audible across the record as a willingness to leave artifacts and glitches in the final print ([Boilerrhapsody, Album Breakdown — The Money Store](https://boilerrhapsody.com/2020/01/14/album-breakdown-death-grips-the-money-store/)).

## Reception, Charts, And Decade-Level Status

Metacritic registered an 81 average across thirteen reviews. Pitchfork's 8.7 Best New Music designation from Jayson Greene framed the appeal in physical-experience terms: the record is "about as intellectual an experience as a scraped knee. But it's just as good at reminding you that you're alive" ([Sputnikmusic review summary](https://www.sputnikmusic.com/review/61949/Death-Grips-The-Money-Store/); [Wikipedia, *The Money Store*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Money_Store)). AllMusic gave five stars, Mojo and Q four, Spin eight of ten, The A.V. Club a B+. Charts were modest but notable for the style — Billboard 200 #130, Alternative Albums #24, Heatseekers #3, Rap Albums #14. Year-end lists placed it as high as number one at The Skinny, number two at Time Out London, number four at Clash, New York Magazine, and Vulture, number six at BBC Music, and number nine on Pitchfork. Decade-level recognition followed — Pitchfork's 200 Best Albums of the 2010s #117, 33 Best Industrial Albums of All Time #25, Genius's 100 Best Albums of the 2010s #27 ([Wikipedia, *The Money Store*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Money_Store)).

## What This Record Teaches The Corpus

- **Drum-as-noise is a viable production primitive.** A three-piece minimal kit, played live and stacked against itself, can function as the central sound design of a hip-hop record rather than as accompaniment — Hella vocabulary metabolised by an electronic production frame.
- **Musique concrète and major-label hip-hop can coexist.** Hill's "build songs out of failures" stance is not incompatible with chart presence; the album charted at #130 on the Billboard 200 with YouTube-found samples and intentional artifacts left in the print.
- **Vocal-as-instrument means the vocal is mixed loud and distorted.** Vocal-forward-and-dry (the Daughters approach) and vocal-forward-and-distorted (the Death Grips approach) are different solutions to the same problem of making a non-singing lead voice carry an aggressive bed.
- **Sample clearance is a compositional constraint, not just a legal one.** The Epic deal forced "a very minimal amount of sampling" compared to *Exmilitary* — the consequence was tighter, more self-referential source material and synth / Casio-library texture as substitutes.
- **Leak-as-rollout was operative on *The Money Store*.** The YouTube leak, Coachella cassette drop, and Record Store Day vinyl together previewed the guerrilla identity the band fully embraced six months later with *No Love Deep Web*.
