## What contour is, and why it matters more than note choice

Melodic contour is the *shape* of a melody plotted over time — the up-and-down silhouette you'd draw if you sketched the line on a napkin. It is described independently of key and even of specific intervals: a tune transposed up a fifth keeps the same contour. Cognitive research consistently shows that listeners encode and recall melodies primarily by contour rather than by exact pitches, especially on first hearing ([Open Music Theory: The Phrase, Archetypes, and Unique Forms](https://viva.pressbooks.pub/openmusictheory/chapter/phrase-archetypes-unique-forms/)). For songwriters working at the topline, this means the *gesture* of a line — where it peaks, where it falls, how steeply it climbs — often determines whether a melody sticks before any lyric or harmony is locked in. Berklee's Jack Perricone, who spent over three decades teaching songwriting at the college, treats shape and intervallic content as the two primary expressive levers in any melody ([Berklee Online: What is Melody in a Song?](https://online.berklee.edu/takenote/conjunct-disjunct-melody-basic-definitions/)). When a chorus isn't landing, audit the contour before you audit the notes.

## The four basic contour shapes

Most working analyses converge on four families: **ascending**, **descending**, **arch** (rises then falls), and **terraced/cascading** (stair-stepped phrases, each starting where the last one ended or higher) ([Phamox Music: Melodic Contour Overview](https://phamoxmusic.com/melodic-contour/)). A fifth family — the **wave** or **undulating** contour — is essentially a chain of small arches and is the default for conversational verse melodies. The "melodic arch hypothesis" in cognitive musicology proposes that arch shapes are a near-universal default across cultures, which is why so many singable choruses follow that profile ([arXiv: Melodic contour does not cluster](https://arxiv.org/html/2604.13119v1)). Descending contours dominate phrase *endings* even when the larger phrase climbs — that's the "concave then resolve down" micro-pattern noted in the Ripollés Lab's contour study ([Goldstein et al., Exploring Melodic Contour: A Clustering Approach](https://www.ripolleslab.com/uploads/1/2/6/7/126798162/goldstein_2023.pdf)). When sketching, default to: stable opening, build, peak around two-thirds in, descend to resolution.

## Verse contour vs. chorus contour

The most reliable structural convention in pop topline is that **chorus melodies sit in a higher register than verses**, and the chorus contour usually contains the song's highest note ([Secrets of Songwriting: Why Chorus Melodies Are Usually Higher](https://www.secretsofsongwriting.com/2020/05/22/why-choruses-melodies-are-usually-higher-than-verse-melodies/)). Verses tend toward conversational, narrow-range, wave-shaped contours that conserve energy and keep lyric density legible. Choruses lean into arch or ascending shapes with a clear climactic note — often called the "money note" — that arrives once or twice per chorus and supplies the emotional payoff ([Secrets of Songwriting: Checking and Comparing Verse and Chorus Range](https://www.secretsofsongwriting.com/2017/07/20/checking-and-comparing-verse-and-chorus-melody-range/)). Pre-choruses typically *launch* — they ascend through unstable harmony to set up the chorus apex. A practical check: sing your verse and chorus back-to-back unaccompanied. If you can't immediately hear which is which from pitch register alone, the contour contrast is probably too weak ([Secrets of Songwriting: Moving a Chorus Range Up an Octave](https://www.secretsofsongwriting.com/2021/08/24/moving-a-chorus-range-up-an-octave-for-musical-power/)).

## Step vs. leap as the core tension lever

Conjunct motion (steps — intervals of a 2nd) is the default vocabulary of singable melody, and it dominates because steps are easy to sing and easy to remember ([Organizing Sound: Steps, Skips, and Leaps in Melody Writing](https://organizingsound.com/steps-skips-and-leaps-in-melody-writing/)). Leaps — intervals of a 4th or larger — inject energy and demand listener attention. The textbook voice-leading convention, well documented in [Open Music Theory](https://viva.pressbooks.pub/openmusictheory/chapter/phrase-archetypes-unique-forms/) and reiterated in the songwriting press, is that **leaps should be approached and resolved by stepwise motion in the opposite direction**. A wide upward leap followed by gentle stepwise descent is one of the most reliable hook-building gestures in pop — it's the move behind countless chorus opens ([Secrets of Songwriting: The Power of the Melodic Leap](https://www.secretsofsongwriting.com/2013/01/16/the-power-of-the-melodic-leap/)). As a working ratio: verses skew 80%+ stepwise, choruses introduce one or two strategic leaps. Too many leaps in a row read as restless and become hard to sing.

## What a motif is, and why songwriters need one

A motif is the shortest melodic-rhythmic idea that still has identity — typically 2 to 6 notes — that the rest of the melody can be built from by transformation. The textbook case remains the four-note "short-short-short-long" figure that opens Beethoven's Fifth Symphony, which the entire first movement (and arguably the whole symphony) is generated from through repetition, sequence, fragmentation, and rhythmic augmentation ([Eastman School of Music: Symphony No. 5 Basics](https://www.esm.rochester.edu/beethoven/symphony-no-5/)). The pop equivalent is the chorus title-phrase motif: a short cell that gets repeated with small variations across the chorus and reappears in the post-chorus or outro. Charlie Puth and Selena Gomez's "We Don't Talk Anymore" builds an entire chorus by repeating one short refrain-like phrase three times ([Flypaper: 7 Melody Writing and Motivic Development Techniques](https://flypaper.soundfly.com/write/7-melody-writing-and-motivic-development-techniques-for-songwriters/)). A song without an identifiable motif has nothing to develop — it's just a string of phrases.

## The classical motif transformations, in pop terms

Every motif-development technique catalogued in conservatory theory still applies to pop topline, though the names change in practice ([Vaia: Motivic Development Examples](https://www.vaia.com/en-us/explanations/music/music-analysis/motivic-development/)):

- **Repetition** — restate the motif exactly. The most powerful and most undervalued move.
- **Sequence** — restate the motif starting on a different scale degree, usually a step or third up or down. Common in verse-2 melodies that echo verse-1 one step higher for lift.
- **Inversion** — flip the intervals (an upward 3rd becomes a downward 3rd). Useful for an answering phrase.
- **Augmentation / diminution** — double or halve the note durations. Heard in bridges that "stretch" the chorus motif.
- **Fragmentation** — keep only the first 2 or 3 notes of the motif and repeat that fragment. The standard build-into-the-final-chorus move.
- **Retrograde** — play the motif backwards. Rare in pop but used at section endings as a "reflection" gesture.

Sequence and fragmentation do most of the practical work in popular songwriting; the more exotic transformations are tools to reach for when a section needs surprise without abandoning the motif's identity.

## Repetition with small variation: the second-iteration trick

When a hook is going to land twice in a chorus, the second iteration is almost never identical to the first — it varies in one of three ways: a higher landing note, a different rhythmic placement of the last word, or an added pickup ([MusicRadar: Songwriting basics — how to use repetition and variation](https://www.musicradar.com/how-to/songwriting-repeated-hooks)). The principle, articulated by Pat Pattison in his "productive repetition" framing, is that exact repetition establishes the pattern; variation on the second statement creates the spotlight by violating the expectation it just set up ([Pat Pattison: Rhythms and Variations](https://www.patpattison.com/rhythms-and-variations)). The simplest, most reliable variation is to raise the final note of the phrase a third or fourth on the repeat — it converts a closed gesture into an open one and propels the listener into the next section.

## The antecedent-consequent (call-and-response) pair

The two-phrase melodic period — an opening **antecedent** that ends inconclusively, followed by a **consequent** that resolves it — is the underlying skeleton of most 8-bar pop sections ([Open Music Theory: The Phrase, Archetypes, and Unique Forms](https://viva.pressbooks.pub/openmusictheory/chapter/phrase-archetypes-unique-forms/)). The antecedent typically ends on an unstable scale degree (2, 5, or 7) or on a half cadence; the consequent ends on the tonic with an authentic cadence. The two phrases usually share their opening motif — that's what makes them sound like question-and-answer rather than two unrelated ideas. In topline practice, this means: write the first four bars, then write four more that *start the same way* but end differently. If the second phrase lands somewhere conclusive while the first felt like it asked a question, the period is doing its job ([Music Theory Puget Sound: The Period](https://musictheory.pugetsound.edu/mt21c/PeriodForm.html)).

## Tension via leap, release via step — applied

The cleanest single-sentence summary of melodic phrase-building is: *generate tension with a leap or a high note, then release it with stepwise descent toward the tonic.* This is why so many memorable choruses share a profile of a large upward interval at the top of bar 1 followed by a slow walk back down across bars 2–4 ([Get More Streams: Mastering Tension and Release](https://getmorestreams.com/building-tension-and-release-in-your-music/)). The corollary, often violated by inexperienced writers: long sustained high notes need to *resolve downward*, not stay parked at the peak. A peak that doesn't release feels stuck. Conversely, a melody that stays in a narrow conversational range for too long feels inert — it needs at least one leap somewhere to establish that the singer *could* go further. Verses earn their later choruses by under-using their highest notes.

## Interval character — what's safe to claim and what isn't

Songwriters and pedagogues frequently assign emotional meanings to specific intervals — "the perfect fifth is heroic," "the minor sixth is longing." Most of these claims are unreliable; they depend heavily on context, harmony, and tempo. Two interval-effect claims do recur consistently enough across reputable sources to use cautiously:

- The **tritone** (augmented 4th / diminished 5th) reliably signals instability, unease, or the uncanny in Western popular music — it is the standout interval in the Simpsons theme and the South Park guitar riff, and it almost never functions as a resting point ([Wikipedia: Tritone](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tritone); [eMastered: Tritone Examples](https://emastered.com/blog/tritone-examples)).
- The **perfect fourth**, especially as an upward leap on a strong beat, is the textbook "anthem" or "fanfare" opening — Wagner's "Here Comes the Bride" opens on one, and it's a default gesture in stadium-rock and anthem choruses ([Open-Minded Singing: Perfect 4th Examples](https://openmindedsinging.com/library/perfect-4th/)).

Beyond those two, prefer functional descriptions ("this interval creates stability against the IV chord") to mystical-feeling claims about what a 6th "means."

## Genre signatures in contour and interval

Different genres carry recognizable contour fingerprints. **Folk and acoustic singer-songwriter** material favors narrow-range, mostly stepwise contours with one or two leaps per section — singable and conversational. **Country** uses moderate-range arch contours with a strong descending tail in the chorus, often landing on the title word. **Pop and EDM-pop** push toward higher chorus registers and short, motif-driven shapes designed to be remembered after one listen — see the analysis on Hooktheory's database showing that strong hooks typically use **five or fewer distinct notes** ([Hooktheory: Song Hooks](https://www.hooktheory.com/blog/what-is-a-hook-in-a-song/)). **R&B** allows wider intervallic leaps and more melismatic ornamentation around a central contour. **Hip-hop hooks** often work with very narrow contours (sometimes 2 or 3 pitches) because rhythmic identity carries the weight. Match your contour vocabulary to the genre's listening expectations or use the mismatch deliberately.

## Long-range contour across a full song

Beyond the phrase, contour operates at the song level. A song has a *macro-arch*: a starting tessitura that gradually climbs, peaks in the bridge or final chorus, and resolves down to an outro. Queen's "Bohemian Rhapsody" is the canonical case study for long-range hidden contour — repeated motifs reappear across radically different sections, and the song's overall pitch register climbs and descends in deliberate stages across its six-minute span ([Academia: "Little High, Little Low" — Hidden Repetition and Long-Range Contour in Bohemian Rhapsody](https://www.academia.edu/19966708/_Little_High_Little_Low_Hidden_Repetition_Long_Range_Contour_and_Classical_Form_in_Queens_Bohemian_Rhapsody)). For pop writers, the practical version: chorus 1 doesn't need to be the highest moment of the song. Holding the absolute peak for the final chorus — often via a key-change lift of a half-step or whole-step — is the single most reliable way to make a song feel like it *goes somewhere* rather than circling.

## Diagnosing a flat melody: a working checklist

When a melody isn't working, audit in this order before touching notes:

1. **Plot the contour** — sketch the up-and-down shape on paper. If it's a flat line or random zigzag with no peak, the problem isn't the lyric or the chord.
2. **Find the motif** — can you sing a 2-to-6-note cell that recurs? If not, the melody is a string of unrelated phrases.
3. **Check verse/chorus register contrast** — sing them back-to-back. Is the chorus clearly higher?
4. **Locate the peak note** — does it land on a stressed syllable of an emotionally important word? If not, move it.
5. **Audit step-to-leap ratio** — too many leaps reads as restless; zero leaps reads as inert. Aim for one or two purposeful leaps per chorus.
6. **Test the antecedent-consequent** — do bars 1–4 and 5–8 share an opening and differ at the cadence?

Most "boring melody" complaints resolve at step 1 or 3. Most "doesn't stick" complaints resolve at step 2.
