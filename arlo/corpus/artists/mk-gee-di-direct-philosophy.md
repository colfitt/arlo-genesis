## Who Michael Gordon Is, Briefly

Michael Todd Gordon — recording as mk.gee — is a New Jersey-raised, LA-trained guitarist, songwriter, and producer whose 2024 debut LP *Two Star & the Dream Police* was an unexpected aesthetic event in indie/pop guitar music. He studied briefly at USC's Thornton School of Music before leaving to focus on home-produced records; his early EPs (*Pronounced McGee*, *Fool*, both 2018) and 2020 mixtape *A Museum of Contradiction* were written, recorded, mixed, and mastered alone in his bedroom ([Wikipedia: Mk.gee](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mk.gee)). Critics positioned *Two Star* as "a massive" leap from that earlier output — a record whose songs "froth and fizz and occasionally freak out," with "mangled production" that the [Rolling Stone review](https://www.rollingstone.com/music/music-album-reviews/mk-gee-two-star-and-the-dream-police-1234971328/) explicitly compared to Bon Iver's *22, A Million*. For ARLO's purposes, mk.gee is the contemporary template for *guitar-as-disguised-source*: the player who treats the instrument as raw material to be reframed, not as a tone object to be served.

## "I Kind of Hate Guitar" — the Framing

mk.gee's most-cited line about the instrument comes from Joe Coscarelli's *New York Times* profile of him: "I kind of hate guitar." The quote has been reported and reposted widely, including by the NYT's *Popcast* (excerpt on [Popcast Deluxe / TikTok](https://www.tiktok.com/@popcastdeluxe/video/7428682559569104174)). He expanded the position to *Dazed*'s [Autumn 2024 cover feature](https://www.dazeddigital.com/music/article/64551/1/mk-gee-michael-gordon-interview-autumn-2024-issue-dazed): "I don't really relate to the guitar any more. I don't like its nature," and "guitar is, like, the least interesting thing about the record to me." This isn't false modesty about chops — he also told *Dazed*, "I am a good guitar player; I like to find new cadences and interesting arrangements." It's a stance against the *role* the guitar plays in conventional rock signifying: the tone-pursuit, the rig culture, the reverence. He plays guitar; he just doesn't want the song to sound like one.

## The Refusal of Traditional Amp Tone

The clearest sonic expression of that stance is the absence of a guitar amplifier from his signal chain. Multiple gear-press outlets have built whole articles around the surprise of it: "He's not using a guitar amp. He's using a Tascam 424," as JHS Pedals' Josh Scott put it in [MusicRadar](https://www.musicradar.com/guitars/guitar-pedals/hes-not-using-a-guitar-amp-hes-using-a-tascam-424-jhs-pedals-puts-a-portastudio-in-a-pedal-to-help-you-recreate-mk-gees-elastic-lo-fi-tones). [Switched On Pop](https://switchedonpop.substack.com/p/breaking-through-doechii-mkgee-rose) described the resulting raw tone as "dead, plucky" — a thin DI source rather than the bloomed, room-coupled sound of a mic'd cab. This is the productive premise: by refusing the amp, mk.gee refuses the entire grammar of "good guitar tone" that comes with it (the speaker break-up, the inherited rock-history flavors, the in-the-room feel) and forces the *character* of the part to come from somewhere else — processing, performance, arrangement.

## The Tascam Portastudio 424 as Preamp

The piece of gear most consistently associated with his tone is the [Tascam Portastudio 424 MKI](https://www.musicradar.com/guitars/guitar-pedals/hes-not-using-a-guitar-amp-hes-using-a-tascam-424-jhs-pedals-puts-a-portastudio-in-a-pedal-to-help-you-recreate-mk-gees-elastic-lo-fi-tones), a cassette four-track from the late '80s/early '90s. He doesn't appear to use it to record to tape — gear writers describe him pushing the 424's preamp section hard and taking the output, effectively using a cheap cassette console as an overdrive box. The reported sonic signature is mild clipping, a midrange "bloom," and a compressed feel. The interest from gear-makers is itself a verification signal: [JHS released a "424 Gain Stage" pedal](https://www.guitarworld.com/gear/guitar-pedals/jhs-pedals-has-put-mkgee-beloved-tascam-424-multi-track-tones-into-a-pedal) explicitly emulating this workflow, and Audio Hertz shipped an [Mk.pre plugin](https://www.musicradar.com/music-tech/before-this-plugin-the-only-way-to-get-this-tone-was-by-chasing-down-old-hardware-or-buying-boutique-pedals-audio-hertzs-mk-pre-recreates-the-tascam-portastudio-preamp-that-colours-mk-gees-sought-after-guitar-tone) targeting the same character. (Note: mk.gee himself has not, in any interview I could verify, walked through his signal chain explicitly. The 424-as-preamp story is reported by third-party gear journalists, not Gordon. It's well-corroborated, but treat it as gear-press inference, not session-sourced fact.)

## The Fender Jaguar, Strung as a Baritone

His most-photographed guitar is a Fender Jaguar — visible on the [SNL performance from November 2024](https://www.guitarworld.com/artists/guitarists/mk-gee-performs-on-snl-mystery-charvel) and across tour footage. Multiple gear outlets report it is strung up as a baritone (heavier strings, tuned down), producing thicker low-mid chord voicings without losing the offset's characteristic short-scale snap ([Guitar World](https://www.guitarworld.com/artists/guitarists/mk-gee-performs-on-snl-mystery-charvel); [Internet Tattoo](https://www.internettattoo.com/blog/mk-gee-guitar-sound-and-tone)). The exact year, pickup configuration, or modifications are not authoritatively documented in any interview Gordon himself has given. For ARLO's purposes, "Jaguar-style offset, strung heavy, tuned low" captures the relevant aesthetic: an instrument whose stock voice already leans dark, jangly, and slightly uncooperative — well-matched to a producer who wants to start from a strange place.

## The "Dead, Plucky" Starting Material

[Switched On Pop](https://switchedonpop.substack.com/p/breaking-through-doechii-mkgee-rose) used the phrase "dead, plucky sound" to describe the raw DI before processing — a thin, characterless attack with no speaker softening. The aesthetic point: this is *deliberately* unmusical. A traditional guitar-record workflow tries to capture a great tone at the source so minimal processing is needed downstream. mk.gee inverts this. The source is intentionally inert so the *character* lives in the post-tracking choices — what gets clipped, what gets washed in reverb, what gets pitched, what gets compressed. This is the same logic Justin Vernon used on *22, A Million* with the Messina rig (capture a clean source, mangle it after) — which is why the Rolling Stone reviewer reached for the *22, A Million* comparison in the first place. For producers, the implication: a "boring" DI is not a failure state. It's a usable raw material if you have a downstream chain you trust.

## Effects-Processing as the Character Stage

The album-level description from [Switched On Pop](https://switchedonpop.substack.com/p/breaking-through-doechii-mkgee-rose) — "carefully gated reverb and distortion," "intimate and close" quiet passages, loud notes that "seem to explode into the stratosphere" — points to a wide dynamic envelope built in processing, not at the amp. [Internet Tattoo](https://www.internettattoo.com/blog/mk-gee-guitar-sound-and-tone) frames the workflow as one of *committing early*: printing effects, drive, and ambience into the tracked file rather than keeping a clean DI for later. (That specific claim is third-party inference, not Gordon's words, but it's consistent with the bedroom-producer arc — when you're tracking and mixing on the same machine in the same session, you have no incentive to preserve flexibility you'll never use.) The [Passion of the Weiss / POW review](https://powmag.net/p/soundcheck-mk-gee-two-star-and-the-dream-police/) gets at the same idea from the aesthetic side: "His guitar playing breaks open the core of the melodies with a discordant surprise" — the guitar is treated as a sound-design element that *interrupts* rather than supports.

## Cheap Gear, Fast Decisions

mk.gee told *Dazed*, "I like taking cheap, bad gear and trying to make the best song with it, because it forces you to say something interesting and to compensate for poor quality" ([Dazed](https://www.dazeddigital.com/music/article/64551/1/mk-gee-michael-gordon-interview-autumn-2024-issue-dazed)). And: "The faster you make things, the less time you have to think, and the more likely it is that you'll capture the spirit." Both quotes are critical context for the DI-direct philosophy. The preference for a cassette four-track over a high-end amp isn't lo-fi cosplay or nostalgia — it's a constraint that forces compositional and arrangement choices to carry the song. The speed-over-deliberation principle also explains why printed-in effects make sense: if you're optimizing for spirit, you don't leave decisions to a later mix pass.

## Self-Taught Lineage and the Rejection of "Guitar Player" Identity

Gordon learned guitar fundamentals from an upright bassist rather than a guitarist — a deliberate choice he framed in [Guitar World](https://www.guitarworld.com/news/mkgee-on-learning-guitar-basics-from-an-upright-bassist): "I never liked the idea of getting lessons from a guitar player, and thought it would be more useful to learn from someone who didn't play the guitar at all." He follows up: "All right, fuck y'all, I can play your instruments even better than you can. I'm just gonna learn how to record myself." This biographical detail rhymes with the gear philosophy. He's structurally allergic to inherited tradition — whether that's a teacher's idiom or a Marshall stack's tonal grammar — and the studio practice is the same refusal expressed in signal-flow terms.

## The Bedroom-Pop → Studio-Experimental Arc

Every mk.gee release through *Two Star* has been home-produced ([Wikipedia: Mk.gee](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mk.gee)). The 2018 EPs were *literally* bedroom records in LA — written, recorded, performed, mixed, and mastered by Gordon alone. *A Museum of Contradiction* (2020) extended that bedroom-pop lineage with what Rolling Stone called "straightforward R&B and bedroom psychedelia." *Two Star and the Dream Police* (February 2024) keeps the same self-produced workflow but expands the sonic ambition — same architect, new scale. This matters for the DI-direct story: the refusal of amps isn't a stadium-act affectation. It evolved organically from making records in a bedroom where a mic'd cab was never an option in the first place. The aesthetic *is* the workflow, and the workflow scaled up without changing its premises.

## Live: Reproducing the Studio, Not Replacing It

Concert reportage and gear press consistently describe his live rig as an extension of the studio approach rather than a "real band" reinterpretation. He moves between front-of-stage performance and a personal setup with keyboards, four-track tape recorders, vocal effects, and synths ([KTSW Concert Review](https://ktswblog.net/2024/09/18/concert-review-a-sonic-journey-of-mystery-and-emotion-with-mk-gee/)). Per [MusicRadar](https://www.musicradar.com/guitars/guitar-pedals/hes-not-using-a-guitar-amp-hes-using-a-tascam-424-jhs-pedals-puts-a-portastudio-in-a-pedal-to-help-you-recreate-mk-gees-elastic-lo-fi-tones), he runs the guitar through the Tascam in live settings as well — preserving the signature preamp clip rather than swapping in a cab for venue volume. (The presence of an actual cassette deck on stage is reported in gear press and concert reviews; whether he uses the same unit every night, or whether tape transport is even engaged, is not directly sourced from Gordon. Treat as observed-in-live-footage, not asserted-as-permanent.)

## The Dijon Connection

mk.gee co-produced [Dijon Duenas's 2021 debut LP *Absolutely*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mk.gee) and has toured as his guitarist. The collaboration is critically important for understanding both artists' aesthetic: *Absolutely* sits in the same lineage of guitar-forward, lo-fi-tendencied, emotionally raw R&B-adjacent songwriting, and POW described mk.gee as "architect" of the EP-era Dijon sound ([POW Magazine](https://powmag.net/p/soundcheck-mk-gee-two-star-and-the-dream-police/)). The DI-direct philosophy reads slightly differently in the Dijon context — there, the guitar is supporting vocal performance and groove rather than carrying the song's identity — but the *refusal of conventional tone* survives the move. For ARLO's purposes, the two artists form a small school: bedroom-produced, vocally-led, harmonically slippery, allergic to "good" guitar sound.

## What This Means for ARLO's Aesthetic Stack

mk.gee occupies a specific slot in ARLO's blend: the *anti-tone-pursuit* axis on the guitar side. Where Bon Iver's Messina-rig story is "capture-then-mangle" with custom electronics, mk.gee's story is "capture-already-mangled" through cheap consumer gear deployed against type. The shared principle is that the guitar's *character* should not come from the guitar itself — it should come from how the signal is shaped after it leaves the instrument. For songwriting workflow this suggests: track guitars DI through a cheap preamp pushed into mild clip; print effects and ambience aggressively rather than preserving wet/dry flexibility; let the part be melodically interesting and the *tone* be deliberately uncooperative; and treat any "this doesn't sound like a guitar record" instinct as a feature, not a problem to fix.
