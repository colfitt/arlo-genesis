## The OP-1 as Vernon's Primary Sketchpad

When Justin Vernon began the work that became *22, A Million* (2016), the Teenage Engineering OP-1 had displaced the acoustic guitar as his composing instrument. In a press conference timed to the album's announcement, Vernon called it "the most important instrument that's come into my life since I first picked up a guitar when I was 12 years old," and described it as functioning "a bit like the Casio SK-1 — it's like a miniature MPC beat maker. You can sit anywhere and sample the radio, your breathing, whatever, and make a song out of it" ([Sound on Sound — Inside Track: Bon Iver '22, A Million'](https://www.soundonsound.com/techniques/inside-track-bon-iver-22-a-million)). The OP-1 is a battery-powered all-in-one synth/sampler/sequencer with eleven synth engines (FM, Cluster, Digital, DNA, DSynth, Dr Wave, String, Phase, Pulse, Sampler, Voltage), a four-track tape emulation, drum and synth samplers, and a built-in FM radio that can be routed into the sampler ([Teenage Engineering OP-1 — Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Teenage_Engineering_OP-1)). Vernon used it not as a finishing tool but as a portable composition surface — he carried it through travel, hotels, and the April Base studio in Eau Claire, Wisconsin, sketching ideas the moment they arrived.

## The "Two-Two" Origin Story

The album's numerological identity began with a single resampling accident. Working through a difficult period during a stay in Greece, Vernon improvised the falsetto phrase "It might be over soon" into the OP-1's sampler. When he chopped the recording into fragments and triggered the slice back at pitch, the syllables stuttered into something that sounded like "two — two" ([Sound on Sound](https://www.soundonsound.com/techniques/inside-track-bon-iver-22-a-million)). Twenty-two was already Vernon's favorite number — he has linked it to paradox and duality, and to the way the phrase "it might be over soon" can mean both *I wanted this to last forever* and *thank God, I don't want to feel this anymore*. The chopped vocal became the opening hook of "22 (OVER S∞∞N)," and the numerical/glyphic logic it set off (every track titled with a number or symbol, the liner notes printed as a code) was downstream of one sample-mangling gesture on a handheld synth. The song that named the album was, literally, made of itself.

## Sample-as-Songwriting-Material vs. Sample-as-Decoration

The conceptual move that distinguishes *22, A Million* from earlier Bon Iver records is that samples are not ornaments dropped over a guitar-and-voice frame — they *are* the frame. In her academic analysis of "33 'God,'" Elizabeth Navarra Varnado observes that on this album "nearly every track radically expands or eschews typical instrumentation and musical texture associated with indie or alternative rock; instead, most tracks employ and prioritize digital samples as the main timbral elements" ([Varnado — The sampling aesthetic of Bon Iver's "33 'God'"](https://www.arpjournal.com/asarpwp/wp-content/uploads/2021/12/Elizabeth-Navarra-Varnado_ARP2019.pdf)). Varnado introduces the term **"sonic totem"** to describe how Vernon's samples function: each sampled phrase carries the timbre of "another time, another place, another emotion" into the track's interior monologue, so that Vernon's authorial voice is "both complicated and strengthened by this presence of other 'characters' in his narrative." For songwriters in the ARLO aesthetic stack, the practical implication is concrete: choose source material whose original context *means something*, and let that meaning bleed through pitch-shift and chopping. The sample is a co-writer, not a paint color.

## Stevie Nicks' "Wild Heart" on "10 (Death Breast)"

The most-cited single sample on the album is a fan-uploaded YouTube clip of Stevie Nicks warming up backstage in 1981, repeating the phrase "wild heart, wild heart" a cappella. Vernon pitched the snippet up and looped it into the chorus of "10 (d E A T h b R E a s T ⊠ ⊠)," where it floats above distorted programmed drums and bass ([The Current — All of the songs sampled on Bon Iver's '22, A Million'](https://www.thecurrent.org/feature/2016/09/03/all-of-the-songs-sampled-on-bon-ivers-22-a-million); [Sound on Sound](https://www.soundonsound.com/techniques/inside-track-bon-iver-22-a-million)). The choice is instructive: the source is not a record, it is a YouTube rip of a private warmup — sonic-totem material with built-in lo-fi character. Vernon and his team have repeatedly described "10 (Death Breast)" as the track that established the album's experimental direction, which means the album's identity was set by a track whose top-line hook is somebody else's accidentally-public vocal exercise.

## Mahalia Jackson on "22 (OVER S∞∞N)"

The opening track pairs Vernon's chopped "two-two" loop with a sample of Mahalia Jackson singing "then I'm going to shout all my trouble over" from a live performance of "How I Got Over," with the sample bookending a Michael Lewis saxophone solo ([The Current](https://www.thecurrent.org/feature/2016/09/03/all-of-the-songs-sampled-on-bon-ivers-22-a-million)). Two sonic totems are stacked: Vernon's own falsetto (private, present-tense) and Jackson's gospel testimony (public, historical, redemptive). The track functions as a thesis statement for the album's method — autobiographical voice multiplied by sampled voice, both run through the same OP-1 sample slots.

## The Sample Inventory for "33 'God'"

Varnado's spreadsheet analysis of "33 'God'" is the most thorough public accounting of any single track on the record. The samples she identifies are:

- **Paolo Nutini, "Iron Sky" (2014, Atlantic)** — Nutini's line "I found God, and religion, too" ([Varnado](https://www.arpjournal.com/asarpwp/wp-content/uploads/2021/12/Elizabeth-Navarra-Varnado_ARP2019.pdf)).
- **Jim Ed Brown, "Morning" (1970, RCA Victor single)** — strings plus Brown singing "When we leave this room, it's gone" and "Here in this room, this narrow room, where life began when we were young last night" ([Varnado](https://www.arpjournal.com/asarpwp/wp-content/uploads/2021/12/Elizabeth-Navarra-Varnado_ARP2019.pdf)).
- **The Isley Brothers' "It's Your Thing" (1969), as covered by Cold Grits** — the percussive drum-break intro, used as a "classic" hip-hop sonic totem ([Varnado](https://www.arpjournal.com/asarpwp/wp-content/uploads/2021/12/Elizabeth-Navarra-Varnado_ARP2019.pdf)).
- **Sharon Van Etten, "DsharpG" (2010, Ba Da Bing)** — long, high female vocal syllables ([Varnado](https://www.arpjournal.com/asarpwp/wp-content/uploads/2021/12/Elizabeth-Navarra-Varnado_ARP2019.pdf); [The Current](https://www.thecurrent.org/feature/2016/09/03/all-of-the-songs-sampled-on-bon-ivers-22-a-million)).
- **Lonnie Holley, "All Rendered Truth" (*Just Before Music*, 2012)** — Holley singing "all my goodness to show" ([Varnado](https://www.arpjournal.com/asarpwp/wp-content/uploads/2021/12/Elizabeth-Navarra-Varnado_ARP2019.pdf)).
- **WCFW Radio Chippewa** — percussive vocal syllables captured off Vernon's local radio station near Eau Claire ([Varnado](https://www.arpjournal.com/asarpwp/wp-content/uploads/2021/12/Elizabeth-Navarra-Varnado_ARP2019.pdf)).
- **A reading of Psalm 22** by Granger Community Church in Granger, Indiana, in a low male register, credited in the liner notes ([Varnado](https://www.arpjournal.com/asarpwp/wp-content/uploads/2021/12/Elizabeth-Navarra-Varnado_ARP2019.pdf)).

One track. Seven distinct sample sources. The texture of "33 'God'" is, in a literal sense, *collaged*.

## Other Album Samples Worth Knowing

Beyond "10," "22," and "33," the record's known sample sources include: **The Supreme Jubilees' "Standing in the Need of Prayer"** sped up on "666 ʇ" with the line "in need of prayer"; **The Toys' "A Lover's Concerto"** chopped past recognition on "21 M♢♢N WATER"; and **Fionn Regan's "Abacus"** on "00000 Million," with the repeated phrase "the days have no numbers" — itself a meta-commentary on the album's numerological obsession ([The Current](https://www.thecurrent.org/feature/2016/09/03/all-of-the-songs-sampled-on-bon-ivers-22-a-million)). Steve Winwood was a recurring sample target during the OP-1 era; Vernon has described "sampling Steve Winwood in between bong rips on my OP-1" while backstage in Montreal in 2014 ([MusicRadar — We were sampling Steve Winwood in between bong rips on my OP-1](https://www.musicradar.com/artists/we-were-sampling-steve-winwood-in-between-bong-rips-on-my-op-1-how-bon-iver-met-haim-backstage-at-a-music-festival-and-ended-up-collaborating-a-decade-later)), an anecdote that documents the *practice* even where the specific *22, A Million* track placement is not independently verified.

## The Resampling Workflow: OP-1 to April Base and Back

The album's deeper trick is that the OP-1 was not used in isolation — it was looped through the studio. In Vernon's own words, the process involved "doing many improvisations with whomever I wanted to make music with, also sampling parts of these sessions in the OP-1, and hooking things up to other tools in the studio to mess them up" ([Sound on Sound](https://www.soundonsound.com/techniques/inside-track-bon-iver-22-a-million)). The cycle was: improvise at April Base with collaborators → capture fragments into the OP-1's sampler → manipulate on the OP-1 (pitch, chop, retrigger, run through tape) → bounce back into Pro Tools → re-mangle through outboard. Sketches and "finals" became indistinguishable. Many album moments are first-take OP-1 captures that survived into the master because the version recorded *as* sketch had more character than any attempt to re-perform it.

## Varispeed Resampling: Antelope Trinity and the Ampex ATR102

The resampling logic extended beyond the OP-1 into the analog signal chain. To get the sped-up, pitched-up country-style vocal on "29 #Strafford APTS," the team used the Antelope Isochrone Trinity master clock to varispeed the entire Pro Tools session down by roughly 20 cents, printed that to an Ampex ATR102 two-track tape machine, then recorded the Ampex back into Pro Tools at the correct speed ([Sound on Sound](https://www.soundonsound.com/techniques/inside-track-bon-iver-22-a-million)). The result is a vocal whose formants and grain are shifted in a way no plug-in pitch-shift quite mimics — and the technique was applied "not only on the vocals, but in some cases on the entire track." This is sampling as production philosophy rather than as a sample-CD lookup: every track is potentially raw material for the next pass.

## The Messina: Live Sampling of the Self

Parallel to the OP-1, Vernon and engineer/April Base manager Chris Messina developed a custom hardware-and-software rig nicknamed "the Messina," which Varnado describes as an extension of Francis Starlite's Prismizer effect first popularized on Chance the Rapper's *Coloring Book* ([Varnado](https://www.arpjournal.com/asarpwp/wp-content/uploads/2021/12/Elizabeth-Navarra-Varnado_ARP2019.pdf); [Sound on Sound](https://www.soundonsound.com/techniques/inside-track-bon-iver-22-a-million)). Mechanically, a microphone runs into an audio interface and a laptop running a harmony engine; a MIDI controller maps the chord changes the operator wants applied; the harmonized signal is converted back to analog and routed through outboard color. Messina, as quoted in Varnado, explains the design intent: "Normally, you record something first and then add harmonies later. But Justin wanted to not only harmonize in real time, but also (to) be able to do it with another person and another instrument. The result is one thing sounding like a lot of things." On *22, A Million* the Messina is most audible on "715 - CRΣΣKS," "666 ʇ," "21 M♢♢N WATER," "8 (circle)," and "____45_____." Conceptually, it is sampling pulled back into live performance — Vernon's voice harmonizing itself through a software model in real time, the way a sample would but with no fixed source.

## How OP-1 Constraints Shaped the Album's Identity

Teenage Engineering CEO Jesper Kouthoofd has said outright that "limitations are OP-1's biggest feature" ([Teenage Engineering OP-1 — Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Teenage_Engineering_OP-1)). The device's constraints — a 24-key keyboard, ~6 seconds of sample memory per slot in the standard sampler, four tape tracks, no traditional copy/paste of arrangements between projects, no plug-ins, a single tiny AMOLED display — push the user toward decisive, committed gestures. For Vernon, those constraints did the work that a producer's restraint would otherwise have to do. He has noted that once the OP-1 sketches were flowing, "it wasn't really obvious any more to pick up an acoustic guitar as often" ([Sound on Sound](https://www.soundonsound.com/techniques/inside-track-bon-iver-22-a-million)). The lo-fi character of OP-1 sample playback (audible aliasing, finite bit-depth, idiosyncratic envelopes) became part of the record's signature timbre — the reason "33 'God'" and "22 (OVER S∞∞N)" sound *cracked* rather than polished. A DAW would have offered too many choices; the OP-1 offered one knob at a time.

## Practical Lessons for an ARLO-Style Workflow

For a producer or songwriter working in the aesthetic ARLO is anchored to, the Bon Iver/OP-1 approach yields four transferable moves. **First, pick a constrained sketch device** — OP-1, Tascam Portastudio, phone voice memos, hardware MPC — and treat its output as legitimate final material, not just demo. **Second, resample yourself** — capture a vocal improvisation, chop it, retrigger it, and let the chopping suggest the lyric (the "two-two" path). **Third, choose sonic-totem sources with intent** — a YouTube warmup, a local radio broadcast, a church reading, a piece of family audio carry meaning that pristine sample packs do not. **Fourth, build a closed resampling loop** — DAW out to hardware (or tape, or a varispeeded clock), back into the DAW; each round of pitch and format degradation adds character that cannot be EQ'd in. Vernon's headline claim — that the OP-1 became more important than the guitar — is less about gear preference than about the discovery that *making the song* and *recording the song* can be the same gesture if the sketch device is good enough to commit to.
