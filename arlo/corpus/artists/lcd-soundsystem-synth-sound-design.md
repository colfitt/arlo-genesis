## The DFA Synth Palette in One Sentence

When ARLO is asked to "sound like LCD," the technically correct answer is not "use analog warmth" — it is a specific, semi-finite shortlist of instruments that James Murphy and DFA Records have leaned on since 2001: EMS Synthi A / VCS3, Yamaha CS-60, Roland SH-101, Roland System 100m, EML Electrocomp 100, Sequential Prophet-600, Korg MS-20, ARP Odyssey, and (on *American Dream* in particular) the Powertran Polysynth. The [Mixdown Magazine gear rundown](https://mixdownmag.com.au/features/rig-rundown-lcd-soundsystem/) lists this lineup straight off the *American Dream* credits, and the [SynthHistory interview with James Murphy](https://www.synthhistory.com/post/interview-with-james-murphy) confirms that several of these instruments — particularly the EMS, the CS-60 and the SH-101 — have been "the staple of early DFA mixes since the beginning." Notably absent: the Minimoog, the Prophet-5, the Jupiter-8, the Juno-106. Knowing what's *not* in the rack is as load-bearing as knowing what is.

## The EMS Synthi A / VCS3 — Murphy's Desert-Island Synth

Murphy bought his EMS Synthi A in 1998 on a tip from either Tim Goldsworthy or Jagz Kooner during a David Holmes session, and has used it for more than twenty years. He calls it "a magical beast with beautiful possibilities" with "pretty limitless potential routings" ([SynthHistory](https://www.synthhistory.com/post/interview-with-james-murphy)). The EMS architecture is a pin-matrix patch bay with three oscillators, a ring modulator, a spring reverb, and the famous two-axis joystick — and unlike a Moog or a Prophet, every parameter routes through the matrix rather than via hard-wired signal flow. That's the whole point: nothing is "default." On LCD records the EMS shows up across the catalog — most prominently "Someone Great," "All I Want," "You Wanted A Hit," "Somebody's Calling Me," and "Home" ([Music Nerds HQ](https://musicnerdshq.com/what-synths-do-lcd-soundsystem-use/)).

## The "Someone Great" Joystick Chord Technique

This is the single most-cited Murphy synth technique on record. The drifting, watery chord pad that floats under the entire arrangement of "Someone Great" is the EMS Synthi A, played by **moving the joystick** rather than the keyboard — Murphy patches the joystick's two axes to oscillator pitches via the matrix, so pushing the stick sweeps notes between chord tones in continuous glides, then snaps to the next chord at the bar line. In his own words: *"The swooping chords in the LCD song 'Someone Great' are the EMS. The ability to flux the notes into freeform chaos during the transitions and then snap them into the next chord was only possible with the EMS"* ([Music Nerds HQ](https://musicnerdshq.com/what-synths-do-lcd-soundsystem-use/), [SynthHistory](https://www.synthhistory.com/post/interview-with-james-murphy)). For ARLO: any time a user wants the "Someone Great drone" the answer is *not* a polysynth pad — it's two or three oscillators with their pitches under continuous controller modulation, plus a slow filter sweep, plus a four-on-the-floor sidechain duck.

## The Yamaha CS-60 as Drum Source

The CS-60 is the eight-voice little sibling of the CS-80 — same architecture, no polyphonic aftertouch, half the price. In Murphy's hands it's not a pad synth: **it's a drum kit.** He told interviewers that the rhythm bed of "Get Innocuous!" — the entire opening pulse — was built from CS-60 patches: *"that beat was just made out of a bunch of sounds from a Yamaha CS60; those are all just a series of kick drums with the synth"* ([XLR8R](https://xlr8r.com/features/lcd-soundsystem-in-the-studio/), [Mixdown](https://mixdownmag.com.au/features/rig-rundown-lcd-soundsystem/)). Snares were synthesized the same way and then sampled back in so they could be sequenced without quantization. The CS-60 also appears as a tonal element on "One Touch," "I Can Change," and "You Wanted a Hit" ([Music Nerds HQ](https://musicnerdshq.com/what-synths-do-lcd-soundsystem-use/)). Per the [SynthHistory](https://www.synthhistory.com/post/interview-with-james-murphy) interview, DFA's own CS-60 was modified by Rayna (Gabe) Russom to take audio at the back panel and convert it to CV — so it can also be played by external signals routed through its filter bank.

## The Roland SH-101 as the Bass Voice

If the EMS is the texture machine and the CS-60 is the drum machine, the SH-101 is the bass. The [Vice DFA gear piece](https://www.vice.com/en/article/kby4nz/dfa-records-has-more-gear-than-you) describes DFA as running multiple SH-101 units in parallel; Juan MacLean (DFA labelmate) notes that the 101 can be synced to other gear via CV/gate/clock, and "LCD has used these live" with up to three on stage simultaneously. On record the SH-101 carries the iconic bass lines of "Oh Baby," "How Do You Sleep?," "American Dream," and "I Can Change" ([Music Nerds HQ](https://musicnerdshq.com/what-synths-do-lcd-soundsystem-use/)). The 101's sound design vocabulary is single-oscillator, single-LFO, single-filter — and that's load-bearing for the LCD palette: a 101 bass leaves room. A Prophet bass with two oscillators detuned would clog the same frequency window.

## The Roland System 100m — The Modular Bass Killer

The System 100m is Roland's semi-modular cousin of the SH-101, and Murphy describes the 100m paired with SH-101 sequencing as *"a bassline killer"* ([SynthHistory](https://www.synthhistory.com/post/interview-with-james-murphy)). The [Reverb Machine](https://reverbmachine.com/blog/lcd-soundsystem-synth-sounds/) deep-dive on LCD synth patches notes that the "How Do You Sleep?" bass — built on "narrow-rectangular waveforms" with deep filter decay — "likely originated on a Roland System 100m modular synth." When ARLO is asked for an LCD-style bass that's more aggressive than the 101's clean square-wave thump, the 100m is the next move: same sonic family, more patch-cable chaos available.

## The EML Electrocomp 100 — Deep Sub Source

The EML Electrocomp 100 is a 1972 American semi-modular built by Electronic Music Laboratories of Connecticut — rare, quirky, and known for unusually deep low-frequency oscillators. Murphy uses it as a sub-bass and low-end texture source. The [Mixdown gear rundown](https://mixdownmag.com.au/features/rig-rundown-lcd-soundsystem/) places it in the *American Dream* studio rack alongside the more famous units. Reverb-machine secondary sourcing attributes the "super bassy part" in several LCD songs to the EML's ability to produce sub frequencies that the SH-101 can't reach on its own. Importantly, the EML is **not** a Moog Taurus — it has more harmonic complexity, less pure-sine fundamental — and that's why Murphy reaches for it.

## The Powertran Transcendent Polysynth — Murphy's *American Dream* Discovery

The Powertran Transcendent Polysynth is a late-1970s kit-built British polysynth — four oscillators, a stripped-down interface, notoriously unstable tuning. Murphy used it heavily on *American Dream*, calling it a "funny 4 osc synth" whose simple interface gives "more independent control of the oscillators" than most polys, and praising it for being *"really unstable and wonderful"* ([SynthHistory](https://www.synthhistory.com/post/interview-with-james-murphy)). He compares its character to "Chris and Cosey sounds" — i.e., the gritty, slightly-out-of-tune industrial-tinged textures of early Throbbing Gristle splinter work. The Powertran is the synth equivalent of finding a great $200 thrift-store amp: nobody's chasing it, but in the right hands it's the secret sauce.

## The Korg MicroKORG — The Live Workhorse

Of all the gear in the LCD arsenal, the Korg microKORG is the outlier: cheap, plastic, virtual-analog, beloved by teenagers in 2003. Murphy programs it ruthlessly. He says *"I've never generated a sound on Korg that I don't like. I wish there were a 16 voice version of it"* ([Music Nerds HQ](https://musicnerdshq.com/what-synths-do-lcd-soundsystem-use/)). Live, it covers "almost everything" because Nancy Whang can program it on the fly and it has zero stability issues at festival temperatures. The microKORG appears on studio tracks too — including "I Can Change," "Somebody's Calling Me," and "You Wanted a Hit." It is the proof point of Murphy's gear philosophy: *the box matters less than what the player does with it.*

## Why Quirky Beats Boutique — Murphy's Gear Philosophy

Murphy's pattern is consistent: prefer the cheap-and-strange over the prestigious-and-clean. He bought a $70 Epiphone Les Paul Junior and had it modified to spec rather than buying the high-end model, telling the [Mixdown](https://mixdownmag.com.au/features/rig-rundown-lcd-soundsystem/) interviewers: *"I bought the $70 model and then thought about what I wanted from it...it still cost me less than the fancy one and has exactly what I wanted."* The same logic governs his synth racks. Moogs and Prophets are *defaults*: every studio has one, every plug-in emulates one, every producer reaches for one. The EMS, the CS-60, the EML, the Powertran are *not* defaults — they have personality precisely because they are quirky and resist being copied cleanly. DFA's [Juan MacLean](https://www.vice.com/en/article/kby4nz/dfa-records-has-more-gear-than-you) sums up the in-house philosophy: *"There's no way any plug-in can emulate this... too many variables."* When ARLO is asked to "sound like LCD," the meta-instruction is: *don't reach for the obvious tool.*

## How LCD's Synth Choices Leave Mix Space

LCD records are *dense* — multiple synth layers, percussion stacks, vocal stacks, live drums — but they don't sound crowded. The reason is partially compositional (Murphy is disciplined about parts entering and leaving) and partially in the synth selection itself. The SH-101 has one oscillator and limited harmonic stacking, so a 101 bass occupies a narrow vertical slice of the spectrum and leaves the low-mids open. The EMS Synthi, played in joystick mode, is *intentionally* unstable in pitch — and that drift means it doesn't compete for the exact frequencies a vocal sits on. The CS-60 used as a drum source produces transients with a synth's controllable envelope rather than a sampled kick's broadband thump, so kicks can be carved precisely. Each piece of gear chosen for LCD's records has been chosen partly because **what it doesn't do** is as important as what it does — they leave frequency space for the next layer.

## Patch Recipes — From Reverb Machine's Track-by-Track

The [Reverb Machine breakdown](https://reverbmachine.com/blog/lcd-soundsystem-synth-sounds/) provides concrete starting-point patches that ARLO can offer when a user wants to chase a specific LCD sound:

- **"Oh Baby" bass** — two oscillators at 16' square waves, mixer pushed to 10 for natural overdrive, filter envelope with long decay, "fuzzy, driven, and somewhat hollow-sounding."
- **"Daft Punk Is Playing at My House"** — two wide-rectangular waveforms tuned to 8' and 16', filter wide open, short release, LFO vibrato applied via mod wheel, play staccato.
- **"How Do You Sleep?"** — narrow-rectangular waves with custom pulse widths, hint of reverb (0.35s, 12% mix), patch family points back to the System 100m.
- **"I Can Change"** — described as "a strong Gary Numan vibe": oscillator 1 at 4' triangle (melody), oscillator 2 at 32' wide rectangular (bass), one source covering both registers.
- **"Someone Great" bass** — two 16' narrow-rectangular waves with long release and long filter envelope decay, sub-bass oscillator at 32' an octave below, sidechain duck under the kick.
- **"Dance Yrself Clean" bass** — all three rectangular waveforms together, one dropped an octave as sub, filter cutoff at 4, resonance at 2, short loudness decay (200ms) for that punchy stab. The arpeggio uses saw-triangular waves at 2' and 4' with resonance and a noise oscillator under reverb.

These are *starting points*, not destinations — Murphy's actual signal chain runs through tape, outboard compression, and the AKG BX-10 spring reverb that DFA puts on "nearly all" their recordings ([Vice / DFA](https://www.vice.com/en/article/kby4nz/dfa-records-has-more-gear-than-you)).

## What ARLO Should Do With This

When a user invokes the LCD aesthetic, ARLO's default reflex should be: (1) propose a single-oscillator bass voice (SH-101 family) over a two-oscillator one; (2) suggest building drum hits from polysynth resampling (CS-60 method) before reaching for samples; (3) when a "pad" is asked for, propose a continuous-pitch-modulated drifting voice rather than a clean polyphonic pad — the EMS joystick approach; (4) avoid the Minimoog/Prophet defaults unless the user explicitly asks for them; (5) treat sidechain ducking under a four-on-the-floor kick as the structural glue that makes dense synth stacks breathe. The aesthetic is *not* "analog warmth" — it's *intentional instability, idiosyncratic sound sources, and disciplined frequency staging.*
