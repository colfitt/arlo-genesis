## The Career Arc — From House Engineer to Sixth Member

Nigel Godrich entered the orbit of Radiohead as the house engineer at RAK Studios in London, working under producer John Leckie on *The Bends* sessions (1995) and engineering the *My Iron Lung* EP (1994) [Wikipedia: Nigel Godrich]. The leap from engineer to co-producer arrived with *OK Computer* (1997), which won Best Alternative Album at the 40th Grammy Awards [Wikipedia]. From that point on, Godrich has produced every Radiohead studio album, earning a Grammy for Best Engineered Non-Classical Album for *Hail to the Thief* (2003) and later being widely characterized as the band's "sixth member" [Wikipedia; Music From Hallam Oakes, 2018].

His arc subsequently broadened: Beck's *Mutations* (1998), *Sea Change* (2002), *The Information* (2006), and *Modern Guilt* (2008); Travis's *The Man Who* (1999) and *The Invisible Band* (2001); Paul McCartney's *Chaos and Creation in the Backyard* (2005) at George Martin's suggestion [Wikipedia: *Chaos and Creation*]; a U2 remix of "Walk On" (2001); Atoms for Peace's *Amok* (2013) [Wikipedia: *Amok*]; Roger Waters' *Is This the Life We Really Want?* (2017) [Wikipedia]; Thom Yorke solo records (*The Eraser*, *Anima*); and The Smile's *A Light for Attracting Attention* (2022) and *Wall of Eyes* (2024) [Wikipedia: *A Light for Attracting Attention*; *Wall of Eyes*].

## The Engineering Philosophy in One Sentence

From a 1997 interview archived on the Nigel Godrich Producer Tumblr: "It's not about getting the most hi-fi sounding thing, what makes a good record good is the vibe" [nigelgodrichproducer.tumblr.com, "The Mix Interview from 1997"]. And, on workflow: "The trick is to do something fast enough and then keep going so you can't get bored with the thing you've just done" [same source]. These two sentences carry most of his method — vibe over fidelity, momentum over polish.

He has also been blunt about the cost of gear obsession: "One of the reasons why music has become generally worse... is that people think about technology more than the actual music they're making" [Wikipedia, citing earlier interviews].

## *OK Computer* (1997) — The Mansion Method

*OK Computer* was tracked largely at St. Catherine's Court, a sixteenth-century mansion outside Bath owned by Jane Seymour. Godrich treated the building itself as an instrument — placing microphones "in every nook and cranny to capture the personality of the place," and recording the lead vocal for "Exit Music (for a film)" in a stone stairwell to harvest natural reverb [KVR Audio production forum summary; Music From Hallam Oakes, 2018]. The console was the **MTA 980 Series** (a vintage British design rare even in 1997), paired with a Soundcraft Spirit 24 for additional channels [The King of Gear — Nigel rig database].

The famous tempo trick on "No Surprises" was Godrich's idea: when the band wanted the song to sound drowsy and slow but couldn't get the feel right, he had them play it faster and then slowed the tape down, recording Yorke's vocal to the slowed playback [Tape Op archive review of *Kid A*; production forum consensus].

## *Kid A* / *Amnesiac* and the Dense-Arrangement Shift

By *Kid A* (2000) Godrich's role had expanded into something closer to compositional collaborator — handling dense, layered electronic arrangement work and the "slammed" extreme-compression drum sound (typically through tube compressors) that became a signature of the *Kid A*/*Amnesiac* era [Tape Op review of *Kid A*]. He has consistently used **reel-to-reel tape, tube compression, and SSL consoles** as the spine of his rigs, while drawing on avant-garde and dub techniques — distortion and pitch manipulation deployed as mixing tools rather than effects [Wikipedia, summarizing engineering profile].

## Beck — *Mutations* (1998) and *Sea Change* (2002)

*Mutations* and *Sea Change* are the records that proved Godrich could do orchestral-folk as cleanly as he could do alienated art-rock. They stand in sharp contrast to Beck's *Odelay* (1996) lo-fi-sample aesthetic — these are warm, live-tracked records with serious string charts.

*Sea Change* was recorded at **Ocean Way Recording in Hollywood** and **Record One in Los Angeles**, beginning 6 March 2002 and finishing mixing by 7 May 2002 [Wikipedia: *Sea Change*]. The core band was James Gadson (drums), Jason Falkner (guitar), Roger Joseph Manning Jr. (keys), Justin Meldal-Johnsen (bass), and cellist Suzie Katayama. **David Campbell** — Beck's father — arranged and conducted the strings [Wikipedia: *Sea Change*]. Beck originally hoped to track one song per day (the *Mutations* pace) but the orchestral overdubs pushed each tune to two days minimum [Wikipedia; MusicRadar feature]. Much of the album was tracked live with overdubs added later, "frequently laying second takes of songs to disc" to keep the performances immediate [Wikipedia].

Godrich on Beck's voice during the *Sea Change* sessions: "His range has dropped. Now when he opens his mouth, a canyonesque vibration comes out" [Wikipedia, sourced from contemporary interviews].

## McCartney — *Chaos and Creation in the Backyard* (2005)

George Martin recommended Godrich to McCartney [Wikipedia: *Chaos and Creation*]. The project was tracked across **RAK Studios** (September 2003), **Ocean Way** (April and September 2004), and **AIR Studios** (April 2005) — eighteen months of intermittent sessions [Wikipedia].

After early RAK sessions with McCartney's touring band, Godrich told McCartney he wanted to work with him alone. McCartney's band, by his account, took it well and were happy to play the tracks live afterward [Wikipedia]. McCartney ended up playing nearly every instrument himself — a deliberate echo of his *McCartney* (1970) self-played debut. Godrich also pushed McCartney creatively: inspired the song "At the Mercy," contributed piano loops to "How Kind of You," and changed the tempo of "Riding to Vanity Fair," which McCartney said "changed the mood completely" [Wikipedia; Ultimate Classic Rock retrospective].

McCartney later acknowledged that Godrich "refused to allow me to sing songs that he didn't like" [Wikipedia]. McCartney also re-fell-in-love with his **Epiphone Casino** during the sessions because Godrich kept suggesting it: "I kept trying to use other guitars, but Nigel would say, 'You know what we should try, don't you?' 'Casino?' 'Yep!'" [Guitar Player 2005 interview, republished].

## *From the Basement* (2006– ) — Live Without the Apparatus

*From the Basement* launched 18 December 2006 and ran for two original seasons through 7 January 2009, with a YouTube revival in May 2022 [Wikipedia: *From the Basement*]. Filmed primarily at the **BBC's Maida Vale Studios** in London (with one Series 1 episode at Bob Clearmountain's Mix This! studio in LA), the show had no host, no audience, and no interviews — only the band playing into Godrich's microphones [Wikipedia].

Godrich's framing: "We were just saying what a shame it was that there wasn't anything that really felt as honest as that any more." He wanted to capture "the true representation" of artists' work, removing "the usual agony of TV promo which everyone has to do but no one seems to enjoy" [Wikipedia, quoting Godrich press]. The reference points he cited were *The Old Grey Whistle Test* and the Rolling Stones *Rock and Roll Circus* film [Wikipedia].

Guests across the run included Radiohead, Thom Yorke (solo), Beck, The White Stripes, PJ Harvey, Sonic Youth, Iggy Pop and the Stooges, Queens of the Stone Age, Fleet Foxes, and Jarvis Cocker [Wikipedia; Brooklyn Vegan coverage of 2020 YouTube relaunch]. The 2022 revival opened with IDLES [Rolling Stone UK, 2022].

The series is essentially Godrich's engineering method in its purest distillation: a fixed room, calibrated mics, no studio audience adrenaline, no broadcast clock — just performance captured cleanly.

## Atoms for Peace — Producer as Bandmate

Atoms for Peace formed in 2009 around Thom Yorke's *The Eraser* (2006) touring band: Yorke, Godrich, **Flea** (Red Hot Chili Peppers, bass), **Joey Waronker** (drums), and **Mauro Refosco** (percussion) [Wikipedia: Atoms for Peace]. *Amok* (2013, XL Recordings) was built from three days of jamming in an LA studio after the tour wrapped in 2010, with Yorke and the band bonding over Afrobeat (Fela Kuti specifically) [Wikipedia: *Amok*; NPR All Songs Considered interview with Yorke, Godrich, and Waronker, 2013].

The method: Yorke described his role as "conducting" — he'd show the band an electronic sketch he'd made and they would re-perform it on live instruments. Yorke and Godrich then edited and arranged the live recordings against the source electronic material over **two years** of post-production [Wikipedia: *Amok*; NPR]. This is a different mode for Godrich — not engineering a band's live take, but assembling a record from the friction between programmed and human-played versions of the same material.

## The Smile — Lockdown, Strings, and a Jazz Drummer

The Smile (Yorke, Jonny Greenwood, drummer **Tom Skinner** of Sons of Kemet) began work on *A Light for Attracting Attention* shortly before the first UK COVID-19 lockdowns. The album was recorded at **Wack Formula** and **Air Edel Studios** in London, both produced and mixed by Godrich [Wikipedia: *A Light for Attracting Attention*]. The **London Contemporary Orchestra** contributed strings under Hugh Brunt's orchestration, with a brass section drawn from contemporary UK jazz players [Wikipedia]. Bob Ludwig mastered.

Yorke described the sessions as "deeply frustrating" — he was tracking vocals between school hours and parts of the recording happened remotely, with Godrich receiving Yorke's vocals through streaming software from Yorke's home [Wikipedia, quoting Yorke]. The follow-up, *Wall of Eyes* (2024), was tracked in part at Greenwood's home studio in Oxfordshire and at Abbey Road Studios [Wikipedia: *Wall of Eyes*].

The Tom Skinner pairing is meaningful for the aesthetic stack: a jazz drummer's looseness against Godrich's tight, dub-aware mixing creates the polyrhythmic-but-cinematic feel that runs through both Smile records.

## Roger Waters — *Is This the Life We Really Want?* (2017)

Godrich met Waters while producing the 2015 live album *Roger Waters: The Wall* and was hired to produce Waters' first studio album since 1992's *Amused to Death* [Wikipedia: *Is This the Life We Really Want?*]. Godrich was characteristically blunt — he told Waters he found some of Waters' solo work "unlistenable" and pushed for a more concise, less theatrical record [Wikipedia, quoting press interviews]. Waters' response: "He did a brilliant job... I sat on my hands with lips zipped. You've rented this dog, let it work" [Wikipedia].

Godrich used tape loops and found sounds to bridge tracks and is credited for arrangement, sound collages, keyboards, guitar, and mixing. At Godrich's insistence, the album contains no guitar solos — a deliberate break from Pink Floyd tradition [Wikipedia].

## Studios and Consoles — The Hardware Picture

Across the career, Godrich's most-used rooms have been **RAK Studios London** (where he started, and where he tracked early McCartney and The Smile material), **Ocean Way Hollywood/LA** (Beck, McCartney, parts of *Hail to the Thief*), **AIR Studios London** (McCartney), **Maida Vale BBC** (*From the Basement*), and his own home/Bookhouse-style setups for development work [The King of Gear — Nigel rig database; cross-referenced from album Wikipedia entries].

Console choices documented across his work include the **MTA 980 Series** (primary on *OK Computer*), **Cadac G-Series** (vintage Wessex Studios desk on *OK Computer*), **API 2488** (purchased from RAK in 1998, his personal 24-channel console), **Audiotronics** 24-channel (used on Zero 7 and on *In Rainbows* — see sibling file), **Dalcon** custom Bill Putnam build from Ocean Way Studio B (*Hail to the Thief*), **Electrodyne 1204** (*In Rainbows* era), and a **Neve 88R** at Studios La Fabrique [The King of Gear].

Outboard staples include vintage **Neve 1081** preamps (he owns multiple), **Pultec EQH-2** and **PEQ1A** tube EQs, **UREI/Universal Audio 1176** compressors (multiple), **Tube-Tech CL1B**, **EMT 140** plate reverb, **AMS DMX 15-80S** and **RMX 16**, **Roland RE-201 Space Echo**, and **Eventide H9000** [The King of Gear].

Vocal-chain reference (1997-era, but stable in pattern): Neumann tube U47 or Australian Rode valve mic → **UREI 1176** compressor → **Pultec** valve EQ [nigelgodrichproducer.tumblr.com 1997 Mix interview]. Monitors: **Yamaha NS10m** as the functional check, **Acoustic Energy AE1** as the pleasure reference [same source].

## Analog Front-End, ITB Back-End

Godrich's documented pattern is to **track through analog outboard onto tape or high-resolution digital**, then **mix in Pro Tools** with hardware inserts where character matters. His own framing — "When you're recording a band, it's a bunch of microphones, a mixing desk, and a multi-track tape machine. That's it" [1997 Mix interview] — is essentially a hybrid manifesto: the front end is uncompromisingly analog, but he has been a heavy Pro Tools user since the late 1990s, and the *In Rainbows* sessions famously combined a vintage Audiotronix console (see sibling file) with extensive digital editing. He has warned repeatedly against the trap of treating Pro Tools as an end in itself — the digital side should serve momentum, not absorb it.

For practical purposes the lesson is: he is **not a "purist analog" engineer** and never has been. He is an engineer who picks the cheapest, fastest path to the vibe and is willing to mix philosophies inside a single record.

## Method Patterns That Travel

Distilled from the work:

- **Fire the band if the band is the problem.** McCartney (2005), early Radiohead arrangement decisions, the no-drums logic on *In Rainbows*' "House of Cards" — Godrich removes whoever is between the song and the listener.
- **Slow it down, speed it up, run it backwards.** "No Surprises" tempo trick; *Amok*'s electronic-then-live-then-edited workflow.
- **The room is an instrument.** St. Catherine's Court stairwells; Maida Vale's neutral coldness on *From the Basement*; Greenwood's house on *Wall of Eyes*.
- **Strings are not decoration.** David Campbell on *Sea Change*; London Contemporary Orchestra on The Smile.
- **Vibe > fidelity, momentum > polish.** Repeated across thirty years of interviews.
