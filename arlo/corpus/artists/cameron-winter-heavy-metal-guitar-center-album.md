## Who Cameron Winter Is on This Record

Cameron Winter is the frontman of the Brooklyn band Geese — covered in the sibling file [`/Users/cfitt/Dev/arlo/corpus/artists/geese-band-context.md`](/Users/cfitt/Dev/arlo/corpus/artists/geese-band-context.md) — and *Heavy Metal*, released December 6, 2024 on Partisan Records / Play It Again Sam, is his debut solo album ([Wikipedia: *Heavy Metal*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heavy_Metal_(album))). The record is a deliberate departure from the dance-punk/3D-country chassis of Geese: piano-led, horn-flecked, string-shaded, and largely without electric guitar or kit drumming for long stretches. Walden Green's Pitchfork "Best New Music" writeup framed it as "a project of catharsis that never comes across like an exercise in vanity, an outpouring of material as necessary to its creator as it is compelling to experience" ([Partisan Records: Pitchfork BNM moment page](https://partisanrecords.com/moment/cameron-winter-heavy-metal)). For ARLO, this is the file for a single specific aesthetic decision: *what happens when a band's frontperson strips out the band's signature instruments on purpose*.

## Loren Humphrey, Producer

The album is co-produced and engineered by Winter and Loren Humphrey. Humphrey is a touring drummer (Tame Impala, Florence and the Machine) who has built a parallel career as a producer and engineer with a specific lineage: recording engineer on Arctic Monkeys' *The Car* (2022); producer of Willie J Healey's *Twin Heavy* (recorded in a nine-day session at Zoo Studios in Eastbourne) and *Bunny*; drums, writing, production and engineering credits on Lana Del Rey's *Blue Banisters* (2021), including "Dealer" and "Thunder"; and recording engineer / additional producer on Tame Impala's "Loser" (2025) ([Rate Your Music: Loren Humphrey credits](https://rateyourmusic.com/artist/loren-humphrey/credits/); [Sound Geist: "You Love Loren Humphrey, You Just Don't Know It Yet"](https://thesoundgeist.substack.com/p/you-love-loren-humphrey-you-just); [NME: Willie J Healey *Twin Heavy* announcement](https://www.nme.com/news/music/willie-j-healey-announces-details-of-new-album-twin-heavy-2641959)). He also produced parts of Geese's *3D Country* (2023), making him not an outside hire on *Heavy Metal* but a returning collaborator ([NME: Cameron Winter solo album interview](https://www.nme.com/news/music/geese-cameron-winter-interview-solo-album-heavy-metal-tom-waits-bob-dylan-3807498)). The through-line in Humphrey's catalog is the *post-Arctic-Monkeys piano-and-strings palette* — *The Car*, *Blue Banisters*, *Twin Heavy*, *Heavy Metal* all share a taste for chamber-pop arrangements, brushed dynamics, and room-mic'd warmth in service of a singular voice. That palette is exactly what *Heavy Metal* draws from.

## Tuxedo Park, Not Guitar Center: The Actual Studio

The headline anecdote about *Heavy Metal* is the Guitar Center claim — addressed in the next section — but the verified production base was Humphrey's home studio in Tuxedo, New York, a converted guest house in the gated Tuxedo Park community upstate. Recording began there in July 2023, with additional tracking in Winter's childhood bedroom in New York ([Wikipedia: *Heavy Metal*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heavy_Metal_(album))). The Face's interview describes Winter taking an early morning bus up from the city to work at Humphrey's place daily ([The Face: Cameron Winter interview](https://theface.com/music/cameron-winter-interview-geese-getting-killed-heavy-metal)). This is the *real* recording setup: an upstate residential studio, daily commute, single-producer relationship. For ARLO's suggestion logic, the Tuxedo Park sessions matter more than the Guitar Center story even if the latter is the press hook.

## The Guitar Center Story, Carefully

Winter has told interviewers — repeatedly and with elaboration — that he recorded portions of the album in a rotating series of Guitar Center retail locations across the New York tri-state area, using in-store instruments and amps, moving on to the next franchise each time staff asked him to leave. Wikipedia summarizes the assertions as "dubious," noting Winter "migrating to a different location each time he was kicked out" and flagging the claim as unverified ([Wikipedia: *Heavy Metal*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heavy_Metal_(album))). The Line of Best Fit interview reports Winter saying, "After a day or two I'd inevitably get kicked out," and that he eventually obtained a "lifetime ban" from these stores ([The Line of Best Fit: "Not Kidding This Time"](https://www.thelineofbestfit.com/features/interviews/cameron-winter-not-kidding-this-time)). An earlier, more embellished version circulated where Winter claimed he recorded the album at Guitar Centers across the entire US, with a five-year-old bass player and other personnel found on Craigslist; that version was later walked back in favor of the more contained New-York-tri-state version, and Winter has separately acknowledged that the studio work happened at Humphrey's place ([Uproxx: Cameron Winter *Heavy Metal* review](https://uproxx.com/indie/cameron-winter-heavy-metal-review/)). The honest summary: Winter has a documented habit of folkloric press answers, the Guitar Center story is at minimum exaggerated, and the verifiable studio is Humphrey's Tuxedo Park guest house. For ARLO, the *aesthetic* of the Guitar Center claim is what matters — that the album sounds, by intention, like it could have been made by someone improvising on whatever piano happened to be on the showroom floor.

## The "Guitar Center Method" as Aesthetic Stance

Even if the literal retail-store recording is partially fictional, the *method* it gestures at is real and operative in the record. Winter has described the writing process as largely improvised — banging on a piano without pre-written material and reverse-engineering songs from the resulting takes, producing work he calls "stitched together and kind of weird" ([NME interview](https://www.nme.com/news/music/geese-cameron-winter-interview-solo-album-heavy-metal-tom-waits-bob-dylan-3807498)). That posture — first-take piano improvisation as the primary writing surface, with arrangement built afterward — is the actual content of the Guitar Center story stripped of its theatrical frame. For ARLO this is a usable axiom: *the demo is the master, and the instrument is whatever is available*. The aesthetic permission the story gives — that you can write the record at a showroom Yamaha and finish it later — is more important than whether it literally happened.

## Anti-Geese Choices: No Drums, No Electric Guitar

The deliberate aesthetic decision underneath the record is the near-total absence of Geese's signature instruments. Where Geese is built on Max Bassin's drumming, dual electric guitar interplay, and country-fried rhythm-section locomotion, *Heavy Metal* mostly drops both. Winter played vocals, piano, guitar, bass, and drum machine; Humphrey played the limited drums and percussion that do appear; the rest of the arrangement work goes to James Richardson (nylon guitar, trumpet, French horn, acoustic guitar), Jesse Kontansky (strings, violin), Xan Aird (electric guitar — used sparingly), and Peter Hess (saxophone, clarinet) ([Wikipedia: *Heavy Metal*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heavy_Metal_(album))). The Face's interview confirms the minimal-electric-guitar, minimal-drums approach was an intentional differentiation from Geese ([The Face](https://theface.com/music/cameron-winter-interview-geese-getting-killed-heavy-metal)). Winter has said he believed the songs would be "weak with a full band arrangement" and had concerns about the album coming off as "diet Geese" — meaning a watered-down version of the band — which is what drove him toward the chamber-pop palette ([Wikipedia: *Heavy Metal*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heavy_Metal_(album))). The negative space is the design: ARLO should treat the absence of drums and electric guitar as the most important compositional choice on the record, not as a limitation to work around.

## "More Impressionistic Than the Geese Stuff": Winter's Own Framing

Winter has described the contrast with Geese in painterly terms. Per NME, the improvisational writing process "led to something that was a little more impressionistic than a lot of the Geese stuff, which ended up being fine, but was also really fucking stressful" ([NME interview](https://www.nme.com/news/music/geese-cameron-winter-interview-solo-album-heavy-metal-tom-waits-bob-dylan-3807498)). He has named his lyrical reference points as Bob Dylan, Leonard Cohen, Tom Waits, Harry Nilsson, and — with characteristic deflection — Weird Al Yankovic, alongside literary touchstones including the Beats, Rimbaud, and James Joyce, summarized in his line "I went to the pretentious stuff really fast" ([Uproxx review](https://uproxx.com/indie/cameron-winter-heavy-metal-review/); [The Line of Best Fit interview](https://www.thelineofbestfit.com/features/interviews/cameron-winter-not-kidding-this-time)). The lyrical posture is image-first rather than confessional-first — vulnerability through obscured imagery rather than direct disclosure. For ARLO that distinction is critical: it is *not* a singer-songwriter record in the autobiographical-narrative sense; it is a record where the lyrics behave like collaged poetic fragments and the emotional weight is carried by performance and arrangement.

## The 18-Month Process

Winter has said the album took roughly 18 months to make, much longer than his initial four-month estimate ([NME interview](https://www.nme.com/news/music/geese-cameron-winter-interview-solo-album-heavy-metal-tom-waits-bob-dylan-3807498)). The Line of Best Fit reports Winter experienced depression during the main tracking period and described the work as grueling self-reliance, summarized by his line "I'm most comfortable putting something out when I've exhausted all other options" ([The Line of Best Fit interview](https://www.thelineofbestfit.com/features/interviews/cameron-winter-not-kidding-this-time)). With tracking starting July 2023 and release on December 6, 2024, the 18-month window lines up. The relevance for ARLO is *not* the length itself — many records take 18 months — but the working pattern: long internal rumination, improvised first captures, slow reverse-engineering into finished arrangements, mood-driven pacing, single-collaborator studio relationship. That is a defensible production posture independent of the actual schedule.

## The Sonic Palette in Specifics

On a track-by-track basis the arrangements lean on a few recurring textures. "The Rolling Stones" opens the record. "Cancer of the Skull" combines acoustic guitar, piano, jaw harp, and what Uproxx's review calls a "punch-drunk horn section." "Drinking Age" is a piano ballad. "Try As I May" rides a repetitive organ riff — a Charli-XCX-meets-Steve-Reich kind of small loop, but built from a churchy timbre. "$0" is the lead single, a roughly six-minute piano ballad with string arrangement, described by Uproxx as a "neo-classical number" ([Uproxx review](https://uproxx.com/indie/cameron-winter-heavy-metal-review/); [NME interview](https://www.nme.com/news/music/geese-cameron-winter-interview-solo-album-heavy-metal-tom-waits-bob-dylan-3807498)). Other named tracks include "Love Takes Miles," "Nina + Field of Cops," "Vines," and "Take It With You." Total runtime is 43:50 across ten tracks ([Wikipedia: *Heavy Metal*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heavy_Metal_(album)); [The Face interview](https://theface.com/music/cameron-winter-interview-geese-getting-killed-heavy-metal)). The aggregate sonic palette — piano, nylon guitar, jaw harp, brass (trumpet, French horn, sax, clarinet), strings, sparse electric guitar, sparse percussion, occasional drum machine — is the negative-image of Geese's full-band rock-bar lineup.

## Critical Reception

*Heavy Metal* received Pitchfork's "Best New Music" designation, and was ranked **#3 on Pitchfork's Best Albums of 2025** list — the late-2024 release date meant the record landed in the 2025 critical-cycle window for most year-end lists ([Brooklyn Vegan: Pitchfork's Top 50 Albums of 2025](https://www.brooklynvegan.com/pitchforks-top-50-albums-of-2025/); [chart data on X confirming #3 ranking](https://x.com/chartdata/status/1995870780417343515)). Pitchfork's accompanying songs list ranked "Love Takes Miles" as the **#1 song of 2025**. Rolling Stone placed the album at #32 on its year-end list, Paste named it one of the best debut albums of 2024, and AnyDecentMusic? scored it 8.4/10 ([Wikipedia: *Heavy Metal*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heavy_Metal_(album))). **Important verification note:** the seed prompt's "Pitchfork #3 album of 2024" should read "Pitchfork #3 album of *2025*"; the album was released in December 2024 but counted as a 2025 record on Pitchfork's list.

## ARLO Application Notes

For ARLO's suggestion logic, *Heavy Metal* is the file to retrieve when the user is:
- A frontperson of a band wanting to record something *adjacent to but deliberately not like* the band's main aesthetic.
- Considering a piano-led, horn-and-string-arranged chamber-folk approach instead of the rock-band default.
- Improvising material at a single instrument and wanting permission to reverse-engineer the resulting takes into songs.
- Working with a single producer-engineer in a domestic studio setting over a long, mood-driven schedule.
- Building a lyrical surface that is image-first and obscured rather than confessional.

The record's two load-bearing principles for the aesthetic stack:
1. *Negative-space instrumentation as design* — defining the record by what is left out (drums, electric guitar) is as strong a compositional move as anything that is left in.
2. *Improvised piano writing with later arrangement build* — the Guitar Center mythos is operative even if not literal: write at whatever piano is to hand, treat the take as the song, layer chamber instruments around it afterward.

The aesthetic *inverts* the LCD Soundsystem / Charli XCX maximalist-loop approach in the ARLO stack and *complements* the mk.gee / Dijon DI-and-room intimacy approach. It is best paired in retrieval with the Geese band file (for the contrast) and with the Bon Iver sample-driven songwriting file (for the related "find-the-song-inside-the-take" approach).
