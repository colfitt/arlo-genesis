## The 13-Year Gap and Why That Frames the Record

*The Tree of Forgiveness* is Prine's eighteenth and final studio album of original material, released April 13, 2018 on his own Oh Boy Records, and it is the first record of new songs he had released in thirteen years — the prior original-material LP was *Fair & Square* in 2005 [(The Tree of Forgiveness — Wikipedia)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Tree_of_Forgiveness). In the intervening period Prine had survived squamous cell carcinoma of the neck (surgery 1998) and lung cancer (surgery 2013), both of which permanently re-textured his voice — Rolling Stone described his "plainspoken tenor" as having taken on a quality that "creaks like a wide-plank old floor in winter" [(Rolling Stone review)](https://www.rollingstone.com/music/music-album-reviews/review-john-prine-keeps-finding-fresh-revelations-on-tree-of-forgiveness-628390/). For ARLO retrieval the load-bearing point is that the production brief on this record is essentially: *don't fight the weathered baritone, build a room around it*. Every other choice — sparse acoustic instrumentation, three-mic drum setups, live tracking, harmony vocals from younger acolytes — descends from that premise.

## Dave Cobb's Tenancy of RCA Studio A

The room itself is non-trivial to the sound. RCA Studio A at 30 Music Square West in Nashville was built in 1965 by Chet Atkins, Owen Bradley, and Harold Bradley, designed by RCA architect John E. Volkmann to record the large ensembles the Nashville Sound required — the live room is 75 by 45 feet with 25-foot ceilings, the largest tracking room in Nashville at the time it opened [(RCA Studio A — Wikipedia)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/RCA_Studio_A). Dolly Parton cut "Jolene" there; Elvis recorded gospel sides there. After RCA's tenancy ended the room cycled through Music City Music Hall, Javelina Recording Studios, and from 2002 Ben Folds' Grand Victor Sound. In 2014 a developer planned to demolish it for condos, and Folds' public campaign — joined by Mike Curb and local philanthropists who bought the building for around $5.6 million — saved it; it was added to the National Register of Historic Places in 2015 [(Nashville Scene)](https://www.nashvillescene.com/music/features/three-years-after-being-saved-studio-a-still-makes-an-impact/article_68f6aa0b-d892-5b40-b368-13d0b7bf32bc.html). Dave Cobb has leased the studio since 2016 as the base for his Low Country Sound imprint, and a $500,000 restoration was completed in October 2017, including replicated period RCA Victor signage on the exterior [(RCA Studio A — Wikipedia)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/RCA_Studio_A). *Tree of Forgiveness* was tracked between July and December 2017, which means it was made in that room essentially the moment the restoration finished.

## Cobb's "Sounds the Same Wherever You Put Them" Note on the Room

Cobb has talked openly about why Studio A is unusual to track in. In Tape Op he said of the live room, "No matter where I put them, it kind of sounds the same. It's bizarre" — meaning that mic placement is forgiving in a way most rooms aren't, which he reads as intentional acoustic design from the 1965 build [(Tape Op #122 — Dave Cobb)](https://tapeop.com/interviews/122/dave-cobb). For ARLO this is a useful concrete fact: the room itself is part of why Cobb-at-Studio-A productions sound consistent across artists. The control room now centers on a 32-channel API Legacy AXS console, with Cobb's personal Helios, Neve, and UA outboard brought in on top [(Mix Online — Dave Cobb Settles into Studio A)](https://www.mixonline.com/recording/profiles/dave-cobb-settles-in-to-rca-studio-a). The Helios is Cobb's prized piece — sourced from Love Studio in Finland — and he has described it as "the best console I've ever heard in my life. It sounds like a record, and that's with no EQ" [(Tape Op)](https://tapeop.com/interviews/122/dave-cobb). Don't model an in-the-box modern Nashville sound for this album; model a 1965 room running through API + Helios + tape.

## Cobb's Method — Fast, Live, Tape Rolling

The documented Cobb method, which is the method *Tree of Forgiveness* was made with, has four moving parts. First, he records bands live in one room and refuses isolation: "I hate cutting separately. I can't stand it," with vocalists tracking live with the band because "their pitch is way better" with the band in the room [(Tape Op)](https://tapeop.com/interviews/122/dave-cobb). Second, he keeps tape rolling continuously, including during run-throughs, because "that's when you usually get your best vocal performance" — usually "between three takes, you got it" [(Universal Audio — Champion of Nashville's New Sound)](https://www.uaudio.com/blog/dave-cobb-nashvilles-new-sound/). Third, he tracks roughly 70% to tape — a Studer A80 figures heavily in his rig — and on past records like Sturgill Simpson's *Metamodern Sounds in Country Music* he printed reverbs and delays live, e.g. a Studer A80 1/4-inch tape machine going into a Studio Technologies Ecoplate III, rather than adding them in mix [(Tape Op)](https://tapeop.com/interviews/122/dave-cobb). Fourth, his miking is famously minimal: three mics on drums (kick, snare, overhead — the Neumann U87 frequently as the overhead, positioned low near the drummer's head), Coles 4038 as a primary vocal mic, Spectra Sonics 610 compressor "just barely tickling" the meter [(Tape Op)](https://tapeop.com/interviews/122/dave-cobb). For ARLO the operative descriptor on this kind of session is "fast and analog" — not because Cobb specifies tape speeds publicly, but because the *workflow* is fast: live cuts, three-takes-max, tape rolling, minimal mics, mix-as-you-go.

## The Band on Tree of Forgiveness

The core band is Prine's longtime road band augmented by Cobb himself. Lead vocal and acoustic guitar are Prine. Electric guitar is Jason Wilber (Prine's longtime band guitarist); fiddle is Amanda Shires; bass is Dave Jacques; drums are Kenneth Blevins. Cobb plays acoustic and 12-string guitar plus mellotron, claps, and kazoo across the record. Brandi Carlile sings harmony on three tracks. Jason Isbell plays electric guitar on one track and slide guitar and background vocals on two others. Amanda Shires sings background vocals in addition to her fiddle parts [(Wikipedia)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Tree_of_Forgiveness). That guest list — Isbell, Shires, Carlile — is itself a piece of cultural mapping: these are the singer-songwriters of the Nashville/Americana revival explicitly framing Prine as their lineage, on a Prine record produced by the producer (Cobb) who made *Southeastern*, *Traveller*, and *By the Way, I Forgive You*.

## The Cobb Lineage — Why This Record Sits Inside a Larger Movement

Cobb is one of the central producers of the 2010s Nashville revival. His discography by the time of *Tree of Forgiveness* already included Sturgill Simpson's *High Top Mountain* (2013) and *Metamodern Sounds in Country Music* (2014), Jason Isbell's *Southeastern* (2013) and several follow-ups, Chris Stapleton's 5x-platinum *Traveller* (2015, co-produced and tracked at RCA Studio A as part of the campaign that saved the building), and Brandi Carlile's *By the Way, I Forgive You* (2018) [(Dave Cobb — Wikipedia)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dave_Cobb). He later co-anchored The Highwomen project with Carlile, Shires, Maren Morris, and Natalie Hemby. For ARLO the retrieval point is that when a user asks about "Cobb-produced Nashville sound," they are asking about a coherent stylistic movement — analog, live-tracked, three-mic-drums, no-vocal-isolation, singer-forward, often using Studio A as the literal physical room — and *Tree of Forgiveness* is the elder-statesman entry in that catalog. Prine is the songwriter the rest of the Cobb roster acknowledges as origin.

## The Hotel Writing Sessions — Auerbach and McLaughlin

The songs were written before tracking. Prine has said he checked into an Omni hotel in Nashville to finish the album because "after 45 years on the road, I just can conduct myself better in a hotel" [(Uproxx — Tree of Forgiveness interview)](https://uproxx.com/music/john-prine-tree-of-forgiveness-interview/). His writing partners on the record are Pat McLaughlin (longtime Prine co-writer; Nashville journeyman whose cuts include Alan Jackson, Trisha Yearwood, Gary Allan) and Dan Auerbach of The Black Keys, who by 2017 was running Easy Eye Sound and had become a Nashville fixture. In one stretch Prine and Auerbach (with McLaughlin) wrote six songs in two days; two of those made the record, including "Caravan of Fools" [(Uproxx)](https://uproxx.com/music/john-prine-tree-of-forgiveness-interview/). Auerbach himself confirmed on X at release that he co-wrote both "Caravan of Fools" and "Boundless Love" [(Dan Auerbach on X, April 2018)](https://x.com/danauerbach/status/983349530069536768). Prine described having to rework "Boundless Love" after the initial co-write because the lyric didn't feel like him yet — he called Auerbach and said, in his framing, that he needed to "John Prine it up" [(Uproxx)](https://uproxx.com/music/john-prine-tree-of-forgiveness-interview/). That's a useful idiom for ARLO: songs come back from Nashville co-writes feeling generically Nashville, and the final pass is making them feel idiosyncratically authored.

## "Knockin' on Your Screen Door" and "Summer's End" — McLaughlin Co-Writes

Both "Knockin' on Your Screen Door" and "Summer's End" are Prine/McLaughlin co-writes [(Rolling Stone — Screen Door premiere)](https://www.rollingstone.com/music/music-country/hear-john-prines-ambling-new-song-knockin-on-your-screen-door-119890/). "Knockin' on Your Screen Door" is an uptempo folk song carrying a downbeat narrator — a lonely, broke man whose only listed possession is a George Jones 8-track tape — and it earned Prine a Grammy nomination for Best American Roots Song. "Summer's End" is a tender ballad framed by Rolling Stone as "an open-armed invitation home to a grown child, perhaps, amidst images of inevitable loss," and it won Best Americana Song at the 2019 Americana Music Honors & Awards and was later read in the post-Prine context as a quiet opioid-epidemic lament without ever naming it as such [(Rolling Stone)](https://www.rollingstone.com/music/music-album-reviews/review-john-prine-keeps-finding-fresh-revelations-on-tree-of-forgiveness-628390/). Both songs sit in a country-folk register Cobb dresses sparingly — acoustic guitar foreground, fiddle and harmony vocals as the color, drums kept low and brushy.

## "Caravan of Fools" — The Auerbach Black-Keys Edge

"Caravan of Fools" is the song on the record that most obviously bears Auerbach's fingerprints. Critics and the Uproxx interview describe it as an apocalyptic blues lament — its imagery centers on distant drumming, the pounding of hooves, and a procession decked in shiny jewels [(Uproxx)](https://uproxx.com/music/john-prine-tree-of-forgiveness-interview/). For ARLO modeling purposes the relevant point is that *Tree of Forgiveness* is not monochromatic: most of the album lives in country-folk warmth, but "Caravan of Fools" and "Boundless Love" pull in a darker, Easy-Eye-Sound, fuzzed-blues posture that's Auerbach's natural register. Cobb mixes the two registers without smoothing them flat. The track later got further visibility via a Dan Auerbach cover and a sync placement in *Yellowstone* season 4 [(Whiskey Riff)](https://www.whiskeyriff.com/2023/04/13/on-this-date-john-prine-releases-final-studio-album-the-tree-of-forgiveness/).

## "I Have Met My Love Today" and the Brandi Carlile Vocal

"I Have Met My Love Today" features Brandi Carlile on harmony [(JohnPrine.com press)](https://yougotgold.johnprine.com/press/john-prine-returns-to-songwriting-jaunty-and-dark-on-the-tree-of-forgiveness-). The arrangement is among the sparsest on the record — acoustic guitar, fiddle, restrained rhythm section, two voices. The retrieval handle for ARLO is the deliberate generational pairing: a weathered Prine lead with Carlile's much-higher and much-cleaner harmony sitting above it. This is the same pattern Cobb uses elsewhere — pairing a singular character voice with younger, technically pristine harmony singers (see his Stapleton/Morgane Stapleton and Isbell/Shires work) — and it's a load-bearing texture of the Cobb-Nashville-revival sound writ large.

## "When I Get to Heaven" — The Album Closer

The closer is the song most people now associate with the record. Thematically it is a self-aware afterlife monologue, played with the band-plus-friends raised to a party — claps, kazoo, gang vocals, a deliberately ramshackle feel. Cobb's production note here is to *not* polish: keep the room sound, keep the gang of friends audible, keep the kazoo in [(Wikipedia)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Tree_of_Forgiveness). Rolling Stone characterized the song as a "mix of punchlines, sweet sentimentality and looming void" [(Rolling Stone)](https://www.rollingstone.com/music/music-album-reviews/review-john-prine-keeps-finding-fresh-revelations-on-tree-of-forgiveness-628390/). The album's other notable themes — mortality, war, drugs, addiction — recur across Prine's catalog and surface again here, but ARLO should describe these themes analytically and never reproduce Prine's lyrics verbatim. Title-level references are sufficient.

## Commercial and Critical Outcome

*Tree of Forgiveness* debuted at No. 5 on the Billboard 200 with over 54,000 album-equivalent units in its first week, the highest chart placement of Prine's career; it topped Billboard's Americana/Folk Albums chart and reached No. 2 on the country and rock albums charts; Metacritic aggregated reviews at 77/100 [(Wikipedia)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Tree_of_Forgiveness). At the 2019 Grammys "Summer's End" and "Knockin' on Your Screen Door" both drew Best American Roots Song nominations, and the album itself was nominated for Best Americana Album. Prine was named Artist of the Year at the 2018 Americana Music Honors & Awards. The record's commercial peak combined with Prine's death two years later — April 7, 2020, from COVID-19 complications — has fixed *Tree of Forgiveness* in the canon as both a late-career artistic statement and a de facto farewell, neither of which it was originally framed as.

## Why This Matters for ARLO's Aesthetic Stack

For ARLO's hybrid songwriter brief, *Tree of Forgiveness* anchors the American-songcraft pole of the project — the corner held down by Punch Brothers, Billy Strings, and the Pikelny acoustic-virtuosity tradition. It supplies four useful patterns. First, the *songs-first* discipline: write at a hotel desk with Pat McLaughlin and Dan Auerbach until the songs are finished, then track. Second, the *don't-fight-the-voice* arrangement principle: when the lead voice is a character voice — weathered, idiosyncratic, not pristine — the production job is to clear space rather than to layer. Third, the *Cobb method* as a model live tracking workflow: three mics on drums, no vocal isolation, tape rolling continuously, three-takes-max, mix-as-you-go, reverbs printed live. Fourth, the *generational-harmony pairing* — putting a character lead next to a technically pristine younger harmony singer (Carlile, Shires) to create a two-voice texture that does emotional work the lead voice alone cannot. None of these patterns require a 1965 75-by-45-foot tracking room to execute, but the room is what makes the Cobb version of them sound like itself.
