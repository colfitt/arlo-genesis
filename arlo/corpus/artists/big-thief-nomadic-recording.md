## The Concept in One Sentence

*Dragon New Warm Mountain I Believe in You* is what happens when a band treats geography as an instrument: drummer-producer James Krivchenia mapped out a five-location, five-month US itinerary, rotated engineers per site, and let landscape, weather, and room acoustics shape a 20-song double album cut down from roughly 45 attempted takes.[^1][^2][^3] It is the Big-Thief-as-band counterpart to Adrianne Lenker's parallel solo intimate-folk recording practice (see `corpus/artists/adrianne-lenker-intimate-folk.md`).

## Origin of the Nomadic Plan

Krivchenia conceived the project in late 2019 in a Copenhagen hotel during the tail of the *Two Hands* tour cycle, pitching the band on a coast-to-coast recording trip designed to "capture a full expression of Lenker's songwriting and the band onto a single album."[^1][^2] When COVID hit, the schedule slid and the geography expanded. The band quarantined together in the Vermont woods for two weeks in July 2020 before the first official session — that quarantine effectively became a pre-production retreat where material was workshopped in close quarters.[^1][^4]

This is the first Big Thief album not helmed by long-time producer Andrew Sarlo; Krivchenia's shift from drummer to drummer-producer is the central production decision of the record.[^1][^5]

## The Five Locations (Verified)

Wikipedia lists five physical recording sites, while most press coverage compresses these to four "official" zones because the Massachusetts session predates the formal album concept and overlaps with quarantine work. The full inventory:[^1]

1. **The Elf Inn — Westhampton, Massachusetts (April 2020).** Earliest tracking, pandemic-era informal sessions.
2. **Flying Cloud Recordings — Upstate New York / Catskills (July–August 2020).** Sam Owens's (a.k.a. Sam Evian) home studio. First "official" album session.[^4][^6]
3. **Five Star Studios — Topanga, California (October 2020).** Engineered by Shawn Everett (War on Drugs, Alabama Shakes).[^1]
4. **MusicGardens / "Studio in the Clouds" — Rocky Mountains, Colorado, near Telluride (November 2020).** Engineered by Dom Monks, who had worked on prior Big Thief records.[^1]
5. **Press On Studio — Tucson, Arizona (December 2020).** Engineered by Scott McMicken (Dr. Dog). Multi-instrumentalist Mat Davidson (Twain) joined for vocals, fiddle, piano, pedal steel, recorder, and accordion — the first time a substantial fifth instrumentalist appeared on a Big Thief LP.[^1]

The rotation matters: by changing engineers and rooms, Krivchenia traded the unified-sonic-signature approach (Sarlo) for a *travelogue* approach where each track can carry the fingerprints of a different studio philosophy.

## James Krivchenia as Drummer-Producer

Krivchenia originally joined Big Thief as a sound engineer on their debut and shifted to the drum throne in 2016.[^5] On *Dragon* he occupies both roles simultaneously — tracking drums live in the same room while shaping the production from inside the take. That dual position is the structural reason the album sounds like *band-in-room-deciding-now* rather than *band-being-produced-at*: tempo decisions, arrangement edits, and "leave that in" calls happen from behind the kit, not from a control-room talkback. Compare to how mk.gee or Dijon work as their own producers — same impulse, different vocabulary.

## Sam Owens (Sam Evian) at Flying Cloud — The "Certainty" Story

The most-cited gear anecdote of the album, and a useful reference point for intimate-folk-band production:

During the July–August 2020 Catskills session at Sam Owens's Flying Cloud Recordings, a three-day power outage hit. On day three, Buck Meek found Lenker writing a new song on the porch and joined her — they finished it with "rain falling from the gutters splashing over our guitars."[^7][^8] Rather than wait out the outage, Owens (engineering) and Krivchenia (producing/drumming) rigged an emergency tracking setup:

- A **four-track tape machine** was placed in the kitchen, powered by a **Ford F-250 pickup's cigarette lighter** running out in the yard — effectively recording off the truck's car battery.[^7][^8][^9]
- **Drums were set up by the sink.**[^7][^8]
- **Max Oleartchik plugged his bass into a Bluetooth speaker sitting on top of the stove** for amplification.[^7][^8]
- Hannah Cohen added harmonies; **Sam and Hannah's dog, Jan, barked during the solo on take two** — kept in the final mix.[^7][^8]
- They got it in three takes — Meek joked it "took us about that long to learn the chords."[^7] Afterwards: pancakes and sausages for dinner.[^7]

The song became "Certainty," a Grammy-nominated single (Best Alternative Music Performance) and a near-perfect demonstration of how *constraint plus presence* can outperform a clean signal chain.[^5][^10]

## The Eight-Track at Flying Cloud (Pre-Outage)

Before the power went out, the broader Flying Cloud session ran on an **8-track tape machine** with Owens at the controls.[^6] So the Catskills site actually generated two distinct sonic signatures on the album: 8-track-tape-band-in-a-room performances, and the lo-fi four-track-on-car-battery "Certainty" outlier. Useful mental model: Flying Cloud = warm 8-track baseline; "Certainty" = emergency four-track artifact within that same site.

## The 20-Song Double-Album Format

Roughly **45 complete songs** were tracked across the five sessions. The 20-track sequence released as a 4AD double LP on **February 11, 2022** is the surviving edit.[^1][^2] The 2.25-to-1 cut ratio is meaningful: it suggests Big Thief treated each location as a *generative pool* rather than tracking a predetermined tracklist. The double-album scope (~80 minutes) is what allows the stylistic range to read as a single statement instead of a sampler — country two-step ("Spud Infinity"), drum-machine pop ("Time Escaping"), kitchen-floor lo-fi ("Certainty"), and electric epic ("Little Things") sit on the same record because the format gives them space.

## What This Imposes on Writing

A double album cut from 45 songs forces writing practices that pair well with ARLO's frame:

- **Volume over polish at the writing stage.** Lenker arrives with surplus material; the band tracks broadly, edits ruthlessly. The pre-tour Vermont quarantine functioned as a writing-room, not a rehearsal-room — songs were *finished into the take* rather than rehearsed into a fixed arrangement.
- **Per-site mood capture.** The Sonoran-desert Tucson tracks read different from the Rocky-Mountain Colorado tracks because the *room and weather* were different, not because the songs were demoed differently. A song's identity is partly the altitude it was tracked at.
- **Engineer-as-collaborator-not-translator.** Rotating Owens / Everett / Monks / McMicken across sites means each song gets the engineer best suited to its physics, rather than one engineer translating everything to a house sound. The trade-off is unity-of-vocabulary; the gain is range.
- **Songwriting that survives format-jumping.** A song that can land on 4-track car-battery tape *and* in a Topanga studio room has structural integrity that a song which only works under one mic plot does not. The nomadic schedule effectively stress-tests the writing.

## The Bon-Iver-Adjacent Intimate-Folk-Band Lineage

There is a direct line from Justin Vernon's *For Emma* cabin recordings through Phil Weinrobe's room-as-instrument work with Lenker solo (see sibling file) to Krivchenia's nomadic *Dragon* concept. All three share:

- **Geography as production decision** (Wisconsin cabin / Brooklyn loft / five US sites).
- **Players in the same room with minimal headphone bleed worry.**
- **Tape as default capture medium** (8-track and 4-track here; analog tape on Lenker solo; Vernon's well-documented tape practice).
- **Found-sound tolerance** — dog barks, rain, room ambience treated as features, not bugs.

The distinction this file owns: *Dragon* is the **band-scale** expression of that lineage. Lenker solo is the *individual-scale* expression. ARLO should keep them separate when retrieving for writing-flow questions vs. production-decision questions.

## Useful Patterns for ARLO Users

- **The "move the room" intervention.** When a song refuses to commit on take five in studio A, the Krivchenia answer is *change studios*, not *change the arrangement*.
- **The "engineer-as-site" mapping.** Match the engineer to the song's physics: Owens for warmth/tape, Everett for sculpted indie-rock, Monks for restraint, McMicken for country-adjacent.
- **The car-battery-as-permission-slip.** When the rig is on a cigarette lighter, *nobody is precious about it*. The looseness in the take comes from the looseness in the chain. Useful when a home demo is getting over-produced.
- **The 2-to-1 cut ratio.** Track more than you intend to release. The editing happens after, not during.
