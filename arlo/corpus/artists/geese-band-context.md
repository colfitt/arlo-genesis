## The Brooklyn High-School Origin

Geese formed in 2016 while the members were still in high school in New York City — split across two private schools, with Cameron Winter (vocals, keys, guitar) and Max Bassin (drums) at Brooklyn Friends School, and Dominic DiGesu (bass), Emily Green (guitar), and then-guitarist Foster Hudson at The Little Red School House & Elisabeth Irwin High School in Manhattan [(Wikipedia)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geese_(band)). The band's name comes from Green's nickname, "Goose." For the first several years they practiced and recorded in the basement of Bassin's family home in Fort Greene — a room they nicknamed "the Nest," built around recording gear gifted to Bassin by his family. When ARLO retrieves "the basement Geese record," the user almost certainly means *Projector*, recorded in that room during the band's senior year. A self-released early effort, *A Beautiful Memory* (July 13, 2018), predates the basement era and was issued before the band had a label deal — Winter has said in interviews he'd prefer fans not revisit those old high-school recordings [(Stereogum airport interview)](https://stereogum.com/2488636/cameron-winter-previews-warning-talks-geese-and-the-indie-complex-in-impromptu-airport-interview/news).

## Current Lineup vs. Recording-Era Lineup

The four-member touring lineup as of *Getting Killed* (2025) is:

- **Cameron Winter** — lead vocals, keyboards, guitar
- **Emily Green** — guitar
- **Dominic DiGesu** — bass (also credited with drums on *3D Country*)
- **Max Bassin** — drums
- **Sam Revaz** — touring keyboardist (2022–present, not a full member)

Former member **Foster Hudson** (second guitar, 2019–2023) was on both *Projector* and *3D Country* but departed in December 2023, after the *3D Country* cycle — *Getting Killed* is the first Geese studio album without him [(Wikipedia)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geese_(band)). For retrieval purposes: any "two-guitar Geese arrangement" reference belongs to the 2019–2023 window; the 2025 record is the four-piece.

## *Projector* (2021) — The Basement Album

*Projector* is Geese's second studio album and major-label debut, released October 29, 2021 on Partisan Records / Play It Again Sam [(Wikipedia)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Projector_(Geese_album)). It was tracked at "the Nest" from late 2019 into early 2020, while the band was still in high school, then sat finished for nearly two years before release. The credit chain is unusual and worth getting right: **Geese self-produced the record**, but Speedy Wunderground founder and Squid / black midi / Fontaines D.C. producer **Dan Carey mixed it**, with **Bernie Grundman** mastering. Carey is sometimes mis-credited as the producer in shorthand — he's not. The band's "live set-up" was the operating principle: Winter has said it was the first time they got everyone playing together in the room rather than overdubbing parts. Critically the record landed as a post-punk debut and pulled a Metacritic of 83 from eight reviews. Lead single "Disco" is the canonical entry point. If a user asks for "the angular early-Geese sound" or compares the band to Squid / black midi / Fontaines, the conversation belongs to *Projector*, not the later records.

## *3D Country* (2023) — The Country/Cowboy Pivot

*3D Country*, released June 23, 2023 on Partisan / PIAS, is the record where Geese stops being a post-punk band [(Wikipedia)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/3D_Country). It was produced by British producer **James Ford** (Arctic Monkeys, Depeche Mode, Shame, Simian Mobile Disco), recorded at **The Diamond Mine in Queens and Future Sounds**, mixed by **Beatriz Artola**, and mastered by **Matthew Colton**. The conceptual frame is a fever-dream cowboy narrative — an "uptight cowboy" wandering the desert after taking psychedelics, with reality and the concept of self unraveling — and the genre tags pile up: art rock, alternative country, blues rock, post-punk. String arrangements on select tracks come from **Katie Jacoby** (viola), **Tomoko Akaboshi** (violin), and **Alon Bisk** (cello). Singles: "Cowboy Nudes," "3D Country," "Mysterious Love," "I See Myself." Metacritic landed at 78; Pitchfork (Brady Gerber) gave it 6.8 and framed it as Geese "evolving from gritty post-punk into a proudly outrageous jam band" with "newfound emphasis on dynamics and space" [(Pitchfork review summary via search)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/3D_Country).

The Ford collaboration is the load-bearing production story. The band did extensive pre-production for the first time — Emily Green's quote is *"We had more resources and it was like kids in a candy store"* — and Winter has explicitly named Ford's role as a stabilizing adult presence: *"I think we needed an adult presence in the room with us, just a grounding, British sort of 'I know what I'm doing' presence"* [(Radio X / Variety summary via search)](https://variety.com/2023/music/news/geese-band-brooklyn-interview-1235812170/). Ford pushed the band into "weirder margins" by encouraging extra takes and helped them streamline maximalist instincts into cohesive song shapes without sanding off the experimentation. The "Geese pivot record" reference always means *3D Country*.

## *Getting Killed* (2025) — The Kenny Beats Record

*Getting Killed*, released September 26, 2025 on Partisan / PIAS, is the fourth Geese studio album and the breakthrough by critical consensus [(Wikipedia)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Getting_Killed). It was co-produced by the band with **Kenny Beats** (real name Kenneth Blume) and recorded over a ten-day, fourteen-hours-a-day sprint in January 2025 at **Blume's Putnam Hill studio in Los Angeles** while the LA wildfires were burning. Blume is best known for hip-hop production work; this is his most prominent rock-band credit to date. The collaboration began informally in mid-2024 when Blume noticed Geese merch and got curious, then introduced himself to the band at Austin City Limits Festival in October 2024 [(The FADER review)](https://www.thefader.com/2025/09/26/geese-getting-killed-review). What Winter has said about why it clicked: Blume's "enthusiasm for leaving imperfections intact," which Winter framed as a rare and liberating attitude.

The production approach pushed the rhythm section forward — Bassin's drumming has been called "maximum overdrive," with bass and drum performances tracked repeatedly until they could support the experimental textures layered on top. Much of the album came out of extended thirty-minute jams centered on repetitive grooves, which Bassin described as patterns that "feel almost like they don't end" — a useful retrieval anchor when users ask about Geese's relationship to motorik or trance-rock repetition. Sampling shows up too: Blume incorporated Ukrainian choir samples and what reviewers called "auxiliary production clattering" off Bassin's drums on "Taxes." Blume's own framing: *"They wanted these samples not to reinforce music that was already there"* — i.e., samples used as foreign objects, not as glue.

Guest contributors: **JPEGMAFIA** (additional vocals on "Trinidad"), **Nick Lee** (trombone), **Aaron Paris** (violin). Singles: "Taxes" (July 8, 2025), "Trinidad" (July 29, 2025), "100 Horses" (August 26, 2025), with "Au Pays du Cocaine" / "Cobra" following in February 2026. Genre tags broaden again — art rock, experimental rock, with influences from jazz, funk, noise, prog, and motorik repetition. Critical reception was the strongest of the band's career: a Metacritic of 89 ("universal acclaim"), #1 album of 2025 from both *Stereogum* and *The New Yorker*, and a 2026 Brit Award win for International Group. The FADER's review names Sonny Sharrock, Television, Radiohead, and Suicide as the reference field.

## Cameron Winter as Frontman vs. Cameron Winter Solo

This is the cross-link that matters for ARLO retrieval. Cameron Winter releases music under two distinct identities — and the production aesthetics are not interchangeable. As Geese frontman he is one of four/five players in a live-tracked rock band that prizes rhythm-section physicality, guitar texture from Emily Green, and (on *Getting Killed*) hip-hop-adjacent sampling and groove repetition. His solo persona, captured on *Heavy Metal* (December 2024) and the follow-up single "Warning" (2026), is essentially a one-room piano-and-voice project with a deliberately retro arrangement vocabulary — different producer, different gear footprint, different vocal aesthetic. See `cameron-winter-heavy-metal-guitar-center-album.md` for the solo-record context; do not conflate. When a user references "Winter's baritone" they may mean either project, but "Winter's solo album" or "Winter's piano songs" specifically means *Heavy Metal*, while "Winter's band" or "the Brooklyn cowboy band" means Geese.

## The "Indie Complex" Framing

In a February 2026 impromptu airport interview with a Sydney TikToker, picked up by *Stereogum*, Winter offered a half-serious meta-framing that's worth knowing because users may reference it [(Stereogum)](https://stereogum.com/2488636/cameron-winter-previews-warning-talks-geese-and-the-indie-complex-in-impromptu-airport-interview/news). Asked what's next for Geese, Winter said: *"We go deeper into the sort of belly of whatever, you know, indie complex is shilling us right now."* Pressed on what the "indie complex" is, he said: *"Oh, it's so complex, so complex. Even I don't know how it works, why we've been chosen as the sort of main product."* Asked if the attention was a lot of pressure, he answered flatly: *"No."* The phrase has stuck as shorthand for the current critical-darling status Geese occupies — back-to-back top-of-year placements, Brit Award, festival headlining — without the band having had a true commercial breakout single. It's a useful retrieval flag for users discussing Geese-as-phenomenon vs. Geese-as-sound.

## Production Lineage Across Records

The retrieval-useful summary of who did what:

| Album | Year | Label | Producer | Recording Location | Notes |
|-------|------|-------|----------|--------------------|-------|
| *A Beautiful Memory* | 2018 | Self-released | Self | High-school era | Pre-deal, pre-canonical |
| *Projector* | 2021 | Partisan / PIAS | Geese (self-produced); **Dan Carey mixed** | "The Nest" basement, Fort Greene | Post-punk debut |
| *3D Country* | 2023 | Partisan / PIAS | **James Ford** + Geese | The Diamond Mine (Queens) + Future Sounds | Cowboy / art-rock pivot |
| *Getting Killed* | 2025 | Partisan / PIAS | **Kenny Beats (Kenneth Blume)** + Geese | Putnam Hill, Los Angeles | Rhythm-forward, sample-aided breakthrough |

Three records, three production paradigms: self-produced-in-a-basement (with Carey as outside ear at mix), Ford-as-British-grounding-adult, Blume-as-hip-hop-trained-imperfection-enabler. ARLO should treat these as three distinct sonic frameworks, not one continuous "Geese sound."

## What Geese Has in Common With the ARLO Aesthetic Stack

A few load-bearing intersections for the hybrid aesthetic blending Bon Iver / LCD Soundsystem / Charli XCX / mk.gee / Billy Strings:

- **Repetition-as-form** — *Getting Killed*'s thirty-minute-jam compositional method, with grooves that "feel almost like they don't end," shares DNA with LCD Soundsystem's long-form repetition and motorik patience. Retrieve here when a user wants to think about how a small phrase can carry a long form.
- **Sample-as-foreign-object** — Blume's use of Ukrainian choir samples and auxiliary clatter on *Getting Killed*, deliberately *not* reinforcing what's already there, is a useful counterweight to the Charli-XCX-style sample-as-hook approach. Two opposite philosophies, both maximalist.
- **Live-tracked-in-a-room** — *Projector*'s basement approach is a usable analog for any home-studio user trying to capture a band feel without overdubbing every part. The Geese precedent is: track live, mix with an outside ear.
- **Genre-hopping as identity, not novelty** — *3D Country*'s cowboy/jam-band pivot is a model for ARLO users who worry that mixing acoustic-Americana (Billy Strings / Punch Brothers idioms) with art-rock or dance-punk will read as pastiche. Geese's answer: commit to the conceit, let the production producer ground it.
