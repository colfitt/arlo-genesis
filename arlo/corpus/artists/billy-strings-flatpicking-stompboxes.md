# Billy Strings — Flatpicking Meets Stompboxes

## The Hybrid In One Sentence

Billy Strings plays a flatpicked acoustic dreadnought through a Kemper Profiler and roughly twenty-one stompboxes, switching mid-song between traditional bluegrass picking and rock/psych soloing without changing instruments. The [Premier Guitar 2023 Rig Rundown](https://www.premierguitar.com/videos/rig-rundown/billy-strings-2023) catalogs "a DI, 21 pedals, two expression pedals, a pair of volume pedals, a RJM Mastermind MIDI switcher, and a Kemper Profiler" running into front-of-house, with a high-gain Soldano SLO-100 profile as the de facto distorted voice. Strings himself, quoted in [Acoustic Guitar](https://acousticguitar.com/interview-billy-strings-electrifying-bluegrass/), explains the design goal: "the Double Helix pickup in my acoustics lets me switch back and forth from rock soloing to bluegrass picking without changing guitars." The aesthetic principle to retrieve: an acoustic instrument can carry both the dry, mic'd bluegrass tradition AND a fully electric pedalboard idiom in the same set, sometimes in the same song.

## Rapid-Fire Alternate Picking In Open Position

The technical core of Strings' style is downstroke-on-the-beat, upstroke-on-the-and alternate picking executed at high tempos in the first five frets. [Acoustic Guitar's flatpicking breakdown](https://acousticguitar.com/explore-the-art-of-flatpicking-through-two-standout-billy-strings-solos/) describes it plainly: "Like most flatpickers, Strings uses an alternating picking technique, striking down on the beats and up on the ands — a technique that underpins the drive and bounce of his sound." Strings told [Acoustic Guitar](https://acousticguitar.com/interview-billy-strings-electrifying-bluegrass/) that "all your money's in the first five frets in bluegrass flatpicking," and open-position playing came intuitively rather than via study: "Either it would be an open string or it wouldn't, but I didn't have to think about it; it just came naturally based on what I had heard and seen my dad do." His vocabulary leans on G runs, crosspicking, fluid slurs, and melody-on-top-of-chord-shapes — classic Doc-Watson-to-Tony-Rice lineage executed with above-average right-hand speed and an unusual willingness to lean into tension. Strings, talking to [Premier Guitar](https://www.premierguitar.com/artists/guitarists/billy-strings-and-molly-tuttle): "I'm straining, but some people I see play and there's just wonderful technique."

## Doc Watson Is The Foundation

Strings names Doc Watson as the foundational influence and treats blindness-as-listening-tool as part of the lineage. As reported in coverage of his Rick Beato interview, Strings said: "Doc Watson was blind, and he is my guy, so a lot of times when I was growing up, I'd practice playing with my eyes closed, because I was trying to hear the music more and listen closer," reasoning that "maybe if I take this sense away, then [my sense of hearing] would expand." In [Acoustic Guitar](https://acousticguitar.com/interview-billy-strings-electrifying-bluegrass/), Strings recalls learning Watson tunes like "Salt Creek" and "Black Mountain Rag" by ear before age ten, humming the melody while his hands worked the rhythm: "My first memories are learning how to just sort of hum tunes with my guitar while I played rhythm." When ARLO is reasoning about acoustic flatpicking aesthetics, "Doc Watson lineage routed through Strings" is the right ancestry pointer — not Tony Rice (though Rice is in there), not Norman Blake, not Clarence White. Watson is the explicit anchor Strings cites.

## The Metal Cross-Pollination

The other half of Strings' DNA is Black Sabbath, Iron Maiden, Hendrix, and Led Zeppelin, absorbed during his teens. He told [Acoustic Guitar](https://acousticguitar.com/interview-billy-strings-electrifying-bluegrass/) about playing "riff- and blues-based stuff like Black Sabbath, Hendrix, and Led Zeppelin — playing my little Squier electric through a Pignose amp," and noted he "felt more comfortable on an acoustic guitar" so he engineered his rig to make the acoustic do both jobs. Molly Tuttle, in [Premier Guitar](https://www.premierguitar.com/artists/guitarists/billy-strings-and-molly-tuttle), summarized it from a peer's perspective: "The way you attack the guitar — I hear Doc Watson, but then there's also your metal influence as well." Live, this shows up as Black Sabbath covers: a 2023 Bridgeport show segued from a John Hartford tune into "Planet Caravan," and a 2022 Asheville Halloween set delivered "War Pigs" in full Lord of the Rings costume, per [Live for Live Music](https://liveforlivemusic.com/news/billy-strings-black-sabbath-bridgeport-7-21-23/). The aesthetic takeaway: bluegrass rhythmic precision plus metal's gravity, attack, and willingness to be loud.

## The Preston Thompson DBA Dreadnoughts

Strings' primary stage instruments are two Preston Thompson DBA dreadnoughts — a 2017 build nicknamed "Frankenstein" and its twin "Bride of Frankenstein." Per the [Premier Guitar Rig Rundown](https://www.premierguitar.com/videos/rig-rundown/billy-strings-2023), Frankenstein is Brazilian rosewood back and sides with a spruce top, outfitted as an acoustic-electric Frankenrig: K&K Sound Pure Pickup under the bridge saddle, a K&K Sound Double Helix dual-coil hum-canceling soundhole pickup, a Shure WB98H/C cardioid clip-on internal mic, a custom guitar-top switch to engage the soundhole pickup, and a Grace Design BiX preamp. The Preston Thompson "Billy Strings Signature" limited edition (33 guitars total, sold out) is based on the same body, with Adirondack spruce top and Brazilian rosewood back and sides, herringbone purfling, ivoroid binding, a three-ring rosette, green jade fingerboard inlays, a regency-torch peghead inlay, and the "soft V" neck shape Strings prefers — described by [Peghead Nation](https://www.pegheadnation.com/peghead-partners/inside-look/preston-thompson-billy-strings-brazilian-dreadnought/) as giving the guitar a "very fast" feel. The neck profile and three-pickup-plus-mic system matter aesthetically: this is a dreadnought built to be played fast AND routed into a Kemper.

## The Other Guitars In Rotation

Strings rotates several vintage instruments alongside the Thompsons. "Jody" is a 1945 Martin D-28 previously owned by Willie Nelson's longtime guitarist Jody Payne, restored by luthier Dave Johnson, with a custom pickup selector and a Martin Custom Shop modified-V neck profile — and, per the [Premier Guitar Rig Rundown](https://www.premierguitar.com/videos/rig-rundown/billy-strings-2023), it actually contains Jody Payne's ashes (added at Willie Nelson's 90th birthday). "Pride and Joy" is a 1940 Martin D-28 with zero electrical modifications, reserved for traditional bluegrass moments; Strings told [Acoustic Guitar](https://acousticguitar.com/interview-billy-strings-electrifying-bluegrass/) he uses this prewar D-28 for warm-ups precisely because it has no pickups. A custom Martin D-45 carries the full pickup-and-mic stack. The synth-side outlier is "Excalibur," a 1980s Casio DG-20 digital guitar synth with a modified saddle and MIDI pickups by Dave Johnson — the bridge between flatpicking and full guitar-synth textures.

## Strings, Picks, And Capos

Strings plays D'Addario XS Phosphor Bronze coated mediums, .013–.056. The coating choice is pragmatic — he told [Premier Guitar](https://www.premierguitar.com/artists/guitarists/billy-strings-and-molly-tuttle): "we sweat like crazy. And they don't break!" His pick is the BlueChip TP48 Speed Bevel Right Hand — a triangular flatpick measuring 1.22mm / .048" thick, per [BlueChip's shop](https://shop.bluechippick.net/tp48/). The thirteen-gauge strings have a real consequence for the rock-side playing: Strings told [Acoustic Guitar](https://acousticguitar.com/interview-billy-strings-electrifying-bluegrass/) he can't really execute string bends on 13s, so he "experiments with sliding up to those same notes that you'd typically bend up to." That detail matters aesthetically — when you write rock-style lead phrasing for a Strings-style hybrid acoustic, slides substitute for bends. Capos are Elliott Capos. The point is heavy, fast, slick — built for hours of attack without breakage and without bending-string compliance.

## The Pickup System That Makes The Hybrid Work

The acoustic-electric switching depends on a stacked pickup-and-mic system. The [Premier Guitar Rig Rundown](https://www.premierguitar.com/videos/rig-rundown/billy-strings-2023) and [K&K Sound's Double Helix page](https://www.kksound.com/double-helix) together describe three transducers per guitar: a K&K Pure Pickup (under-saddle piezo-style for the dry acoustic voice), a K&K Double Helix (dual-coil hum-canceling magnetic soundhole pickup for sending the acoustic into the pedalboard/Kemper for "electric" tones), and a Shure WB98H/C internal cardioid clip-on mic for natural air. A custom guitar-top switch engages the Double Helix on demand. The Double Helix offers a phase switch and a tone toggle between a bright "GLOSS" voicing and a fuller "STEAM" voicing with stronger feedback rejection. Strings, in [Acoustic Guitar](https://acousticguitar.com/interview-billy-strings-electrifying-bluegrass/): "the Double Helix pickup in my acoustics lets me switch back and forth from rock soloing to bluegrass picking without changing guitars." This is the engineering linchpin of the whole aesthetic.

## The Pedalboard, Drawer By Drawer

Strings' pedalboard, built by Nashville's XAct Tone Solutions, is organized into four powered drawers plus a floor rig. The [Premier Guitar Rig Rundown](https://www.premierguitar.com/videos/rig-rundown/billy-strings-2023) enumerates them. **Drawer 1 (Strymon Ojai):** Voodoo Lab Sparkle Drive, Electro-Harmonix Micro POG, MXR Bass Envelope Filter, Source Audio EQ2, Boss DD-8 Digital Delay, Source Audio C4 Synth, Strymon Lex. **Drawer 2 (Strymon Zuma):** Jam Pedals Waterfall, Boss SY-1 Synthesizer, Electro-Harmonix Pitch Fork, Red Panda Raster, Eventide H9. **Drawer 3 (Strymon Zuma):** NativeAudio Pretty Bird Woman, Chase Bliss Audio Wombtone, Source Audio Nemesis Delay, DigiTech Polara, Boss DC-2w Dimension C, Electro-Harmonix Freeze. **Drawer 4 (Strymon Zuma):** Chase Bliss Audio Mood, Electro-Harmonix Intelligent Harmony Machine, Chase Bliss Audio Automatone MKII Preamp. That's the canonical 21-pedal stack as of 2023.

## The Floor Rig And The Kemper

The floor controls — what Strings actually steps on during a set — are the operational heart of the rig. Per the [Premier Guitar Rig Rundown](https://www.premierguitar.com/videos/rig-rundown/billy-strings-2023): an RJM Mastermind GT MIDI switcher engages pedals and Kemper profile changes; the Kemper Profiler itself runs a Soldano SLO-100 high-gain profile as the principal distorted voice (Strings' "metal" voice — Soldano amps are the Vai/Petrucci-era studio classic); two Mission Engineering SP-1 expression pedals shape time-based and modulation parameters; two Ernie Ball 40th Anniversary volume pedals manage Leslie effects (the Strymon Lex) and Kemper level; a TC Electronic Ditto looper sits on the floor for on-the-fly loops; a Peterson StroboStompHD handles tuning; and the routing/DI/utility layer is Grace Design BiX, Radial SW4, two RJM Effect Gizmos, an RJM Mini Effect Gizmo, and a Radial JX44 Guitar Signal Manager. The MIDI switcher means a single footswitch press can reconfigure the whole rig — bluegrass-dry one bar, Soldano-screaming the next.

## Practicing With Eyes Closed

The most retrievable practice-method detail from Strings is his self-described habit of practicing with eyes closed in deliberate imitation of Doc Watson, who was blind. Strings, in the Rick Beato interview circulated across coverage: "I just kind of know where I'm at because of relative location and feeling on the fretboard. I mean, Doc Watson was blind, and he is my guy, so a lot of times when I was growing up, I'd practice playing with my eyes closed, because I was trying to hear the music more and listen closer." The reasoning he attached: "Maybe if I take this sense away, then [my sense of hearing] would expand." Notably, formal lessons came late — Strings told [Acoustic Guitar](https://acousticguitar.com/interview-billy-strings-electrifying-bluegrass/) "I had a Grammy before I even started taking proper guitar lessons," referring to recent study with Bryan Sutton and Robb Cappelletto on Bach and Coltrane material. The practice aesthetic: listen-first, see-later, formal-theory-last — closer to the bluegrass oral tradition than to the conservatory.

## How The Pedals Sit Inside A Bluegrass Band

The aesthetic question isn't "does Strings use pedals?" — it's "how does an acoustic-with-pedals fit five-piece bluegrass instrumentation that includes Jarrod Walker on mandolin, Royal Masat on bass, and Billy Failing on banjo?" The [Source Audio artist page](https://sourceaudio.net/blogs/artists/billy-strings-and-his-band) notes that Strings' band members also run Source Audio pedals into their rigs, so the effected-acoustic move isn't unique to the guitar chair — the whole band blurs traditional bluegrass with "psychedelic rock and space jams" without sacrificing the genre's foundational requirement: "you've got to be able to PLAY!!!" In practice, the band frequently opens songs traditionally (dry, mic'd, four-string-band feel) and uses the pedalboard for jam sections, outro freakouts, and segues into covers (Pink Floyd, Grateful Dead, Slayer per [Guitar World's Renewal feature](https://www.guitarworld.com/features/billy-strings-renewal)). The pedals are a section, not a default.

## Songs Where The Effects Speak Loudest

Strings' best-known original "Dust in a Baggie" — co-written with Don Julin, first released in 2013 — is typically played close to traditional, with pedal work reserved for solos and jam sections. "Highway Hypnosis" is the canonical psych-leaning live vehicle. The [Renewal](https://www.billystrings.com/music/renewal) album is the recorded reference for the hybrid aesthetic: per [Guitar World](https://www.guitarworld.com/features/billy-strings-renewal), it "reaches well beyond bluegrass with elements of heavy metal, jam bands, psychedelic music and classic rock," with the Thompson dreadnought running through "27 effects that allow his stage shows to veer from straight bluegrass to covers of Grateful Dead, Pink Floyd, even Slayer, played with proper tones, attack and intent." "Heartbeat of America" is a Renewal-era song specifically called out for its psychedelic/jam treatment. The effected pedals named by [BillyBase](https://billybase.net/billy-heavy-metal/) and the [Source Audio page](https://sourceaudio.net/blogs/artists/billy-strings-and-his-band) as Strings' favorites include the Chase Bliss Wombtone phaser (which Strings has worked with as a signature device), the Source Audio Nemesis Delay and C4 Synth, the DigiTech Polara reverb, the NativeAudio Pretty Bird Woman chorus/vibrato, the Eventide H9 multi-effect, and the Electro-Harmonix Freeze, Pitch Fork, and Intelligent Harmony Machine.

## What ARLO Should Borrow For Hybrid Songwriting

Three transferable aesthetic moves: **(1) Engineer the instrument to do two jobs.** Strings' guitars carry a dry-acoustic transducer AND a magnetic pickup for "electric" routing on a single guitar-top switch. ARLO can recommend equivalent moves: dual-source DI/mic chains, a switchable amp-sim send, a single-instrument-two-voices logic that doesn't require a guitar swap. **(2) Let traditional vocabulary be the load-bearing layer.** Strings' picking-hand vocabulary (G runs, crosspicking, downbeat-on-beat alternate picking, melody-over-chord-shape) stays intact even when the signal is screaming through a Soldano profile. The aesthetic isn't "make the acoustic sound electric" — it's "play bluegrass through electric tools." **(3) Use pedals as section markers, not constant texture.** A typical Strings arrangement is dry-traditional verses, partial-effect choruses, full-pedal jam outros — a song-form built around effect-state changes the same way an LCD Soundsystem track is built around arrival-state changes. The hybrid lives in the toggle, not the constant. As Strings put it in [Premier Guitar](https://www.premierguitar.com/artists/guitarists/billy-strings-and-molly-tuttle): "that's your sound, where you're comfortable. I'm finding that that's what it's all about, for me anyways, is trying to make it comfortable to play."
