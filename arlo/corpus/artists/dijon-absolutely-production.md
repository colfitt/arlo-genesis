## Who Dijon Is, Briefly

Dijon Duenas — performing as Dijon — is a Baltimore-born, Los Angeles–based singer and producer whose 2021 debut full-length *Absolutely* sits at the seam between R&B, Americana, and what one might call hi-fi-allergic indie soul. Before going solo around 2017, he co-fronted the duo Abhi//Dijon with Abhirath Raju, releasing the EPs *stay up* and *Montana* — work that critics positioned in a "slick, emotional R&B" lineage with a "sturdy, soulful core" ([The FADER: Guide to Dijon](https://www.thefader.com/2025/08/18/guide-to-dijon-discography-abhi-dijon-collaborations)). His solo run through *Sci Fi 1* (2019) and *How Do You Feel About Getting Married?* (2020) gradually braided folk and Americana into the R&B chassis, setting up *Absolutely* as the consolidation record. *Absolutely* released November 5, 2021 on R&R / Warner, runs 31:32, and was named #3 in *The FADER*'s 50 Best Albums of 2021 ([Wikipedia: *Absolutely*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Absolutely_(Dijon_album))). For ARLO, Dijon is the template for *fidelity-as-aesthetic-choice* in an R&B-leaning songwriting context — a counterweight to glossy contemporary production.

## The Two-Site Recording: LA Home + Upstate New York

Recording took place across two physical sites in 2020, the first pandemic year. Dijon described the geography to *The FADER* as "a lot of the record in my house where I'm at right now and then a big chunk of it in upstate New York" ([The FADER interview podcast](https://www.thefader.com/2021/11/16/dijon-fader-interview-podcast-absolutely)). The LA portion was a domestic, untreated home setup; the upstate sessions were a more communal residency, where Dijon brought in collaborator Jack Karaszewski and others to "try for a few nights to make music around a table." That upstate stretch was when Dijon was, by his own account, "extremely obsessed with The Band" and trying to make "a demented version of one of their songs" — a process that crystallized into "Annie." The split locations are audible: rooms ring differently, and the record is happy to let that show.

## "Songwriting and Recording Happened Simultaneously"

The methodological core of *Absolutely* is the collapse of demo and master. Dijon told *The FADER*: "I don't do demos or anything like that. I just sit there and I just think about music for as long as I possibly can." Internal pre-recording rumination lasted roughly two years; the actual cutting took only "one to two months" once it started ([The FADER](https://www.thefader.com/2021/11/16/dijon-fader-interview-podcast-absolutely)). The pieces were sung into existence on tape rather than re-performed from a prior reference. He has framed himself as "not much of a refiner," favoring first-take captures and the "beautiful imperfections" that come with them ([The FADER](https://www.thefader.com/2021/11/16/dijon-fader-interview-podcast-absolutely)). For ARLO this is a usable axiom: when songwriting and recording are the same act, every iteration becomes part of the artifact, and there is no "clean" version waiting backstage to be re-tracked.

## The AKG C414 in Omni: "That's the Sound of the Record"

The single most-cited piece of gear-press on *Absolutely* is Dijon's vocal-capture chain: an AKG C414 large-diaphragm condenser, set to its omnidirectional polar pattern, running into a Heritage Audio 1073 preamp (a faithful reissue of the Neve 1073 channel). Every vocal on the record except "The Dress" used this chain, and Dijon's stated rule was to "make it as hot as possible without clipping" — pushing preamp gain aggressively to get input level riding the top of the envelope. He summarized the stance plainly: "The omni polar pattern, that's the sound of the record" ([Reverb News: Dijon Talks Absolutely and His Omnidirectional Process](https://reverb.com/news/dijon-interview), as quoted across [Equipboard's gear breakdown](https://equipboard.com/albums/dijon-absolutely)). Most home recordists avoid omni because it picks up everything the cardioid would reject — floor creaks, snare-side rattle, wall slap. Dijon used the pattern *for* that reason: a 360-degree image of the room as the record's default vocal source.

## Taming the Room with a Graphic EQ

Because the omni pattern was capturing a "boxy" untreated home space, the chain compensated downstream rather than upstream — Dijon and engineers used a graphic EQ to "remove most of the mids" and tame the boxiness, which is why much of the record reads "thin" in the lower-mid register on first listen ([Reverb News via Equipboard](https://equipboard.com/albums/dijon-absolutely)). This is a useful inversion for ARLO's suggestion logic: instead of treating the room or repositioning the mic to avoid the problem, *Absolutely* accepts the room's acoustic signature and then carves the mids out post-tracking. The result is a vocal sound that feels close and present without being "warm" in the usual ballad-pop sense — it sits forward, slightly papery, room-tail audible.

## Elektron Octatrack, Eurorack, and Ableton

The non-acoustic side of the record's sound design ran through a small hardware-plus-DAW stack. Dijon worked with various guitars and keys plus an Elektron Octatrack loaded with breakbeats, "a bit of his Eurorack skiff," and everything recorded into Ableton Live ([Reverb News via Equipboard](https://equipboard.com/albums/dijon-absolutely)). Eurorack made limited but identifiable appearances: a Make Noise *Strega* on "Talk Down" and a looped pluck pattern from a Mutable Instruments *Rings* on "Did You See It?" Additional hardware in his orbit included an Elektron Analog Four and an MPC he could use to sample Eurorack patches. The Octatrack/Eurorack/Ableton triangle is worth flagging for ARLO: it is identical in shape to several artists in the aesthetic stack (the LCD Soundsystem hardware-into-DAW model, James Murphy's analog-synth philosophy) but applied to R&B/folk material rather than dance-punk.

## Mike Gordon (mk.gee) as Core Collaborator, and Drum Treatment

Michael Gordon — recording as mk.gee, and the subject of [the sibling artist file](/Users/cfitt/Dev/arlo/corpus/artists/mk-gee-di-direct-philosophy.md) — is the most consequential collaborator on *Absolutely*. Wikipedia credits him on bass, drums, guitar, piano, synthesizer, and vocals across the record; Dijon describes his "grooves" as the underpinning of many songs and frames their relationship as a "competitive edge in the studio" that introduced new vocabulary and pushed him out of an isolationist working pattern ([The FADER](https://www.thefader.com/2021/11/16/dijon-fader-interview-podcast-absolutely)). Gordon's general DI/refusal-of-amp-tone philosophy is covered in the sibling file and is *not* re-litigated here, but its presence on *Absolutely* is the load-bearing reason the record's guitar tones read so unconventional: thin, plucky, processed, and never doing the "guitar hero" role a song like "Many Times" might invite. The drum aesthetic across the record is similarly Gordon-inflected — Olivia Powell notes that both producers "saturate drums heavily, with echoing snares reminiscent of 1980s production and slightly 'flabby' kicks," and quotes Gordon's approach as "just saturation…play light and then push it hard after" ([Olivia Powell: Dijon and Mk.gee's Intuitive Groove](https://oliviapowell.substack.com/p/dijon-and-mkgees-intuitive-groove)). The Octatrack-loaded breakbeats sit under live drum overdubs (Jack Karaszewski is credited with drum programming) to get a "wet" pocket that does not sound like a programmed kit. The partnership continued into mk.gee's 2024 *Two Star & the Dream Police* and 2025's Justin Bieber *SWAG* and Bon Iver *Sable, Fable*.

## The Other Hands: Sarlo, Karaszewski, Le Gros

Beyond Gordon, *Absolutely*'s personnel is a small revolving cast. Andrew Sarlo — known for Big Thief, Nick Hakim, and Bon Iver work — is credited as producer, mixer, and programmer, and Dijon said of him that he "rearranged parts and removed elements," a role Dijon "had never trusted anybody with before" ([The FADER](https://www.thefader.com/2021/11/16/dijon-fader-interview-podcast-absolutely)). Jack Karaszewski handled drum programming, synthesis, production, and mixing presence, and was around for much of the upstate sessions; Dijon credited him with understanding that the project needed "a new fidelity for ourselves," because it was "very strange to record stuff as poorly as we do and then expect to either re-track it at a studio or have somebody who wasn't a part of the record manipulate it" — so they "opted for varying fidelities" ([Reverb News via Equipboard](https://equipboard.com/albums/dijon-absolutely)). Noah Le Gros contributed slide guitar. Henry Kwapis also produced.

## Mixing Philosophy: "Increase What's Already There"

The mix stance is consistent with the recording stance: do not pretend the source is something it isn't. Dijon described the mix process as "throwing in a lot of things that already existed within the sessions and increasing them or riding the volumes," with the explicit philosophy of "not trying to enhance what wasn't really enhanced to begin with" ([Reverb News via Equipboard](https://equipboard.com/albums/dijon-absolutely)). In practice this means automation-heavy mixing — volume rides, fader moves, sub-mutes, level pushes — rather than spectral surgery or polish-pass plugin chains. The "varying fidelities" idea is the corollary: different songs and different moments on the record sit at different fidelity levels on purpose, and the mix preserves the gap rather than averaging it out.

## R&B / Americana / Soul Fusion, "Intentional Lo-Fi," and Silence

The aesthetic positioning of *Absolutely* is genre-promiscuous in a deliberate way. Pitchfork's Stephen Kearse described Dijon's voice moving "from coos to wails to murmurs" across folk and soul arrangements; Dijon himself called the record "an abstracted interpretation of what making music feels like to me," blending "rapturous Americana and delicate soul" ([The FADER](https://www.thefader.com/2021/11/16/dijon-fader-interview-podcast-absolutely)). Olivia Powell quotes Dijon naming a shared frustration with Gordon over "the lack of anger that people had at the state of young music," and questioning "why nobody else was as feverishly...reaching" for new ideas ([Olivia Powell](https://oliviapowell.substack.com/p/dijon-and-mkgees-intuitive-groove)). The R&B-Americana-soul fusion is the surface expression of an anti-polish stance — pulling on traditions that *don't* default to glossy modern production as a way to escape glossy modern production. Powell's piece is also the clearest articulation of the record's compositional posture: both Dijon and Gordon embrace "intentional lo-fi" backed by years of experimentation, "messing things up" purposefully — overdriving guitars and layering vocals unevenly because of how it feels, not despite it. The "wetness" (saturation, reverb, room) creates a "slightly anxiety-inducing" quality that amplifies emotional impact. Equally important is what Powell calls "reverence for silence": both artists "treat pauses as compositional elements, using hand movements and vocalizations to punctuate silence." On *Absolutely* this is audible across the record's many mid-song gear-shifts — sudden drops, abrupt re-entries, vocal moments that hang on their own without instrumental support.

## "Absolutely" the Film, and the Live-in-the-Room Frame

Director Jack Karaszewski's short film *Absolutely* — released December 2021, free on YouTube through end-of-year ([The FADER: Dijon shares short film *Absolutely*](https://www.thefader.com/2021/12/23/dijon-shares-short-film-absolutely)) — frames the record as a live-band, around-the-table experience. It compiles previously released performances of "Big Mike's" and "Many Times" with new versions of "Scratching," "Talk Down," "Annie," and "Rodeo Clown." A moment during "Annie" — Dijon and Gordon trading a three-word phrase back and forth — is the clearest on-camera glimpse of the duo's working chemistry ([The FADER](https://www.thefader.com/2021/12/23/dijon-shares-short-film-absolutely)). For ARLO the film matters because it confirms the record's premise visually: this is music that wants to be heard as a room of people, not a stack of overdubs, even when it is in fact a stack of overdubs.

## Reception and Aesthetic Lineage

Critical reception was strong without being unanimously hot. Pitchfork awarded a 7.2, with Kearse highlighting the "pliant, raspy" voice and the unpredictable arrangements; *The FADER* ranked it #3 of 2021; Clash put it at #44; The Line of Best Fit #41 ([Wikipedia: *Absolutely*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Absolutely_(Dijon_album))). The record's downstream influence has been larger than its initial reception — by 2025, both Dijon and Gordon were core contributors to Justin Bieber's *SWAG* and Bon Iver's *Sable, Fable*, with the toni truale essay arguing that pop itself became the "third party" their aesthetic was now visibly shaping ([toni truale: Dijon, Mk.gee, and the Third Party Called Pop](https://www.tonitruale.com/post/dijon-mk-gee-and-the-third-party-called-pop)). The pop-architecture exposure means *Absolutely*'s production grammar — omni-vocal capture, hot input, in-room imperfection, breakbeat-under-live drums, mk.gee guitar — is now a recognized contemporary register, not a regional curiosity.

## ARLO Translation Notes

If a user is gesturing at "Dijon's vocal sound" on a write, the priors worth surfacing are: large-diaphragm condenser in omni rather than cardioid, preamp gain pushed close to clipping, accept the room's reflections as part of the take, then EQ mids down rather than re-tracking. If the gesture is "Dijon's drum feel," the priors are: breakbeat sample loaded into a hardware sampler (Octatrack-style) sitting underneath a live drum overdub, both heavily saturated, snare bright and 1980s-leaning, kick slightly loose. If the gesture is "Dijon's songwriting flow," the prior is: do not demo separately — start the recording with the song still incomplete, let arrangement decisions happen at capture time, and accept first takes. For the broader aesthetic stack, *Absolutely* is the bridge between the bedroom-pop bedroom (mk.gee) and the dance-punk hardware rig (LCD Soundsystem) — same hardware-into-DAW topology, different genre destination.
