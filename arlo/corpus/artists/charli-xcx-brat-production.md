# Charli XCX *Brat* Production Approach

**Scope:** artist-anchored — Charli XCX *Brat* production philosophy and the AG Cook / Finn Keane / Cirkut collaboration
**Source:** Deep-research synthesis (2026-05) — verify before treating as authoritative
**Aesthetic-stack relevance:** Charli XCX, hyperpop, rave
**Tags:** `charli-xcx`, `ag-cook`, `brat`, `hyperpop`, `rave`, `pop-production`

## The Central Claim: "Tight Collection of Sounds"

The defining production-philosophy statement for *Brat* is Charli's own: she described the record to *Billboard* as having "this unique minimalism that is very loud and bold," built from "a tight collection of sounds" ([Brat (album) — Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brat_(album))). This is the inversion of conventional maximalism. Rather than piling sounds on top of each other, *Brat* picks a small, deliberately limited palette of timbres and detonates them — pushing each element to the point where one synth, one vocal, one drum hit fills the room. A.G. Cook described his executive-producer role in the same language: "how maximalist and minimalist can I get everyone to work? So that was sort of my role, to make sure every track was intentional and all the production was as bratty and confident as it should be" ([Variety](https://variety.com/2024/music/global/ag-cook-britpop-charli-xcx-brat-1235994011/)). For ARLO's purposes, this is the aesthetic anchor: *Brat*-style production is not "more sounds." It is "fewer sounds, louder, more committed."

## The "Sonic Rule Book" — Constraint as Creative Strategy

Co-producer Jon Shave told *M Magazine* that A.G. Cook established a "sonic rule book" for the record — most strikingly, **drum samples were banned**, and the **Roland TR-909** served as the primary percussion source ([M Magazine](https://www.prsformusic.com/m-magazine/features/charli-xcx-brat-making-of-finn-keane-jon-shave)). The 909 is the classic late-80s / 90s dance machine — house, techno, hardcore — and choosing it as the only-drum-machine is itself a thesis statement: this record is going to sound like rave, not like contemporary pop drum design. Shave articulated why the constraint mattered: "We're in a world now where, as a producer, you can access any sound of any type and any variety at any time. It's amazing when someone gives you limitations, because it suddenly unlocks another layer of creativity" ([M Magazine](https://www.prsformusic.com/m-magazine/features/charli-xcx-brat-making-of-finn-keane-jon-shave)). For producers used to having every sample library at their fingertips, the *Brat* discipline is the discipline of saying no.

## A.G. Cook as Executive Producer

A.G. Cook — founder of PC Music, long-term Charli collaborator since *Vroom Vroom* (2016) and *Pop 2* (2017) — was executive producer across *Brat*'s 15 tracks and is co-credited on the majority of them ([Brat (album) — Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brat_(album))). His role was less "make every sound" and more "keep every track aligned with the thesis." Cook told *MusicRadar*: "We knew it was going to be called 'Brat' for the entire two years of making it, and we cut anything that wasn't 'Brat.' If it didn't feel 'Brat', it was gone! We'd barely even talk about it again out of embarrassment of it not being 'Brat' enough" ([MusicRadar](https://www.musicradar.com/artists/she-was-the-one-to-say-if-it-was-brat-or-not-she-works-really-really-fast-and-if-were-bored-by-something-well-just-move-on-charli-xcx-producers-finn-keane-and-a-g-cook-on-the-pop-stars-relentless-creative-process)). Cook also described his preferred working mode as preserving impulsiveness: "My favorite thing to do with Charli is to almost remind her of the immediacy of the early stages" ([Grammy.com roundtable](https://www.grammy.com/news/charli-xcx-brat-explainer-ag-cook-finn-keane-george-daniel-roundtable)). The executive-producer job, in *Brat*'s case, was vibe-policing: protecting the record's identity against drift.

## Finn Keane (formerly Easyfun)

Finn Keane — who released earlier PC Music work under the name Easyfun, before a 2024 legal dispute with the EasyGroup corporate family forced him to revert to his birth name — is credited on six *Brat* tracks: "360," "Sympathy Is a Knife," "Von Dutch," "So I," "I Think About It All the Time," and "365" ([Variety](https://variety.com/2024/music/global/finn-keane-easyfun-name-change-legal-battle-charli-xcx-brat-1236196061/)). He has worked with Charli since 2017, putting him in roughly the same long-arc collaborator tier as Cook. Keane has been the most articulate of the *Brat* team about the record's sonic duality: "Something both feeling very slick and shiny, but also completely messy and chaotic. By putting such an extreme spotlight on one sound or Charli's vocal, it then fills up the space" ([Grammy.com roundtable](https://www.grammy.com/news/charli-xcx-brat-explainer-ag-cook-finn-keane-george-daniel-roundtable)). That "slick AND messy" framing is the cleanest summary of how the record sits.

## Cirkut — The Mainstream Pop Bridge

Henry Walter (Cirkut) is the *Brat* team member with the deepest mainstream-pop CV (Katy Perry, The Weeknd, Maroon 5). His credits on *Brat* are "360," "Rewind," and "365" — i.e., the record's three most overtly rave-leaning bangers, including its opening and closing statements ([Brat (album) — Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brat_(album))). The choice to put a top-40 pop producer on the rave-iest tracks is part of why *Brat* crosses over: Cirkut brings hit-radio low-end and arrangement instincts to material that, in PC Music's hands alone, might have stayed scene-coded. Notably, Cook later revealed that "365" began life as "a DJ edit from a small club night, speeding it up by one semitone" ([Wikipedia citation of M Magazine](https://www.prsformusic.com/m-magazine/features/charli-xcx-brat-making-of-finn-keane-jon-shave)) — meaning even the most "produced" closing track is anchored to a real club mix, not a studio fantasy.

## The Illegal London Rave Lineage

Charli has been explicit, since well before *Brat*, that the early-2000s London illegal-rave scene is her formative musical home. At 14 — around 2007 — she performed at Club Cool warehouse parties in Shoreditch, accompanied by her parents from the Hertfordshire suburbs ([Charli XCX — Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Charli_XCX); [The FADER](https://www.thefader.com/2024/08/29/charli-xcx-brat-new-rave)). In a 2012 *Rolling Stone* interview (predating *Brat* by 12 years), she said: "Everyone at those shows was out of their minds on ketamine and tossing glitter around. That whole scene really inspires me. The fashion, the music, etc." — and named her original ambition as making "music that the rave kids could dance to" ([The FADER](https://www.thefader.com/2024/08/29/charli-xcx-brat-new-rave)). *Brat* is, in part, the record she has been claiming she wanted to make since she was a teenager. The FADER positioned the album as drawing on the same DNA as Klaxons, Test Icicles, Late of the Pier, CSS, and Crystal Castles — the mid-2000s UK "new rave" wave — and pointed to "Von Dutch" and "365" as the most direct lineage.

## SOPHIE as the North Star

George Daniel (of The 1975, who co-produced "Club Classics" and "Apple") told *GRAMMY.com* that *Brat* "almost feels like a love letter to [SOPHIE]," describing SOPHIE's aesthetic as "a North Star" for the project ([Grammy.com roundtable](https://www.grammy.com/news/charli-xcx-brat-explainer-ag-cook-finn-keane-george-daniel-roundtable)). SOPHIE — the Scottish hyperpop producer who collaborated heavily with Charli on *Vroom Vroom* and *Pop 2* before her death in 2021 — pioneered the rubbery, hyper-treble, latex-bright sound-design vocabulary that Cook, Keane, and the broader PC Music sphere all extended. The throughline from SOPHIE to *Brat* is the conviction that synthetic sounds can be physical — that a synth hit can be *gripped* the way an analog instrument is gripped. *Brat*'s drier, more 909-anchored palette is sonically less SOPHIE-like than *Pop 2* was, but the underlying belief in production-as-sculpture is inherited.

## Charli's Working Method — Speed and Instinct

Multiple collaborators describe a writing process that is fast, instinct-driven, and intolerant of laboring over anything that isn't working. Finn Keane: "Charli likes to work very, very quickly. That's something that actually me, Alex and Charli all have in common. We can make a track in 10, 15 minutes... have something that resembles a song in 30 minutes" ([Grammy.com roundtable](https://www.grammy.com/news/charli-xcx-brat-explainer-ag-cook-finn-keane-george-daniel-roundtable)). "We did Speed Drive and Von Dutch in one day. It was quite a chill session too, probably about four hours" ([MusicRadar](https://www.musicradar.com/artists/she-was-the-one-to-say-if-it-was-brat-or-not-she-works-really-really-fast-and-if-were-bored-by-something-well-just-move-on-charli-xcx-producers-finn-keane-and-a-g-cook-on-the-pop-stars-relentless-creative-process)). Jon Shave added that Charli arrived at sessions with the lyrical concept already locked: "Charli had thought about every single song and what she wanted to say on *Brat*. By the time she got on the mic she was searching for moods to match her ideas, rather than trying to work out what she wanted to say to match a musical mood" ([M Magazine](https://www.prsformusic.com/m-magazine/features/charli-xcx-brat-making-of-finn-keane-jon-shave)). This inverts the usual pop-session order — lyrics chase production — and it is one of the most actionable lessons in the *Brat* method: know what the song is *about* before you turn on the synth.

## Mission-Statement Document — Pre-Production Discipline

A detail that is reported but rarely highlighted: collaborators were briefed on *Brat* via a written mission-statement document from Charli that outlined the album's vision, described by participants as "laser-focused and crystal clear" ([M Magazine](https://www.prsformusic.com/m-magazine/features/charli-xcx-brat-making-of-finn-keane-jon-shave)). The combination of (a) a pre-written vision document, (b) the fixed album title decided at the start, (c) the sonic rule book banning drum samples, and (d) the merciless "is it Brat or not" gate — together constitute a pre-production framework most pop records simply do not have. *Brat*'s identity is not an emergent property of sessions; it was a constraint imposed *on* sessions.

## *Brat* vs. *Pop 2* vs. *how i'm feeling now*

Charli herself said *Brat* "is probably the closest album to [her 2017 mixtape] *Pop 2* in its ethos" ([Brat (album) — Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brat_(album))). *Pop 2* — also executive-produced by Cook, also Asylum/PC-Music-aligned — was the moment Charli fully released her grip on mainstream-pop expectations and went all-in on hyperpop's avant-garde edge, featuring a who's-who of guests (Carly Rae Jepsen, Tove Lo, Caroline Polachek, Dorian Electra, MØ, Kim Petras) ([Pop 2 — Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pop_2_(mixtape))). *Brat* shares *Pop 2*'s no-compromise ethos but trades its glitter-and-glitch sound design for a drier, 909-driven rave palette. *how i'm feeling now* (2020) was a different beast: written and recorded in five weeks during COVID lockdown, "very online" in aesthetic, made over Zoom with fan input. *Brat*, by contrast, was two years in the making with a fixed crew. Where *how i'm feeling now* sounds urgent and unfinished by design, *Brat* sounds urgent and finished — the difference between a sketch made fast and a thesis made fast.

## From Hyperpop to "Mainstream Rave"

The MIDiA Research case study frames *Brat*'s commercial breakthrough as a deliberate strategic move: Charli "traded the depth of hyperpop for the breadth of *Brat* summer" ([MIDiA Research](https://www.midiaresearch.com/case-studies/how-charli-xcx-leveraged-the-hyperpop-scene-into-a-global-movement)). This is interpretation, not artist statement, but it tracks with the sonic evidence — *Brat* is less aggressively futuristic than *Pop 2* and far less "broken" than the SOPHIE/PC Music canon. Anthony Fantano's *Brat* review, framed as critical interpretation, makes a similar point: the album "doesn't come across quite as futuristic as some of those hyperpop classics in recent years," with Fantano describing the production as "vibrant... colorful... very visceral, at times, aggressive" and serving the songs more than the sound design ([The Needle Drop](https://theneedledrop.com/album-reviews/charli-xcx-brat-album-review/)). The architecture is rave-club rather than hyperpop-club. The functional reference point Charli and Cook have invoked is the early-2000s London illegal-rave era, not the late-2010s PC Music scene — even though the personnel overlap heavily.

## "The Sound of Something Fighting Itself"

The *GRAMMY.com* roundtable's framing of the record — "*Brat* is the sound of something fighting itself" ([Grammy.com roundtable](https://www.grammy.com/news/charli-xcx-brat-explainer-ag-cook-finn-keane-george-daniel-roundtable)) — captures a quality the team kept returning to: that the record is simultaneously confident and insecure, slick and messy, club-built and diaristic. Finn Keane: "That tension of having both those things at once really summed up the spirit of the album." Charli herself called it her "most aggressive and confrontational record," yet "her most vulnerable" ([The Face, via Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brat_(album))). For producers working in a hybrid aesthetic, this is the takeaway: *Brat*'s emotional power does not come from picking a lane (party vs. confessional) but from forcing both into the same constrained sonic frame. A 909 four-on-the-floor under a lyric about whether your friend secretly hates you is the *Brat* move.

## What ARLO Can Steal — Methodological Lessons

Concrete takeaways, drawn only from sourced material:

1. **Decide the title and the rule book first.** Cook: "We knew it was going to be called 'Brat' for the entire two years."
2. **Constrain the palette.** No drum samples; one drum machine (the 909). One synth choice deployed maximally beats five synths deployed politely.
3. **Write the lyric concept *before* the session.** Charli arrived with the song's meaning already locked; production chased emotion, not the reverse.
4. **Work fast and discard fast.** 30 minutes to a working song; if it's not the album's identity, cut it without sentimentality.
5. **Find a cultural anchor outside contemporary pop.** For *Brat*, that's the illegal London rave scene of the 2000s. The anchor doesn't have to be obscure — it has to be *specific*, and the producers have to be able to point at it.
6. **Maximalism through minimalism.** Pushing one element to the front of a sparse mix reads louder than layering five elements into a dense one.

These are not plugin choices. They are workflow choices — and they are the part of *Brat*'s production that can be verified from primary sources and ported into another studio.
