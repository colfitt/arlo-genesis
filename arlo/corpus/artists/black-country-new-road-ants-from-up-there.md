## The Framing: A Live Sound Engineer in the Producer's Chair

The most consequential production decision on *Ants From Up There* is the one that happened before any microphone was placed. After working with Andy Savours on their debut *For the first time* (2021), Black Country, New Road chose to hand the second record to Sergio Maschetzko — their front-of-house live engineer, who had never produced a studio album ([Ants from Up There — Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ants_from_Up_There)). Bassist Tyler Hyde has stated the logic plainly: "Maschetzko had not recorded an album before, but they picked him because the band wanted to capture their material in a live setting" ([Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ants_from_Up_There)). Drummer Charlie Wayne has framed it as a deliberate reversal of the first-album model: "we just needed someone who could make us sound great as we did live and he was already our sound engineer" ([Northern Transmissions — BCNR on *Forever Howlong*](https://northerntransmissions.com/black-country-new-road-are-having-fun-with-new-sounds-on-forever-howlong/)). The framing is important for ARLO: this is a record where the production thesis is not "find a sound" but "do not lose the sound that already exists in the room."

## Three Weeks at Chale Abbey, Isle of Wight

The sessions took place over three weeks in July 2021 at Chale Abbey Studios, a residential facility at the southern tip of the Isle of Wight ([Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ants_from_Up_There)). The studio's defining feature is its live room: a converted stone barn dating to the 1500s, with original oak trusses and stone walls preserved as natural diffusion, plus a solid oak floor added for acoustic response ([Studio Profile: Chale Abbey — Audio Media International](https://audiomediainternational.com/studio-profile-chale-abbey/)). The control room runs an SSL 4000G+ console with ATC SCM150 ASL mains and Adam Audio S3-A nearfields; the mic cabinet includes Coles 4038s, Neumann U87s, Neumann KM 184s, a Soundelux E251, and vintage AKG D25s, with reverb options spanning an EMT 240 gold-foil plate, a Bricasti M7, and a Space Echo RE-201 ([Audio Media International](https://audiomediainternational.com/studio-profile-chale-abbey/)). The room is residential — the band lived and ate in the same building they tracked in. For a seven-piece ensemble committing to live performance, a 500-year-old stone barn with a natural ambience and a single large tracking room is close to ideal: the geometry forces the players to sit in earshot of one another, and the room's diffusion handles the bleed that aggressive close-miking would otherwise have to fight.

## The Seven-Piece, By Instrument

The lineup on the record is the full classical-rock chamber configuration: Isaac Wood (vocals, guitar), Luke Mark (guitar, backing vocals), Tyler Hyde (bass, backing vocals), Charlie Wayne (drums, backing vocals), May Kershaw (keyboards, marimba, glockenspiel, backing vocals), Georgia Ellery (violin, mandolin, cello, backing vocals), and Lewis Evans (saxophone, flute, backing vocals) ([Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ants_from_Up_There)). That is two guitars, bass, drums, keys/mallets, strings, and reed/woodwinds — a chamber-folk instrumentation in the same family as Punch Brothers' five-piece quintet logic, but expanded with a rock rhythm section and a wind voice. Maschetzko's own framing of the ensemble is consistent with this view: every instrument has "a part in the composition," and his mixing philosophy is to never bury any voice but to ensure all elements "interact between the instruments as a unit" ([Sergio Maschetzko interview — Mixing A Band](https://mixingaband.com/sergio-maschetzko-foh-black-country-new-road-band/)). That is a chamber-music description, not a rock-band description.

## "Capture Our Material in a Live Setting" as Methodology

The operative phrase from the band — repeatedly attributed to the group's collective intent — is that they wanted Maschetzko because they wanted to "capture their material in a live setting" ([Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ants_from_Up_There)). The actual tracking workflow followed the phrase literally: the album was recorded with each member in the same room as the others, with overdubs reserved for select instruments and vocal processing rather than for replacing the live performance ([Album of the Year — *Ants From Up There* review](https://www.albumoftheyear.org/user/pipepanic/album/424961-ants-from-up-there/)). For a record that runs through multi-section pieces in the eight-to-twelve-minute range — "Bread Song," "Concorde," "Haldern," "Snow Globes," and the closer — live tracking is not a neutral choice; it is the only way to preserve the rubato and dynamic contour that the ensemble negotiates by feel. Wayne has described the rehearsal-to-tracking transition as a re-education: "Fundamentally, we relearned an entirely new style of playing with each other. We learned a lot about how to express ourselves just for each other rather than for anything else going on externally" ([Recounting: *Ants From Up There* — Rough Trade](https://blog.roughtrade.com/gb/recounting-ants-from-up-there-by-black-country-new-road/)). The phrase "just for each other" is the chamber-music ethic.

## Maschetzko's Microphone Approach: DPA Density Over Multi-Mic Layering

The single most concrete technical detail from Maschetzko's own published interview concerns his mic approach. He has talked about settling on DPA 2011 small-diaphragm condensers for drums and other sources after experimenting with placements; he runs approximately twenty condenser microphones simultaneously with "no issue, zero," and consistently chooses fewer-but-better mics over dense multi-miking — recording the snare drum with a single microphone rather than the typical top/bottom/side array, and A/B-ing the violinist's internal pickup against external DPA clip mics, finding the external mics produced superior results ([Mixing A Band](https://mixingaband.com/sergio-maschetzko-foh-black-country-new-road-band/)). The pattern is consistent: prioritize source quality and natural capture over signal-chain transformation. That is a live-engineer's instinct — the live engineer cannot fix things in the mix, so the live engineer commits to the source — and it explains why the *Ants From Up There* productions sound un-processed in a way that is not "lo-fi" but "presented." Strings sound like strings in a room; the saxophone has air around it; the drums sit with their natural attack envelope intact.

## Chamber-Folk Arrangement: The Violin-Sax-Piano Triangle

The structural backbone of the record's arrangement language is the trio of violin (Ellery), saxophone (Evans), and keys (Kershaw), whose intertwining melodies and counterpoint carry many of the record's most memorable instrumental lines ([Spectrum Culture review](https://spectrumculture.com/2022/02/08/black-country-new-road-ants-from-up-there-review/)). "Haldern" builds from staccato strings and saxophone into a denser texture; "Mark's Theme" is a roughly two-minute solo saxophone piece written by Evans in tribute to his uncle Mark, who died of COVID-19 — it sits in the tracklist with structure isolated from any other instrument and is the album's clearest single-voice chamber statement ([Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ants_from_Up_There)). Across the record, critics have noted the dominance of "carefully constructed" arrangements organized around strings and woodwinds rather than guitar-band textures — orchestrated indie rock with post-rock structural logic and chamber-pop voicings. This is the closest the record sits to Punch Brothers: a fixed acoustic-instrument set treated as a compositional unit, with melodic responsibility distributed across the voices rather than monopolized by a single lead.

## The Isaac Wood Vocal Era

The vocal performance on *Ants From Up There* is Isaac Wood's last with the band, and stylistically it is also his most extended. The record marks Wood's first use of double-tracked vocals — Wikipedia notes the band "experimented extensively with vocal overdubs, particularly on 'Concorde,' marking Wood's first use of double-tracked vocals" ([Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ants_from_Up_There)). The shift away from the more declamatory, sprechgesang-adjacent delivery of *For the first time* toward sung melodic phrasing is one of the record's defining production moves. Backing vocals from Hyde, Kershaw, Ellery, Mark, Wayne, and Evans appear across the album in choral stacks — Hyde has described the overall posture as one of being "willing to expose ourselves more" through backing vocals and overall vulnerability compared to the debut ([Rough Trade](https://blog.roughtrade.com/gb/recounting-ants-from-up-there-by-black-country-new-road/)). The content-filter framing applies here: Wood's lyrics on this record are dense, personal, and emotionally direct, and ARLO should describe them analytically rather than quote them. The relevant production fact for ARLO is that the band built around Wood's voice as a melodic instrument, not as a recitation.

## The Departure: Four Days Before Release

Wood announced his departure from the band on January 31, 2022, citing mental health reasons, four days before the album's release on February 4, 2022 on Ninja Tune ([Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ants_from_Up_There)). The band has consistently characterized the parting as on good terms; the post-Wood configuration has not performed any of the *Ants From Up There* material live, and the band's subsequent records *Live at Bush Hall* (2023) and *Forever Howlong* (2025) feature entirely new material with vocal and songwriting duties redistributed among Hyde, Kershaw, and Ellery ([Northern Transmissions](https://northerntransmissions.com/black-country-new-road-are-having-fun-with-new-sounds-on-forever-howlong/)). For ARLO this is a useful boundary: *Ants From Up There* is a self-contained artifact of a band configuration that no longer exists, and the production decisions on it should be analyzed as a closed system rather than projected forward onto the post-Wood records.

## "Its Own Sound World" — The Album as Cohesive Object

The band's own framing of the production is that the record was conceived as a single sonic environment rather than a song collection. Hyde and others have described it as "the first album we have done. A grown-up album," with the music having "its own sound world and production-wise, has its own sound world too" ([Rough Trade](https://blog.roughtrade.com/gb/recounting-ants-from-up-there-by-black-country-new-road/)). Songs were "worked on... to fit with the other songs" rather than tracked as discrete units. The album title itself, per Lewis Evans, was decided on the final day of mixing — the record was conceived sonically before it was named. The pop framing the band uses is structural rather than sonic: "more conceptually pop than sounding like pop." That is a useful distinction for ARLO when reasoning about hybrid aesthetic: pop sensibility as restraint, restraint as composition — leaving space, not overdoing things — rather than pop as a surface texture.

## "Concorde" as the Production Thesis Track

Wayne has singled out "Concorde" as a track that captures the band's working method on the record: "I love how it follows the same chord progression the whole way through, and it's driven but very soft. It's got real moments of delicacy, and it's a song that we all thought quite a lot about when we were getting it together" ([Rough Trade](https://blog.roughtrade.com/gb/recounting-ants-from-up-there-by-black-country-new-road/)). For ARLO, "Concorde" is the clearest single artifact of the *Ants From Up There* production thesis: a static harmonic field that the band animates through dynamic and timbral variation, with Wood's double-tracked vocal as the new element on top. Live tracking with a chamber instrumentation, recorded by a live engineer who knows the band's dynamic range from years of mixing them in venues, is the only production stance that lets a piece like this work — a studio-construction approach would have to render the dynamic envelope after the fact, and the breath of the performance would be lost.

## Cross-Reference: Chamber-Folk Parallels with Punch Brothers

The hybrid-aesthetic relevance of this record sits on the chamber-folk axis. The structural parallel to Punch Brothers is direct: a fixed, mostly-acoustic instrumentation treated as a compositional unit, with melodic responsibility rotated across voices, long multi-section forms, and a recorded ethos that privileges live ensemble interaction over overdub construction. Punch Brothers' debut *Punch* was tracked live with no overdubs at Legacy Studios in 2007 ([Punch Brothers — Progressive Bluegrass Composition](punch-brothers-progressive-composition.md)); *Ants From Up There* used overdubs more freely but committed to the same first principle — capture the ensemble in a room, in real time, and let the instruments interact. The differences are also instructive: BCNR's seven-piece adds a rock rhythm section and a wind voice to the Punch Brothers chamber template, and the band routes the result through a post-rock structural sensibility (long crescendos, repeated harmonic cells, dynamic terracing) rather than through bluegrass-derived form. For ARLO's purposes, when a session under discussion combines acoustic chamber instrumentation with rock rhythm and long-form arrangement logic, both anchors apply.

## Production Takeaways for ARLO

If a session is reaching for the *Ants From Up There* texture, the operative production choices are: (1) commit to live tracking with the full ensemble in one room, with overdubs reserved for vocals and select textural additions rather than as the primary construction method; (2) prioritize source quality and natural capture — fewer microphones, better microphones, room ambience accepted as part of the sound; (3) trust a live engineer's ear over a studio producer's instinct when the material depends on dynamic interaction between players; (4) treat the chamber-instrument voices (strings, winds, keys) as compositional equals to the rhythm section, with melodic responsibility distributed across the ensemble; (5) build pieces as static harmonic fields animated by dynamic and timbral variation rather than as verse-chorus structures; (6) reserve vocal layering (double-tracking, harmony stacks) as a structural rather than ornamental device. The deeper lesson is the inversion that defines the record: the studio production was good because the live performance was, and the band's choice was to find a producer who would not stand between the ensemble and the tape.
