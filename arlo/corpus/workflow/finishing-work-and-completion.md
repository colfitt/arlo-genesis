## The finishing problem, framed

The problem is not that producers can't write — it is that they can't stop. Dennis DeSantis built *Making Music* around three categories of obstacle, and the third — Problems of Finishing — gets its own section because "almost (but not quite) everything is in place" is the most common stuck-state and the one that doesn't resolve itself with more inspiration [(Cycling '74 review)](https://cycling74.com/articles/a-review-of-making-music-74-strategies-for-electronic-music-producers). The fix is not motivation; it is process. This document collects the concrete, repeatable tactics that working producers use to close tracks: time budgets, decision criteria, shipping rituals, and the disciplines that turn 90% songs into 100% songs.

## The 80/20 calibration

The Pareto pattern reliably applies to mixing and arrangement: roughly 80% of the perceptible result comes from the first 20% of decisions — primarily balance, EQ, and arrangement — and the remaining 80% of effort produces 20% of the perceptible improvement [(Production Expert on the 80/20 rule in production)](https://www.production-expert.com/production-expert-1/2019/12/10/how-to-use-the-8020-rule-in-music-production-to-jumpstart-your-productivity). The practical calibration: if a session has crossed the 80% mark, the producer should know it, and the work that follows should be deliberate polish on a small list of named issues, not exploratory tweaking. The trap is the polish phase that expands without bound — each additional hour produces less perceptible change, but the producer hears change anyway because the brain accommodates the small comparisons. The countermeasure is to write down the actual remaining issues before each session and refuse to touch anything outside that list.

## The first-session-is-longest rule

EDMProd's finishing-system writeup states a concrete time budget: the first session on a new track should be a minimum of 90 minutes, because it is where the most progress is made and the most ideas are committed [(EDMProd, finishing music)](https://www.edmprod.com/finishing-music/). The cognitive reason: starting cold burns warm-up time, so distributing track work across many short sessions multiplies that warm-up tax. The corollary rule: subsequent sessions can be shorter (30–60 minutes), but they should pick up a specific deliverable — finish the second verse, commit the drum bus, lock the arrangement — rather than re-open the entire track for inspection.

## Deadline compression as Parkinson's-law lever

Set deadlines shorter than the producer's intuitive estimate. EDMProd's specific formula: if a track feels like a one-week job, set a four-day deadline [(EDMProd, finishing music)](https://www.edmprod.com/finishing-music/). Parkinson's Law — work expands to fill available time — applies in the DAW as cleanly as anywhere else: a track with two weeks to live tends to acquire two weeks of revisions, most of them neutral or negative. The deadline doesn't need external enforcement to work; the producer simply has to refuse to slip it. The accountability scale that EDMProd recommends runs from P2P (a friend checks weekly), to public (a shared spreadsheet or group channel), to reliant (a collaborator depends on the deliverable) — escalate only if self-enforcement fails [(EDMProd)](https://www.edmprod.com/finishing-music/).

## The "is this good enough" test

DeSantis's pragmatic substitution — replace "how do I make this the best?" with "is this acceptable?" — is the single most consequential tactic for finishing [(EDMProd summary of DeSantis-aligned strategies)](https://www.edmprod.com/finishing-music/). "Acceptable" is a yes/no decision the producer can actually make; "best" is an asymptote. Apply it per element: is this snare acceptable? Yes — move on. Is this transition acceptable? No — fix it with one specific move, then re-ask. The test forces decisions because it has only two answers, and it forces motion because "acceptable" is permission to stop.

## "Slay the dragon" — hardest task first

The avoidance pattern in finishing sessions is consistent: the producer opens the track, fiddles with the kick for 20 minutes, and avoids the actual broken section (often the bridge, often the second verse vocal, often the outro). DeSantis's "slay the dragon" recommendation reverses the order [(EDMProd)](https://www.edmprod.com/finishing-music/). At the start of each session, name the worst section of the track and work on it first, while energy is highest. Put it off and the next session inherits the same dragon plus the accumulated dread. The tactic generalizes beyond DeSantis — Mixerman's framing on decision compounding has the same structure, just in different language [(Tape Op review)](https://tapeop.com/reviews/gear/93/zen-and-the-art-of-producing-book).

## Fresh-ears protocols

Ear fatigue is real and measurable. The Pro Audio Files protocol is specific: take a 15-minute break every two hours, and cap total mixing time per day with a hard ceiling, because perception of high-frequency content degrades measurably after extended exposure [(Pro Audio Files)](https://theproaudiofiles.com/7-ways-to-keep-your-ears-fresh-for-mixing/). The tactical move on a finishing day: limit yourself to three full passes — initial assessment, mid-session check, final closure — and between passes leave the room. Lower listening volumes during the polish phase, because both ear fatigue and confirmation bias scale with SPL. The cleanest test for "is the work done": bounce the mix, leave the studio for an hour, and listen on a system you haven't used during the session.

## The consumer-system rotation

The bounce-and-listen-elsewhere ritual is the most reliable translation check. Rotate the bounced mix through at least three systems before declaring it done: studio monitors at reference level, a consumer headphone or earbud (AirPods Pro have become a working reference because they approximate how the majority of listeners actually hear music), and a real-world environment such as a car or kitchen speaker [(Gearspace discussion on AirPods as reference)](https://gearspace.com/board/so-much-gear-so-little-time/1416799-airpods-pro-amazing-reference-pros.html). Take notes on each pass; don't open the DAW until the rotation is complete. Mix translation problems usually reduce to four issues — the room is lying, only one playback system was checked, references weren't loudness-matched, and the low end is unbalanced [(adrianmilea.com)](https://adrianmilea.com/mix-translation/). The rotation catches all four.

## Shipping vs. iterating — the decision criteria

The decision to ship is not "I can't think of anything else to change" — that state never arrives. The criteria are practical: (1) the rough mix translates across the consumer-system rotation without obvious failures, (2) the artist (or producer in solo mode) has stopped finding new problems on consecutive passes, (3) the issues that remain are stylistic preferences rather than execution flaws, and (4) the deadline is here. Tape Op's "Working With a Mix Engineer" piece frames it from the mixer's side — when the rough is past the comping-and-tuning stage and into "the production decisions are made," it is ready to hand off [(Tape Op #158)](https://tapeop.com/interviews/158/working-mix-engineer). The mirror principle for solo finishing: when revisions stop changing the listener's experience, the track is done — even if the producer can still hear differences.

## The session-close ritual

The end-of-session ritual matters as much as the close-of-track ritual. The Production Expert workflow habits list recommends a short, mechanical end-of-session sequence: bounce a stereo rough dated to the day; document the session's state in a notes track or session log; back up to a second drive or cloud; walk away [(Production Expert)](https://www.production-expert.com/production-expert-1/the-workflow-habits-every-audio-engineer-should-master). The mental component is critical — don't listen to the rough after closing the session. Fresh ears are an asset that can only be earned by stopping.

## The finishing ritual for a closed track

When a track is actually done — past the consumer-system rotation, past the artist's "no more changes" call — there is a longer ritual that ensures the track survives future recall. The canonical sequence from working producers:

1. **Master bounce** at the delivery format (typically 24-bit/44.1kHz or 48kHz WAV for digital distribution).
2. **Stems bounce** — every group bus rendered to its own file starting at bar 1, named consistently, exported to a `stems/` folder separate from session files [(Pro Mix Academy)](https://promixacademy.com/blog/how-to-bounce-stems-pro-tools/).
3. **Session freeze** — commit any live plugins, save a `_FINAL` version, and stop editing it. Future revisions get a new version number.
4. **Project archive** — collect all media into the session, save a "collect all" version, and back up to redundant storage (local drive + cloud).
5. **Project log** — a short text file noting the session's tempo, key, sample rate, the artist/collaborators, the mix engineer (if external), and any notes about plugin licensing or third-party dependencies. Future-recall sessions one or three years later depend on this note existing [(Sound on Sound on archiving)](https://www.soundonsound.com/techniques/studio-one-future-proof-your-song-archives).

The ritual exists because finished-but-unarchived projects are how producers lose tracks to plugin license changes, drive failures, and DAW version updates.

## The "throwaway song per week" practice

The single largest finishing accelerator is volume. EDMProd's recurring recommendation — finish one song a week, even if most of them are throwaways — is grounded in the deliberate-practice argument: a producer who finishes 52 tracks in a year goes through composition, arrangement, mixing, and shipping 52 times, while a producer with one perfect album goes through each stage perhaps once [(EDMProd, why finish more music)](https://www.edmprod.com/why-you-should-finish-more-music/). Finishing requires going through every stage; not finishing means skipping the stages that are hardest. The "throwaway" framing matters because it removes release pressure: the song doesn't need to be released, listened to, or even shared. It needs to be bounced, archived, and ended. Improvement compounds across volume in a way it doesn't across polish.

## The time-constrained completion exercise

The structural opposite of the polish-without-bound trap is the time-boxed completion exercise — a track started and finished in a single 60–120 minute session. The exercise forces every decision into the "is this acceptable" frame because nothing else fits. The educational value isn't the resulting track; it is the calibration the producer develops about how much of their normal session time is genuine decision-making versus exploratory drift [(eMastered on production challenges)](https://emastered.com/blog/music-production-challenges). Run the exercise periodically — once a month is a reasonable cadence — and the calibration transfers back to the longer-form work.

## Common failure modes and their countermeasures

- **The endless "alt mix" stack.** If the session has more than two alternate mixes, finishing has failed. Pick one, freeze the others, and stop.
- **Tweaking after the consumer rotation.** Once the bounced mix has passed the rotation, every additional change risks breaking what works. Cap revisions at one or two named fixes; if more emerge, the rotation needs to be redone.
- **The "one more reference track" loop.** Reference tracks are calibration, not target. Three references is enough; ten is procrastination.
- **The mix that lives forever because no one called it.** Self-imposed deadlines work; absent deadlines invite indefinite revision. Set a date, ship on it.
- **Re-opening a finished track to "improve" it.** Don't. The version on the master bounce is the version that exists. Future improvements belong in the next track.

The Mixerman framing applies across all of these: producing is the management of decisions, and finishing is the management of the last decisions — the ones the producer is most tempted not to make [(Tape Op review of Zen)](https://tapeop.com/reviews/gear/93/zen-and-the-art-of-producing-book).

## What "done" actually means

A finished track has four properties: it has been mastered (or rendered through a mastering chain that the producer trusts), it has been bounced as stems and archived, it has been logged with enough metadata that the producer can recover it in two years, and the producer has decided in writing that it is finished. The decision is the load-bearing element. Tracks that are finished-but-the-producer-hasn't-said-so tend to drift back into the active queue. Tracks that are stems-bounced, archived, dated, and marked done in a project log tend to stay done. The discipline is administrative, not creative — and that is exactly why it works.
