## What This File Is For

Intros and outros are the sections writers most often *don't decide* — they default to a four-bar instrumental front and a fade-out back without asking whether either serves the song. This file is the decision framework: **how do you open a song, and how do you end it, on purpose?** The opening sets the listener's expectations and, in the streaming era, decides whether they stay at all. The ending is where the song either resolves, surprises, or quietly wastes the listener's goodwill. Both are short sections that carry disproportionate weight, and both reward a conscious choice over a habit. The general structural picture lives in `corpus/structure/arrangement-arcs-and-transitions.md`; this file is specifically about the front door and the back door.

## The Cold Open

The **cold open** drops the listener directly into the first verse (or even the chorus) with no instrumental intro. It's the most aggressive front-loading move and a natural fit for the streaming era: the vocal arrives immediately, so there's nothing to skip past. A cold open says "we're already moving" — it suits songs with urgency, songs whose first line is strong enough to grab on its own, and any context where you want the listener oriented in the lyric from second one. The decision test: **is your first vocal line strong enough to be the first thing anyone hears?** If the opening line is a grabber, the cold open weaponizes it. If the opening line is a throat-clear that only makes sense after setup, a cold open exposes its weakness. The cold open also forfeits the *mood-setting* an intro provides, so it's the wrong call when the song needs atmosphere established before the voice enters.

## The Instrumental Intro — and Choosing Its Length

The **instrumental intro** establishes key, tempo, groove, and mood before the vocal. The conventional lengths are 8, 4, or 2 bars, and the choice is a real one. An **8-bar intro** gives a full mood-establishing statement — room for a groove to settle and the listener to arrive — and suits album contexts, dance music, and songs where the instrumental world is part of the appeal. A **4-bar intro** states the vibe and gets out of the way; it's the safe middle default. A **2-bar (or shorter) intro** is barely a count-in — a fast lean-in for streaming-conscious pop. The decision: **how much does the listener need to feel before the voice enters, and how much patience does your context afford?** Album rock can spend eight bars; a playlist-targeted single often can't. The honest rule: every bar of intro is a bar the listener experiences *before* you've given them a reason to stay, so spend them deliberately.

## The Riff-as-Intro

A special, high-value case: the **riff-as-intro**, where the opening instrumental figure is itself a hook strong enough to be the song's signature. Tom Petty's "American Girl" and the Rolling Stones' "(I Can't Get No) Satisfaction" open on guitar figures so identifiable they *are* the song's calling card. A riff-intro isn't dead time before the vocal — it's a hook delivered first, which means it survives the streaming front-loading pressure that kills mood-only intros. The decision test: **is your intro figure a hook, or just a vamp?** A hook earns its bars (and can run longer because it's giving the listener something memorable immediately). A vamp — a generic groove with no signature — should be cut short or cut entirely. The trap is mistaking a comfortable groove you like playing for a hook the listener will remember. If you couldn't hum the intro after one listen, it's a vamp.

## The Streaming-Era Front-Loading Pressure

The single biggest contemporary pressure on intros is the **skip threshold**. The well-documented economics: Spotify counts a play (and pays) only after roughly 30 seconds, and skip-rate research has found that a large share of streams are abandoned in the first few seconds — reporting and Switched On Pop's coverage cite figures on the order of a quarter of plays skipped within five seconds and a third before 30 seconds. The practical takeaway, named as era-bound: **for streaming-targeted songs, the front of the track is under intense pressure to deliver something compelling fast.** This is why intros shortened and disappeared across the 2010s (see `corpus/songwriting/form/section-lengths-by-genre-and-era.md`). Note the precise thresholds are 5-second and 30-second in the published research; treat any single "X-second rule" as a rounded heuristic rather than an exact law. The decision the pressure forces: how long can you make the listener wait for a hook before they bail?

## When to Ignore the Front-Loading Pressure

The front-loading pressure is real but genre- and context-bound, and plenty of great music ignores it on purpose. **Album tracks, dance music (where a long DJ-friendly intro is a feature), ambient and atmospheric work, prog, and any context where the audience is committed rather than browsing** can and should spend time on the front. A long intro is part of the aesthetic in dance music and in album-oriented rock; cutting it to satisfy a playlist algorithm would damage the song. The decision rule: **honor the front-loading pressure only when the song's actual context is browse-and-skip discovery.** If you're making an album, scoring a mood, or working in a genre with patient audiences, the skip threshold is irrelevant — and writing toward it would be optimizing for the wrong listener. The error is applying a streaming-single constraint to a song that will never live as a streaming single.

## Outro Types — The Menu

Outros come in a small set of recognizable types, and choosing among them is a decision about the *feeling you leave behind*. The **fade-out** (the song repeats and gradually drops in volume) signals "this continues beyond the frame" — once near-universal, now less common in streaming-era pop because a fade is dead time a listener skips. The **cold cut** (an abrupt, definitive stop) signals finality and punctuation — clean, confident, increasingly the default. The **tag** (a final repeated phrase or a modified last chorus that ends the song) gives a memorable last impression and a sing-along button. The **instrumental departure** lets the band or production take the song somewhere new at the end. The **false ending** (the song appears to stop, then resumes) is a structural surprise used sparingly. Each leaves a different aftertaste; pick by the emotional punctuation you want.

## The "Resolve or Surprise" Decision

Allan Moore's analytical framing — that the ending carries meaning, not just closure — sharpens the core outro decision into a binary: **should the outro resolve or surprise?** A *resolving* outro returns to stable harmony, restates the hook, and gives the listener the satisfaction of completion (the tag, the final chorus, the clean cadential cold cut). A *surprising* outro departs — a new section, an unexpected harmonic turn, a dissolution into texture, a bridge-as-outro (see `corpus/songwriting/form/modern-forms-post-chorus-drop-beat-switch.md`). The choice follows the song's emotional logic: a song about arriving, resolving, or affirming wants a resolving outro; a song about leaving, unraveling, or ambiguity wants a surprising one. The failure mode is the *defaulted* outro — a fade-out chosen by habit on a song that emotionally demanded a hard stop, or a tag bolted on where the song wanted to dissolve.

## The Dance-Music Outro Convention

Dance music has its own outro grammar driven by function: the **extended/DJ-friendly outro** strips the track back toward its rhythmic skeleton (often a percussion-and-bass section) so a DJ can beatmatch and blend into the next record. This is why club mixes run long and end on a vamp rather than a tag — the outro is *tooling for the mix*, not a statement for the home listener. The "radio edit" of the same track typically cuts this down or replaces it with a clean ending. The decision for a dance writer: **who is this version for — the DJ or the listener?** A track meant for club play wants the extended functional outro; a track meant for streaming wants it trimmed. Knowing which you're making prevents the mistake of either a club track that won't mix or a radio edit padded with a DJ outro nobody at home needs.

## The Outro as Permission to Experiment

A useful working principle several writers and producers cite: **the outro is the safest place in the song to experiment**, because the structural job is done and the listener has already gotten what they came for. A late instrumental departure, a key change, a stripped reharmonization, a spoken coda, a textural drift — these risk less at the end than they would mid-song, where a failed experiment derails the song's spine. This makes the outro a low-cost place to try the idea you weren't brave enough to put in the body. The discipline, though, is that "experiment freely" is not "noodle indefinitely": an experimental outro still needs to *end*, and an outro that overstays converts experimentation into self-indulgence. The good experimental outro takes the song somewhere and then stops; the bad one wanders until the listener leaves first.

## The "Where Does This Song End" Decision

The hardest outro question is simply *when to stop*. A song can resolve and then keep going on momentum; a fade can disguise the fact that the writer never decided on an ending. The discipline: **find the strongest possible last moment and end there, rather than trailing off.** Ask where the song has said everything it has to say — often that's the end of the final chorus or tag, and everything after is the band reluctant to stop playing. Pattison's prosody logic applies here too: the ending should feel inevitable, not arbitrary. If you're fading out, ask whether the fade is a choice or an avoidance of the decision. A definitive ending — even a slightly imperfect one — almost always beats an indecisive trail-off, because it tells the listener the song *meant* to end, which is part of feeling finished.

## How to Decide Intros and Outros at the Workbench

A practical pass. **(1)** For the intro, ask: is my first vocal line a grabber (favor cold open), is my opening figure a real hook (favor riff-intro, can run longer), or do I just have a vamp (cut it short or to a 2-bar lean-in)? **(2)** Identify the context — streaming single, album track, dance track — and apply the front-loading pressure *only* if it's a browse-and-skip context. **(3)** Time the front: how many seconds until the first vocal and the first hook? Decide consciously whether that wait is earned. **(4)** For the outro, name the feeling you want to leave — resolution or surprise — and pick the type (fade, cold cut, tag, departure, false ending) that delivers it. **(5)** Find the strongest possible last moment and end there; if you're fading, confirm it's a choice not an avoidance. **(6)** If experimenting, let the outro be the laboratory — but make it land and stop. The whole discipline reduces to: open with intention about *staying*, close with intention about *finishing*.
