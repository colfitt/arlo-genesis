## The Principle — Co-Writing Is a Craft of Its Own

Co-writing is not solo writing with an extra person; it is a distinct craft with its own moves, conventions, and discipline. The Nashville Music Row tradition has codified that craft over decades, and the [Nashville Songwriters Association International (NSAI)](https://www.nashvillesongwriters.com/) maintains the working standard for how co-writers behave in the room, divide the work, and assign credit. Jason Blume, who developed and teaches the [BMI Nashville Songwriters Workshop](https://americansongwriter.com/jason-blume-offers-steps-to-success/) and wrote [*Six Steps to Songwriting Success*](https://www.amazon.com/Steps-Songwriting-Success-Revised-Comprehensive/dp/0823084779), formalizes much of that working practice in writing. Holly Gleason's reporting across [American Songwriter](https://americansongwriter.com/author/holly-gleason/) and her broader [music-journalism archive](https://www.rocksbackpages.com/Library/Writer/holly-gleason) documents the Nashville room from the inside, particularly the women-songwriter perspective. This file synthesizes the working-room craft those sources teach — the lyric-specific moves co-writers make at the table, not the business side of splits. For the broader workflow protocol, this file pairs with `corpus/songwriting/workflows/nashville-co-writing-protocol.md` (A15 in the workflow recipes bucket); the present file focuses on the lyric-level mechanics.

## The Default Split Convention

The Nashville convention NSAI documents on its [co-writing guidance page](https://www.nashvillesongwriters.com/5-tips-co-writing) and reinforced across its [songwriting business pages](https://www.nashvillesongwriters.com/so-you-re-lookin-co-write): **however many people are in the room, that is how many ways the song is split, unless otherwise agreed in advance**. Two writers in the room = 50/50. Three writers = thirds. The convention is intentionally indifferent to *what* each writer contributed — a writer who provided a single line, a single melodic phrase, or even a critical edit gets the same share as the writer who drove the session. The practical reason: arguing line-by-line attribution after the fact is a relationship-destroyer, and the convention removes the argument by making the answer mechanical. The craft implication for the room itself: because contribution is not tracked line-by-line for the splits, writers are free to pitch, edit, and revise each other's lines without anxiety about giving away credit. The room runs on the shared assumption that everyone's hands are on everything.

## Driver and Navigator Roles

A working-room pattern NSAI describes in its [co-writing guidance](https://www.nashvillesongwriters.com/5-tips-co-writing): on any given day, "somebody's driving, somebody's navigating or steering." The driver-and-navigator model — one writer pushes forward with line ideas while the other steers, edits, and pulls back — is the implicit role structure of most productive co-writes, even when not named explicitly. The driver brings the next line; the navigator decides whether to take it or send it back. The roles can swap mid-session (the writer who drives the first verse often navigates the second), and most writers describe the most productive sessions as ones where both roles fluidly trade. The discipline the model imposes: when you are driving, throw lines without self-editing — the navigator's job is to filter, not yours. When you are navigating, accept that your job is to evaluate the line being pitched, not to write the next one yourself. The failure mode is two writers both trying to drive — every line gets pitched twice, nothing gets accepted, and the session stalls.

## The "Yes, And" Convention

A cross-genre co-writing norm Blume teaches in *Six Steps* and most working co-writers describe in interview: when a co-writer pitches a line, the default response is to build on it before rejecting it. The "yes, and" move borrowed from improv theatre: take the line, try to make it work, see what it opens up, and only after engaging with it decide whether it stays. The reverse — instant rejection of pitched lines — kills the room's momentum, because the pitching writer stops pitching. The discipline is hard because most pitched lines are not the final line; they are the seed of the final line. The skilled navigator's move is to receive the pitch, repeat it back with a small alteration ("what if it's *not* the train, but the platform?"), and let the pitching writer take the alteration further. The lyric that emerges is genuinely co-authored — neither writer wrote it alone, and neither can fully reconstruct who contributed which word. This is the convention's intended result.

## Save the Best Line for the Chorus

A discipline Blume teaches explicitly in [*Six Steps to Songwriting Success*](https://archive.org/details/6stepstosongwrit0000blum) and Nashville writers cite consistently: **when a brilliant line surfaces in a co-write, the first question is whether it belongs in the chorus**. The instinct of most writers is to use the strong line where it surfaces — usually a verse line during verse-writing — but the Nashville discipline asks whether the line is too good for the verse. The chorus is the line the listener will hear most; if the room's best line is buried in verse two, the song is structurally inverted. The mechanical move: when a co-writer pitches a line that produces visible agreement (the other writers nod, smile, or repeat it), pause and ask, "is that the chorus?" If yes, the line moves; the verse gets a different line. The discipline requires both writers to relinquish ownership of where the line lives — the line goes where the song needs it, not where it was first written. This move is one of the strongest arguments for co-writing as a craft: solo writers often miss the chorus-line buried in their verse because they cannot see the line from outside.

## The Take-Turns Line Convention

A common Nashville-room move documented across NSAI guidance and Sodajerker interviews on [Sodajerker on Songwriting](https://www.sodajerker.com): the writers take alternating lines, especially in verse construction. Writer A pitches line 1; Writer B pitches line 2; A pitches 3; B pitches 4. The convention serves two functions. First, it distributes the cognitive load — neither writer has to hold the whole verse in their head while pitching the next line. Second, it enforces variety — the next line is forced to differ from the previous one because a different writer is producing it. The take-turns move is not absolute (writers often pass on a turn or hand the pen back when stuck), but the underlying convention is that no one writer carries a whole section alone. The lyric-craft consequence: lines in a co-written verse often show small stylistic shifts that a solo lyric would not — a slight register change at line 3, a different image-source at line 5 — and these shifts are typically features, not bugs. They give the verse texture a solo writer would have to construct deliberately.

## The Veto Convention

The most-discussed and most-misunderstood Nashville room rule, documented across Blume's teaching and NSAI etiquette guidance: **either writer can veto a line without explanation**. If a co-writer says "I can't sing that" or "that's not for me," the line is gone — no debate, no defense, no asking why. The convention exists because the room cannot survive line-by-line argument, and because writers often have intuitions about what fits the song that they cannot articulate in the moment. The veto is the room's release valve. The craft discipline the veto imposes on the pitching writer: do not take the veto personally; pitch another line; the next pitch is often better because the veto narrowed the search. The discipline on the vetoing writer: use the veto sparingly and trust your collaborator that overuse will end the room. Most working co-writers describe the veto as a tool used a few times per session — enough to keep the song honest, not enough to stall the room.

## Lyric vs. Melodic Attribution

A frequent question in mixed co-writes — one writer "is the lyric writer" and the other "is the music writer" — is whether the lyric writer can pitch melody and vice versa. The Nashville convention NSAI and Blume both teach: yes, and you should. The role labels describe each writer's strength, not their permitted contribution. The splits remain even regardless of which writer contributed which element — and the convention explicitly encourages cross-role pitching because the lyric writer's melodic instinct often catches what the melody writer missed, and vice versa. The craft implication: when a lyric writer hums a melodic shape for a line, the melody writer does not push back on territorial grounds; the line gets evaluated on its merits. When a melody writer suggests a rephrasing of a lyric for prosodic fit, the lyric writer does not defend the original on territorial grounds. The territorial reflex is the failure mode the convention is designed to prevent.

## The Pre-Write Conversation

A specific Nashville room practice NSAI documents on its [co-writing tips page](https://www.nashvillesongwriters.com/5-tips-co-writing): the session begins with conversation, not writing. The pre-write — sometimes called "the catch-up" — is the room's warm-up. Writers talk about their week, recent listening, what they want to write about, what they have been stuck on, what the artist (if the song is being written for a specific cut) is looking for. NSAI's framing: "Jumping right in isn't always the best way to reach your goal." The pre-write surfaces the song's potential subject before the lyric construction begins; the strongest co-writes often emerge from a pre-write moment when one writer says something offhand that becomes the title. The discipline: do not skip the pre-write under time pressure. The 20 minutes spent on conversation often save the next two hours of unfocused line-pitching, because the song's argument is established before the lines start.

## The Concept-First Move

A Blume-taught and NSAI-reinforced sequence for the Nashville co-write: **establish the concept and the title before writing any lines**. The concept is the song's one-sentence argument — the same essential question Pattison teaches for solo lyric writing, covered in `corpus/songwriting/lyric/theme-consistency-checks.md`. The title is the line the refrain will deliver, the line the verses must earn, addressed in `corpus/songwriting/lyric/refrain-construction-earning-the-title.md`. In the Nashville room, the concept-and-title pass happens at the table before lyric construction begins, often with both writers throwing concept fragments and titles until one produces visible agreement. The discipline forces both writers to align on what the song is about before either invests in lines; the lines then have a target. The failure mode is starting lines before the concept is settled — the lines drift, the writers disagree on what they are drifting toward, and the room produces fragments rather than a song. Blume's framing: a co-write without a settled concept is two writers writing two different songs that share a chord progression.

## The "What Does She/He Want To Say" Frame

A specific Nashville-room move documented in Holly Gleason's reporting on [Music Row co-writing](https://americansongwriter.com/author/holly-gleason/) and Blume's teaching: when the song is being written for a specific artist (the standard Nashville cut-pitch model), the room's organizing question is *what does this artist want to say right now*. The artist's voice, current life situation, recent interviews, and last single all become inputs to the room. The discipline is that neither writer is writing in their own voice; both are writing in the artist's voice. The lyric-craft implication: pronouns, vocabulary, and register all get filtered through the question "is this how this artist would say this." A line that is beautiful but does not fit the artist gets cut, even if both writers love it. This is one of the hardest disciplines for non-Nashville writers entering the room — the singer-songwriter habit of writing in your own voice is exactly the habit the cut-pitch model penalizes. Gleason's reporting documents this discipline across decades of Nashville rooms; it is the difference between a song that gets cut and a song that gets passed on.

## Holding the Pen vs. Handing It Off

A specific lyric-room move Blume teaches: at any moment in the session, one writer is "holding the pen" — they are the one writing down the lines as they are agreed. Holding the pen is not a status role; it is a logistical one, and it should rotate. The reason: the writer holding the pen tends to have small editorial control (which words go down, how the line is punctuated, what gets crossed out) that accumulates over a session. Trading the pen, especially at section breaks (after the first verse, after the chorus is settled), distributes that editorial control. The mechanical move at the table: when the chorus is settled and the room moves to verse 2, the writer who held the pen for the verse 1 and chorus passes the pen. The pen-holder for verse 2 brings small but real authorial input that changes which lines get tried. This is a craft detail few non-Nashville writers think about, but it is the kind of small protocol that distinguishes a productive room from a stalled one.

## When ARLO Should Surface Co-Writing Dynamics

A practical heuristic for the working writer. Signals that co-writing dynamics is the likely topic. **(1)** "I'm going into a co-write tomorrow" — the canonical signal; the user is preparing for a room and needs the conventions. **(2)** "my co-writer keeps shooting down my lines" — usually a veto-overuse signal or a missing pre-write alignment. **(3)** "we have a lot of good lines but no song" — usually a concept-first failure; the room produced lines without a settled argument. **(4)** "I don't know how to ask for more credit" — a splits-convention question; the answer is usually that the conversation should have happened at the start of the session, not after. **(5)** "I'm writing for [artist] and the lines don't feel right" — the what-does-she/he-want-to-say frame; the lines are probably in the writer's voice, not the artist's. For each signal, ARLO's first move is to surface the relevant Nashville convention and ask whether the room used it. The conventions are not absolute — they are tools, and writers can break them deliberately — but most stalled co-writes have failed at a specific named convention, and naming it is half the fix.
