# Theme Consistency — Stress-Testing Lines Against the Song's Subject

## The Principle — A Song Is About One Thing

The theme-consistency school of lyric revision rests on a single, contested claim: **a song is about one thing, and every line of the lyric must serve that one thing**. This is Pat Pattison's central organizing claim across *Writing Better Lyrics* and his Berklee teaching, and it is echoed by Jimmy Webb in *Tunesmith* and Sheila Davis in *The Craft of Lyric Writing*. The school is not universal — some writers (Joanna Newsom in long form, Bowie in *Berlin* trilogy mode, Sufjan in atmospheric mode) deliberately work in multi-thematic or productively ambiguous registers. But for a stuck musician revising a draft that "isn't working," the most common diagnosis is **theme drift** — the song started about one thing, drifted into another, and the writer cannot say in one sentence what the song is about anymore. The theme-consistency pass is the workbench technique for catching and fixing that drift. Pattison teaches it as "the essential question." Webb teaches it as the title-as-anchor. Both share the underlying claim: if you cannot name what the song is about, the song does not know what it is about, and the listener will feel that.

## Pattison's "Essential Question" Frame

Pat Pattison's framing across his books and his [lyric tips page at patpattison.com](https://www.patpattison.com/pat-s-lyric-tips) is to ask before writing — and again during revision — **the song's essential question**. The essential question is a single sentence that names what the song is grappling with: *"What does it cost to forgive someone who didn't apologize?"* *"What does it feel like to be in the room your mother used to live in?"* *"Why does the same fight keep happening?"* The essential question is more useful than a one-word topic ("forgiveness," "grief," "frustration") because it carries an *angle* — a question implies a stance the song will take. Every line of the draft can then be stress-tested against the question: does this line move toward the answer, complicate the question, or wander off? Pattison's revision rule, paraphrased from his teaching: **lines that do not engage the essential question are decoration**, no matter how well-written. Cut them. The pass is brutal because most first-draft lyrics contain at least one line the writer loves that doesn't belong; the discipline is to recognize the line is good and cut it anyway because it belongs in a different song.

## Webb's Title-as-Theme Test

Jimmy Webb's parallel framing in [*Tunesmith*](https://archive.org/details/tunesmith00jimm) (Hyperion, 1998) approaches theme through the title. Webb's claim, drawn from a writing career anchored by titles that *are* the song's argument ("By the Time I Get to Phoenix," "Wichita Lineman," "MacArthur Park," "The Highwayman"): **if you cannot title the song, you do not know what it is about**. The title is the song's center of gravity; it is the line the listener will remember; it is the candidate phrase for the refrain. Webb's discipline: before declaring the lyric finished, write down the title and read each verse asking whether the verse is doing work the title implies. *"By the Time I Get to Phoenix"* implies distance, time, and travel; verses that don't engage distance or time are off-theme. *"Wichita Lineman"* implies a worker, a place, and a kind of longing; verses that don't engage at least one of those are off-theme. The title-as-test is the Webb version of Pattison's essential question — different vocabulary, same craft move. The two together form the canonical American theme-consistency framework: name the song's one thing, then check every line against it.

## The "What Is This Song Actually About" Exercise

A practical revision exercise drawn from both Pattison and Webb and reported across Paul Zollo's [*Songwriters on Songwriting*](https://www.amazon.com/Songwriters-Songwriting-Revised-Paul-Zollo/dp/0306812657) interviews: **after the first draft, set the draft aside and write, in one sentence, what the song is actually about.** Do not write what you *wanted* the song to be about; write what the draft has become. Often these are different. A song that started as "what does it cost to forgive" can become, in draft, "I am angry at this person" — same subject, different angle, different song. Naming the actual draft theme is the first step in deciding whether to keep the original theme and revise the draft toward it, or accept the new theme and revise away the original-theme residue. Either choice is legitimate; the wrong choice is to leave both themes in the song. Songs with two themes tend to be songs with no theme — the listener cannot tell which line is doing which work, so the cumulative effect is incoherence.

## The Line-by-Line Stress Test

The mechanical version of the theme-consistency check, executable in 15–20 minutes on a complete draft. **(1)** Write the song's theme statement (one sentence) at the top of the page. **(2)** Read each line of the lyric in order. For each line, ask: *does this line engage the theme?* Mark each line: ✓ (engages), ~ (tangentially relates), ✗ (off-theme). **(3)** Scan the marks. If the column has more than one or two ✗ marks in a single verse, the verse is doing different work than the song. **(4)** For each ✗ line, decide: rewrite the line to engage the theme, cut the line, or — if the line is undeniably load-bearing — interrogate whether the theme statement itself is wrong. Sometimes the off-theme line is the line revealing what the song is *really* about. The discipline is the same as Pattison's "essential question" pass: most first-draft lines pass with ~ rather than ✓, and the work of revision is moving them to ✓ or cutting them.

## The Off-Theme Cleverness Trap

The hardest line to cut in a draft is the **brilliant line that doesn't belong**. Every working writer in Zollo's archive — John Prine, Paul Simon, Tom Waits, Carole King — describes the same experience: a line they love, that works on its own, that the song cannot accommodate because it is in a different emotional register or argues a different point. The Pattison and Webb discipline is identical: **cut the brilliant off-theme line, and save it in a notebook for the song it actually belongs to.** Working writers across the canon describe maintaining a "scraps" file or notebook of lines that didn't fit but were too good to throw away; weeks or years later, the line finds the song that needs it. The hardest version of this discipline is the brilliant *chorus* line that doesn't belong — these are especially seductive because choruses do so much work — but if the chorus line argues a different point from the verses, the lyric will read as two songs glued together. The Webb test: does the chorus's claim follow from what the verses showed? If not, one of them is in the wrong song.

## Productive Ambiguity vs. Drift

Theme-consistency is one school of thought, not the only school. There is a legitimate craft case for **productive ambiguity** — songs whose theme is intentionally multiple, whose meaning shifts on rehearings, whose lyric supports several readings. Joni Mitchell's "Both Sides Now," David Bowie's *Hunky Dory* tracks, Bob Dylan's mid-career symbolic work, Sufjan's *Carrie & Lowell* memory songs, Joanna Newsom's narrative-meets-cosmology lyrics — these are not theme-drift, they are *theme-layering*. The distinction the school flags: productive ambiguity is **intentional and architected**. The writer can name what the multiple themes are and why they coexist. Drift is **unintentional and unrecognized**: the writer cannot name what is happening. The diagnostic question: *if a careful listener asked what this song is about, could you give a coherent answer — even a complicated, layered answer?* If yes, you have ambiguity. If you find yourself shrugging or contradicting yourself, you have drift. ARLO should not flatten ambiguity into single-theme orthodoxy. ARLO should flag drift.

## The Cutting Move — Why Off-Theme Lines Are Usually the Cuts

A pattern observable in working-writer revision practice and named explicitly in Pattison's teaching: **when a draft is too long and needs cutting, the off-theme lines are almost always the right cuts**. The writer's instinct is often to cut the line that sounds worst; the school's instinct is to cut the line that is *off-topic*, even if it sounds fine. A four-verse song that should be three usually has one verse pulling at a different theme; cutting that verse tightens the song. A six-line bridge that should be four usually has two lines that argue a different point; cutting them sharpens the bridge. The mechanical move: after the theme-statement pass, identify the lines marked ✗ or ~ — these are the cut candidates before any line marked ✓ becomes one. The discipline is hard because off-theme lines often *sound* finished while on-theme lines often need revision. The school's claim is that an on-theme line that needs revision is more valuable than an off-theme line that is already polished, because the song's coherence rests on the on-theme work.

## The Title-Declares-the-Theme Rule

A specific Webb claim from *Tunesmith*, paraphrased and echoed in [the Blinkist summary of the book](https://www.blinkist.com/en/books/tunesmith-en): **if you cannot title the song, you do not yet know what the song is about**. The rule is diagnostic, not prescriptive — it doesn't mean every song must title easily, but it means a song that resists every title attempt is probably a song whose theme is not yet clarified. Working writers across genres test this when stuck: try titling the draft with five different titles. If none of them feel right, the song's theme is probably the issue, not the lyric craft. If one title clicks immediately, the click is information: that title names what the song actually is, and the lyric can be revised toward it. The Webb-trained move: when stuck on a chorus or bridge, return to the title question. The title-as-theme test is a faster diagnostic than the line-by-line pass when the writer suspects but cannot pinpoint that the song is unfocused.

## The Theme Check at the Chorus

Choruses are the highest-leverage theme-consistency check, because the chorus is the line the listener will hear most. Pattison's claim across his teaching: **the chorus is the song's argument**. Whatever the chorus repeatedly claims is what the listener will conclude the song is about, regardless of what the verses showed. A chorus that argues "I will love you forever" with verses showing a relationship in collapse will be heard as ironic at best and incoherent at worst — unless the writer is deliberately working that contradiction (a legitimate craft move, but one that must be earned with explicit signals to the listener). The theme-consistency pass at the chorus: read the chorus aloud, write down what it claims in one sentence, then read each verse asking whether the verse builds toward that claim or contradicts it. If a verse contradicts the chorus without explicit signaling, the contradiction will read as drift, not as craft.

## The Bridge as Theme Pressure-Point

The bridge — covered structurally in `corpus/structure/section-function-verse-prechorus-chorus-bridge.md` — is the place where theme is most often broken or most pointedly clarified. Pattison's framing: the bridge should either **deepen the theme** (introduce a new angle on the same question) or **resolve the theme** (deliver the conclusion the verses set up). The failure mode is a bridge that introduces a *different* theme — a song about forgiveness whose bridge is about loneliness reads as a song that lost its argument. The theme-consistency test at the bridge: does the bridge add to what we already know about the essential question, or does it change the subject? If it changes the subject, the bridge is doing the wrong job. The fix is sometimes a rewrite; sometimes the bridge is fine and the *verses* changed subject earlier without the writer noticing. Either way, the bridge-test catches the drift.

## When ARLO Should Surface a Theme Problem

A practical heuristic for ARLO and the working writer. Signals that theme-consistency is the likely diagnosis. **(1)** "I don't know what this song is about anymore" — the canonical signal; the writer is naming the problem. **(2)** "the bridge isn't working" — bridges fail more often from theme drift than from harmonic problems. **(3)** "the second verse doesn't feel as strong" — second verses often drift because the writer has used the strongest material in the first verse and is reaching for new content. **(4)** "the chorus doesn't feel earned" — when the chorus's claim doesn't follow from the verses, the issue is usually theme misalignment rather than the chorus itself. **(5)** "I have a lot of lines I love but it isn't a song" — the classic theme-incoherence signal; the lines are not arguing the same thing. For each signal, ARLO's first move is the essential-question pass: ask the user to state in one sentence what the song is about. If they hesitate, hedge, or contradict themselves, the diagnosis is confirmed.
