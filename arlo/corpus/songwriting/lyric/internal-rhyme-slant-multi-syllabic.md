## Rhyme as a Stability Dial — Pattison's Reframe

The single most useful idea about rhyme in modern English songwriting craft is Pat Pattison's reframe, repeated across *Songwriting: Essential Guide to Rhyming* and the [Berklee Online prosody article](https://online.berklee.edu/takenote/prosody-in-music-and-songwriting/): **rhyme is not decoration; rhyme is a stability dial.** Different rhyme types produce different degrees of closure. Perfect rhyme locks a section closed. Slant rhyme suspends it. Internal rhyme accelerates the line. Multi-syllabic rhyme increases density. Chain rhyme builds momentum. The writer is not picking rhymes from a thesaurus — they are choosing how stable or unstable they want the listener to feel at this point in the song. This is a school of thought (Pattison teaches it; Sheila Davis teaches a compatible framework) but it is the dominant working framework in contemporary American songwriting education and is the lens this file uses.

## The Pattison Taxonomy — Six Rhyme Types

Pattison's canonical taxonomy in *Songwriting: Essential Guide to Rhyming*, also summarized in [Andrea Stolpe's rhyme article](https://www.andreastolpe.com/articles/rhyme-types) and the [Berklee Online prosody overview](https://online.berklee.edu/takenote/prosody-in-music-and-songwriting/): **perfect, family, additive, subtractive, assonance, consonance.** Stability ranking, most resolved to least: perfect > family > additive/subtractive > assonance > consonance. Adjacent to these six, working pop and hip-hop add: **internal rhyme** (rhyme within a line rather than at line-end), **multi-syllabic rhyme** (two or more syllables in the rhyming unit), and **chain rhyme** (a sequence of rhymes daisy-chained across lines or within a long line). These are not in Pattison's six because they describe placement and density, not vowel-consonant matching — but they intersect the six (e.g., a multi-syllabic rhyme can be perfect or slant). The full kit, then, is: six rhyme types × placement choices (line-end or internal) × density choices (single-syllable, multi-syllabic, chain).

## Why Slant Rhyme Earned Its Place

Slant rhyme (Pattison calls the various near-rhymes additive, subtractive, assonance, consonance) does work that perfect rhyme cannot. Per the [Sound on Sound Pattison interview](https://www.soundonsound.com/techniques/pat-pattison-writing-better-lyrics), perfect rhyme everywhere in a song about heartbreak makes the song sound like a greeting card — the form contradicts the content. **Slant rhyme creates forward motion because it does not fully close.** The listener's ear half-hears a rhyme and is pulled into the next line. Williams, Mann, Bridgers, and the Pattison school all use slant rhyme as their default and reserve perfect rhyme for moments of deliberate closure. The diagnostic: a chorus's last line usually wants the most-perfect rhyme available (closure); the rest of the lyric usually wants slant (motion). The reverse — perfect rhyme throughout the verse and slant at the chorus's end — fights the section function and rarely works.

## Internal Rhyme — What It Does to Pace

**Internal rhyme is rhyme inside a single line, not at its end.** "She *waited* in the *station* / for the *late train*" contains an internal rhyme cluster (waited / station; late / train). Pattison and Adam Bradley's [*Book of Rhymes*](https://www.amazon.com/Book-Rhymes-Poetics-Hip-Hop/dp/0465003478) both note the same mechanical effect: internal rhyme **accelerates a line by giving the ear closure points before line-end**, increasing perceived density and energy. Hip-hop is the genre where internal rhyme is most-developed (covered below), but folk, country, and indie use it too — Joni Mitchell's *"The Last Time I Saw Richard"*, Springsteen's *"Born to Run,"* Aimee Mann's *"Save Me,"* and dozens of others stack internal rhymes to pack a line with energy. The craft move: internal rhyme is the tool for "this line needs to move faster without being shorter." The risk: too many internal rhymes per line read as forced, and the listener loses the song. Calibrate per-line, not per-song.

## Multi-Syllabic Rhyme — The Hip-Hop Tradition

The most-developed multi-syllabic rhyme tradition in English-language popular music is in hip-hop, documented in Paul Edwards's [*How to Rap*](https://www.amazon.com/How-Rap-Art-Science-Hip-Hop/dp/1556528167) and Adam Bradley's [*Book of Rhymes*](https://www.amazon.com/Book-Rhymes-Poetics-Hip-Hop/dp/0465003478). A multi-syllabic rhyme is one where **two or more syllables across the rhyming pair match in stress, vowel, and consonant patterns** — not just the last syllable. Edwards's book documents — via interviews with over 100 working rappers — that multi-syllabic rhyme is the genre's central craft achievement. Single-syllable rhyme (cat/hat) is amateur in this idiom; the working standard is two-syllable (matter/batter), three-syllable (catcher in the rye / patch her in the eye), and beyond. The most-cited contemporary practitioners include **Eminem** (whose multi-syllabic schemes in tracks like *"Lose Yourself,"* *"Stan,"* and *"Rap God"* are catalogued across hip-hop journalism), **Black Thought** of The Roots (especially the 2017 Funkmaster Flex freestyle, widely analyzed), **Pusha T**, **Kendrick Lamar**, **MF DOOM**, and **Big Pun**. The catalog of named practitioners matters: the tradition has visible craft heroes, and ARLO should retrieve them by name.

## How Multi-Syllabic Rhyme Actually Works

Edwards's [*How to Rap*](https://www.amazon.com/How-Rap-Art-Science-Hip-Hop/dp/1556528167) breaks multi-syllabic rhyme into the working components rappers describe. **(1) Vowel matching** — the vowels in the rhyming unit must align (often via assonance rather than perfect match). **(2) Consonant matching** — consonants don't have to be perfect; the ear accepts close matches. **(3) Stress pattern** — the stressed syllables in the rhyming units must align with the beat (covered in detail in C5). **(4) Length matching** — the rhyming units should be roughly the same syllable count. The most-quoted Eminem example in hip-hop analysis: *"nobody listens to techno"* rhymed against *"and even if I did all I'd say is 'I don't like Brian Eno.'"* The matched syllabic chunks across the two lines are the move. The craft is not "find a word that rhymes with X" — it is "build a multi-syllable phrase whose vowels and stresses match another multi-syllable phrase, both of which sit naturally on the beat." This is genuinely difficult, which is why hip-hop respects the move as a marker of skill.

## Chain Rhyme — Daisy-Chained Internal Rhymes

A subset move worth naming on its own: **chain rhyme is a sequence of rhymes — usually internal — daisy-chained across or within lines.** Bradley's [*Book of Rhymes*](https://www.amazon.com/Book-Rhymes-Poetics-Hip-Hop/dp/0465003478) documents this in detail. The first line introduces a rhyme; the second line picks up that rhyme and introduces a new one; the third line picks up the second's rhyme and introduces another. The effect is a propulsive forward pull. MF DOOM's discography is the most-cited chain-rhyme catalog ([Mike Eagle's MF DOOM essays for Pitchfork](https://pitchfork.com/) document specific examples). Outside hip-hop, chain rhyme appears in Stephen Sondheim's lyric craft (acknowledged in Sondheim's [*Finishing the Hat*](https://www.amazon.com/Finishing-Hat-Collected-1954-1981-Attendant/dp/0679439072)), in Lin-Manuel Miranda's *Hamilton* lyrics, and in some 19th-century English poetry. The risk is the same as with all dense rhyme: at high density, the listener can lose the meaning of the line under the sound of the rhyme. Calibrate.

## Matching Rhyme Density to Section Function

A working rule from the Pattison framework, generalized to include placement: **rhyme density should match section function.** Sparse rhyme (slant, line-end only) is often correct for verses, which do narrative work and need the listener to follow language. Dense rhyme (internal, multi-syllabic, chain) is often correct for hooks and choruses, which do incantation work and benefit from sonic density. The reverse — dense verses, sparse chorus — produces a song where the hook lands flat because the verses already exhausted the listener's rhyme attention. The exception is rap: in dense hip-hop, the verse is where multi-syllabic complexity lives and the hook simplifies. The genre inversion is real, and ARLO should not generalize a folk-prosody rule onto hip-hop or vice versa. **Name the genre, then calibrate density.**

## The "Fire / Desire" Trap — Tired Perfect Rhymes

Pattison's standing complaint in *Songwriting: Essential Guide to Rhyming* and the Sound on Sound interview: the worst song-writing-by-rhyme tic is the **tired perfect-rhyme pair**. *Fire / desire*, *tears / fears*, *heart / apart*, *love / above*, *night / light*, *gone / on*. These rhymes are so over-used that the listener hears the second word coming as soon as the first is named. The result is a song where the listener can predict the lyric and stops listening. The craft move: when a draft contains a tired perfect-rhyme pair, the right move is rarely "use the rhyme anyway" — it is to **break the pair**. Replace the rhyme with a slant version. Use family or additive rhyme. Or restructure so the trigger word lands somewhere else. The diagnostic: read the draft to a friend and pause before each rhyme. If they can fill it in, the rhyme is dead. The exception, named by Pattison: a tired rhyme used with full self-awareness as a hook — Adele can rhyme *fire / desire* because the song is operating in a genre where the cliché is the point. Most contexts are not that.

## Genre Boundedness — Folk Slant, Hip-Hop Density

The honest framing of this material: **rhyme conventions are genre-bound.** Folk and Americana reward slant rhyme heavily; Williams's *Car Wheels on a Gravel Road* uses slant rhyme as its default and rarely closes a line with perfect rhyme. Hip-hop rewards multi-syllabic complexity; a folk-style sparse-rhyme rap track would read as amateur in the idiom. Country pop is largely perfect-rhyme; a Nashville chorus that violates this convention often fails on Music Row. Stadium rock often uses heroic perfect rhyme; U2 closes choruses on perfect-rhyme pairs because the function is anthemic. Bedroom indie tolerates wide latitude. The rule: **before deciding what rhyme type to use, name the genre and the section.** A move that works in Eminem will not work in Phoebe Bridgers; a move that works in Bridgers will not work in Eminem. Both are valid; neither is universal. This is the honest answer to "how should I rhyme?" — name the genre first.

## The Workbench Decision Procedure

A working procedure for rhyme decisions on a draft. **(1) Name the section function** — narrative verse, anthemic chorus, momentum-building pre-chorus, contrasting bridge. **(2) Default rhyme type by section** — slant in verses (motion), perfect or family at chorus-end (closure), additive/subtractive in bridges (instability). **(3) Density check** — for each line, ask whether internal rhyme would help pace or hurt clarity. **(4) Genre boundedness** — is the song in a tradition that expects high density (hip-hop), tight rhyme convention (country), loose slant (folk/indie)? **(5) Tired-pair scan** — flag every fire/desire and decide whether to keep, break, or repurpose. **(6) Read aloud** — does the rhyme feel natural to the speech or forced? The decision is per-section, per-line, with the genre and section function as the binding constraints. The most-common amateur failure is choosing rhymes by what is easy to find a rhyme for, then writing a song that rhymes everywhere and lands nowhere. The discipline is matching rhyme to function, not the reverse.

## Common Failure Modes

Three patterns. **(1) Perfect-rhyme-everywhere** — the writer rhymes every line at the end with a perfect rhyme. The song reads as a children's poem regardless of subject matter. The fix is to substitute slant rhyme in verses and reserve perfect for closure points. **(2) Forced multi-syllabic** — the writer attempts multi-syllabic rhyme in a non-hip-hop idiom and produces lines that nobody would say. The genre is fighting the technique. The fix is to use the rhyme type appropriate to the idiom. **(3) Internal-rhyme overload** — the writer stacks internal rhymes per line until the listener cannot follow the meaning. The lines pop with sound but communicate nothing. The fix is to thin the internal rhyme density and let the line breathe. All three failures are the same underlying mistake: choosing rhyme without reference to function. The Pattison framework, applied honestly, prevents all three.
