# Show Don't Tell — The Concrete-Noun Discipline

## The Principle, Named Honestly

"Show don't tell" is the most-quoted maxim in English-language songwriting craft, and the easiest to misapply. In Pat Pattison's working framing — repeated across *Writing Better Lyrics* and his Berklee lectures and recorded in the [Berklee Online "Prosody" overview](https://online.berklee.edu/takenote/prosody-in-music-and-songwriting/) and the [Sound on Sound interview](https://www.soundonsound.com/techniques/pat-pattison-writing-better-lyrics) — the principle is mechanical, not mystical. **Concrete nouns and action verbs activate the listener's senses; abstractions slide off them.** A line that names *a screen door, a kitchen, a Tuesday* gives the listener something to picture. A line that names *love, sadness, freedom* gives them a category. Categories don't land emotionally; instances do. This is a school of thought (Pattison teaches it; so does Sheila Davis; so do Aimee Mann, Lucinda Williams, and John Prine practice it) — not a universal rule, and not the only way to write a good lyric. But for a stuck musician revising a draft, it is the single highest-leverage diagnostic in the kit.

## The Aimee Mann / Lucinda Williams / John Prine School

Three working writers anchor this school in different American idioms. **Aimee Mann** — across her *Magnolia* soundtrack work and the *Whatever* / *Bachelor No. 2* records — builds whole songs out of specific household objects and overheard speech. In her [Sodajerker on Songwriting episode](https://www.sodajerker.com/), she describes starting from a concrete image and refusing to let the verse "lift off into generality." **Lucinda Williams** — *Car Wheels on a Gravel Road* is the textbook — names roads, cars, weather, and brand-name food until the song reads like a deposition. The album's title track stacks "Sittin' in the kitchen," "screen door slams," "Loretta singin' on the radio" — every line is something you can point at. **John Prine** — in his Paul Zollo interview in *Songwriters on Songwriting* — talks about writing what the eye sees first, what the ear hears next, and never naming the feeling. *Sam Stone*'s "There's a hole in Daddy's arm where all the money goes" tells you about addiction without using the word. Naming this school matters: this is not "the right way to write." It is the school that prizes pictorial precision, and ARLO should retrieve it by name when the user is working in or adjacent to that tradition.

## The Noun-Verb-Noun Diagnostic

A working revision pass Pattison teaches in *Writing Better Lyrics* — paraphrased in [Berklee Press summaries](https://berkleepress.com/) and the [iZotope object-writing guide](https://www.izotope.com/en/learn/object-writing-your-secret-to-vivid-song-lyrics) — is to read each line and ask: **does this line contain a concrete noun, an action verb, and another concrete noun?** Not as a hard rule, but as a smell test. *"She slammed the screen door"* passes (she / slammed / screen door). *"She felt so sad"* fails (no concrete noun, no action verb — "felt" is stative). *"Love is a battlefield"* passes only because both abstractions get pulled into the concrete by the metaphor — and is one reason that song works despite the high abstraction count. The diagnostic isn't "count nouns and verbs." It's "after I read this line, can I draw anything?" If the answer is no, the line is telling, not showing.

## "Test the Line Without Context"

Pattison's second revision move, recorded across his books and Sound on Sound interview, is the **context-stripping test**: cover the surrounding lines and read the candidate line cold. If the line still produces an image or a feeling on its own, it's earning its place. If it only "works" because the listener already knows what the song is about, it is a placeholder you wrote when you couldn't think of the real line. The test is brutal — most first-draft lines fail it — and that is the point. The Aimee Mann / Williams / Prine school passes this test by default because every line is anchored in a thing. The test also flags the worst first-draft tic in English-language songwriting: the **placeholder emotion line** ("and I just feel so lost") that the writer intended to replace and forgot to. Run the cover-the-context pass on every chorus you have ever written and you will find at least one.

## The Action-Verb Half of the Move

The concrete-noun fixation under-discusses the verb. **Action verbs do the same job nouns do; stative verbs ("is," "feels," "seems," "becomes") leak the energy out of a line.** Compare *"she walks the dog at sunrise"* with *"she's a person who has a dog."* The first earns the image; the second tells you about her. Per the [Berklee Take Note article](https://online.berklee.edu/takenote/prosody-in-music-and-songwriting/), Pattison flags "to be" verbs as the most frequent first-draft offender. A useful revision sweep: highlight every "is / was / am / are / were / be / been / being" in the draft and ask whether a stronger verb is available. Often the line is salvageable; sometimes the whole line goes. Prine's *"There's a hole in Daddy's arm"* uses "is" — but "is" is doing concrete locative work (there is a hole; it is in this place) rather than the abstract identity-claim work it does in *"I am sad."* The distinction is worth keeping.

## The Abstract-Emotion-Word Trap

The hardest line in popular songwriting to write well is **the one that names the emotion**. *Love, sadness, freedom, lonely, broken, lost, alone* — these words carry no information because the listener has to import their own definition. They are not banned. The school's actual rule, paraphrased from Pattison's teaching and audible in Mann/Williams/Prine practice: **abstract emotion words earn their place when surrounded by enough concrete detail that the abstraction lands as a summary rather than a substitute.** Prine's *"It's a half an inch of water and you think you're gonna drown"* earns its abstract conclusion in the same song because the verses are anchored in concrete agricultural decline. Joni Mitchell can write "I really don't know love at all" at the end of *Both Sides Now* because she spent two full verses on clouds and circus rides first. The trap is using the abstract word *instead* of doing the concrete work. When ARLO sees a draft with three abstraction words in four lines, that's the diagnostic signal.

## Concrete-But-Clichéd Nouns — When Roses and Rain Die

A concrete noun is not automatically alive. *Roses, rain, fire, heart, road, stars, wings* — these are concrete in the dictionary sense (you can picture them) but **so over-used that the picture they produce is blurred and generic**. The Mann/Williams/Prine school cuts dead nouns hard. Williams will write "Vicksburg" before she writes "town." Prine writes "Demerol" before "painkiller." Mann writes "Diet Coke" before "drink." The principle, from Pattison's *Writing Better Lyrics* via [Soundfly's Flypaper article on object writing](https://flypaper.soundfly.com/write/how-to-generate-great-songwriting-ideas-with-object-writing-technique/): the freshness of a concrete noun is a function of its specificity. *Rose* is concrete; *the seven dollar rose she bought at the gas station* is specific. The revision move when you find a dead concrete noun: ask what particular instance you can name in its place. If you can't think of one, the noun isn't earning a line.

## Brand Names, Place Names, Proper Nouns

A proven move in this school is **proper-noun substitution**. Pop's most-cited recent example, discussed in the [Switched On Pop "folklore" episodes](https://switchedonpop.com/), is Taylor Swift's swerve into named objects on *folklore* and *evermore* — *"the old cardigan under someone's bed,"* *"I haven't met the new me yet,"* *"James, Betty, Inez"* as recurring characters. Williams names *Lake Charles, Louisiana*. Prine names *Muhlenberg County* and *Sam Stone*. Mann names *Wise Up* directly to the listener. Proper nouns are the highest-specificity concrete category — a place name does work no descriptor can do, because the listener either knows the place or can look it up. The risk is over-naming (the song becomes an inventory) or naming things the listener can't reach (an obscure street name from your hometown that produces no image for anyone else). The discipline is to name the one thing per verse that does the most pictorial work, not five.

## When Telling Beats Showing

This school is honest enough to name its exception. **Direct emotional statement lands when the song has earned the right to make it.** The last line of Prine's *Hello in There* — "say 'hello in there, hello'" — is a direct address that tells the listener exactly what to do. It works because the previous three verses showed elderly isolation in concrete detail; the telling line is the conclusion, not the substitute. The rule, paraphrased across Pattison's teaching and Sheila Davis's [*The Craft of Lyric Writing*](https://www.amazon.com/Craft-Lyric-Writing-Sheila-Davis/dp/0898792827) (Davis is the second canonical American lyric-writing textbook after Pattison): **show first, then tell — and only if the showing has built up enough pressure that the telling discharges it.** A chorus that tells without verses that showed lands flat. A chorus that tells after verses that showed becomes the hook.

## The Read-Aloud Diagnostic

A move shared across the Mann / Williams / Prine school and recorded in Pattison's revision chapters: **read every draft line aloud, slowly, looking up at the ceiling between lines.** Two failures surface: (1) lines that you stumble on because the syntax doesn't match natural speech, and (2) lines that produce no image when you close your eyes. Williams talks about this in her [Sodajerker episode](https://www.sodajerker.com/) — she rewrites lines until they sound like something she'd actually say. Mann describes the same test as "does it sound like a person." Prine — per his Zollo interview — wrote out loud at his kitchen table, reading drafts to his wife. The diagnostic catches the placeholder lines that survive silent reading. If you cannot picture the line out loud, the listener cannot either.

## Common Failure Modes

Three failure patterns this school warns against. **(1) Abstraction stacks**: three or more abstract emotion words in four consecutive lines. The line *"I feel so lost and so alone and so afraid"* has no image and three identical syntactic moves; the verse is doing zero work. **(2) Borrowed concretes**: writing about *whiskey* when you don't drink whiskey, *trains* when you have never been on one, *Nashville* when you have never been there. Borrowed concretes ring false because the writer can't supply the specific sensory detail that turns a category into an instance. The school's rule: write the concrete nouns you have actually touched. **(3) Concrete decoration without function**: piling specific details that don't serve the song's emotional center. A verse about a kitchen that names every appliance is not a song; it is an inventory. Pattison's stress test for this — the "theme consistency" pass detailed elsewhere in C8 — is: does each concrete noun connect to what the song is actually about? If not, cut the noun even though it is concrete.

## How to Apply at the Workbench

A practical revision pass that combines all of the above, executable in 20–30 minutes on a draft. **(1)** Highlight every abstract emotion word in the draft. Count them. If the count is over three per verse, you have a telling problem. **(2)** For each abstract word, ask what concrete thing produced the feeling — and write the concrete thing in the margin. Often the margin word is the better line. **(3)** Highlight every "to be" verb. For each, ask whether an action verb is available. **(4)** Run the cover-the-context test on every line in the chorus first (the chorus is where lines repeat and earn the most scrutiny). **(5)** Read the whole draft aloud, slowly. Mark the lines you stumble on. **(6)** Pick the three weakest lines and rewrite them concretely, even if the rewrite is worse-sounding at first. **(7)** Sit on the revision overnight, then read again. The goal of this pass is not to make the lyric "good" — it is to find the placeholder lines and replace them with lines that show. The polishing is downstream.
