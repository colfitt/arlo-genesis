## The Principle — The Title Is the Song's Center of Gravity

The Jimmy Webb school of songwriting, codified in [*Tunesmith: Inside the Art of Songwriting*](https://www.amazon.com/Tunesmith-Inside-Songwriting-Jimmy-Webb/dp/0786884886) (Hyperion, 1998), rests on a claim that has organized American popular-song craft for half a century: **the title is the line the listener remembers, the line the refrain repeats, and the line the verses must earn**. Webb's writing career — "By the Time I Get to Phoenix," "Wichita Lineman," "MacArthur Park," "The Highwayman" — is anchored by titles that *are* arguments, that compress the song's situation into a phrase the listener can carry away. Pat Pattison's parallel teaching at Berklee, summarized at [patpattison.com](https://www.patpattison.com/pat-s-lyric-tips), reinforces the discipline from a different angle: Pattison recommends starting with the title whenever possible, because "the quality and flexibility of the title you select will play a major role in the effectiveness of the song." The refrain — the repeated line that contains or implies the title — is therefore the highest-leverage line in the lyric. Earning that line is the central craft problem this file addresses.

## What "Earning the Title" Means

A title is *earned* when the verses make the listener want to hear it. The Webb formulation, paraphrased from *Tunesmith*: by the time the refrain arrives, the listener should feel that the title is inevitable — that the situation the verses set up has only one possible summary, and the title is it. The unearned title is the title that arrives without preparation, that announces a feeling the verses have not shown, that demands an emotional response the song has not built toward. The earned title arrives as release: the listener has been holding the question the verses opened, and the title answers it. The mechanical implication: the verses do not exist to "set up" the chorus in a generic sense; they exist to make this particular title-line necessary. A title that could attach to any verse content is a title doing no work. A title that requires *these* verses is a title that has been earned.

## The Verse-to-Title Setup Mechanic

The setup question every verse must answer: *what does this verse make the listener need to hear the title say?* Webb's "By the Time I Get to Phoenix" is the canonical case study — the verses track a man driving away from a relationship, naming the city he will pass through at each hour ("Phoenix… Albuquerque… Oklahoma"), so when the title-line returns it carries the accumulated weight of distance and time. The verses are not decoration; they are the title's evidence. Pattison teaches the same move with different vocabulary: the verses establish the **essential question** the title will resolve or sharpen. The setup mechanic, executable at the workbench: write the title at the top of the page, then ask of each verse line, *does this line make the title more necessary?* Lines that drift from the title's argument weaken the refrain's arrival. Lines that build toward the title strengthen it. The discipline is not to make every verse line *about* the title; it is to make every verse line *prepare* the title's return.

## Title Position — Front-Loaded vs. Back-Loaded

A specific craft decision Webb, Pattison, and Sheila Davis all teach: **where in the chorus does the title sit?** [Pattison's teaching on phrasing](https://www.patpattison.com/art-of-phrasing) frames this in terms of weight and stability. A title at the front of the chorus (front-loaded — "Sweet Caroline / good times never seemed so good") arrives as declaration; the rest of the chorus elaborates. A title at the back of the chorus (back-loaded — "...by the time I get to Phoenix, she'll be rising") arrives as conclusion; the chorus builds toward it. A title at both the front and the back (bookended — "Hey Jude…na na na…hey Jude") frames the chorus inside the title. Each position implies a different setup. Front-loaded titles need verses that build to the declaration without requiring the chorus to. Back-loaded titles need choruses that earn their own conclusion line. The Webb-trained instinct: choose the position before writing the chorus, because the position determines the rhythmic shape of the chorus, the stress pattern, and the way the verses must hand off.

## Stress and Syllable Fit — The Title Must Sit on the Beat

A specific Pattison-taught discipline from [*Writing Better Lyrics*](https://www.amazon.com/Writing-Better-Lyrics-Pat-Pattison/dp/1582975779): **the title's strongest syllable must land on the strongest beat of the title's bar**. "By the *time* I get to *Phoe*-nix" places "time" and "Phoe-" on downbeats; the natural English stress of the phrase aligns with the musical stress of the bar. A title whose strong syllable falls on a weak beat fights the music; the listener feels the mismatch even without naming it. The workbench test: speak the title in natural English stress, then sing it against the chorus melody. If the spoken stress and the musical stress disagree, one of them must move — usually the melody, sometimes the title's phrasing, occasionally the title itself. The same test applies to syllable count: a title that does not fit the chorus's bar shape either gets crammed (rushed delivery, mumbled syllables) or stretched (held vowels that distort the natural phrase). Webb's "MacArthur Park" famously stretches "park" across multiple beats; the stretch is the craft choice, not an accident.

## The Title-Elsewhere Trick

A specific Webb-and-Pattison teaching: titles do not have to live exclusively in the refrain. The **title-elsewhere** technique places the title's words in the verses — sometimes intact, sometimes partial, sometimes inverted — so the refrain's arrival is recognized rather than introduced. "Wichita Lineman" places "lineman" inside the second verse ("And the Wichita Lineman is still on the line") before the refrain claims it; the listener experiences the title-line as completing something already begun. The opposite move — the title appearing *only* in the refrain — is also valid, but it requires the verses to set up the title without ever touching it directly. Either choice is craft. The trap is the unintended middle: the title-words leaking into verses by accident, weakening the refrain's claim without being deliberately deployed as foreshadowing. Pattison's discipline: decide whether the title appears in the verses; if yes, place it consciously; if no, scrub the verses of title-adjacent language so the refrain's arrival is fresh.

## The Single-Line Refrain vs. The Full Chorus

A structural distinction Pattison teaches across [*Songwriting: Essential Guide to Lyric Form and Structure*](https://books.google.com/books/about/Songwriting_Essential_Guide_to_Lyric_For.html?id=0XESAQAAQBAJ) and his Berklee courses: the **refrain** and the **chorus** are different structural objects. A refrain is a single repeated line (typically containing the title) attached to the end of each verse — the AABA tradition (think "Yesterday," "Something," "Norwegian Wood"). A chorus is a separate harmonic and lyrical section that repeats — the verse-chorus tradition that dominates contemporary pop. Both contain the title; both serve as the title's repeated return. The craft difference: a single-line refrain must do all its work in one line, so the line must be saturated with the song's argument. A chorus has multiple lines, so the title can be earned by the chorus's other lines rather than by the title-line alone. The workbench implication: when revising, identify which structure the draft is using, and judge the title-line against that structure's demands. A refrain-line judged against a chorus's standard will seem too compressed; a chorus-line judged against a refrain's standard will seem under-saturated.

## The Hook Inside the Title

A claim drawn from Switched On Pop's analysis of contemporary pop on the [Switched On Pop podcast](https://switchedonpop.com/episodes) and consistent with Webb's *Tunesmith* on the older tradition: **the strongest titles contain a hook of their own — a sonic, rhythmic, or imagistic compression that makes the title memorable independent of the music**. "Bohemian Rhapsody" hooks on the unexpected pairing of "bohemian" (foreign, exotic) with "rhapsody" (a musical-form word). "Shake It Off" hooks on the rhythmic compression of three monosyllables. "Bad Guy" hooks on the comma between "duh" and the title. The title-as-hook test: does the title, spoken aloud without the melody, have a sonic shape the listener wants to repeat? If yes, the title is doing its own work; the refrain only needs to deliver it. If no, the title needs the melody to carry it, which means the melody must be strong enough to compensate. Both are valid; the craft is knowing which case applies.

## The Repetition-with-Variation Move

A pattern observable across the canon and named explicitly in [Pattison's verse-development teaching](https://www.patpattison.com/developing-verse-ideas): when the title repeats across the song, each repetition should land *slightly differently* because the verse content before it has shifted what the title means. The first chorus of "By the Time I Get to Phoenix" arrives after verse 1 (he's leaving, she's just waking); the second arrives after verse 2 (more time has passed, she's at lunch); the third arrives after verse 3 (night, she's calling). The title-line is identical; the *weight* the listener gives it changes because the context has changed. Pattison's phrase for the effect: "Each refrain takes its color from what it attaches to." The craft move on revision: read the title-line as it appears after verse 1, then after verse 2, then after verse 3 (and bridge if present). If the line feels identical in weight each time, the verses are not doing developmental work; they are restating rather than developing. The fix is at the verses, not the refrain.

## The Title That Earns Itself by the End

A specific Webb-and-Davis move discussed in *Tunesmith* and [Sheila Davis's *The Craft of Lyric Writing*](https://www.amazon.com/Craft-Lyric-Writing-Sheila-Davis/dp/0898792827): the **delayed-meaning title**, where the title's full meaning is not clear at first hearing and only resolves by the last chorus or bridge. The classic case is a title whose surface meaning shifts under the verses — "Wichita Lineman" reads at first as a job description and reveals across the song as a metaphor for distance and longing. The delayed-meaning title trades immediate accessibility for the payoff of recognition on repeat listening. The craft trap: a delayed-meaning title that *never* resolves reads as obscure; a delayed-meaning title that resolves too early loses its power. The Webb-trained instinct: the title's full meaning should arrive in the bridge or final chorus, when the listener has accumulated enough verse content to receive it. The discipline is patience — trusting the listener to hold the question across the verses without spelling out the answer until the song has earned it.

## When the Title Is Wrong — The Diagnostic

The hardest diagnosis on a stalled song: **the title is the problem**. Signals that the title rather than the verses is the issue: the verses are strong but the chorus feels small; the chorus melody is strong but the title-words feel arbitrary; multiple revision passes on the chorus do not lift it; the writer hesitates when asked to say the title aloud. Pattison's diagnostic move, drawn from his "essential question" teaching: try titling the draft with five alternative titles, written in the margin without judging them. If one of them produces a small jolt of recognition — *that's the song* — the original title was wrong and the new one is the song's actual argument. The verses may then need light revision to align with the new title, or none at all. The reverse move is also valid: if the title is right and the verses are wrong, the diagnostic produces the opposite jolt — the alternative titles all feel worse, and the writer realizes the verses have drifted from the title's claim. Either way, the title-vs-verses diagnostic catches misalignment that line-level revision cannot.

## The Last Line Before the Title

A specific craft point Pattison teaches in revision: the **line immediately before the title-line** does disproportionate work. That line is the handoff — it must deliver the listener into the title's stress pattern, harmonic resolution, and emotional register. A weak setup line dampens the title's arrival; a strong setup line makes the title detonate. The workbench test: read the setup line and the title-line as a couplet, ignoring the rest of the chorus. Does the couplet feel like a unit? Does the setup line make the title-line necessary? If the couplet is the strongest two lines of the chorus, the chorus is structurally sound. If the couplet feels weaker than other chorus lines, the title is being undersold by its own handoff. The fix is usually at the setup line, not the title. Webb's craft instinct, evident across his catalog: the line before the title is often the line he revised most, because that line is the title's launchpad.

## When ARLO Should Surface a Refrain Problem

A practical heuristic for the working writer. Signals that refrain-or-title construction is the likely diagnosis. **(1)** "the chorus is flat" — when the chorus's other elements are strong (melody, harmony, arrangement) but the chorus does not land, the title is often the cause. **(2)** "I don't know what to call this song" — the canonical signal that the song's argument has not yet clarified. **(3)** "the title feels generic" — usually a sign the title is summarizing a feeling rather than naming a specific situation. **(4)** "the chorus doesn't feel earned" — covered in `corpus/songwriting/lyric/theme-consistency-checks.md`; the title is usually the focal point of the unearned-chorus diagnosis. **(5)** "the second chorus feels the same as the first" — the refrain is not accumulating weight across the song, usually because the verses are not developing. For each signal, ARLO's first move is the title-as-test pass: ask the user to say the title aloud, then ask whether the verses make that title-line necessary. If the user hesitates on either question, the diagnosis is confirmed.
