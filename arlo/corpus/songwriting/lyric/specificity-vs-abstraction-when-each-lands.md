# Specificity vs Abstraction — When Each Lands

## The Default Rule, Named as a Default

In the Pattison / Davis school — the dominant American craft tradition for English-language songwriting — **specificity beats abstraction roughly 90% of the time in contemporary pop, country, indie, and folk.** This is documented across Pattison's *Writing Better Lyrics* and Sheila Davis's [*The Craft of Lyric Writing*](https://www.amazon.com/Craft-Lyric-Writing-Sheila-Davis/dp/0898792827), and audible in the working practice of Aimee Mann, Lucinda Williams, John Prine, Taylor Swift's later work, and Billie Eilish's lyric craft as analyzed in the [Switched On Pop podcast](https://switchedonpop.com/). The reason — per the [Berklee Online prosody overview](https://online.berklee.edu/takenote/prosody-in-music-and-songwriting/) — is mechanical: concrete sensory detail activates the listener's body and memory; abstractions activate only their dictionary. The default should be specific. **But this is the default, not the universal law.** Some genres and song types reward abstraction. The craft skill is knowing which lever you are pulling on a given line, and why.

## What "Specific" Actually Means

Specificity has three independent axes that often get conflated. **(1) Sensory specificity** — naming what can be seen, heard, smelled, tasted, touched, felt in the body, sensed kinesthetically (the seven senses Pattison enumerates in [*Writing Better Lyrics*](https://www.izotope.com/en/learn/object-writing-your-secret-to-vivid-song-lyrics)). **(2) Particularity** — naming this instance rather than the category: *the green Toyota* rather than *the car*, *Tuesday at 4* rather than *the afternoon*. **(3) Personal-ness** — drawing on the writer's actual experience rather than a generic version of an experience anyone could have. A line can be high on one axis and low on another. *"My heart was on fire"* is sensory (fire, heart) but not particular (anyone's heart, any fire) and not personal (it's a cliché). *"The Camry stalled out at the second light on Westheimer"* is particular and personal but only moderately sensory. The strongest pop lyrics tend to score high on at least two of the three.

## The Exception — When Abstraction Is the Move

Three song-types where abstraction is the correct lever. **(1) Anthem and declaration songs** — *"We Are the World," "We Are the Champions," "Imagine," "I Want to Break Free."* These songs need to scale to stadiums and across cultures; specific detail would narrow the audience. The whole rhetorical move is universality, and abstraction is the tool. **(2) Hymn-mode songs** — gospel, devotional, certain country balladry. The mode requires elevated language and category-level claims. **(3) Mantra and chant** — Sia's *"Chandelier,"* Florence's *"Dog Days Are Over,"* large chunks of dance-pop. The chorus is a repeated abstract phrase because the function is sonic incantation, not narrative. **The rule:** when the song's function is collective affirmation, ceremonial declaration, or chant, abstraction is correct. When the song's function is narrative, confession, or witnessing, specificity is correct. Most pop songs are some mix, which is why the question is per-section rather than per-song.

## The Eilish Case — Specificity at Scale

Billie Eilish's *"Happier Than Ever"* (2021) — analyzed in detail in the [Switched On Pop episode on the song](https://switchedonpop.com/) and in [Sound on Sound's production coverage](https://www.soundonsound.com/) — is a contemporary case study in specificity. The lyric names a specific behavior pattern (*"you call me again, drunk in your Benz, driving home under the influence"*) and a specific complaint (*"you make me hate this city"*). The song scaled to a worldwide hit while keeping its specifics intact. The Switched On Pop hosts cite this as evidence that the streaming-era pop audience tolerates — and rewards — concrete confessional detail at the same scale that 1980s pop reserved for abstraction. Eilish and brother / co-writer Finneas O'Connell consistently take the specific noun: *"three AM,"* *"the bad guy,"* *"my strange addiction."* The lesson is not that all pop should sound like Eilish, but that the "abstraction sells better at scale" assumption is empirically wrong as of 2026.

## The Swift Case — Named Objects on *folklore* / *evermore*

Taylor Swift's pivot on the 2020 *folklore* and *evermore* records — discussed across the [Switched On Pop *folklore* episode](https://switchedonpop.com/) and the [Pitchfork *folklore* review](https://pitchfork.com/reviews/albums/taylor-swift-folklore/) — was a deliberate move from pop's abstract emotional vocabulary into named characters, places, and objects. *"The old cardigan under someone's bed."* *"James, Inez, Betty."* *"The Cornelia Street townhouse."* *"The 1830s without all the racists."* The records were her most critically acclaimed. The Switched On Pop analysis ties this to a broader 2018–2024 trend in mainstream pop: detail returned as a marker of artistic seriousness. ARLO should not generalize this into "specifics always sell" — Swift's pre-*folklore* hits were largely abstract and also sold massively. The honest framing: specificity is no longer a commercial liability the way Brill Building–era producers sometimes believed it was, and for a writer drawn to it, the cultural moment is supportive.

## The Universal-Via-Specific Principle

Pattison's central reframe, recorded in [the Sound on Sound interview](https://www.soundonsound.com/techniques/pat-pattison-writing-better-lyrics): **the universal is reached through the specific, not by skipping past it.** Naming *your* one detail is what makes the listener feel *theirs*. The mechanism is associative — your "screen door slamming on a Sunday afternoon in August" is not a screen door the listener has ever heard, but it activates the listener's stored memory of *their* screen door, *their* August. An abstraction ("summer made me sad") gives them no anchor point, so they recall nothing. This is why John Prine's Muhlenberg County feels like every reader's hometown; why Springsteen's specific New Jersey reads as every listener's town. The principle is paradoxical and load-bearing: **to write for everyone, write for one person, including one detail nobody else has.**

## Cliché as Failed Abstraction

A useful diagnostic from Sheila Davis's [*The Craft of Lyric Writing*](https://www.amazon.com/Craft-Lyric-Writing-Sheila-Davis/dp/0898792827), echoed in Pattison's revision chapters: **most clichés are not failed images — they are failed abstractions.** *"My heart is broken."* *"Walking through the rain."* *"Love is a battlefield."* These were once concrete metaphors that lost their concreteness through repetition. The brain processes them as abstractions because the picture has worn off. The revision move: identify the cliché, ask what concrete thing the cliché was originally trying to say, and write the concrete thing instead. *"My heart is broken"* → *"I dropped the plate she gave me and didn't pick up the pieces."* The replacement may not always be better, but the diagnostic flags lines that are doing zero work.

## The "Cut the Proper Noun" Test

A working revision pass: **cover the proper nouns in the draft and read the lyric.** If the lyric still means something, the proper nouns are decoration. If the lyric collapses, the proper nouns are doing structural work. The test is from the lyric-craft tradition of editing for compactness — credited in Davis and audible in any successful pop lyric. Two outcomes are informative. **(1)** If the song still works without the place name (you can swap *"Tupelo"* for *"my hometown"* with no loss), the proper noun was added for color but is not earning its keep. Cut or replace it. **(2)** If the song collapses without the proper noun, you have a song whose meaning is structurally place-based. *"Born in the U.S.A."* without the U.S.A. is a different song. *"Empire State of Mind"* without "New York" doesn't exist. Both outcomes are fine — the test is meant to surface what your song is actually about.

## Genre and Era Boundedness

Where this question lands varies. **Folk and country (Williams, Prine, Isbell)** reward extreme specificity; the genre's whole rhetorical pose is witnessing. **Indie / alternative (Mann, Sufjan, The National)** reward specificity with literary flair. **Mainstream pop pre-2015** tolerated more abstraction; post-2015 mainstream pop has trended specific (Eilish, Olivia Rodrigo, Swift's later work). **Hip-hop** has always been specific — *names, brands, blocks, dates* — per the analysis in [Paul Edwards' *How to Rap*](https://www.amazon.com/How-Rap-Art-Science-Hip-Hop/dp/1556528167). **Anthemic stadium rock (U2, Coldplay)** still rewards abstraction because the function is collective. **Dance-pop and EDM vocal hooks** reward chant-mode abstraction because the function is communal repetition. **Hymn, gospel, and certain country balladry** require elevated abstract language as a genre marker. The lever-pull decision is genre-aware. ARLO should retrieve this nuance when the user is writing in a tradition that breaks the default.

## When the Whole Song Is One Image

A craft move that splits the difference: **the song states an abstract thesis and proves it with one extended concrete image.** Joni Mitchell's *"Both Sides Now"* states the abstraction *"I really don't know love at all"* but spends the verses on clouds, circus rides, fairy tales. Leonard Cohen's *"Hallelujah"* states a religious abstraction in the chorus and proves it through King David and Bathsheba in the verse. Bob Dylan's *"Like a Rolling Stone"* states a generalized accusation in the chorus but anchors the verses in a specific scene (*Miss Lonely*, the mystery tramp, Napoleon in rags). This is the most-resilient pop song architecture in the canon, partly because it gives the listener both: the abstraction to remember and the specifics to feel. The lesson for revision: if your verses are abstract and your chorus is abstract, the song has no anchor. Move at least one to the concrete.

## The Workbench Decision Procedure

A working procedure for the per-section choice. **(1) Name the song's function** — narrative, confession, witnessing, anthem, declaration, mantra, hymn. **(2) The default is specific** unless the function is one of the abstract-leaning categories. **(3) Verses almost always reward more specificity than the chorus** because verses do narrative work; choruses do hook work. **(4) The bridge is the place to introduce an abstract conclusion** if the verses have earned it. **(5) When in doubt, write the specific version first; the abstraction is the safer rewrite, not the bolder one.** **(6) Run the "cut the proper noun" test and the cover-the-context test (from C1)** to surface the lines that are leaning on abstraction by default rather than by choice. **(7) Be honest about scale-of-image** — a song with one named brand can land as confessional; a song with five named brands lands as inventory. Calibrate per song. The point of the procedure is to make the specificity-vs-abstraction choice a deliberate craft move per section, not a default reflex.

## Common Failure Modes

Three patterns. **(1) Default-abstract-everywhere** — the writer leans on "I feel," "I want," "I need," "love," "heart," "alone" because those words are easier to find rhymes for. The song reads as generic. **(2) Default-specific-everywhere** — the writer lists detail after detail without an emotional through-line. The song reads as inventory. **(3) Mismatched section function** — abstract verses and concrete chorus, where the architecture should usually be the reverse (concrete verses build to an abstract or anthemic chorus). This last failure is the most-common in earnest indie songwriting: the writer fills the verses with feeling-words and saves the one concrete image for the chorus, where it gets repeated to death. The fix: redistribute. Put the concrete image into the verse where it can do narrative work; let the chorus do its repetition job.
