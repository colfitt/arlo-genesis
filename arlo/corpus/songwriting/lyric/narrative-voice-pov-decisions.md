## Why POV Is a Craft Decision, Not a Default

The general-corpus file `corpus/lyrics/point-of-view-tense-and-narrator.md` covers the vocabulary — first person, second person, third person, direct address — at the level of definition. This file goes deeper into the *decision*: when each POV is the right move for a song, and what the cost is of switching. Working songwriters across the canonical books (Pattison's *Writing Better Lyrics*, Sheila Davis's *The Craft of Lyric Writing*, and Paul Zollo's *Songwriters on Songwriting* interviews collected over four decades) agree on one thing: **POV is not the default you slipped into; it is the choice you made.** Most first-draft lyrics drift into first person because the writer's emotion is driving the page. That's fine as a starting position; it is not fine as the final POV unless the writer has interrogated the choice. The craft move is to ask, after the first draft, whether the song would be more honest, more interesting, or more emotionally precise in a different POV. The decision is genre-bound — pop's default is first-person-confessing-to-direct-address-"you"; country's default is more often third-person narrative; hip-hop's is first-person-as-self; folk's can be any of the four — so the named decision is always genre-aware.

## First Person — The Confession Mode

First-person lyric — "I walked," "I saw," "I want" — is the default emotional mode of contemporary popular songwriting. Its strength is intimacy: the listener stands inside the speaker's experience, and identification is automatic. The Pattison framing across his Berklee lectures and *Writing Better Lyrics*: first person is the easiest POV to start in and the easiest to misuse, because the writer's actual emotion can substitute for craft. The trap is *unearned confession*: the first-person speaker reports feelings the song hasn't built. The fix isn't to switch POV; it's to remember that first person doesn't excuse the writer from the concrete-noun discipline. Joni Mitchell's *Blue* is first person and brutally specific. Adele's *21* is first person and concrete in its grief. The first-person POV doesn't make the lyric work; concrete detail under the first person does. Use it when the song's emotional center genuinely belongs to one speaker and the speaker is willing to be specific.

## Second Person — The "You" Question

"You" is the most flexible and most ambiguous pronoun in lyric craft. As Pattison's [Sound on Sound interview](https://www.soundonsound.com/techniques/pat-pattison-writing-better-lyrics) details and Sheila Davis's *The Craft of Lyric Writing* makes structural, **"you" can mean at least four different people**: a specific lover, the listener directly, the writer's own self (the second-person-as-self move), or a higher power. The craft decision is *which "you"* the song means, and how soon the listener learns it. A lyric that addresses "you" without the listener ever knowing whether it's a partner, the audience, the self, or God reads as evasive. A lyric that establishes the "you" early — through a concrete detail that fixes who is being addressed — has access to one of the most intimate POVs available. Adele's "Someone Like You" addresses an ex-partner from the first line. Taylor Swift's "the 1" addresses a specific past relationship within four bars. The discipline: decide who "you" is, and let the listener know within the first verse.

## Third Person — The Story Mode

Third person — "she walked," "he saw," "they want" — is the narrative POV. It is the default of the country story-song tradition (Tom T. Hall, Bobbie Gentry's "Ode to Billie Joe," Jimmy Webb's narrative work, Kacey Musgraves' character-driven verses) and recurs throughout folk, indie, and a small but persistent strain of pop. Its strength is distance: the listener watches the events from outside, which allows the song to deliver plot the listener cannot get in first person. The cost is intimacy: third person makes identification harder, so the song has to earn it through specificity and detail rather than through the autobiographical contract first person assumes. Sheila Davis's *Craft of Lyric Writing* (the standard American textbook on POV decisions) treats third person as the POV that requires the most concrete-noun discipline, because the writer cannot lean on "I feel" to do emotional work — every feeling must be inferred from the action. Use third person when the song needs to deliver a story whose meaning emerges from events, not feelings.

## Direct Address — The Letter Mode

Direct address is the POV where the speaker speaks *to* a specific named or implied addressee for the duration of the song, often without a return to neutral narration. The clearest contemporary example is **Nick Cave's letter method** (covered in `corpus/songwriting/workflows/nick-cave-letter-method.md`): write the song as a letter to a real person, and let the song hide inside the letter. Direct address has emotional access that first person alone cannot reach because the listener overhears one person speaking to another. The risk is exclusion: if the addressee is too private — if the listener can't reach the relationship from outside — the lyric becomes a closed conversation. The discipline is the same as the second-person "you" question: the listener needs enough information about who is being addressed to feel the address land. Direct-address songs that work — Cave's, Tom Waits's "Christmas Card from a Hooker in Minneapolis," Joni's "A Case of You" — give the listener the relationship in concrete strokes before the address begins doing its work.

## The Randy Newman School — The Untrustworthy Narrator

Randy Newman's POV move is the high-craft case: **first person from a character who is not the writer**. In Paul Zollo's [*Conversations with Randy Newman*](https://www.amazon.com/Conversations-Randy-Newman-Paul-Zollo/dp/0306823306) and his earlier Newman interviews in *Songwriters on Songwriting*, Newman explains the technique directly. As summarized in the [PopMatters analysis "Randy Newman and the Magic of the Unreliable Narrator"](https://www.popmatters.com/randy-newman-and-the-magic-of-the-unreliable-narrator-2495389438.html), Newman's catalog is built on first-person narrators who are bigots, fools, slave traders, predators, and adolescent boys — none of them Newman himself. Newman's own framing (recorded in [Bluerailroad's long interview](https://bluerailroad.wordpress.com/randy-newman-the-bluerailroad-interview/)): "The people in my songs are generally exaggerations; what they say and think is colored by who they are. They are sometimes liars and braggarts and puffed-up people, but I wouldn't sing about them unless I cared about them." The craft cost: every misreading — every listener who hears "Short People" as a sincere endorsement of its narrator — is the price of admission. The technique requires the listener to be able to tell that the narrator is not the writer, which is its hardest demand.

## The Father John Misty School — Persona as Editorial Distance

Josh Tillman's Father John Misty project is a contemporary variant of the untrustworthy-narrator move. In his [NME cover interview around *Pure Comedy*](https://www.nme.com/features/father-john-misty-interview-pure-comedy-2017-2045392) and his [Huck conversation](https://www.huckmag.com/article/father-john-misty-pure-comedy-interview), Tillman describes the persona as a license for editorial distance: Father John Misty can say things Josh Tillman cannot, because the persona is a layer of irony separating the writer from the speaker. His own framing — wanting to be "authentically bogus rather than bogusly authentic" — captures the move: the persona is honest *about* being a performance, which is different from a sincere first-person confession. The risk Tillman has had to manage publicly is the same risk Newman manages: listeners who flatten the persona and treat the lyric as straight first-person testimony. The craft lesson for working songwriters: a persona POV is most legible when the writer has built enough public irony around the persona that the listener arrives already knowing not to flatten it. Without that scaffolding, persona-POV reads as the writer's own sincere speech, which can be a serious misfire when the persona is intentionally repulsive.

## Tense Decisions — Past, Present, Future

POV is inseparable from tense. Past tense — "I walked," "she sang" — is the reflective mode; the speaker is remembering or recounting, which lets the lyric do interpretive work the events themselves couldn't. Present tense — "I walk," "she sings" — is the immediate mode; the speaker is inside the moment, which forces a kind of breathless intimacy and disallows the interpretive distance past tense permits. Future tense — "I will," "she'll" — is the anticipatory mode; the speaker is projecting forward, which fits longing, threat, or promise but rarely sustains a full lyric on its own. Pattison's tense discipline, drawn from his lyric-form teaching: choose one tense as the song's spine, and switch only with intention. Joanna Newsom's *Ys* — discussed in her [Wire interview](https://www.thewire.co.uk/in-writing/interviews/joanna-newsom.1) — uses present tense throughout to force real-time narration ("I'm narrating it as it is happening"), which is a deliberate craft constraint. A song that drifts between past and present without intention reads as confused; a song that uses both with intention can do work neither tense alone can do.

## When to Switch POV Mid-Song (And When Not To)

POV switches mid-song are powerful and dangerous. The legitimate uses: a verse-to-chorus switch from third person (story) to first person (feeling) — the bridge of a song that pulls the camera in from observation to confession. A second-verse-to-bridge switch from first person to second person — the speaker turning to address someone they have been describing. A direct-address song that breaks into third person for a single line — the speaker stepping outside the conversation to comment on it. The illegitimate uses: a switch the writer didn't notice, where the lyric drifts from "I" to "you" to "she" because the writer's attention slipped. The Pattison test: can you justify each POV change? If you can't name what the change does emotionally, it is a slip, not a craft move. Slips are the most common POV problem in first-draft lyrics, and the cover-the-context revision pass catches them: read each line cold and ask whether the POV matches what the line before and after established.

## The Direct-Address-"You" as Pop's Default

Pop's modal POV in 2020s is first-person-confessing-to-direct-address-"you" — the speaker says "I" and addresses "you" with no other characters in the song. Olivia Rodrigo's catalog, much of Taylor Swift's recent work, Billie Eilish's confessional registers, and most contemporary pop choruses sit in this POV configuration. The reason is mechanical: the listener can occupy the "I" (identification) and project anyone they want into the "you" (universalization), so the song fits the listener's life rather than locking them out of someone else's. The cost — flagged by Pattison and Sheila Davis both — is sameness: when every pop lyric is the same POV configuration, the genre's voices flatten into one voice. The craft move for a writer working in pop: use the default when the song wants the universalizing access it provides; choose against the default when the song wants something the default can't give. A pop song in third person is rare and often memorable for being so.

## The Unintended POV Slip — The Most Common Lyric Bug

The single most common POV problem in first-draft lyrics is the **unintended slip**: a verse that starts in first person, drifts into addressing "you" without establishing who, and ends in a third-person observation the speaker had no business making. The fix is a structural pass: in revision, mark each line's POV (1, 2, 3, or DA) in the margin, then scan the marks. If the column reads "1, 1, 2, 1, 1, 2, 3, 1," the song has at least two slips. The Pattison revision discipline, paraphrased from his teaching: pick one POV as the song's spine, and force every line to that POV unless the line is doing specific work the spine cannot do. The diagnostic catches slips that silent reading misses, because the writer's ear is fluent in their own voice and won't notice the slide. Working writers across Zollo's interview archive describe the same pass — Joni Mitchell, John Prine, Lucinda Williams all mention some variant of "reading for the I and the you" as a revision step.

## When ARLO Should Surface POV as the Issue

A practical heuristic for ARLO and the working writer. The signals that POV is the problem in a stalled draft: **(1)** the lyric "feels" wandering or unfocused, but each individual line is fine. Likely an unintended POV slip — diagnose with the column-of-margin-marks pass above. **(2)** the song is emotional but not landing; the speaker says "I feel" but the listener doesn't. Likely a missing-concrete-noun problem in first person — but consider whether *third person from a character witnessing the speaker* would let the emotion land harder by giving it observable specificity. **(3)** the song addresses "you" and the listener can't tell who. Likely a "who is 'you'" problem — name the addressee earlier with a concrete identifier. **(4)** the song is in first person but the speaker is hard to like and the listener is bouncing off. Consider whether the song is actually a Newman/Misty-style character POV the writer hasn't yet acknowledged. Reframing the lyric as "a character is saying this" rather than "I am saying this" can unlock a stuck draft. **(5)** the lyric keeps coming out universal and vague. Try Nick Cave's letter method — switch to direct address to a specific person — and see if the lyric finds its specificity.
