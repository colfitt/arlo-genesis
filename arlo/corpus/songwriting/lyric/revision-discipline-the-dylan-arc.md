## The Premise — Revision Is the Craft

The amateur's belief about songwriting is that the song arrives in a flash and either works or doesn't. The Pattison / working-songwriter consensus is the opposite: **the first draft is the seed; the revision is the craft.** Pattison repeats this across *Writing Better Lyrics* and Berklee lectures, as recorded in the [Sound on Sound interview](https://www.soundonsound.com/techniques/pat-pattison-writing-better-lyrics). The myth of the spontaneous masterpiece is exactly that — a myth — and the working canon of revision-heavy songwriters confirms it: Dylan, Cohen, Joni Mitchell, Springsteen, Sufjan, Aimee Mann, Lucinda Williams, Phoebe Bridgers. Each has multiple-draft archives or interview testimony about months- or years-long revision arcs on songs the public hears as effortless. This file is named for Dylan because his revision archive is the most extensively catalogued and verifiable, but the discipline he embodies is general. The mission-relevance for ARLO: a stuck musician often believes "the inspiration phase is over and I can't fix this." The truth is that the fixing phase is where the song actually gets made.

## The Bob Dylan Archive and What It Documents

The [Bob Dylan Archive](https://www.utulsa.edu/academics/colleges/oxley-college-of-health-natural-sciences/our-departments/center-for-american-research/bob-dylan-archive/) at the University of Tulsa's Helmerich Center for American Research, opened to scholars in 2016 after Dylan sold his papers to the George Kaiser Family Foundation, contains roughly **6,000 items including notebooks, draft lyrics, session tapes, and correspondence**. Scholars including Clinton Heylin and Sean Wilentz have published partial accounts of what the archive contains. The headline finding, repeated across the [New York Times coverage](https://www.nytimes.com/2016/03/02/arts/music/bob-dylan-archive-tulsa-oklahoma.html) and the [Wall Street Journal feature](https://www.wsj.com/articles/bob-dylan-archive-opens-1456891202) at the archive's opening: **the same Dylan song often exists in five or more drafted versions**, with verses written, cut, recombined, and rewritten across notebooks and across sessions. The archive corrects the romantic myth: songs like *"Like a Rolling Stone,"* *"Tangled Up in Blue,"* and *"Idiot Wind"* arrived through documented multi-stage revision processes, not single inspired sittings.

## The *Tangled Up in Blue* Case

The most-discussed Dylan revision artifact is *"Tangled Up in Blue."* Dylan recorded the song for *Blood on the Tracks* (1975) in September 1974, then [scrapped most of the album and re-recorded in Minneapolis in December](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Blood_on_the_Tracks). The Minneapolis version is the one released. He then continued to revise the lyric in performance, **substantially rewriting verses for the 1984 *Real Live* version**, swapping point of view (third-person to first-person and back), reordering events, and changing key images. Per Wilentz's *Bob Dylan in America* and the [Pitchfork *Blood on the Tracks* feature](https://pitchfork.com/features/article/9999-bob-dylan-blood-on-the-tracks/), the song exists in at least four distinct lyric versions Dylan has performed publicly, plus the notebook drafts in the Tulsa archive. The lesson: a song the listener thinks of as fixed is, for the working writer, a movable target for as long as they keep playing it. Revision does not end at release.

## What Dylan Says About His Own Process

Dylan's published statements about revision are characteristically oblique but consistent. In Paul Zollo's [*Songwriters on Songwriting*](https://www.amazon.com/Songwriters-Songwriting-Revised-Paul-Zollo/dp/030680777X) interview — the most-cited primary source on Dylan's process — Dylan describes writing as a process of "uncovering" lines that already exist, then "putting them in the right order." Cameron Crowe's [*Biograph* liner notes](https://www.bobdylan.com/albums/biograph/) from 1985 contain Dylan's own commentary on song after song, repeatedly noting that finished songs began as fragments, were set aside, and were later combined with unrelated fragments to find their form. Dylan's *Chronicles, Volume One* (2004) describes notebooks full of half-songs awaiting their other half. The pattern across primary sources: **Dylan treats the notebook as a reservoir, not a draft.** Songs assemble from accumulated fragments through revision rather than springing whole from a single sitting.

## The "Every Line Earns Its Place" Rule

Pattison's working revision principle, repeated in *Writing Better Lyrics* and audible across the Dylan archive: **each line must earn its place in the song.** The test is not "is the line good in isolation" but "does the line do work this song needs done?" A line can be brilliant and still need to be cut because it does not serve the song the writer is actually making. Pattison's diagnostic — paraphrased in the [Berklee Online prosody article](https://online.berklee.edu/takenote/prosody-in-music-and-songwriting/): for each line, ask: (1) what does this line tell the listener that the previous line did not? (2) what does this line set up that the next line needs? (3) if I cut this line, does the song collapse, or does it tighten? Lines that fail all three should be cut. The Dylan archive contains dozens of cases of brilliant fragments that did not survive into the released versions because they did not earn their place in the final structure.

## The Line-by-Line Pass

The most-cited Pattison revision technique, named in [*Writing Better Lyrics*](https://www.amazon.com/Writing-Better-Lyrics-Pat-Pattison/dp/1582975779) and elaborated across Berklee lectures: the **line-by-line pass** is the smallest unit of revision and the most rigorous. The writer takes one line at a time and asks: (1) is the concrete-noun and action-verb count high enough (see C1)? (2) is the line's rhyme type matching the section function (see C4)? (3) does the line's syllable count and stress pattern match the prosodic demand of the section (see C5)? (4) is this the strongest available verb? (5) is there a stronger noun? (6) is this the most-economical phrasing or are there filler words ("just," "really," "kind of")? The pass is slow, deliberate, and exhausting. It is also the difference between a draft and a song. Pattison teaches that one line-by-line pass per draft is non-negotiable.

## The Verse-by-Verse Pass

The line-by-line pass operates at the smallest scale; the **verse-by-verse pass** operates at the song-architecture scale. The question per verse: **does this verse develop the previous one, or does it merely restate it?** A second verse that recapitulates the first is the most-common amateur failure (covered in detail in stall file B2). Pattison's rule, paraphrased from Berklee teaching: each verse should advance the song's narrative, emotional, or sensory progression by at least one substantial step. The diagnostic: write a one-sentence summary of each verse. If the summaries are interchangeable, the verses are not earning their separation. The Dylan archive contains evidence of this exact pass — verses written, judged redundant against neighbors, and replaced with verses that advance the song. The pass requires zooming out from the line and asking what each chunk is contributing.

## Read Aloud, Mark the False Notes

A move shared across Pattison's teaching, Lucinda Williams's [Sodajerker interview](https://www.sodajerker.com/), Aimee Mann's working practice, and Dylan's own kitchen-table revision habit per multiple biographers: **read the draft aloud, slowly, and mark every false note.** A false note is a line that the writer stumbles on, that does not sound like something a person would say, that breaks the song's tonal frame, or that the writer is faintly embarrassed to read out loud. The body knows before the conscious mind does. The pen marks the line; the writer does not stop to fix it; the read-through continues. After the full read, the marked lines get rewritten. The discipline is not to over-think during the read-through — the goal is to surface the false notes, not to immediately solve them. The body's flinch is the diagnostic; the rewriting is downstream.

## The Trap of Over-Revision

Pattison warns about this explicitly in *Writing Better Lyrics*, and every working songwriter who revises heavily reports the same trap. **A draft can be revised until the life is out of it.** The signs: the lines are technically perfect but feel airless; the emotional intent has been polished off; the song reads as competent but inert. The cause is usually a draft revised too many times in a single sitting, where each pass strips a layer of rawness that the song needed. The countermove, per Pattison and per the working consensus: **time between passes**. Revise. Set the draft down for at least a day, ideally a week. Revise again. The intervening time gives the writer fresh ears and prevents the cumulative over-polishing that destroys a draft. Aimee Mann reports similar discipline in [her Sodajerker episode](https://www.sodajerker.com/) — sitting on drafts for weeks before deciding what to cut.

## The Sit-and-Return Discipline

A practical schedule. **(1) First draft session** — write to completion, do not revise within the session. **(2) Wait at least 24 hours.** **(3) Second session** — read aloud, mark false notes, do not yet rewrite. **(4) Wait again, ideally a few days.** **(5) Third session** — line-by-line pass, fixing the false notes. **(6) Wait a week.** **(7) Fourth session** — verse-by-verse pass, asking whether each verse develops the previous. **(8) Wait again.** **(9) Read aloud cold, with fresh ears, and ask: would a stranger think this is one song?** The wait times are doing real work — the writer's ear refreshes between passes, and lines that seemed essential in pass 1 reveal themselves as filler in pass 4. This is the discipline the Dylan archive documents: songs treated as objects worth returning to, not single sittings to be defended. Lucinda Williams famously sat on lyrics for years; her perfectionism is documented in [her *Car Wheels on a Gravel Road* sessions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Car_Wheels_on_a_Gravel_Road), which she re-recorded multiple times across a six-year process.

## Common Failure Modes

Three patterns this discipline corrects. **(1) Single-pass syndrome** — the writer drafts once, calls it done, and is surprised when the song doesn't connect with listeners. The fix is the multi-pass schedule above. **(2) Revision-as-defense** — the writer revises only to remove lines they're embarrassed by, not to make the song stronger. The fix is reframing: revision is offensive (the song is improving), not defensive (the writer is protecting their reputation). **(3) Revision-until-dead** — the writer revises so many times in such close succession that the song's life is polished off. The fix is the time-between-passes discipline. The Dylan archive contains evidence of all three failures in early-career notebooks, and evidence of mature practice that avoids them. Revision is not the opposite of inspiration; it is the discipline that lets inspiration's first products mature into songs.

## When to Stop Revising

The practical question every revising songwriter eventually faces. **Three honest signals that a draft is done.** (1) The line-by-line pass produces no new changes — every change you try makes the song worse, not better. (2) The verse-by-verse pass shows each verse advancing the previous, with no redundancies. (3) The aloud-read produces no flinches. **If all three are clear, ship it.** The reverse — refusing to ship a draft that passes all three because the writer believes there must be a better version somewhere — is the demo trap (stall file B4). The working consensus across Pattison, Mann, Williams, and the Dylan-via-biographer accounts: at some point, the song is done, and continuing to revise is now harming, not helping. Recognizing that point is itself a craft skill. For ARLO's purposes, the discipline of revision and the discipline of shipping are two halves of the same finishing practice.
