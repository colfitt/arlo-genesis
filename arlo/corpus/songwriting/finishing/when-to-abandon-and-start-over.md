# When to Abandon and Start Over

**Scope:** songwriting
**Source:** Deep-research synthesis (2026-05) — Songwriting Craft — verify before treating as authoritative
**Canonical references:** Steven Pressfield, *The War of Art* (Resistance); Dennis DeSantis, *Making Music*; Ira Glass, taste-gap / volume argument; Rick Rubin, *The Creative Act* (volume / subtraction)
**Tags:** songwriting-craft, finishing-songs, finishing-psychology, abandonment, graveyard-folder, partial-rescue

---

## The premise: abandonment is a craft decision, not a failure

Abandoning a song feels like quitting, so writers avoid the decision — they leave the song half-open, returning to it without progress, neither finishing nor abandoning. That limbo is more costly than a clean abandon, because it occupies the writer's attention and blocks the next song. This file is the *framework* for the abandon decision — the thinking that produces a clean call. The *playbook* — the move-by-move recovery ARLO reaches for at the workbench — lives in `corpus/songwriting/stalls/abandoning-vs-persisting.md` (B8); this file supplies the reasoning B8 operationalizes. The core reframe: abandonment is a normal, healthy part of a productive practice. Most songs are sketches; only some are songs (Rubin, Glass). A writer who never abandons anything is not more committed — they are more stuck.

## The two opposed truths the decision sits between

The reason the call is hard is that two legitimate schools point in opposite directions, and both are right in different cases.

**The persistence school** (Pressfield, DeSantis, most working songwriters): finishing is itself a skill, and abandoning cheaply trains you to quit at the first resistance. Pressfield's *The War of Art* names the danger precisely — Resistance whispers "this isn't working, start something new" exactly when the work is closest to breaking through, and the more a project matters the louder that whisper [(Pressfield summary, Calvin Rosser)](https://calvinrosser.com/notes/war-of-art-steven-pressfield/). The persistence school says: finish ugly, ship ugly, build the muscle.

**The volume school** (Rubin, Glass, the Guided By Voices / Robert Pollard ethos): writers who produce a lot improve faster than writers who labor over single pieces, and abandoning the sketches frees time for the real songs. Glass's taste gap closes across *many* finished works, not within one perfected one [(Ira Glass, via The Marginalian)](https://www.themarginalian.org/2014/01/29/ira-glass-success-daniel-sax/). The volume school says: cut your losses, the next song is cheaper than the rescue.

A framework is needed precisely because mood cannot adjudicate this — the call gets made after a bad session when "this is bad" and "I'm bad" both feel true, which is the worst possible condition for a reliable decision.

## The first discriminator: Resistance or genuine dead-end?

Before any abandon decision, run Pressfield's test, because the most common error is abandoning out of fear and calling it judgment. **Resistance is strongest on the work that matters most** [(Pressfield, Calvin Rosser)](https://calvinrosser.com/notes/war-of-art-steven-pressfield/). So a sudden conviction that a song you loved last week is worthless is *suspicious* — it may be fear of finishing wearing the mask of quality assessment. The discriminating moves: don't decide at the end of a bad session; sleep on it; listen fresh on a different day. If the song still feels dead when you're rested and the session anxiety is gone, the assessment is more trustworthy. If it only feels dead when you sit down to do the hard part, you're looking at Resistance (F1, Category 1), and the move is to push, not abandon.

## The core-idea test (the central framework)

The single most useful framework — the one B8's Move 1 operationalizes — is the **core-idea test**: is there *one* concrete element in this song you are genuinely proud of? Not the whole song. One piece — a hook, a line, a chord move, an image, a vocal moment — that you would defend if a stranger questioned it.

- **If yes:** finish around it. The core idea is the spine; the unfinished song probably buried the spine under non-spine material. The move is to strip to the core and rebuild outward, not to abandon. A song with a strong core is worth persisting on.
- **If no:** this is likely a sketch, not a song, and abandonment is the honest call. The absence of any element you're proud of is the signal that the piece was an exercise more than a song — valuable as practice, not owed a completion.

This test works because it converts the unanswerable "is this good?" into the answerable "is there one thing here I'm proud of?" — and it routes cleanly: pride → persist, no pride → abandon.

## The opportunity-cost frame

The decision is never abandon-in-a-vacuum; it is *abandon this vs. spend the same hours on a fresh one*. Your time is the binding constraint, so the real question is: would finishing this song be a better use of the next N hours than starting something new? DeSantis's pragmatism and the volume school both point the same way — if the rescue would cost more than the song is worth, and a fresh start is likely to produce more, abandon is the rational call, not the cowardly one. B8's Move 2 (the "what would it cost to finish" estimate) and Move 5 (opportunity cost) operationalize this. The framework's value is that it makes abandonment a *comparison* rather than a *verdict* — you're not declaring the song bad, you're allocating your limited time to its best use.

## The "next concrete move" test

A fast diagnostic that distinguishes stuck from done: **can you name the next concrete move?** If you can ("rewrite the second verse," "find a better chorus melody," "cut the bridge"), then you are not actually stuck — you are avoiding a known task, and the move is to do it. If you genuinely cannot name any next move — if the song has no identifiable problem to solve and no identifiable thing to add — you may not be stuck at all; you may be *done*, either done-and-finished or done-as-far-as-this-sketch-goes. B8's Move 3 runs this test. The framework point: "I don't know what to do next" splits into two very different states (avoidance of a known task vs. genuine exhaustion of the material), and the abandon decision depends on which one you're in.

## The graveyard-folder practice

The practice that makes abandonment *safe* — and therefore makes the decision easier — is the **graveyard folder**: archive, don't delete. When you abandon a song, move it to a dedicated folder of parked songs rather than trashing it. This matters for three reasons. First, it lowers the emotional stakes of the decision — "archiving" is reversible in a way "deleting" is not, so the writer makes the call instead of avoiding it. Second, abandoned songs come back: a verse that didn't work in 2024 becomes the bridge of a 2026 song; a chorus orphaned from a dead song finds a new home. Third, the graveyard is a *parts library* — the core-idea element you were proud of survives the song's death and can be transplanted. The discipline: when you abandon, write one line in the file noting what was good about it (the salvageable core), so future-you can find the part without re-listening to the whole.

## The partial-rescue option (abandon the song, keep the part)

Abandonment is not binary. The most underused move is the **partial rescue**: abandon the *song* but extract the part worth keeping. This is the bridge between the two schools — you honor the volume school by cutting the losing song, and you honor the persistence school by not throwing away the core idea you were proud of. The operational moves: bounce or notate the keeper element (the hook, the chord move, the lyric couplet, the eight bars that worked) into the graveyard folder tagged for reuse; then start fresh. The partial rescue dissolves the false choice between "finish this whole song I don't believe in" and "lose everything." Most abandoned songs contain one or two transplantable organs. Harvest them.

## Abandoning the take vs. abandoning the song

A distinction that prevents over-abandoning: sometimes what needs to die is the *take* or the *production*, not the *song*. A song can be strong while a particular demo of it is unsalvageable — the wrong key, the wrong tempo, a production you committed to too early (the "production lock-in" trap). The move there is not to graveyard the song but to **start the production over** with the song intact: same words and melody, blank session. This is adjacent to the finish-ugly / break-the-demo workflows (F3; `corpus/songwriting/workflows/waits-break-the-demo.md`). The framework point: before abandoning a song, check whether you're actually trying to abandon a *bad recording of a good song* — in which case keep the song and abandon the session.

## How ARLO should use this framework

When a user asks "should I keep going or scrap this?" ARLO's job is to run the framework, not to give an opinion on the song it hasn't heard. The sequence: (1) Resistance check — *"are you deciding this at the end of a frustrating session? sleep on it and tell me tomorrow"*; (2) core-idea test — *"is there one element in this song you're proud of? not the whole thing, one piece"*; (3) next-move test — *"can you name the next concrete thing to do? if yes, that's not stuck, that's the task"*; (4) opportunity cost — *"would the hours to finish this beat the hours to start fresh?"*; (5) if abandoning, *graveyard it, don't delete*, and *harvest the keeper part first*. ARLO should surface that the two schools disagree and that the framework is how to choose between them for *this* song — not pretend there's a universal answer. The deeper move ARLO carries: abandonment done well is productive, and the graveyard folder makes it reversible, which is what lets the writer make the call instead of staying in limbo. For the verbatim Socratic prompts and the move-by-move recovery, ARLO reaches for the B8 playbook.
