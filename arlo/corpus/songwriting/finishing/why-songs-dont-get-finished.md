# Why Songs Don't Get Finished — A Diagnostic

**Scope:** songwriting
**Source:** Deep-research synthesis (2026-05) — Songwriting Craft — verify before treating as authoritative
**Canonical references:** Steven Pressfield, *The War of Art*; Cal Newport, *Deep Work*; Mihaly Csikszentmihalyi, *Flow*; Ira Glass, "The Gap" / taste-gap interview; Dennis DeSantis, *Making Music* (finishing chapters)
**Tags:** songwriting-craft, finishing-songs, finishing-psychology, diagnostic, resistance, taste-gap

## The premise: "stuck" is not one problem

Most advice about finishing songs assumes the writer is stuck for one reason and prescribes one fix — usually "just push through." That fails because *stuck* is a family of distinct conditions, each with a different correct move. A writer paralyzed by the gap between their taste and their output needs the opposite advice from a writer who is genuinely out of material on a sketch that was never a song. This file is a diagnostic tree: it sorts the stuck-state into categories and names the move per category. The goal is not motivation — it is accurate triage. The single most common error a stuck musician makes is applying the wrong remedy: grinding harder on a song that should be abandoned, or abandoning a song that is one decision away from done. Frame this as craft, not therapy: these are creative-process patterns that working writers and teachers have named, and each has a workbench response.

## Category 1: Resistance (the fear wearing a disguise)

Steven Pressfield's *The War of Art* names the first and most common category: **Resistance** — the internal force that opposes any meaningful creative work, experienced as procrastination, distraction, sudden urges to reorganize the sample library, and the specific lie *"this isn't working, start something new"* that arrives precisely when the song is closest to a breakthrough [(Pressfield summary, Calvin Rosser)](https://calvinrosser.com/notes/war-of-art-steven-pressfield/). Pressfield's diagnostic tell: the strength of Resistance scales with how much the work matters. The song you most fear finishing is usually the one most worth finishing. **The move:** treat finishing as showing up, not as feeling ready. Pressfield's amateur-vs-professional distinction is operational — the amateur waits to feel inspired; the professional does the next concrete task regardless of mood. For a stuck song, that means naming one small, finishable unit (commit the second verse melody, bounce a rough) and doing only that. Resistance dissolves under motion, not under more deliberation.

## Category 2: The taste gap (your ear is ahead of your hands)

Ira Glass's widely-cited "taste gap" describes a second, completely different stuck-state. In his telling: people who do creative work get into it because they have good taste, but "there's a gap — for the first couple years that you're making stuff, what you're making isn't so good… but your taste, the thing that got you into the game, is still killer, and your taste is good enough that you can tell that what you're making is kind of a disappointment to you" [(Ira Glass, via The Marginalian)](https://www.themarginalian.org/2014/01/29/ira-glass-success-daniel-sax/). This is not Resistance — it is accurate self-assessment producing despair. The song genuinely isn't as good as the writer can hear it should be. **The move** is Glass's own prescription: *volume*. "The most important thing you can do is do a lot of work… put yourself on a deadline so that every week or every month you know you're going to finish one story. It's only by going through a volume of work that you're going to catch up and close that gap" [(Ira Glass, via The Marginalian)](https://www.themarginalian.org/2014/01/29/ira-glass-success-daniel-sax/). For a songwriter: finishing *this* song imperfectly is the only path to the song that closes the gap. Polishing it forever does not close the gap; finishing many songs does.

## Category 3: Shallow work crowding out deep work

Cal Newport's *Deep Work* supplies a third category that masquerades as the first two. The writer is not afraid and not despairing — they simply never enter the sustained, distraction-free state that the hard middle of a song requires. The work fragments into ten-minute bursts between notifications, and a song's structural problems (a second verse that needs to *develop* not restate, a bridge that needs genuine contrast) cannot be solved in ten-minute bursts. **The move** is environmental, not motivational: schedule a protected block, remove the phone from the room, and accept that the first 20–30 minutes are warm-up cost. DeSantis's own observation aligns — the first session on a track should be the longest because starting cold burns warm-up time, and fragmenting work multiplies that tax [(Cycling '74 review of *Making Music*)](https://cycling74.com/articles/a-review-of-making-music-74-strategies-for-electronic-music-producers). If a song stalls only when worked in scraps, the problem is not the song.

## Category 4: No flow because the challenge is mismatched

Mihaly Csikszentmihalyi's *Flow* gives the fourth category. Flow — the absorbed state where the work pulls you forward — occurs when challenge and skill are balanced. Too-easy produces boredom; too-hard produces anxiety, and anxiety reads as "this song is bad" when it is actually "this song is currently above my skill at this one task." **The move** is to rescale the task. If the arrangement is overwhelming, shrink the unit (mute everything, work one part). If the song is too simple to engage you, the boredom may be the signal that it is *done* and you are mistaking completion for failure. Csikszentmihalyi's frame is useful precisely because it separates "I'm anxious because this is hard" from "I'm bored because this is finished" — two states that both feel like being stuck but require opposite responses.

## Category 5: It was never a song (the sketch mistake)

DeSantis's *Making Music* is built around the observation that the most common stuck-state is "almost (but not quite) everything is in place," and that this state does not resolve with more inspiration — it resolves with process [(Cycling '74 review)](https://cycling74.com/articles/a-review-of-making-music-74-strategies-for-electronic-music-producers). But a real subcategory is the song that *is not actually a song yet* — a loop, a chord cycle, a single image with no second thought. Trying to "finish" it stalls because there is nothing structural to finish. **The move** is honest reclassification: this is a sketch, and sketches are valuable (they build muscle and explore territory) but not every sketch is owed a completion. This routes directly to the abandon-vs-persist framework — see `corpus/songwriting/stalls/abandoning-vs-persisting.md` for the playbook, and F5 below for the framework.

## The diagnostic question that sorts the categories

Ask one question first: **"When you sit down to work on it, what actually happens?"** The answer sorts the category:

- *"I open it, fiddle with the mix, and avoid the broken part"* → Resistance (Category 1). Move: name the dragon, do it first.
- *"I can hear it's not good enough and it's demoralizing"* → Taste gap (Category 2). Move: finish it anyway, start the next one.
- *"I only work on it in scraps and never get traction"* → Shallow work (Category 3). Move: protect a deep block.
- *"It overwhelms me / it bores me"* → Flow mismatch (Category 4). Move: rescale the task up or down.
- *"There's nothing there to finish"* → It's a sketch (Category 5). Move: reclassify, route to abandon-vs-persist.

The categories overlap and a single song can sit in two at once. The value of the tree is that it stops the writer from applying "push through" to a problem that "push through" makes worse.

## The trap of mislabeling Resistance as judgment

The subtlest failure is Category 1 disguised as Category 5. The writer says *"this song isn't worth finishing"* — a Category 5 abandon decision — but the real driver is Category 1 fear of finishing. Pressfield's frame is the test: Resistance is strongest on the work that matters most, so a sudden conviction that a song you loved last week is worthless is itself suspicious [(Pressfield summary, Calvin Rosser)](https://calvinrosser.com/notes/war-of-art-steven-pressfield/). The discriminating move: separate the assessment from the session. Don't decide a song is dead at the end of a bad working day, when "this is bad" and "I'm bad" both feel true. Sleep on it, listen fresh, and ask whether there is still one element you are proud of (the core-idea test from F5).

## When the diagnosis is "it's done and you can't tell"

A final category that the others miss: the song is finished and the writer cannot perceive it because of repetition-blindness and ear fatigue. After enough passes, perception of the work degrades — high-frequency content fades from attention, and small comparisons read as meaningful change when they are not. This is not stuck-on-an-unfinished-song; it is stuck-on-a-finished-song. **The move** is fresh ears: bounce it, leave the room, and listen on a system you didn't mix on, or get an outside ear who will say "it's done." See `corpus/songwriting/stalls/i-hate-this-song-now.md` for the dead-ear playbook and F4 below for the outside-ear threshold rule.

## How ARLO should use this tree

When a user signals stuck-ness, ARLO's first job is triage, not solutions. The wrong move is to offer arrangement tips to a writer in Category 1 (Resistance), or to offer motivation to a writer in Category 5 (it's a sketch). ARLO should ask the diagnostic question — *"when you sit down with it, what actually happens?"* — and route to the matching move. The categories are not a ranking; a working writer cycles through all five across a catalog. The honest message ARLO carries: being stuck is normal, it has nameable causes, and the cause determines the cure. Push-through is right for Resistance and wrong for almost everything else.

## A note on honesty and subjectivity

These five categories are a synthesis, not a settled taxonomy — Pressfield, Glass, Newport, Csikszentmihalyi, and DeSantis are describing overlapping territory from different vantage points, and none of them wrote "the five reasons songs don't get finished." The frame is useful as triage precisely because it refuses the single-cause story. Where the schools disagree — Pressfield says push through; the volume school (Glass, DeSantis) says finish fast and move on — the disagreement is itself diagnostic: push-through serves Resistance, volume serves the taste gap. ARLO should name the school rather than present any one as the universal truth.
