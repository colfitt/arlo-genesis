# "Done > Perfect" Frameworks for Songwriting

**Scope:** songwriting
**Source:** Deep-research synthesis (2026-05) — Songwriting Craft — verify before treating as authoritative

## The premise: a slogan with no method is useless

"Done is better than perfect" is the most-repeated finishing advice in creative work, and most of the time it is a slogan that produces nothing. The writer nods, agrees, and goes right back to tweaking the reverb tail. This file does two things: traces the slogan's actual lineage (which is misattributed in three different ways), and converts it into operational frameworks a songwriter can act on. The honest version of the idea is not "lower your standards" — it is "the version that exists and ships teaches you more than the version that stays perfect in your head." For a songwriter trying to finish, the slogan only helps once it becomes a decision rule with a defined stopping point.

## Lineage part 1: it was not Voltaire (exactly)

The cousin slogan, "perfect is the enemy of good," is universally attributed to Voltaire — but the attribution is loose. Voltaire quoted an Italian proverb, *"il meglio è l'inimico del bene"* ("the best is the enemy of the good"), in his 1770 *Questions sur l'Encyclopédie*, and used the line in his moral poem *La Bégueule*: *"Dans ses écrits, un sage Italien / Dit que le mieux est l'ennemi du bien"* — "in his writings, a wise Italian says that the best is the enemy of the good" [(Perfect is the enemy of good — Wikipedia)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Perfect_is_the_enemy_of_good). Two corrections matter for accuracy: Voltaire credited an *Italian* source, not himself, and the original word is **"best" (le mieux)**, not "perfect" — the "perfect" version is a translation drift into English. Montesquieu had written a near-identical line around 1726. The point for a songwriter is not trivia: the idea is centuries old and cross-cultural because the failure mode (chasing the best at the expense of the good) is permanent.

## Lineage part 2: "done is better than perfect" was not Sandberg

The operational tech-era version — "done is better than perfect" — is popularly credited to Sheryl Sandberg, who quotes it in *Lean In* and championed it at Facebook, where it was painted on a wall and printed on the company's motivational posters around 2010 [(Goodreads: Sheryl Sandberg quote)](https://www.goodreads.com/quotes/749769-done-is-better-than-perfect). But Sandberg popularized it; she did not originate it. The phrase is traced to Anne Mollegen Smith, a magazine editor (Redbook, Working Woman), who used it around 1979 [(Constanze Munz on the quote's origin)](https://constanzemunz.substack.com/p/done-is-better-than-perfect-sheryl). The lineage matters because the two contexts give the slogan two different meanings: in a magazine newsroom it meant *the deadline is real and the issue prints on schedule*, and in a software company it meant *ship and iterate, the product is never finished*. Songwriting borrows from both — the deadline discipline of the newsroom and the ship-and-learn ethos of software.

## Lineage part 3: Ira Glass made it a method, not a slogan

The most useful version for songwriters comes from Ira Glass's taste-gap talk, which converts the slogan into a *practice*. Glass's argument is that beginners produce work below their own taste, and the only way to close the gap is volume: "put yourself on a deadline so that every week or every month you know you're going to finish one story… it's only by going through a volume of work that you're going to catch up and close that gap" [(Ira Glass, via The Marginalian)](https://www.themarginalian.org/2014/01/29/ira-glass-success-daniel-sax/). This is "done > perfect" with teeth: the reason to ship the imperfect song is not that quality doesn't matter — it is that *shipping is the mechanism by which quality improves*. A perfect-but-unfinished song teaches nothing. A finished, flawed song teaches the writer where they actually are.

## The operational version: DeSantis's "is this acceptable?"

Dennis DeSantis's *Making Music* gives the single most actionable reframe. Replace the unanswerable question "how do I make this the best?" with the answerable question "**is this acceptable?**" [(EDMProd summary of DeSantis-aligned finishing strategies)](https://www.edmprod.com/finishing-music/). "Best" is an asymptote — there is no point at which you reach it, so the question never closes a decision. "Acceptable" is a yes/no the writer can actually answer. Apply it element by element: *Is this snare acceptable? Yes — move on. Is this second verse acceptable? No — fix it with one specific move, then re-ask.* The test forces motion because it has only two outcomes, and "acceptable" is explicit permission to stop. This is the slogan's most concrete form: done > perfect means *acceptable > best*, asked one element at a time.

## Framework: the deadline as the forcing function

Every version of "done > perfect" that actually works has a deadline attached, because work expands to fill available time (Parkinson's Law applies in the DAW as cleanly as anywhere). The practical move from finishing-system writers: set a deadline *shorter* than your intuitive estimate — if a song feels like a week's work, give it four days [(EDMProd, finishing music)](https://www.edmprod.com/finishing-music/). The deadline doesn't need external enforcement to function; the writer simply has to refuse to slip it. Glass's "one story a week" is the same lever in a different shape. For ARLO's user: a self-imposed ship date converts "perfect" from a goal into a constraint, and the constraint is what produces a finished song instead of an endlessly improving fragment.

## Framework: define "done" before you start

"Done > perfect" collapses if "done" is undefined, because then every session re-opens the question of whether the song is finished. The fix is to define the finish line *in advance*, at the start of the project, when judgment is least clouded by sunk cost. For a demo: done = "structurally a complete song, rough-mixed, one bounce." For a release master: done = "translates across the consumer-system rotation and the issues remaining are taste, not flaws." Writing the definition down before you begin means the end-of-session question becomes "did I hit the definition?" rather than "could this be better?" — and the first question is answerable while the second is not.

## The honest counterweight: when "done > perfect" is wrong

Be honest about subjectivity: "done > perfect" is one school, and it has an opposing school worth naming. The **polish-until-shipping** school — much of modern commercial pop, label A&R, sync-placement standards — holds that the last 20% of polish is exactly what separates a demo from a release, and that shipping the rough version forecloses the song's actual potential. This school is *correct in its context*: a topline pitched to a major artist, a track competing for streaming-playlist placement, an album where sonic consistency is the product. The "done > perfect" frameworks above are calibrated for the writer who *never finishes* — the chronic non-shipper. For the writer whose problem is the opposite (shipping sloppy work that a few more hours would meaningfully lift), the advice inverts. ARLO should diagnose which problem the user actually has before prescribing the slogan.

## The 80/20 calibration as the bridge

The two schools reconcile through the Pareto pattern: roughly 80% of the perceptible result comes from the first 20% of decisions — balance, arrangement, the core of the mix — and the remaining 80% of effort produces 20% of the perceptible improvement [(Production Expert on the 80/20 rule)](https://www.production-expert.com/production-expert-1/2019/12/10/how-to-use-the-8020-rule-in-music-production-to-jumpstart-your-productivity). "Done > perfect" is the instruction to stop somewhere inside that final, low-yield 80%. *Where* inside it you stop is the genre-and-context decision the two schools argue about. The chronic non-finisher should stop early in the final 80%; the commercial-release writer stops late. F4 (`corpus/songwriting/finishing/80-20-finishing-rule-mix-master.md`) develops this calibration for the mix-and-master phase specifically.

## What "done" buys you that "perfect" never will

The deepest argument for the slogan is structural, not motivational. A finished song can be: heard by an outside ear, A/B'd against your other work, learned from, and *left behind so the next song can be better*. An unfinished perfect song does none of these — it sits in the project folder accumulating the writer's anxiety. Glass's taste gap closes only across finished works, never within one [(Ira Glass, via The Marginalian)](https://www.themarginalian.org/2014/01/29/ira-glass-success-daniel-sax/). The writer who finishes ten flawed songs in a year has ten data points and a closing gap; the writer who perfects one has zero finished songs and the same gap. "Done > perfect" is, at bottom, a bet that your career is a volume problem, not a single-song problem.

## How ARLO should deploy this

ARLO should not throw the slogan at every stuck user — for the polish-until-shipping writer it is actively wrong advice. When the user's signal is chronic non-finishing (Category 2 taste gap or Category 1 Resistance from F1), ARLO can offer the operational tools in order: (1) define "done" for *this* song in one sentence, (2) set a deadline shorter than the estimate, (3) ask "is this acceptable?" element by element rather than "is this the best?", and (4) name the deal — finishing this imperfect song is how the next one gets better. ARLO should attribute carefully: the lineage corrections above (not Voltaire's word, not Sandberg's coinage) keep ARLO from repeating the common misattributions, which matters because users will quote ARLO back to other people.
