# Bowie's Cut-Up Technique — Burroughs-Derived Lyric Generation

## The premise

The cut-up technique: write prose, cut it into fragments, rearrange the fragments. The juxtapositions that survive are often stranger and more truthful than anything you would have written linearly. The method comes from William S. Burroughs and Brion Gysin in the late 1950s, was systematized in Burroughs and Gysin's [*The Third Mind*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Third_Mind), and was adopted by David Bowie in the early 1970s and used across his career — from *Diamond Dogs* (1974) through *Outside* (1995) and the late records *The Next Day* (2013) and *Blackstar* (2016).

The premise for songwriters: a linear lyric can be too on-the-nose. The mind, told to "write a song about X," reaches for clichés about X. The cut-up sidesteps the reaching. The fragments collide and the writer becomes an editor, picking the lines that hit.

## Origin and the Burroughs lineage

Burroughs credited Brion Gysin with discovering the technique in 1959 — Gysin sliced through a stack of newspapers with a Stanley knife and noticed the resulting strips of text formed unexpected sentences. Burroughs systematized it across the 1960s in *The Soft Machine*, *The Ticket That Exploded*, and *Nova Express*, then with Gysin produced *The Third Mind*, the canonical document of the technique.

Bowie discovered Burroughs in the late 1960s and the two met in late 1974 (the interview was published in *Rolling Stone* and is reproduced in multiple Burroughs biographies). The cut-up entered Bowie's working method around the *Diamond Dogs* sessions — Bowie has said in multiple interviews that he initially tried to adapt Orwell's *1984* into a Broadway-style record, was refused the rights by the Orwell estate, and rebuilt the material using cut-ups.

## Bowie's own description

The cleanest verbatim account from Bowie is the 2008 *Daily Mail* interview: "You write down a paragraph or two describing several different subjects… and then cut the sentences into four or five-word sections; mix 'em up and reconnect them." He added that he didn't always use the output verbatim: "I'll either take sentences verbatim as it spews them out, or there might be something within a sentence that triggers off an idea."

That's the two-modes acknowledgment. Some Bowie cut-ups are nearly raw output (the *Diamond Dogs* "Sweet Thing / Candidate / Sweet Thing (Reprise)" suite is the canonical example). Some are seed material that triggered an entirely different lyric, with the cut-up itself discarded. ARLO's workflow should honor both modes.

## The 1995 Verbasizer

By the mid-1990s Bowie wanted a faster cut-up engine. Working with programmer Ty Roberts (later co-founder of Gracenote), Bowie commissioned a Mac application called the [Verbasizer](https://www.vice.com/en/article/the-verbasizer-was-david-bowies-1995-lyric-writing-mac-app/) — described by Roberts as a tool where Bowie would enter 3–25 source sentences, the program would sort the words into grammatical columns (noun, verb, adjective), and then randomize-recombine into new sentences. The output for *Outside* (1995) was, in Bowie's words, "a real kaleidoscope of meanings and topic and nouns and verbs all slamming into each other."

A modern equivalent web tool [Verbasizer.com](https://verbasizer.com/) reproduces the practice. The mechanism — paper-and-scissors, Verbasizer, or modern LLM-as-cut-up-engine — is interchangeable. The cut-up itself is the constant.

## What this workflow fits

The clean trigger: the user has draft lyrics that feel linear, on-the-nose, paint-by-numbers. They might say "this lyric sounds like a Hallmark card" or "every line is the obvious line." The cut-up is the lateral-collision tool to dislodge that.

Other fits:

- The user has reams of journaling/prose about the song's subject and no lyrics
- The user wants imagistic, dream-logic lyrics rather than narrative
- The user is in a sonic-led project (production-first) where the lyric needs to feel as strange as the music
- The user has been writing the same kind of lyric across many songs and needs out

When this workflow does not fit:

- The user is writing a story-song that depends on narrative clarity (Lucinda Williams, John Prine, Townes Van Zandt school) — the cut-up will destroy the through-line they want
- The user is writing for a specific listener (use [`nick-cave-letter-method.md`](./nick-cave-letter-method.md))
- The user is writing a beat-first topline where the lyric has to scan to a rhythmic pocket (cut-up output rarely scans)

## The mechanical workflow — paper version

1. Pick a subject. Even a vague one — "the year my dad got sick," "what it feels like to leave a city," "the dream I had Tuesday."

2. Write 3–5 paragraphs of prose about it. Don't think songs yet. Just write. Burroughs encouraged using found text too — newspaper headlines, journal entries, conversations overheard, even passages from books that have nothing to do with your subject.

3. Print or hand-copy the prose with wide margins.

4. With scissors, cut the prose into strips of 3–7 words each. Don't be careful — let some cuts fall in the middle of phrases.

5. Mix all the strips. Lay them out on a table in a new order, drawing strips at random or by feel.

6. Read the new sequence aloud.

7. Mark every line that surprises you, that you'd never have written linearly, or that makes you laugh in recognition.

8. Those lines are the seed of the lyric. Build verses and choruses around them, in whatever order they suggest. You'll add connective tissue. The connective tissue is yours; the seed is the cut-up's.

## The digital workflow

Use [Verbasizer.com](https://verbasizer.com/), a Burroughs-style cut-up tool, or any text-randomization tool. Modern AI chat tools can be used as cut-up partners — ARLO itself can run this — but the discipline is the same: feed in source text, ask for randomized recombination, edit ruthlessly.

A specific recipe: paste 5–10 sentences of your prose draft. Ask for the words to be sorted by part of speech and recombined into 20 new sentences. Read all 20. Mark the 3–5 that hit. Discard the rest. Build the lyric around the marked lines.

## What proportion of the output is usable

Bowie's own implication, across interviews: very little. The cut-up generates a large volume of nonsense in order to surface a few lines of unexpected gold. The proportion is something like 5–20% usable on a generous day. Writers who expect to use 80% of cut-up output will conclude the technique is broken; it isn't, the expectation was.

The editing pass is the work. The cut-up is not the lyric — it's the rough quarry the lyric is extracted from.

## What it produces lyrically

Cut-up lyrics have a recognizable signature. They are:

- Image-rich and juxtaposition-led (a single line will hold two unrelated images and the friction is the meaning)
- Loose in narrative through-line (the song circles its subject rather than telling its story)
- Often dream-logical (the cohesion is associative rather than causal)
- Hard to "explain" but easy to feel

This is why Bowie used it most heavily for surreal/dystopian/Gothic material (*Diamond Dogs*, *Outside*, *Hallo Spaceboy*, parts of *Blackstar*) and largely set it aside for the more direct emotional material (*Hunky Dory*, *Heathen*'s ballads, "Where Are We Now"). When the song wants directness, cut-up is the wrong tool.

## Songs Bowie produced with the method

Verified examples from interviews and biographies:

- *Diamond Dogs* (1974) album generally, with the "Sweet Thing / Candidate / Sweet Thing (Reprise)" suite as the most-cited extended cut-up
- *Outside* (1995) — first major Verbasizer use
- *The Next Day* (2013) and *Blackstar* (2016) — continued use, particularly the more elliptical tracks
- "Hallo Spaceboy" (1995) often cited as cut-up

ARLO should resist attributing specific lines to cut-up generation unless the attribution is in a primary source — Bowie was protective of process and rarely identified which lines were cut-up output vs. which were authored linearly.

## Common failure modes

**Cutting too small.** Strips of 1–2 words produce word-salad. Burroughs and Bowie both worked with 3–7 word strips because phrase-level fragments retain enough grammar to suggest meaning. ARLO should encourage this strip length.

**Using everything.** The cut-up's output is mostly noise. If the user is trying to honor every strip, they've misunderstood the technique. The cut-up is a generator; the writer is the editor.

**Cutting too clean.** Sterile prose cut up produces sterile fragments. Burroughs liked cutting *charged* text — political slogans, his own most urgent journals, surveillance transcripts. The source material's heat carries through to the cut-up.

**Sanding the surprises out in revision.** The reason a cut-up line works is its strangeness. A revision pass that smooths every strange line back to the expected lyric destroys what the technique produced. Resist.

**Using cut-up for a song that wants directness.** A cut-up cover of a story-song will fail. The cut-up is for songs that benefit from dream-logic, not narrative.

## Variations and partner techniques

**Cut-up plus letter method.** Write the [Nick Cave-style letter](./nick-cave-letter-method.md), then cut it up. Some writers find this the easiest way to combine emotional truth with surrealist surprise.

**Cut-up plus Oblique Strategies.** Use a card draw to bias the cut-up — e.g., *Use an old idea* prompts the writer to feed in source text from years ago.

**LLM-as-cut-up partner.** ARLO can act as the Verbasizer. The user pastes prose; ARLO returns recombinations. This preserves the mechanism but the user is still the editor.

**The "found-text-only" cut-up.** Source text exclusively from outside your own writing — a newspaper page, a chapter of a novel, a forum thread. Burroughs' purest mode. Often produces output that resembles nothing the writer would have produced and is closest to the original surrealist spirit of the practice.

## A closing note on attribution honesty

The cut-up technique predates Bowie by fifteen years and was not invented by him. The ARLO file name (`bowie-burroughs-cut-up`) preserves the dual attribution because Bowie is the songwriter most associated with the technique in popular memory, but Burroughs and Gysin are the originators. When ARLO surfaces this workflow it should preserve both names in the user-facing description.
