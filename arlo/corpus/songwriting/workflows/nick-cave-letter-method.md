# Nick Cave's Letter Method — Writing a Song as a Letter to a Real Person

**Scope:** songwriting
**Source:** Deep-research synthesis (2026-05) — Songwriting Craft — verify before treating as authoritative
**Canonical references:** Nick Cave, *The Red Hand Files* (theredhandfiles.com); Nick Cave & Seán O'Hagan, *Faith, Hope and Carnage* (Canongate, 2022); Nick Cave interviews in Interview Magazine, NPR, Kill Your Darlings, Sharp Magazine
**Tags:** songwriting-craft, finishing-songs, workflow-recipe, cave, letter-method, lyrics-first, emotional-specificity, direct-address

## The premise — honest about what this is

This workflow recipe is a synthesis. Nick Cave has not, on the public record, described "write the song as a letter to a specific person" as a named method. What he *has* documented — across [The Red Hand Files](https://www.theredhandfiles.com/), his book-length conversation [*Faith, Hope and Carnage*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Faith,_Hope,_and_Carnage) with Seán O'Hagan, and interviews — is a constellation of practices that this workflow stitches into one operational recipe. Treat the recipe as Cave-adjacent, not Cave-canonical. The constituent moves are verifiable; the bundle is the ARLO-side construction.

The premise: when a lyric keeps coming out vague, abstract, or universal, the writer has lost contact with the actual person, moment, or feeling that originated it. Reaching back to a single recipient — writing the song *to* someone — restores the contact. The song then hides inside the letter, and the writer's job in the second pass is to extract it.

## Why Cave is the right name for the bundle

Three documented Cave practices justify the attribution.

First, The Red Hand Files is itself a letter project. Since 2018, Cave has answered fan questions in weekly email letters laid out on cream-coloured paper, signed "with love, Nick." The Cambridge University Press chapter on the Files calls them an exercise in epistolary spiritual intimacy ([Cambridge / *Epistolary Entanglements*](https://www.cambridge.org/core/books/abs/epistolary-entanglements-in-film-media-and-the-visual-arts/spiritual-intimacies-of-the-red-hand-files-how-long-will-i-be-alone/A8FE7169B308D3A41708F7909AA1BAA4)). The format isn't decorative; Cave has chosen direct address as his prose mode.

Second, Cave repeatedly describes songs as emerging from a single concrete image. In his 2014 NPR interview he describes [*Ghosteen* growing from "the image of a huge ice sculpture of a man melting slowly in the sun"](https://www.npr.org/2014/09/16/348727370/nick-cave-the-creative-process-is-an-altered-state-in-itself). In the Kill Your Darlings interview he describes writing "As The Waters Cover The Sea" by observing his wife and transforming the ordinary moment into the visionary ([Kill Your Darlings](https://www.killyourdarlings.com.au/article/gerard-elson-in-conversation-with-nick-cave/)).

Third, Cave treats songwriting as a daily desk job. The Line of Best Fit summary of [Red Hand Files Issue #218](https://www.thelineofbestfit.com/news/latest-news/nick-cave-important-part-of-songwriting-is-to-sit-down-at-desk-pick-up-pen) reports him saying the most important undertaking of his day is "to simply sit down at my desk and pick up my pen," and that songs arrive only "when I am at work." Long-form interviews confirm he keeps roughly 9-to-5 hours in a bare office when a record is being written.

The letter method is the songwriter's-bench fusion of these three: the form (letter), the seed (one concrete image of one real person), and the discipline (sit down and write the letter even when nothing is coming).

## When this workflow fits

The clean trigger condition: the user has a strong emotion attached to a real person or real event, but every draft they produce comes out as a universal pop statement that could be about anyone. The classic signature is the line that uses the word *we* or *somebody* when it should use a name.

Adjacent fits: the writer is grieving, in love, furious at one person specifically, or wrestling with an unresolved conversation. Cave's own *Skeleton Tree* and *Ghosteen* both emerged from grief over the death of his son Arthur and circle the dead without ever pretending to address the universal ([Wikipedia / Ghosteen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ghosteen)).

Mis-fits: a purely sonic exploration where no real person is the subject; a producer-led track waiting for a topline; a beat-first workflow where the lyric exists to fit a rhythmic pocket. In those cases see [`beat-first-topline-workflow.md`](./beat-first-topline-workflow.md) instead.

## The recipient choice

Two recipient archetypes, with different consequences.

**The unreachable recipient.** Someone dead, gone, estranged, or far away. The letter will never be read by them. This loosens the writer's editing — they're free to say the thing they couldn't say. Cave's *Skeleton Tree* is the towering example: the songs cluster around Arthur without naming him in a way that closes the door.

**The reachable recipient.** Someone who could, in theory, read this. The constraint is sharper. The writer must reckon with consequences. The discipline tends to produce more specific, less melodramatic work — but some writers seize up entirely under it.

Pick deliberately. The recipient choice changes what the song can do.

## What the letter looks like before it becomes a song

Plain prose. Date in the corner. "Dear ___" at the top. The user writes for thirty to sixty minutes without lifting the pen, addressing this person directly, in the present tense if they're alive and past tense if they're not. No line breaks, no rhymes, no choruses. Sensory specifics — what the room smelled like, what they said, the color of the jacket. The Pat Pattison object-writing discipline applies here: the seven senses, including the kinesthetic and organic (see [`corpus/lyrics/object-writing-and-sensory-imagery.md`](../../lyrics/object-writing-and-sensory-imagery.md)).

Two prohibitions: no abstractions and no universals. *Love* is an abstraction. *Loss* is an abstraction. *Hope* is an abstraction. The image of her standing at the sink in the green sweater is not. The line "everybody hurts" is universal. The line "you wouldn't get out of the car" is not.

## What to do when the letter is finished

Print or save the letter. Walk away for a few hours; ideally sleep on it. Then read it as a stranger would.

The song hides in the most concrete passages. Mark them. The chorus often appears as the line the writer keeps almost-saying and pulling back from — the line that's been building. The verses are the surrounding scenes. The bridge is frequently the moment the letter turns — the paragraph where the writer admits something they hadn't planned to admit.

Extract those. Reshape into meter and rhyme. Most of the letter will be cut. That's the design — the letter was the contact-restoration tool, not the finished artifact.

## The "you" question — POV consequences

A letter is second-person by definition: *you*, addressed. The extracted song inherits this. Direct address is the default register of pop ([corpus/lyrics/point-of-view-tense-and-narrator.md](../../lyrics/point-of-view-tense-and-narrator.md)), but the letter method tends to produce a particular *kind* of direct address — specific second-person rather than generic second-person.

Generic "you": "you make me feel" (could be anyone). Specific "you": "you smoked at the funeral" (only the recipient). The second is what Aimee Mann calls earning the abstraction by way of the specific — and Cave's lyrics, line by line, are full of it (see [`aimee-mann-specificity-first.md`](./aimee-mann-specificity-first.md) for the adjacent workflow).

If the second-person address is going to alienate the listener, the writer can switch POVs in revision. But the letter method's gift — the *specificity* — should survive the POV change.

## When the letter won't come

Cave's own answer to writer's block, given in [Red Hand Files Issue #108](https://www.theredhandfiles.com/the-lyrics-just-arent-coming/): "when something's not coming, it's coming." His advice is patience plus showing up at the desk. The line "ideas are not in here, they're out there, struggling to get in" appears across multiple interviews and is consistent with his Red Hand Files cosmology.

Operationally: if the letter won't start, the user should write the date and "Dear ___" and the first three sentences of any conversation with that person. Often the bottleneck is the opener, not the content. Once the first paragraph is on the page, the rest usually follows.

If after a forty-five-minute attempt nothing has come, the workflow should be abandoned for the day — not the song. Cave has been clear that the discipline is showing up, not forcing output. The next day's session may unlock what today's did not.

## The agonizing-early-stages reality

[*Faith, Hope and Carnage*](https://www.amazon.com/Faith-Hope-Carnage-Nick-Cave/dp/0374607370) is explicit that songwriting in its early stages is "agonising," and Cave describes himself as "wracked with anxiety." This isn't a problem to be solved. It's the texture of the work. The letter method does not eliminate the anxiety; it gives the anxiety something concrete to attach itself to — the recipient, the room, the smell of the jacket — instead of the impossible task of saying everything to everyone.

Telling the user that anxiety is the cost, not the bug, is part of what this workflow accomplishes.

## Common failure modes

**The letter becomes a journal entry.** The writer reaches for catharsis instead of addressing the recipient. Fix: re-anchor with "Dear ___" and the recipient's eyes. If you're not still writing *to* them, you've drifted.

**The letter is too polished.** The writer self-edits in real time, producing a draft that's already pre-cooked into pop lines. Fix: instruct the user to handwrite, fast, no crossing out.

**The extracted song loses the letter's heat.** In the extraction pass, the specifics get sanded down into pop-able lines and what made the letter alive is gone. Fix: in revision, replace any sanded-down line with the original letter sentence verbatim. The pop will improve; the song will live.

**The writer cannot pick a recipient.** This is a signal that the song's subject matter may not be ready yet. Park the workflow. Consider [`david-lynch-catching-big-fish.md`](./david-lynch-catching-big-fish.md) or [`aimee-mann-specificity-first.md`](./aimee-mann-specificity-first.md) instead.

## Variations

**The letter you'll never send vs. the letter you might.** Already covered. Choose one before starting.

**The letter from the recipient back to you.** A second-pass variation. After extracting the song from the first letter, have the recipient write back. What would they say? This often unlocks the bridge — the moment of the song where another perspective is needed.

**The letter to a younger or older self.** When no other recipient surfaces, the self-at-a-different-age is a fallback. Sufjan Stevens' *Carrie & Lowell* circles his late mother but is also clearly written to and from his younger self ([cross-link `sufjan-research-then-write.md`](./sufjan-research-then-write.md)).

**The letter as bridge-only tool.** Some writers use the method only for the bridge, not the whole song — the bridge is where the song confesses something, and the letter format is the easiest way to find the confession.

## When the song is finished, what survives

Almost never the whole letter. Sometimes a chorus is one verbatim line from the letter. Sometimes a verse is two letter sentences compressed to a couplet. Often the song is structurally a song while the letter is structurally prose, and the bridge is the one passage that survives almost unchanged because the prose was already singing.

The letter goes in a folder. Cave has said that for him the song is the survivor — the prose draft is process. The user should keep their letters; they're a record of what the songs were trying to do.
