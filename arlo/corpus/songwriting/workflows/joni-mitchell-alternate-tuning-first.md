## The premise

Joni Mitchell built her songwriting around a single, radical claim: **the tuning is the song.** Change the tuning, the chord shapes change. The chord shapes change, the harmonic vocabulary changes. The harmonic vocabulary changes, the melody and the emotion change. By the time the tuning is set, half the song is already decided — into the wood of the guitar.

Mitchell has used over 50 different alternate tunings across her catalog ([Acoustic Guitar](https://acousticguitar.com/the-guitar-tuning-odyssey-of-joni-mitchell/)). The [JoniMitchell.com database](https://jonimitchell.com/music/tuningpatterns.cfm) catalogs roughly 45 distinct essential patterns, with more than 60 apparent tunings counting capo positions and letter-name variants. Only two of her songs are in standard tuning ("Tin Angel" and "Urge for Going"). Everything else lives in an invented harmonic environment.

This workflow is named after Joni because she pushes it furthest, but it's a tradition: Nick Drake, Richard Thompson, John Martyn, Sandy Denny, Stephen Stills, the entire DADGAD-and-beyond folk lineage, plus modern players like Andy McKee and Kaki King all work this way. If you play guitar and your songs keep coming out sounding the same, the tuning-first approach is the most reliable lateral move available.

## How a tuning produces a song

The mechanic is concrete: a new tuning produces unfamiliar chord shapes which produce unfamiliar music. Your left hand cannot rely on muscle memory; it has to find shapes. The shapes you find are the shapes that *fit the hand on this tuning* — not the shapes any guitarist would reach for.

Mitchell's own description in [Acoustic Guitar](https://acousticguitar.com/the-guitar-tuning-odyssey-of-joni-mitchell/): *"You're twiddling and you find the tuning. Now the left hand has to learn where the chords are, because it's a whole new ballpark, right?"* That phrase — *whole new ballpark* — is exactly the songwriting effect. You can't write a Joni song in standard tuning because Joni's hand isn't moving the way standard tuning moves a hand.

She also says, more philosophically: *"It's only through error that discovery is made, and in order to discover you have to set up some sort of situation with a random element — a strange attractor, using contemporary physics terms."* The tuning is the strange attractor. It introduces error on purpose.

## Joni's discovery process — how she finds a tuning

Mitchell describes the tuning-discovery as both intuitive and environmental. She'll twist the pegs until "these chords I was getting by twisting the knobs on the guitar until I could get these chords that I heard inside that suited me, they feel like my feelings."

She has also written tunings to the *day*. For "The Magdalene Laundries" from *Turbulent Indigo* she tuned to "the day in a certain place, taking the pitch of birdsongs and the general frequency" ([Acoustic Guitar](https://acousticguitar.com/the-guitar-tuning-odyssey-of-joni-mitchell/)). The tuning is B F# B E A E.

For most working guitarists, the practical version is less mystical: pick a tuning, sit with it, find a shape that surprises you, follow the shape.

## Canonical Joni tunings to try

A starter list from her catalog ([JoniMitchell.com](https://jonimitchell.com/music/tuningpatterns.cfm), [Acoustic Guitar](https://acousticguitar.com/the-guitar-tuning-odyssey-of-joni-mitchell/)):

- **D modal (DADGBD)** — early-era Joni; folk-rooted, open and ambiguous.
- **Open G (DGDGBD)** — "Marcie", "Nathan LaFraneer", "Little Green" (capo 5).
- **Open D (DADF#AD)** — blues-derived, common starting point.
- **CGDGBD** — used on "Sweet Bird" and "Cold Blue Steel" — slightly darker open-tonal feel.
- **Open E (EBEG#BE)** — "Both Sides Now", "Big Yellow Taxi" (early arrangements), "Chelsea Morning".
- **BF#C#EF#B** — the *Hejira* tuning; floating, ambiguous, characteristic of her mid-70s sound.
- **DADEAD** — "Slouching towards Bethlehem", "Cool Water" — close to DADGAD with a sus character.
- **C#G#D#F#G#C#** — "My Secret Place" — low and shimmering.
- **BF#BEAE** — "The Magdalene Laundries" — exceptionally low; haunted.

She systematized her own naming convention because the existing notation didn't scale. The Joni system: write the low-string letter, then the fret-numbers needed to tune each successive open string. Standard tuning in Joni notation is "E 55545". The "Marcie" tuning DGDGBD is "D 57543" ([Howard Wright's notation explainer](https://www.hakwright.co.uk/music/joni_notation.html); [JoniMitchell.com](https://jonimitchell.com/music/tuningpatterns.cfm)).

## The "shape, not chord" approach

When you sit with an unfamiliar tuning, **don't try to play the chords you already know.** That is the failure mode. You re-derive standard-tuning shapes and end up writing a standard-tuning song that happens to be in weird tuning.

The Joni discipline is closer to this:
1. Drop into the tuning. Strum the open strings.
2. Move one finger to a fret. Listen. Move it to a different fret. Listen.
3. Find a *shape* — a finger placement — that produces a sound you want to keep hearing.
4. Move that shape up and down the neck. Each fret is a new chord because the tuning is open.
5. Find a second shape that goes well with the first.
6. *Now* you have a progression — and you have no idea what the chords are called, and you don't need to.

The chord names are post-hoc. Mitchell herself often couldn't and didn't name the chords she was playing; she described them by feel and by shape.

## Writing into the tuning

Once you have two or three shapes that move together, the writing process is:

1. **Find the song's tempo and meter** by strumming or fingerpicking the shapes naturally. Don't force a meter; let the tuning suggest one.
2. **Sing nonsense over it.** The harmonic ambiguity of open tunings (lots of unresolved sus and add chords) often invites a vocal that floats above rather than locking to the root. Sing until you find a melody that fits the hand-of-the-tuning.
3. **Let the lyric come from the feel.** Mitchell's tunings *suggest* emotional registers — open G feels warm and pastoral; the *Hejira* tunings feel road-and-distance; the very low tunings feel underground. Trust the register.
4. **Don't transcribe to standard tuning until you have to.** If you have to teach the song to a band, the chords have names, you can find them. But while you're writing, keep it in the tuning's vocabulary.

## The challenge of capo plus tuning

Mitchell uses capo extensively. "Little Green" is the Marcie tuning (DGDGBD) with a capo on fret 5, transposing the open strings to GCGCEG. This is how she expanded her tuning library — same pattern, different absolute pitches, different range for her voice.

The pragmatic implication: you can re-use a tuning pattern at different capo positions to fit different vocal ranges or different emotional registers. The pattern is the harmonic identity; the capo is the pitch slot.

## When this workflow fits

- You're a guitarist whose songs all sound the same — same shapes, same progressions, same feel.
- You've exhausted standard-tuning chord vocabulary and want new harmonic territory without learning jazz voicings.
- You're writing a song that wants ambiguity (lots of unresolved chords, droning notes, sus colors).
- You write best at the instrument with your hands, not at a piano or notation.
- You're stuck in a writing rut and need a structural reset.

## When this workflow does NOT fit

- You don't play guitar. Pianists have a piano-only analogue (write the song in a key you don't normally use), but the tuning-first move specifically isn't available.
- You're writing for a band that doesn't want a single-guitar-driven song.
- The song needs to be performed at sessions where re-tuning between songs is impractical (open-mic, busking).
- You're a co-writer working with someone who plays standard-tuning lead — they'll have to navigate the unfamiliar chords too.
- You're writing a specific arranged production and the tuning's harmonic ambiguity will get buried.

## Common failure modes

- **Standard-tuning shapes in disguise.** You re-derive familiar shapes and end up writing a song you could have written in standard tuning. Discipline: only use shapes that *only work in this tuning*.
- **Tuning-as-cosmetics.** You write a standard-tuning song, then tune the guitar weird for the recording. That's not this workflow; that's overdub texture.
- **No commit.** You keep changing the tuning every session. The tuning is supposed to be the song's environment. Commit to one tuning for one song.
- **Capo confusion.** Re-tuning your strings AND capoing introduces enough complexity that transcribing the song later becomes painful. Notate as you go.
- **Lost in the strings.** Open tunings have a lot of sympathetic resonance; you may write a 6-minute song you can't edit. Set a length constraint up front.

## How a non-guitarist can adapt the principle

The deep principle — *introduce a structural change to your instrument that breaks muscle memory* — applies beyond guitar:

- **Pianists:** write in a key with lots of black keys you don't normally use. F# major. Db major.
- **Keyboardists:** swap to an unfamiliar synth patch and let the patch's affordances dictate the writing.
- **DAW writers:** force a tempo or time signature you don't use (5/8, 6/4, 87 BPM).
- **Singers:** write to a melody an octave outside your usual range.

The mechanic is the same: a structural constraint that produces a *whole new ballpark*.
