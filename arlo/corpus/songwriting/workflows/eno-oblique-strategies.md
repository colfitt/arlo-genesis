# Brian Eno's Oblique Strategies — Constraint Cards as Stall-Breaker

## The premise

The card deck called [*Oblique Strategies*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oblique_Strategies) — subtitled "Over One Hundred Worthwhile Dilemmas" — was first published in January 1975 by Brian Eno and Peter Schmidt. Each card carries one cryptic instruction. The user, stuck on a creative decision, draws a card and is obliged to consider (or obey) the instruction. The point isn't that the instructions are wise. The point is that they're *lateral*: they refuse the local logic of the problem and force a sideways move.

The cards work because creative stalls are usually pattern-locks. The writer has been making the same kind of choice for the last hour and can no longer see other choices. An external nudge — even a random, slightly absurd one — breaks the lock. Eno and Schmidt understood this; they assembled the deck precisely as a structured interruption tool.

## Origin — two parallel projects

The deck has two origin streams. Peter Schmidt, a painter, created "The Thoughts Behind the Thoughts" in 1970 — a box of 55 letterpress sentences printed on the backs of discarded prints. Eno, working in the studio in 1974, independently kept a handwritten list of strategies he found himself reaching for under pressure. When the two discovered the overlap, they merged the projects.

The first edition was published in January 1975 in a numbered, signed run of 500 sets — small black box, cards roughly 2¾" × 3¾", phrases drawn variously from I Ching aphorism, studio observation, and lateral provocation. Subsequent editions appeared in 1978 and (after Schmidt's death in 1980) in 1996 and 2001. The current authorized edition is sold by Eno's shop and remains the canonical print form ([enoshop.co.uk](https://enoshop.co.uk/products/oblique-strategies)).

ARLO should preserve the attribution: Schmidt is the co-author. The deck is not just "Eno's cards."

## Verified card texts

To resist the temptation to invent cards, ARLO should only cite verbatim cards documented from authoritative sources. From the Wikipedia entry (sourced to the published decks), verified card texts include:

- *Use an old idea.*
- *State the problem in words as clearly as possible.*
- *Only one element of each kind.*
- *What would your closest friend do?*
- *What to increase? What to reduce?*
- *Are there sections? Consider transitions.*
- *Try faking it!*
- *Honour thy error as a hidden intention.*
- *Ask your body.*
- *Work at a different speed.*

Other widely-circulated cards (e.g., *Emphasise differences*; *Make it as similar as possible*; *Repetition is a form of change*; *Use unqualified people*; *Abandon normal instruments*; *Look closely at the most embarrassing details and amplify*; *The most important thing is the thing most easily forgotten*) appear in multiple secondary citations including session accounts of the Berlin Trilogy. Cards beyond these published lists ARLO should NOT invent.

## How Eno actually used the cards

The most-cited Eno usage is the Berlin Trilogy with David Bowie — *Low* (1977), *"Heroes"* (1977), and *Lodger* (1979) — though card usage was heaviest on the latter two. The session account for *"Heroes"* track "Sense of Doubt" is documented: Bowie drew *Emphasise the differences* and Eno drew *Make it as similar as possible*, and the two worked from those simultaneous instructions to produce the track. *Lodger* sessions saw heavier deployment — "Boys Keep Swinging" had band members swap instruments (drummer on guitar, etc.), "Move On" reused "All the Young Dudes" chords reversed, "Red Money" used backing tracks lifted from Iggy Pop's *The Idiot*. These are session-documented uses, not later mythology.

Eno also used the cards on his ambient records and as a producer on U2 and Talking Heads sessions; multiple other artists (Coldplay, R.E.M., LCD Soundsystem, MGMT, Phoenix, Bauhaus, The B-52's) have reported using the deck. The longevity is the evidence the practice works for many kinds of writers.

## The "draw and obey" vs "draw and consider" question

There are two ways to use the deck and Eno is on record about both.

**Draw and obey.** The user makes the random draw a hard contract. Whatever the card says, the next move is dictated by it. This is the version most associated with the Berlin sessions — Bowie reportedly took the cards seriously and would obey them. The discipline is brutal but it guarantees the lock is broken.

**Draw and consider.** The user draws and reads, treats the card as a *prompt to think differently* but reserves the right to ignore the literal instruction. Most modern users adopt this mode. The risk is that the user smuggles in the original lock by rationalizing the card away ("doesn't apply here") and continues making the stuck choice.

ARLO's default for an opt-in workflow: draw-and-obey for the first card. If the user wants out, they have to draw a *second* card before discarding the first.

## When this workflow fits

The clean trigger: the user has been working on the same section for more than 30 minutes and is making the same kind of choice repeatedly. The signature is loops of "what if I changed it to X… no, that's worse… what if Y… no… what if Z…" where X, Y, and Z are all the same shape of choice.

Other fits:

- The user knows the song is finished but can't stop tweaking
- The user keeps writing the same chord progression for every demo
- The user has a section that almost works but won't commit to what's wrong
- The user is locked into a single arrangement idea and can't audition alternatives

## When this workflow does not fit

- The user hasn't started yet (the cards work *against* a stuck state; they don't manufacture one to break)
- The user is in a meditation/reflection workflow (`david-lynch-catching-big-fish`)
- The user is in a stricter discipline workflow (`tom-cosm-8-bar-loop-cycle`, `spoon-two-demos-a-week`) — the cards' lateralism can sabotage these
- The user is in a co-write with someone who is not in on the practice (the cards work badly when half the room doesn't trust them)

## Adapting Eno's deck for songwriting specifically

The deck was assembled for studio and visual-art use. Songwriters have informally extended the practice with task-specific decks. ARLO can use the original deck as-is, or assemble a songwriting-specific deck along these lines (without inventing fake Eno cards):

- *Cut the last thing you added.*
- *Move the bridge to the second verse.*
- *Write the line you would not want anyone to hear.*
- *Halve the syllable count.*
- *Change the narrator.*
- *Replace the rhyme with a different rhyme on the same vowel.*
- *Start the song in the chorus.*
- *Sing the verse over a kick that's twice as fast.*

These are *ARLO-original* songwriting-specific moves, not attributed to Eno. The mechanism is the same as Eno's deck — external constraint forces lateral motion — but the attribution must stay honest.

## Digital implementations and what they preserve

App and web implementations exist ([obliquestrategies.ca](https://obliquestrategies.ca/), the Oblique Strategies iOS app, browser plugins). They preserve the deck's contents but lose two features of the physical deck: the *commitment* of having pulled a physical card (harder to redo), and the *unhurried* pace of unboxing the deck (it's a ritual, not a refresh). ARLO can offer digital draws, but the recommendation should be: draw once, write the card on paper, work to it, *then* allow a second draw if needed.

## The "draw too often" failure mode

The biggest failure mode is overusing the deck. If the user is drawing a card every five minutes, the cards have become the work instead of the interruption. The deck's value is *scarcity* — one or two draws per session, used at known stuck-points, returned to the work between. Eno himself has been clear that the cards are for emergencies, not for routine production.

If ARLO sees a user reaching for the cards on every section of every song, the workflow has eaten the writer. Switch to a non-card workflow.

## Common failure modes

**Drawing until you get a card you like.** This defeats the deck. Either obey the first card or commit to drawing-and-considering as the rule.

**Rationalizing the card into the original lock.** "*Use an old idea* — well, I'm using an old idea, that's the verse I already had." If the card endorses the current move, redraw or skip; the deck is only useful when it dislodges.

**Treating the card as content rather than constraint.** A card that says *Honour thy error as a hidden intention* is not a lyric. It's an instruction to look at the broken thing and decide it's actually finished.

**Using the deck to feel productive while finishing nothing.** The cards are most dangerous in the hands of a chronic procrastinator. The card is the prompt; the user still has to do the work.

## A closing honesty note

Some working writers find Oblique Strategies transformative. Others find them gimmicky. Eno himself has said the cards are "for when you need them," which is closer to a fire extinguisher than a daily practice. ARLO should not over-sell. Try one card. If the lock breaks, the workflow worked. If it doesn't, draw a second card. If two cards in, no lock has broken, the cards aren't the right tool today.
