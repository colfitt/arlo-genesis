# Bob Dylan's Notebook + Cassette Discipline — Capture Everything, Edit Later

**Scope:** songwriting
**Source:** Deep-research synthesis (2026-05) — Songwriting Craft — verify before treating as authoritative

## The premise

The discipline has two parts:

1. **Capture everything.** Every line, every melody, every dream, every overheard phrase. You write it down — not into your head, not "I'll remember it," but onto paper or tape — in the moment it arrives.
2. **Edit ruthlessly later.** Capture and editing are separate phases. While capturing, no judgment. While editing, no mercy. The bridge between them is a quiet sit-down session, often weeks or months later, in which the capture archive becomes the writing material.

This is the practice the [Bob Dylan Archive in Tulsa](https://bobdylancenter.com/archive/) documents through its 100,000+ items of working materials. It is the practice Dylan describes throughout Cameron Crowe's *Biograph* (1985) liner-note interviews. And it is, plainly, the practice that produces a writer who can sit down and finish "Tangled Up in Blue" because he has a notebook full of lines waiting.

For ARLO users, the workflow is one of the most universally applicable in this stream — every writer who has ever lost an idea before they wrote it down has a use for it.

## The capture half — what Dylan actually did

The [Tulsa archive collection](https://woodyguthriecenter.org/archives/collections/bob-dylan-archive/) preserves Dylan's working materials on, as one researcher put it, "notebooks, shards of note pads, hotel stationery, business cards, even bits of brown paper bags." The capture surface is whatever's at hand. The discipline is: *write it before it disappears*.

For *Blood on the Tracks* alone there are three documented notebooks ([No Depression](https://nodepression.org/bob-dylans-three-blood-on-the-tracks-notebooks-not-just-red/)):
- **The "Red Notebook"** — the famous spiral notebook in which Dylan drafted 17 songs during summer 1974 in Minnesota.
- **Notebook 5** — coverless, worn, containing lyrics interleaved with phone numbers, art-supply lists, daily observations.
- **Notebook 6** — pale marine blue, described as "a gold mine" of compositional material.

Notebook 5 is the model. It's not a *songwriting* notebook in the precious sense — it's a *capture* notebook into which everything goes. Lyrics. Phone numbers. Lists. Things he overheard. The discipline is keeping a single capture surface always to hand, not segregating creative material from ordinary life.

The modern equivalent is the phone — voice memos, a Notes app, a single text file. The discipline is the same: one capture surface, always to hand, no editing during capture.

## The revision half — what the notebooks reveal

The notebooks also document the *opposite* discipline: extreme revision. As [Anne Margaret Daniel](https://www.annemargaretdaniel.com/blog/2018/11/30/bob-dylans-blood-on-the-tracks-notebooks-1) describes, nearly every page contains heavy revisions — "scribbles above, below, and in margins" with words added between lines, words crossed out, lines rewritten. She compares the density of revision to W.B. Yeats and finds Dylan even more thorough.

The two halves are not contradictory. They are sequenced. The capture phase is non-judgmental. The revision phase is ruthless. The notebook is the workspace for both — but on different days, in different states of mind.

Dylan himself, quoted in 1965: *"Now somebody else is going to be allowed to see what I said to myself."* That phrase — *what I said to myself* — is the capture phase. The revision phase is what makes the captured material into songs the world hears.

## Why capture and editing must be separate

The deep insight: **the same mental state cannot produce material and judge material.** When you're generating, judgment closes the channel. When you're judging, generation feels precious and protective.

The discipline of two phases — capture (no judgment) and edit (no mercy) — is how working writers across genres handle this. It's the same logic behind Pat Pattison's 10-minute object-writing (no editing during the timer), Dorothea Brande's "morning pages" (in *Becoming a Writer*), Anne Lamott's "shitty first drafts," and Mixerman's "decide once, move on" discipline. Dylan ran it longer and at scale: years of capture, then sit-down editing sessions.

The single biggest practical implication for an ARLO user: **the act of capture is not the act of writing the song.** Capturing is its own discipline. You're not allowed to think about whether the line is good. You're allowed to think about whether you got it down before it disappeared.

## What counts as "worth capturing"

The honest answer from the archive: *everything*. Hotel-stationery scraps. Phone numbers next to lyric fragments. Observations that may or may not become songs. Words you overheard. Half a melody hummed into the cassette in a car.

A practical filter for modern users: if you would feel a flicker of regret losing the thing, capture it. The flicker is the signal. You don't have to know yet whether it will become something. The job of the capture phase is *not* to evaluate. It's to preserve.

## The modern equivalent — phone-based capture

The notebook + cassette discipline maps cleanly to phone-based capture:

- **Voice memos** = the cassette. Hum the melody, sing the line, record the chord progression on the guitar. Don't title carefully; the date is fine.
- **Notes app / single text file** = the notebook. Lyric fragments, overheard phrases, titles, images, dream-words. One file, not 40 files. The friction of opening a new file is enough to lose ideas.
- **Photos** = the visual notebook. A chord diagram you drew on a napkin; a shape your hand is making on the neck.

The key discipline: **open the capture surface immediately.** Not later. Not "I'll remember this." Not "I'll write it down at home." Immediately. The five-second delay is the difference between a captured idea and an idea you'll never see again.

## The periodic review pass

The other half of the practice — and the half most casual notetakers skip — is the **periodic review**. The captured material does not become songs by itself. It becomes songs because the writer sits down with the capture archive and *reads it back*.

A working version:
- **Weekly or monthly cadence** (Dylan's appears to have been less regular; pick yours).
- **Block 60–90 minutes.** Single session.
- **Read every capture from the period.** Voice memos: listen to all of them in order. Text: scroll the file.
- **Mark the live ones.** A simple tag — star, asterisk, highlight. You're identifying which captures still have heat.
- **For the live ones, draft.** Take a starred fragment and write into it for 20 minutes. See what wants to come out.
- **Archive the rest.** They sit. Some will become songs in six months. Most won't. That's fine.

Without the review, the archive grows unused. With the review, it becomes the well you drink from.

## Examples from Dylan's archive

- "Tangled Up in Blue" exists in multiple drafts in the Red Notebook and others. Verses are rewritten, characters renamed, the time-jumping structure refined draft by draft.
- "Idiot Wind" exists in significantly different lyrical forms across drafts.
- *Blood on the Tracks* was drafted across summer 1974 in Minnesota, then revised heavily before recording, then partially re-recorded in Minneapolis after Dylan's brother's notes. The notebooks document the path.

For a user, the relevant takeaway is not that Dylan revised heavily (which he did) but that the *capture archive made the revision possible*. You can only edit what you have on paper. The capture discipline produces the material that the revision discipline finishes.

## When this workflow fits

- You have ideas during walks, showers, driving, sleep — and you keep losing them.
- You're a lyrics-first writer who works in language.
- You have no capture discipline yet, or your captures are scattered across 40 apps.
- You want to write more without forcing more sessions — the capture lets you accumulate material in life.
- You're stuck in a project and have a *feeling* that there's relevant material in your phone notes from six months ago.

## When this workflow does NOT fit

- You already have more material than you can use. Don't capture more; review what you have.
- You're production-first and your ideas are sonic, not linguistic. (Use A9 or A10 instead — the equivalent practice is recording snippets into your DAW's idea-bin.)
- You're in a tight deadline and need to finish, not generate.
- You're prone to capture-as-procrastination (collecting more material instead of finishing songs). The review phase is the fix here.

## Common failure modes

- **Capture without review.** You build a mountain of notes and never read them back. Schedule the review.
- **Capture-as-procrastination.** You always have time to collect more material but never to develop any of it. Force a ratio: for every capture session, one development session.
- **Friction in the capture surface.** You use a notes app that's slow to open, or a notebook that's not in your pocket. Reduce friction or change tools. The five-second delay loses the idea.
- **Captures that aren't legible later.** Voice memos with no context, lyric fragments without dates. Add minimal metadata at capture time — date, location, why you grabbed it.
- **Treating every capture as precious.** It's a capture, not a song. Most won't become anything. The few that do justify the discipline.
