## The premise

"Ideas are like fish," David Lynch writes in [*Catching the Big Fish*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Catching_the_Big_Fish) (Tarcher, 2006). "If you want to catch little fish, you can stay in the shallow water. But if you want to catch the big fish, you've got to go deeper. Down deep, the fish are more powerful and more pure. They're huge and abstract. And they're very beautiful." The book is Lynch's account of how Transcendental Meditation, which he began practicing in 1973, became the engine of his creative process.

For ARLO's purposes, the workflow is a daily practice — not a "get unstuck on Tuesday" tool. It is a *bed* for ideas to land in, plus a discipline for catching them when they do. It works on a horizon of weeks, not minutes. If the user needs a fix-this-stall-tonight tool, they want [`eno-oblique-strategies.md`](./eno-oblique-strategies.md) or [`aimee-mann-specificity-first.md`](./aimee-mann-specificity-first.md) instead.

## Why a filmmaker's practice belongs in a songwriting corpus

Lynch is primarily known for film and television (*Eraserhead*, *Twin Peaks*, *Mulholland Drive*) but he is a credentialed musician — three solo records (*Crazy Clown Time*, *The Big Dream*, the *Twin Peaks* music), a long collaboration with Angelo Badalamenti, soundtrack work, and Lynch's own writing about music in *Catching the Big Fish*. He treats music and image as outputs of the same underlying ideational process, and the language he uses (fragments, fish, abstraction, intuition) maps onto songwriting cleanly.

The cross-medium translation isn't fabricated by ARLO. Lynch himself writes about songs and lyrics as fish in the same school as images and scenes. ARLO inherits the framework.

## The two parts of the practice

There are two parts and they are not the same. Conflating them produces the most common failure mode of this workflow (treating meditation as a creative *exercise* rather than as the preconditions for one).

**Part one is the meditation itself.** TM is a specific technique: 20 minutes, twice a day, eyes closed, seated comfortably, using a mantra given by a certified TM teacher ([TM.org](https://www.tm.org/en-us/blog/david-lynch)). Lynch has practiced it daily, twice a day, since 1973. The practice is *not* visualization or guided imagery or "thinking about your song." It is a specific technique whose mechanism Lynch describes as letting the mind settle to its quietest level.

**Part two is the catching.** When a fragment surfaces — during meditation, after, on a walk, in the shower, in the middle of editing something else — the writer's job is to capture it before it's gone. Lynch is emphatic on this: "If a person forgets an idea that they love, it's a horror." He talks about the big ideas he has lost over the years that he will never get back, and uses that loss as the argument for writing everything down immediately ([The Marginalian](https://www.themarginalian.org/2014/05/09/david-lynch-interview-ideas-creativity/), [Austin Kleon](https://austinkleon.com/2020/09/24/david-lynch-on-ideas/)).

The workflow is the daily pairing of (a) the practice that produces fragments and (b) the capture discipline that doesn't let them escape.

## What counts as "a fish"

Not a finished song. Not even a verse. A fish is:

- A phrase that arrives intact ("the strangest dance," "she came home with the moon")
- A single sonic image (a guitar that sounds like a sigh, drums that drag behind by a tick)
- A character — who the song is *about*, suddenly clear
- A title
- A tempo and a feeling
- An emotional contour — "the song that starts angry and ends laughing"
- A juxtaposition (cathedral and gas station, baby and gun)

What it almost never is: a finished structural plan. That's the work of subsequent sessions, not the fishing trip.

## The capture rules

Lynch's two rules for capture, drawn from his interviews and writing:

1. **Write it down immediately.** Don't wait. Don't trust yourself to remember. Have something near you at all times that can record an idea — Lynch favors paper, but a phone voice memo is the modern equivalent. He has compared losing an idea to losing a loved one.

2. **Write it down *as it arrived.*** Don't pre-edit, don't elaborate, don't try to extract the song from the fragment yet. The fish is the fish. Note it, return to the activity you were doing, leave it for the development pass.

For songwriters, the practical translation is: keep a song-fragment notebook (or notes app, or voice memos folder) that is separate from in-progress songs and separate from your journal. Its only contents are fish.

## How songwriters have adapted the practice

Lynch's TM-specific technique is one path; the broader pattern of *protected interiority + immediate capture* is reachable by many means. Sodajerker and other working-songwriter podcasts repeatedly surface the pattern across writers with no TM connection: Paul Simon's morning walk, Joni Mitchell's after-tea hours alone with the guitar, Dylan's "dreamtime" cassette habit. The mechanism is the same: a daily, defended, low-stimulus interval that lets the mind settle until something surfaces.

Writers without a TM background can substitute:

- Twenty minutes of unguided sitting in silence, twice daily
- A long walk without phone or podcast
- Morning pages (Julia Cameron's variant — three handwritten pages, stream-of-consciousness)
- A daily improvisation session at the instrument with no goal of producing anything

ARLO should be honest here: the specific TM-vs-substitute argument is contested. Some writers find TM transformative; others find equivalent benefit in unguided sitting or daily walks; some find no benefit at all. Lynch's *Catching the Big Fish* is the unambiguous primary source for the TM-specific claim. The "find your daily interval" generalization is ARLO's.

## When this workflow fits

The clean trigger: the user has been forcing material that isn't there. They sit down, produce three drafts, none of which are alive, and they feel creatively dry — not blocked, exactly, but barren. The diagnostic question is "have you written a song you liked in the last month?" If no, and the user has been *trying*, this workflow is the right one.

Also fits:

- The user is between projects and wants the next thing to surface organically
- The user has been deep in production/editing for weeks and feels the well is dry
- The user reports their ideas feel "thin" or "small" or "I can only catch little fish"

## When this workflow does not fit

- The user has a clear assignment with a deadline. Catching the Big Fish is not a sprint tool.
- The user has plenty of material and needs structure/finishing — go to [`rubin-reduction-philosophy.md`](./rubin-reduction-philosophy.md) or [`spoon-two-demos-a-week.md`](./spoon-two-demos-a-week.md).
- The user wants to write a topline over an existing beat — go to [`beat-first-topline-workflow.md`](./beat-first-topline-workflow.md).
- The user is in the middle of finishing a song and is procrastinating by starting a meditation practice. ARLO should call this out gently.

## The "suffering" question — Lynch's productive heresy

Lynch is famous for the position that the artist need not suffer to produce dark or difficult work. "Let your characters do the suffering" is the line. He argues that inner peace and external edginess can coexist; the popular romance of the tormented artist is, in Lynch's view, a productivity drag dressed up as authenticity.

For a songwriter who has been waiting for a *crisis* to unlock the next song, this is a genuine intervention. The implication: build the daily practice *before* you need it, fish for ideas from a stable baseline, and your output will be both deeper and more sustainable than the periodic-collapse method.

This is one school. Nick Cave, Tom Waits, and many others have written some of their best material out of acute personal turbulence. ARLO should not flatten the disagreement.

## Common failure modes

**Treating meditation as the creative session.** It isn't. The practice is the preparation; the creative work still happens at the desk or the instrument. Fish don't write themselves.

**Failing to capture.** The bigger failure than not meditating. Lynch is emphatic — most people lose more ideas than they keep. Without the capture discipline, the practice produces fish that get away.

**Trying to develop fragments during meditation.** Notice them, return to the mantra (or to the breath), capture afterward. Forcing development *during* the practice tends to short-circuit both.

**Picking up the notebook only when stuck.** The capture habit has to be ambient. The big fish often surfaces during a phone call or while doing the dishes. The notebook (or phone) is always within arm's reach, or it isn't a practice.

**Expecting big fish quickly.** Lynch's view is that the depth comes with years of practice. A first-month user may catch shallow fish, miss them, get frustrated, and abandon. The workflow runs on a horizon of months. ARLO should set this expectation.

## The development pass — what the user does with caught fish

Outside the scope of the workflow itself, but worth naming: the user accumulates a fragment notebook over weeks. Once a week or every fortnight, sit down with the notebook and read everything in it. Some fragments will be cold; some will spark. The ones that spark are the songs that want to be written next.

This is the contact between the Lynch practice and the actual song-finishing work. Cross-link [`dylan-notebook-cassette-discipline.md`](./dylan-notebook-cassette-discipline.md) — Dylan's archive review practice is the development-pass discipline for which Lynch's catching is the input.

## A note on Lynch's own music

Lynch's albums — *Crazy Clown Time* (2011), *The Big Dream* (2013), and the soundtrack work on the 2017 *Twin Peaks: The Return* — were produced over decades but feel like they emerged from the same atmosphere that produced *Inland Empire* and the late films. The catalog is a useful audit of what the practice can produce when sustained. The songs are not radio pop. They are slow, image-led, sonically committed. A user who tries the practice and produces something Lynch-adjacent should not be surprised. Trying the practice and producing late-period Beatles is also possible; the practice doesn't dictate the style, only the depth.
