# Beat-First / Topline-Over-Beat — The Hip-Hop and Modern Pop Workflow

## The premise

The producer (or a track-maker) builds the music first — beat, chord progression, sometimes a bass riff or sketch of an arrangement. The lyric and melody — together called the "topline" — are written *over* the existing track. The writer of the topline may never meet the producer. The song's existence depends on this division of labor.

This workflow is the dominant model for hip-hop since its inception (the beat-then-rhyme tradition), for modern pop since the Cheiron/Max Martin era of the 1990s, and for much of contemporary R&B, K-pop, dance, and Latin pop. John Seabrook's *The Song Machine* (Norton, 2015) is the journalistic reference; the formalized lineage runs through Denniz PoP at Cheiron Productions in Stockholm through Max Martin, Shellback, Stargate (Mikkel Eriksen and Tor Erik Hermansen), Dr. Luke, and later toplines from Ester Dean, Bonnie McKee, Julia Michaels, and Justin Tranter.

For songwriters trained in the singer-songwriter tradition — guitar in hand, lyric and music developed together — beat-first feels backwards. It isn't. It's a separate discipline with its own muscles, and many writers thrive in it once they learn the moves.

## The "track-and-hook" model

Seabrook's term for the modern industrial workflow: a producer creates a track (beat + chord progression + arrangement skeleton), then ships it to one or more topline writers. The topline writer auditions melodies and lyrics over the track. The best ideas get demoed. The demo goes through label A&R and ultimately to an artist (who may add their own writes, may not). Multiple writers may be on the same track; multiple tracks may be in play for the same artist on the same day.

The Seabrook narrative makes the industrial scale visible. But the underlying *workflow* — start from a track, write a melody and lyric over it — is independent of the industrial setting. A bedroom producer who's made a 16-bar Logic loop and now wants a song over it is doing the same workflow at a one-person scale.

## Why this is its own discipline

A lyric-and-melody-first writer makes choices about syllable count, meter, and stress from the inside, building the rhythm of the song from the words and notes simultaneously. A beat-first writer inherits the rhythm from the track and has to find the melody and lyric that *fit the pocket the track already established.*

The pocket is the constraint. The track's snare on 2 and 4, the kick pattern, the rhythmic feel of the chord stabs — these dictate where vocal phrases want to start and end. A topline that fights the pocket sounds wrong; a topline that lives in the pocket sounds inevitable.

For many writers, the constraint is liberating. The blank page has been replaced by a structured field. The job becomes finding what fits, rather than inventing from nothing.

## Max Martin's "melodic math"

The most-analyzed beat-first toolkit in modern pop is what the songwriting community has come to call "melodic math" — a set of compositional rules associated with Max Martin and his Cheiron-lineage collaborators. The term was popularized by analyst Asaf Peres and discussed across Switched On Pop, American Songwriter, and music-business outlets.

Verified principles associated with Max Martin's working method:

- **Match syllable count line-to-line.** If line one of the verse is 8 syllables, line two is 8 syllables. Mirror imagery in meter. Adding or removing one syllable changes the melody enough that Martin treats it as a different melody.
- **Land the strong syllable on the strong beat.** The accented word in the line falls on a downbeat or strong subdivision; weak words fall on weak beats.
- **Match the melodic shape across symmetrical sections.** Verse 1 and verse 2 use the same melodic skeleton; the chorus is the chorus is the chorus.
- **Hook previews.** Plant fragments of the hook in the verse or pre-chorus so the chorus feels inevitable when it arrives.
- **Engineer the climax.** The chorus's highest note typically lands on the title or key phrase.

These rules are documented across multiple Switched On Pop and Peres analyses ([Top40 Theory](https://www.top40theory.com/melodic-math-course); [American Songwriter](https://americansongwriter.com/all-those-pop-songs-you-love-thats-not-magic-thats-melodic-math-and-this-is-how-it-works/); [Music Business Worldwide](https://www.musicbusinessworldwide.com/how-max-martins-songwriting-techniques-are-used-to-write-hit-after-hit-after-hit/)). ARLO should call them a *school*, not a universal rule — they describe Martin's practice and a wider lineage, not all of pop.

## The hip-hop origin

Beat-first as a discipline did not begin with Max Martin. The hip-hop tradition — from the 1970s block parties through MC Shan, Rakim, Nas, Eminem, Lauryn Hill, Kendrick Lamar — has always been beat-then-rhyme. The DJ or producer made the beat; the MC wrote to it. The lineage runs through Marley Marl, Pete Rock, DJ Premier, J Dilla, the Neptunes, Pharrell, Kanye, Mike WiLL Made-It, Metro Boomin.

The hip-hop beat-first move differs from pop melodic math in one key respect: the lyric is often more important than the melody, and the pocket is more rhythmic than tonal. But the underlying workflow — start from the track, write the lyric to fit — is the same.

## What actually happens in the room (or remotely)

Seabrook's reporting and many Sodajerker interviews describe a recognizable session shape:

1. The producer opens the track. Plays it on loop.
2. The topline writer listens without writing, sometimes for ten or twenty minutes, letting the track suggest a vocal energy and pocket.
3. The writer starts mumbling and humming over the track — what Ester Dean called working from "grunts and groans" toward words, with phrases pulled from a notebook or phone list of stockpiled images.
4. A melodic idea catches. The writer demos it on a scratch vocal mic.
5. Words are filled in, often after the melody is set. The melody-first / lyric-second order is normative in this workflow.
6. The hook is found first if possible — once the chorus is real, verses can be written backwards from it.
7. Demos go through revision passes; ones that don't catch are scrapped after a session.

This is one shape. Some topline writers prefer to write the lyric first and then fit it to the track. Some pairs work word-and-melody together in real time. The common thread is that the *track exists first*.

## When this workflow fits

The clean trigger: the user has a beat, a loop, a track, or an instrumental sketch — and they want a song over it. Or: the user has been forcing lyric-first songwriting and producing the same kind of song over and over, and wants out.

Other fits:

- The user is a producer who's made a track and doesn't know how to write the topline
- The user is a topliner working remotely with producers
- The user writes better with constraints — the open page is hostile, the track is friendly
- The user is in a genre where beat-first is the norm (hip-hop, modern pop, R&B, electronic, dance, Latin)
- The user has a co-write partner who makes tracks but doesn't write lyrics

When this workflow does not fit:

- The user is a fingerpicking singer-songwriter where the lyric, melody, and guitar evolve together as one organism (the workflow can be learned, but the user should know it's a discipline change)
- The user has a story-song where the lyric's narrative arc is the song
- The user is in the early ideation stage and doesn't have a track yet (use [`david-lynch-catching-big-fish.md`](./david-lynch-catching-big-fish.md) or [`tom-cosm-8-bar-loop-cycle.md`](./tom-cosm-8-bar-loop-cycle.md) to build to a track first)

## The mechanical workflow

1. **Listen to the track 5–10 times before writing anything.** Let the pocket land. Notice where the snare hits, where the chord changes are, where the empty space is, where the track wants vocal energy. Do not start writing yet.

2. **Find the empty space.** The places in the loop where there are no big production elements are where the vocal naturally goes. The track's emptiness is the writer's room.

3. **Mumble first.** Vocal melody on nonsense syllables. Don't try to write a lyric yet. The melody comes first because the melody has to fit the pocket; if you start with words, the words will fight the pocket.

4. **Find the chorus first if possible.** The hook is the song's identity. Once the chorus is real, verses can be written to lead into it.

5. **Mirror the meter line-to-line.** If verse-1 line-1 is 9 syllables, verse-1 line-2 wants to be 9 syllables (or a deliberate variation like 11). Verse 2 should match verse 1's syllable count line by line.

6. **Land strong words on strong beats.** Mark the downbeats; the accented words go there.

7. **Plant hook previews.** A fragment of the chorus melody or lyric appearing in the verse or pre-chorus makes the chorus feel inevitable when it lands.

8. **Engineer the climax.** Chorus's highest note typically lands on the title or key phrase. If it doesn't, consider why.

9. **Demo on scratch vocal.** Get it down. Listen back. Edit.

## Common failure modes

**Fighting the pocket.** The most common failure. The writer ignores the track's rhythmic skeleton and tries to phrase the vocal in the patterns they're used to from lyric-first writing. The result is a melody that sounds disconnected from the production. Fix: re-listen to the track and identify the snare hits; phrase the vocal to land *with* them, not *across* them.

**Writing the lyric first.** Lyric-first inside a beat-first workflow tends to produce a topline where the syllable counts don't match the pocket. Even melodic-math writers usually melody-first, lyric-second. The lyric can be drafted as nonsense syllables that mark rhythm, then filled in.

**Ignoring the track's emptiness.** Some sections of the track are dense; some are sparse. The vocal goes in the sparse parts. Stacking vocals on top of an already-busy production decision is a mix problem the topline shouldn't create.

**Not matching meter section-to-section.** Verse 1's first line is 7 syllables; verse 2's first line is 11. The melody can't match unless the writer rewrites — and the listener feels the mismatch even without naming it. The discipline is to lock the syllable count once verse 1 is set.

**Treating the workflow as creatively diminished.** Some writers from the singer-songwriter tradition view beat-first as a lesser discipline. It's a different discipline. Max Martin's catalog is not a body of bad songs; it's a body of songs made under a different set of constraints. The constraints are real and learning to work inside them is a skill.

**Topline shopping the wrong track.** If the track is uninspiring, no topline will save it. The first listen should be a decision: is there a song here? If not, ask for a different track.

## The lyric question — pop melodic math vs. hip-hop bars

In melodic-math pop, the lyric serves the melody. The line "I'm in your eyes, somehow" works because it lands on the right beats and contains the title; the meaning is built from a small palette of accessible imagery.

In hip-hop, the lyric *is* the song. The bars deliver characterization, narrative, wordplay, and meaning, with the beat as the framework. A great hip-hop topline can be intricate prosody (Rakim, MF DOOM, Kendrick Lamar) where the lyric demands re-listening.

Both are beat-first workflows. The internal weight of lyric vs. melody differs by genre. ARLO should not flatten this — when the user is writing pop topline, the melodic-math discipline is the rule; when the user is writing rap bars, the prosody-and-flow discipline (different topic, separate corpus file) takes over.

## Where the workflow connects to the user's bigger writing practice

A producer-songwriter who hasn't internalized lyric-and-melody-first writing may stay stuck in beat-first forever — and produce tracks that are sonically inventive but lyrically thin. A singer-songwriter who refuses to learn beat-first will lock out a large portion of contemporary collaboration. ARLO's stance: both disciplines are worth learning, and the user choosing this workflow is deliberately leaning into the beat-first muscle. That's fine.
