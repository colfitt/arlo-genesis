## The premise

The research-then-write discipline says: **deep research into a subject produces a kind of specific imagery and emotional connection that direct-write-from-self cannot.** You spend weeks or months reading, traveling, interviewing, taking notes — *without writing songs yet*. The songs emerge later, from the research, with specifics in them that no one could have invented in a single writing session.

The clearest documented practitioner is Sufjan Stevens, particularly on *Illinois* (2005). Stevens read Carl Sandburg and Saul Bellow, studied immigration records and Illinois history, contacted friends and "internet chat room users" for anecdotes, took trips through state locations, and *then* wrote songs that weave personal narrative through historical fact ([Wikipedia, citing Stevens interviews](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Illinois_(Sufjan_Stevens_album))). The album's research phase ran most of 2004; recording was late 2004 into early 2005.

The principle generalizes. It's the practice behind every album whose specificity feels excavated rather than imagined — Joanna Newsom's *Have One on Me*, Phoebe Bridgers's *Punisher* (the Skyped-into-Memphis research), Andrew Bird's bird-and-language obsessions, Sufjan's own *Illinois* and (in a different mode) *Carrie & Lowell*. It is genuinely a school. It is also genuinely contested — songwriters in the Pattison-school object-writing tradition will note that *any* writing session begins with sensory specificity, and that research is just object-writing for a topic. Both are right; the difference is duration and depth.

## What "research" actually means here

For an *Illinois*-style album, research has three documented components:

1. **Reading.** Stevens read literature by Illinois authors (Sandburg, Bellow), biographies (Lincoln), and history books, including out-of-print illustrated works on small towns like Peoria and Jacksonville ([Under the Radar 2005 interview](https://www.undertheradarmag.com/interviews/throwback_thursday_sufjan_stevens_interview_from_2005/)).
2. **Field gathering.** Trips through Illinois locations. He also reached out to friends who had lived there and posted in internet forums asking for personal anecdotes.
3. **Document study.** Immigration records, criminal records (the John Wayne Gacy material), local newspapers — "criminal, literary, and historical documents" interwoven with personal stories ([Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Illinois_(Sufjan_Stevens_album))).

The discipline during research is **not** writing songs. It is taking notes — accumulating images, names, dates, phrases, juxtapositions. The note-taking phase is closer to what a documentary filmmaker does than to what a songwriter typically does.

## Why the research has to come first

The deep insight: **a song's specificity feels different depending on where it came from.** A specific image you invented from imagination has a slightly thin quality. A specific image you encountered in research — a name on an immigration record, a phrase in a 1937 history book, a story your friend told you about her aunt — carries an irreducible weight. The listener can't always articulate why one song sounds documentary and the other feels made-up, but the difference is audible.

Stevens described the principle in his early-2000s interviews ([Laura Kay Kelly excerpt](https://www.laurakaykelly.com/post/sufjan-stevens-vintage-interview-there-s-a-problem-with-music-that-is-purely-emotive)): there's "a problem with music that is purely emotive." Emotion without grounded specifics drifts toward sentiment. Research-grounded specifics keep the song honest.

Stevens also explicitly traces this back to his first love, fiction writing, which "values concrete terms and sensory information." The discipline is literary: he's importing fiction-writer practice into songwriting.

## The internal-research variant — *Carrie & Lowell*

*Carrie & Lowell* (2015) is the other documented case, and it's instructive because the research is *internal*. After his mother Carrie's death in 2012, Stevens did not research Illinois history. He did research his own memory, his relationship with his mother and stepfather Lowell, and (per [Radio Milwaukee's 2025 reevaluation interview](https://radiomilwaukee.org/discover-music/artist-interviews/2025-05-28/sufjan-stevens-carrie-lowell-review)) he *fabricated* substantial material — imagined conversations and scenarios — because his actual relationship with Carrie had been limited.

This is important. The research-then-write workflow is not "go to the library." It is *spend deep, structured, non-songwriting time with the material until the material has specifics in it the song can use*. For *Illinois* that meant external research. For *Carrie & Lowell* it meant grief work, memory-excavation, and acts of fabrication treated as research into imagined possibilities.

Stevens, on the *Carrie & Lowell* process, in the 2025 reflection: *"I write every day. I'm always in a practice of writing, songwriting. So it wasn't as if I sat down to write an album about my mother; it just sort of happened."* The research was the daily practice; the album emerged from it.

## The threshold question — when does research end and writing begin?

The hardest question in this workflow. Research has the failure mode of becoming procrastination — you read forever and never write. Stevens's own threshold appears to have been somewhere around "I have enough specific imagery to start a song." Not "I know everything." Not "I can defend a thesis." Just *enough texture for one song's worth of writing*.

A practical version of the threshold:
- You've filled a notebook (or several) with notes, images, names, fragments.
- You catch yourself wanting to write a *specific song* about a specific thing in your notes — a person, an event, an image.
- You can name three concrete details that belong in that song.
- You feel the song pulling more than you feel the research pulling.

When all four hit, start writing. The research continues in parallel — you'll go back to it for the second song and the third — but the song-writing phase has begun.

## The risk — research as procrastination

The workflow's most well-known failure mode is research-as-procrastination. Reading is pleasant. Taking notes feels productive. Writing a song is hard. You can spend a year preparing to write the album and never write it.

Defenses:
- **Set a research-phase deadline.** "Three months of research, then I start writing." Even if research continues, songwriting starts at the deadline.
- **Cap the daily research time.** "60 minutes of research per day, no more." Forces the rest of the day to be for writing or living.
- **Force a weekly draft.** Once you're 2 weeks into research, you must produce at least one rough draft per week even if it's bad. The drafts feed back into what research you do next.
- **Honest self-check.** When you reach for more research, ask: *am I doing this because I'm missing something, or because I'm afraid to start the song?* Both answers happen. Be honest.

## What this workflow produces

When it works, the songs have a documentary-emotional hybrid quality. They feel found rather than invented. Specifics that you couldn't invent — a real person's name, a real date, a real geographic detail — sit alongside imagined material. The combination produces a kind of authority.

This is the *Illinois* effect. "Casimir Pulaski Day" interweaves a real Illinois state holiday (commemorating a Polish-American Revolutionary War hero) with a personal grief narrative. The state-holiday research is not decoration; it's load-bearing. Without it, the song is generic grief. With it, the song is *this specific Tuesday morning, this specific friend, this specific place where it's also Pulaski Day*.

## The "make the personal universal via the specific" rule

The research-then-write workflow runs on a version of an old fiction-writing rule: **specificity is the path to universality.** A vague song about loss feels false. A specific song about losing this specific person on this specific day in this specific place can be heard as universal — the listener's own losses load into the specifics.

Sufjan, [in the 2025 Carrie & Lowell reflection](https://www.npr.org/2025/05/27/1253209950/sufjan-stevens-interview-carrie-and-lowell), characterizes the album as a kind of failure — that he "failed to locate the clarity he was searching for." This is interesting because for many listeners the album achieved exactly the universal-through-specific effect. Stevens's own measure of success was different from the listener's. Both can be true.

## When this workflow fits

- You're writing a thematic album or a concept project — songs about a place, a person, a historical event, a body of work.
- You're stuck on what to write about and want a subject larger than your immediate life.
- You're a fiction-and-poetry reader who already takes notes naturally.
- You're working on a long project (months, not weeks) and can sustain a research phase.
- You suspect your work has been thin on specifics and you want the texture research provides.

## When this workflow does NOT fit

- You're writing from immediate feeling and the research would dilute the urgency.
- You're under tight deadline. Research won't fit.
- You're prone to research-procrastination. The workflow will eat you. Use a tighter cadence (Spoon-school demos) instead.
- The album is collaborative and your collaborators write from feeling, not from research.
- You've never tried writing about a non-self subject before. Maybe try one song first, not a whole album.

## How to start — a one-album version

1. **Pick the subject.** A place, a person, a body of work, an event. Specific enough that you can name it in one sentence.
2. **Set a research period.** Default 6–8 weeks. Hold the line.
3. **Set the cap.** 60 minutes a day, max, on research. Daily.
4. **Keep one notes file.** Everything goes in it. Quotes, images, names, dates, fragments. No songwriting yet.
5. **At week 2, start the writing cadence.** One draft per week minimum, even if bad. The drafts are how you find out what the research is teaching you.
6. **At the deadline, the writing phase begins.** Research continues in parallel but ceases to be the primary activity.

## Common failure modes

- **Endless research.** You read for a year and write nothing. Fix: enforce the deadline. Force the first draft.
- **Research with no notes.** You read and assume you'll remember. Fix: capture everything, daily, into one file (cross-link Dylan notebook discipline).
- **Songs that read like Wikipedia summaries.** You're loading the song with research instead of weaving it through personal narrative. Fix: every song needs a *you* in it, even if disguised. Research is the texture, not the theme.
- **Topic exhaustion before the songs are done.** You picked a subject too narrow. Either expand it or accept the shorter EP.
- **Performance anxiety from "high concept" framing.** You're paralyzed by trying to match Stevens. Lower the bar; write one weird research-grounded song and see what happens.
