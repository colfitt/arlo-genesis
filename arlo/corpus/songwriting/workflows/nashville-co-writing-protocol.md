## The premise

Music Row in Nashville operates on a documented, repeatable co-writing protocol that has produced the majority of mainstream country songs (and a large share of the modern country-pop, country-rock, and Nashville-affiliated singer-songwriter material) for several decades. The protocol is not a vibe; it is a working method with concrete rules. Writers who learn the protocol can walk into a room cold and have a finished song by the end of the day.

This file documents the protocol as taught by NSAI (Nashville Songwriters Association International), as written by Jason Blume in *Six Steps to Songwriting Success*, and as discussed in interviews with working Music Row writers (Liz Rose, Bobby Braddock, Don Schlitz, Holly Gleason, Don Henry, and others). The protocol applies most cleanly to Nashville country and country-adjacent writing; it adapts for pop and other co-writing contexts but should not be assumed universal.

The single most important framing: **this is a *room* discipline.** Most of what follows breaks down if writers are working asynchronously over email and file-shares.

## The room — who, how many, how long

The typical Nashville co-write session: **2–3 writers**, occasionally 4. Sessions run **roughly 3 hours**, with a hard cap around 4. The aim is **one song per session**. If you don't have a song at the end, you have a draft to finish later — but the discipline is to finish.

Roles in the room vary by writer combination, but a common configuration is **one strong topliner (melody), one strong lyricist, and one strong "hooker"** (someone whose strength is hook concepts and titles). Writers often rotate roles depending on the song. Some sessions add a track producer who brings a beat/track ([Songpact](https://songpact.com/songpact-blog/co-writing-splits-co-writer-agreements-guide)); this is increasingly common in country-pop and is the bridge to the modern toplining workflow (A8).

The room has a single working table or guitar circle. Phones are mostly off. The vibe is professional but warm — closer to a doctors-collaborating-on-a-case than to a jam session. NSAI's [5 Tips for Co-Writing](https://www.nashvillesongwriters.com/5-tips-co-writing) explicitly encourages "allowing time for connection" — first-time co-writes start with conversation, catching up, getting to know each other before any songwriting starts.

## The pre-write

Working Nashville writers do not show up empty-handed. NSAI's workshop guidance ([George Teren](https://nashvillemusicguide.com/nsai-the-basics-of-co-writing/)) calls this the "pre-writing" phase: before the session, each writer accumulates **titles, hook concepts, and partial ideas** in a personal list. Some writers maintain a running "title book" they bring to every session.

Before the session begins, the writers share what they brought. The first half-hour is often spent hunting for a concept — a title, a hook, an angle — that "has legs." A concept has legs when:

- The writers can all imagine the chorus.
- It's specific enough to write toward but open enough to develop.
- It's not too close to a recent hit.
- It connects to a current commercial slot (a specific artist, a specific genre direction).

Once the concept is picked, the song's *target* is also often named: "this is for [artist], or for [type of artist], or for the cut date next month." The target shapes the writing.

## The order of work — chorus first

The Music Row convention is **write the chorus first**. This is doctrinal in NSAI's teaching and in Blume's *Six Steps*. The reasoning:

- The chorus carries the title (which is usually the hook).
- If the chorus doesn't work, the verses won't save it.
- Writing the chorus first forces the writers to agree on what the song is actually about.

Once the chorus is solid, verses follow. The bridge (if there is one) is often written last. Many modern country songs skip the bridge or use a minimal "tag" instead. The structure decisions are usually informed by what's getting radio play in the relevant slot.

## The "no bad ideas" opening, the "kill your darlings" close

The protocol has two opposing modes that the writers move between:

- **Generative mode** (early): no bad ideas. Anything goes. Throw out lines, melodies, concepts without judgment. This protects the creative flow and keeps the room open. NSAI's *5 Tips* explicitly notes: "you can fix a song that's rough around the edges, but you can't fix a song you don't write."
- **Editorial mode** (later): ruthless. The writers actively cut lines that don't serve the song. Killed darlings include your own best line if it doesn't fit. The discipline of letting your best line go for the song's sake is one of the protocol's hardest demands.

A working room can shift between modes multiple times per session. Skilled writers signal the shift verbally ("OK, let's tighten this"). Less experienced writers can stall in either mode — too generative and the song never closes; too editorial and nothing gets written.

## Veto power and "what if instead"

A working convention: **any writer can veto any line**, but the veto comes with an obligation. You don't just say "no" — you say *"what if instead...?"* and propose. This keeps the room collaborative rather than adversarial. NSAI's workshops teach this directly: avoid settling for mediocrity, but propose alternatives rather than block.

Working writers describe the discipline as needing strong opinions, lightly held. You advocate hard for what you think serves the song, but you let go quickly when the room moves a different direction. The Golden Rule, per NSAI's George Teren: "Do unto your co-writers what you would have your co-writer do unto you."

## The split-sheet protocol

The split sheet is the **contractual record of who owns what percentage of the song**. It is signed at the end of the session, before anyone leaves the room. Skipping or deferring the split-sheet conversation is one of the protocol's named failure modes — disputes years later are vastly harder to resolve than a five-minute conversation in the room.

### What the split sheet contains

Per [Songpact's guide](https://songpact.com/songpact-blog/co-writing-splits-co-writer-agreements-guide):

- **Song title** (working title is fine).
- **Writer names**, full legal names.
- **Agreed percentages**, summing to 100%.
- **PRO membership** for each writer (ASCAP, BMI, SESAC, or international equivalent).
- **IPI / CAE numbers** — the international identifier for each writer.
- **Publisher information** if a writer has a publisher.
- **Contact info** — email, phone.

The sheet doesn't have to be elaborate. A photo of a handwritten sheet, or a digital one signed in Songpact/Splitsifter/email, is enough. What matters is *that it exists* and is signed by all writers the day of the session.

### The Nashville Rule — equal splits

The default Music Row convention is **equal splits between everyone in the room**, regardless of who contributed what. Three writers = 33⅓% each. Two writers + a producer who brought the track = often three-way equal, sometimes producer gets a different cut.

The reasoning ([LANDR](https://blog.landr.com/songwriting-splits-explained/)): contribution is genuinely hard to quantify. The lyricist who wrote one perfect line may have done more than the topliner who wrote four mediocre verses. Equal splits avoid an unwinnable post-hoc argument. They also preserve the relationship — co-writers who fight about splits don't write together again.

### The "change a word, get a third" convention

A controversial Nashville convention: if a writer with star-level leverage changes a single word, they get a full writer credit and corresponding percentage. Whether you accept this depends on your situation. The honest version of the rule: leverage matters in splits. Writers who are willing to walk away from a deal preserve negotiation power. Writers who need the cut will accept terms that reflect that need.

### Contribution-based alternative

Some writers prefer **contribution-based splits**: percentages that reflect actual input. Lyrics 50%, melody 40%, arrangement 10%, for example. This is more common in pop and rock contexts than in classic Nashville. The discipline is the same: agree before recording, sign before leaving the room.

## Taking the song home vs. finishing in the room

The aspirational version of the protocol is "finish in the room." A song that leaves the session unfinished often dies in email rounds. But experienced writers recognize when the room has done what it can:

- **Finish in the room** when: chorus, verses, bridge if needed, all sections complete; you can play the song from start to finish; everyone leaves with the same demo file or working lyric sheet.
- **Take it home** when: there's a structural question that needs sitting with; a verse isn't landing and a fresh ear will help; the room is fading and forcing the close will hurt the song.

If you take it home, the protocol still wants a finishing deadline. NSAI workshop guidance: agree on a return date before leaving. "We'll have V2 and bridge by Friday." Otherwise the song stalls indefinitely.

## When the session is going badly

A real Nashville co-write can be uncomfortable. Three creative people in a room for three hours produces friction. Working writers have signals:

- **Hour 1 with no concept.** Probably the wrong room or the wrong day. NSAI's *5 Tips* explicitly says "sometimes your writing session turns into a therapy session, and that is ok!" — but the session also has to remain a writing session at some level.
- **Two writers carrying the third.** Either reset the room dynamic or accept that this session is one of those where one writer pulled back. Adjust splits if it's structural; let it go if it's a one-off.
- **A line you love that no one wants.** The veto is in play. Either propose-and-let-go, or save the line for another song.
- **Genuine creative mismatch.** Some writer pairs don't work. Finish the session politely, mark each other off the list, move on.
- **Push through or call it.** Around the 2.5-hour mark, working writers know: is there a song here, or are we manufacturing? If the latter, finish what you have and call it.

## The downstream — publishers, tape day, pitch

The Music Row protocol doesn't end at the session. The song moves into:

- **Demo recording.** Sometimes the writers record the demo themselves; sometimes a publisher commissions a demo (a "tape day"). The demo is what gets pitched.
- **Publisher pitch.** Each writer's publisher (or the unattached writer themselves) pitches the song to artists, A&R, producers. Many songs are written for specific cut dates; others are pitched cold.
- **Cuts and royalties.** If the song is recorded by an artist, royalties flow through the PROs to the writers per the split sheet. This is why the split sheet is contractually load-bearing.

This downstream isn't optional in the Music Row context — it's why the protocol exists. The protocol is shaped by the goal: write commercial songs that get cut. A first-time co-writer doesn't have to engage the full downstream, but the protocol's shape is informed by it.

## Adapting outside Nashville

The room protocol adapts cleanly to pop co-writes, less cleanly to indie collaborative writing. Pop adaptations:
- Often more writers in the room (4–6 for major-label sessions).
- Producer + topliner + lyricist roles more separated.
- Splits often pre-negotiated by publishers before the session.

Indie adaptations:
- Smaller rooms (2 writers).
- Equal splits are still the default.
- Less pre-write structure; more jamming.
- The split sheet discipline is *still* worth preserving even in casual indie co-writes.

## When this workflow fits

- You have a co-write scheduled and don't know the rules of engagement.
- You're writing for a commercial slot (artist cut, sync, publisher pitch).
- You write better with structure and other writers in the room.
- You're a strong specialist (lyricist OR topliner OR hooker) and want to partner with complementary writers.

## When this workflow does NOT fit

- You're a solo writer who works best alone.
- You're writing personal-confessional material with no commercial intent.
- The co-writer in question isn't on the same protocol page (mismatched expectations destroy sessions).
- You're allergic to the chorus-first discipline (some songs really do start from the verse).
- You're in a stem-trading remote collaboration, not a room. The protocol adapts but a lot of its discipline depends on the room.

## Common failure modes

- **Skipping the split sheet.** Highest-priority failure. Always sign before leaving.
- **Hour-1 generative drift.** No concept by hour 1. Force a concept by hour 1.5 or end early.
- **Stuck in editorial mode.** The room kills every line. Reset to generative, agree to come back to editing in 30 minutes.
- **Writers not on the same time scale.** One writer wants 6 hours, another wants 2. Agree on length at the start.
- **Mismatched stakes.** One writer treats it as a session for a cut date; another treats it as casual. Discuss the target before starting.
- **Veto without proposal.** Killing lines without "what if instead" — the room turns adversarial. Enforce the proposal rule.
- **Taking the song home without a deadline.** Songs die in indefinite email rounds. Agree the return date in the room.
