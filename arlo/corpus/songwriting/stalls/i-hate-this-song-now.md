# Stall: The "I Hate This Song Now" Reset

**Scope:** songwriting
**Source:** Deep-research synthesis (2026-05) — Songwriting Craft — verify before treating as authoritative
**Canonical references:** Dennis DeSantis, *Making Music* (fresh-ears chapters); Mike Senior, *Mixing Secrets for the Small Studio* (consumer-systems rotation); Sodajerker on Songwriting interviews on writers' love/hate cycles with their own material; Ira Glass on the taste gap
**Tags:** songwriting-craft, finishing-songs, stall-recovery, aversion, fresh-ears, finishing-psychology, repetition-blindness

## Why it stalls

You loved the song last week. This week you hate it. The chorus that lit up the room a month ago now grates. The lyric that felt true now feels embarrassing. You consider deleting the project. You wonder if you ever had any taste.

This stall has two completely different root causes, and the recovery move depends on which one you're in. The cruelty of the stall is that, from inside it, the two causes feel identical.

**Cause one: repetition-blindness (or repetition-deafness).** You've listened to the song hundreds of times in the last two weeks. Your auditory system has adapted to it. The novelty is gone. Every part is over-familiar. This is a known phenomenon in music perception research — repeated exposure to a stimulus reduces emotional response over time, regardless of the stimulus's quality. Dennis DeSantis describes this in *Making Music* as the "monitoring blindness" that affects everyone who self-mixes, and the cure is distance, not revision. The song is fine. Your ear is tired.

**Cause two: the dead ear is honest.** Sometimes you hate the song because the song *is* the level of bad you can now see but couldn't see when you wrote it. Ira Glass's taste-gap framing applies: your taste outgrew your work, and you're seeing the gap. The song that thrilled you a month ago revealed problems you didn't have the ear to notice. This is also a known phenomenon — writers' taste develops faster than their craft, and they hate yesterday's work because they have today's ear.

The diagnostic problem is that both causes produce identical phenomenology: "I hate this song." The treatment is different. Treating repetition-blindness as a song problem leads to over-revision that destroys what was working. Treating a real taste-gap problem as "just take a break" leads to shipping a song you'll regret.

The recovery: separate first to diagnose which cause, then act. Most of the moves below are diagnostic before they're corrective.

## The 6 recovery moves

### Move 1 — Separation: one full week, no session

The cheapest move and the most diagnostic. Close the session. Do not open it for one week. Do not listen to the bounce. Do not think about the song. If possible, work on a completely different song or a different creative project. Let your ear cool.

After seven days, listen to the bounce — once, on a system you don't usually mix on (your phone, a friend's car, headphones you don't normally use). Don't take notes. Don't analyze. Just listen.

Your reaction tells you the diagnosis. If you hear the song fresh and it lands — you were in repetition-blindness, the song is fine, ship it. If you hear it fresh and the problems are unignorable — the dead ear was honest, and now you know what to fix.

Mike Senior's *Mixing Secrets* describes the consumer-systems rotation as the essential translation check; for the "hate the song" stall, the rotation is also a diagnostic for whether you have a perception problem or a song problem.

### Move 2 — The outside-ear test: does anyone else hear what you hear

Send the song to one trusted person — another writer, a producer, a musician whose taste you respect — without telling them about your relationship to the song. Ask one question: "What's your honest reaction to this?"

Their reaction tells you the diagnosis. If they like it and you don't, the problem is probably your ear (repetition-blindness). If they raise specific issues that match what you hate, the problem is the song. If they like parts you hate and dislike parts you like, the problem is neither — it's that you're comparing the song to a version of itself that exists only in your head.

The outside ear has not been calibrated to your song. They are hearing it for the first time. Their reaction is approximately what a stranger's reaction would be on first listen — which is the actual condition the song will face in the world. Trust their hearing more than yours during this stall.

### Move 3 — The "name three specific things you hate" exercise

Vagueness is dead-ear. Specificity is real information. Try to name three specific things you hate about the song. Not "the whole thing." Not "the vibe." Specific items: "The kick is too clicky." "The second line of verse 2 is a cliché." "The vocal sounds nasal in the bridge."

If you can name three specific things, those are real problems and you can fix them. Make a session, fix those three things, ship. If you cannot name three specific things — if every attempt is general dissatisfaction, "I just don't like it anymore" — you are in repetition-blindness. The song doesn't need revision; your ear needs rest.

The discipline is honesty about the specifics. Often the writer thinks they hate the song but actually hates one specific thing that has poisoned the rest by association. Identifying the specific thing isolates the actual problem.

### Move 4 — The partial-rewrite option

If specific things are wrong but the bones of the song are sound, the move is partial rewrite, not abandonment. Keep the chorus, rewrite the verses. Or keep the verses, rewrite the chorus. Or keep everything but replace the bridge. The discipline is to commit to keeping *something* — to acknowledge that part of the song is working — rather than throwing the whole song out.

Sodajerker interviews are full of working writers describing songs that almost died but were rescued by a partial rewrite — a single new verse, a replaced chorus, a key change, a tempo change. The original draft contained the song; it just contained the song wrapped in some material that wasn't working.

The risk of this move: deciding too quickly which part to keep. If you've been in the hate-the-song mode for a week, your judgment about what's working is suspect. Combine with Move 2 — let the outside ear tell you which part is the keeper.

### Move 5 — Change of context

Different speakers. Different room. Different time of day. Different headphones. The song you hate on your studio monitors at 11pm may sound completely different on car speakers at noon, on a phone at the gym, on AirPods on a walk.

The principle: your taste is not absolute; it is calibrated by listening context. The song that grates in the studio because you've heard it 200 times there might land fresh in the car, where you've never listened to it. Some songs reveal themselves only in specific listening environments — Sodajerker has interviewed writers who describe songs that "didn't make sense" until they heard them in a car or on a friend's stereo.

The move: bounce the current state, load it onto your phone, listen in three different places over a day. Note where the song works and where it doesn't. The song that works in some contexts and not others is fine; the song that doesn't work in any context has real problems.

### Move 6 — The abandon decision: graveyard it and write a new one

Sometimes the song is dead. The writer's taste outgrew it, the song's problems are real and pervasive, and no amount of revision is going to make the writer love it again. The honest move is to graveyard the song — move it to a folder of "shipped, won't release" or "archive, don't return to" — and write a new song.

This is hard because the writer feels they have to justify the time they already spent. But sunk cost is sunk; the choice now is whether to keep losing time on the dead song or to redirect to a song that's alive. Steven Pressfield's framing in *The War of Art* is that the artist's job is to keep producing, not to defend any single piece of work. The graveyard folder honors the work without forcing the writer to release something they hate.

The criterion: if you wouldn't be proud to share the song with a stranger right now, AND a week of separation AND the outside-ear test AND a partial rewrite haven't changed that — the song is graveyard material. Archive it, save the lessons, write the next one. Some of those graveyard songs come back years later when the writer rediscovers them with fresh ears and finds the song they couldn't see at the time.

See [abandoning-vs-persisting](abandoning-vs-persisting.md) for the full decision framework.

## Socratic prompts ARLO uses verbatim

When ARLO detects the I-hate-this-song stall, it pastes these:

1. "When was the last time you listened to this song without working on it? How many days ago?"
2. "Name three specific things you hate. Not 'the vibe' — specific items. EQ choices, lyric lines, chord moves."
3. "If a friend sent you this exact song and asked your honest opinion, what would you say?"
4. "When you wrote this song, what did you love about it? Is that still in the song? Is it still there but you've stopped hearing it?"
5. "Have you played this song in your car? On your phone walking? On a friend's speakers? What did it sound like in each?"
6. "If you had to keep one section of the song and rewrite the rest, which section would you keep?"
7. "Is there one specific moment in the song that still moves you when you hear it fresh? What's it doing?"
8. "When did you last write a new song? Are you sure you hate this song, or are you avoiding the blank page?"
9. "If you had to ship this song or delete it forever — no third option — which would you choose, right now?"

## When to abandon and re-arrange

The hardest part of this stall is that the move depends on the diagnosis, and the diagnosis is hard to make from inside the hate. The default rule: never abandon a song you hate without first taking a week off and getting an outside ear. The hate is often perception, not song quality, and abandoning under repetition-blindness conditions costs you songs that were actually fine.

Signals the song should be abandoned (even after separation):

- A week of distance did not change your reaction; the fresh listen confirmed the hate
- The outside ear confirmed specific issues you can name; those issues are structural and would require rewriting most of the song
- You've already attempted partial rewrites and still don't love it
- The song's subject — what it was about — no longer feels true or meaningful to you, and you can't reframe it
- You haven't written a new song in weeks because this dead one is occupying the workbench

Signals the song should be shipped despite the hate:

- The outside ear says it's good
- You can name what's working but the gestalt feels stale
- Your hate is gestalt, not specific
- The song has been finished for a while and you've just been waiting; this is shipping anxiety dressed up as song-hate

The move when the signals say ship: ship. The song that you hate at the moment of shipping has the same chance of finding listeners as the song you loved at the moment of shipping. Your relationship to a song often returns to balance once it's out of your hands; the love sometimes comes back when you hear it in the world, not in the studio.

When the signals say abandon: archive it (don't delete it — see the graveyard practice in [abandoning-vs-persisting](abandoning-vs-persisting.md)). Some abandoned songs return as different songs years later; some return as exactly what they were, surprising the writer with how good they were. Either way, the archive is honest about the present state.
