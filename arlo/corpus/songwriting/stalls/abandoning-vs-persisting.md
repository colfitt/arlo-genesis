# Stall: Abandoning vs Persisting — When to Call It

**Scope:** songwriting
**Source:** Deep-research synthesis (2026-05) — Songwriting Craft — verify before treating as authoritative
**Canonical references:** Steven Pressfield, *The War of Art*; Dennis DeSantis, *Making Music*; Cal Newport, *Deep Work*; Sodajerker on Songwriting (interviews on abandoned songs); Rick Rubin, *The Creative Act*
**Tags:** songwriting-craft, finishing-songs, stall-recovery, abandonment, decision-framework, finishing-psychology, sunk-cost

## Why it stalls

The writer is at a decision point. The song is partially finished. It might be a good song. It might be a sketch. Persisting could pay off in a finished song. Persisting could also be wasted time that should be spent on a different song. Without a framework for the call, the writer freezes — opens the session, doesn't make progress, closes it, opens it again, doesn't make progress.

The decision is genuinely hard because two opposed truths are both real:

**Truth one: the persistence school.** Dennis DeSantis, Steven Pressfield, and most working songwriters argue that finishing songs is itself a skill, and that abandoning songs cheaply trains the writer to give up at the first resistance. Pressfield's *The War of Art* names this directly: Resistance is the force that whispers "this isn't working, start something new," precisely when the work is closest to breaking through. The persistence school says finish ugly, ship ugly, learn at the finish line. The graveyard of half-finished songs is the graveyard of writers who never built the muscle of finishing.

**Truth two: the volume school.** Rick Rubin, Sodajerker interviews with high-output writers, the Robert Pollard / Guided By Voices ethos, and Steven King's writing advice all argue the opposite: writers who produce a lot of work get better faster than writers who labor over single pieces. Most songs are sketches; only some are songs. Abandoning the ones that aren't songs frees time for the ones that are. The graveyard folder is honest about the proportion of work that doesn't make it.

Both schools are correct in different cases, which is why a framework is needed. The framework is the recovery from the stall. Without it, the writer is making the call based on mood, which means the call is made under conditions of high uncertainty and low reliability — usually after a bad session, when both "this is bad" and "I'm bad" feel true.

The deeper trap: the writer often asks "is this song worth finishing" when the real question is "am I avoiding this song because it's failing or because I'm afraid of finishing it." The fear of judgment, the taste-gap, the Resistance — these all dress up as "this song isn't worth it." The framework below distinguishes the two.

## The 5 recovery moves

### Move 1 — The "core idea" test: is there one element you're proud of

Ask yourself: is there ONE element in the song that I'm proud of? One concrete element — a chord move, a hook, a line, a vocal performance, a single image. Not the whole song. One piece you would defend if a stranger questioned it.

If yes: finish the song around that element. The core idea is the spine; the rest of the song is the body. Strip back to the core idea. Build outward from there. The unfinished song probably had non-spine material that was burying the spine; the recovery is to make the spine load-bearing and let the rest of the song serve it.

If no: this might be a sketch, not a song. The absence of a core idea you're proud of is the signal that the song was a writing exercise more than a song. Sketches are valuable — they build the muscle, they explore territory — but not every sketch should be completed into a finished song.

This is the first move because it's the cheapest diagnostic. If you can name the core idea, persistence is justified. If you can't, the persistence question may be misdirected.

### Move 2 — The "what would it cost to finish" estimate

Be honest about the remaining work. How many sessions would it take to finish the song to your current standard? Three sessions? Eight? Twenty? Estimate.

Now ask: is the song the result will be worth the time? Not "is it possible to finish" — almost everything is possible to finish. The question is opportunity cost: what would you do with those sessions if you weren't finishing this song? Would you write a new song? Two new songs? Five?

Cal Newport's *Deep Work* framing: your finite hours of focused creative time are the constraint, not the song. Every hour on this song is an hour not on another song. The opportunity cost is real. If finishing this song would take eight sessions and you could write three new songs in those sessions, the math may favor abandonment.

The honest caveat: writers chronically underestimate how long finishing takes. The "two more sessions" estimate is almost always wrong. Multiply by three. If the realistic finish-cost is still worth it, persist. If multiplied by three it isn't, archive.

### Move 3 — The "what's the next move" test

Try to name the next concrete move on the song. Not "finish the bridge." A specific move — "write a second verse using the time-shift method," "rerecord the lead vocal in a different key," "mute everything but vocal and piano and listen for a pass."

If you can name a specific next move and you don't dread doing it: do the move. You're not actually stuck, you're hesitating. Persistence is the right call.

If you can name a specific next move and you dread doing it: there may be a real obstacle. What is the dread about? Are you afraid the next move will reveal the song is bad? Are you tired of the song's emotional content? Is the dread legitimate or is it Resistance? Diagnose before deciding.

If you cannot name any specific next move: you are not stuck, you are done. The song has reached the end of what you can do with it. Either ship the current state or archive it; staring at the session waiting for the next move to surface is the stall.

Sodajerker interviews repeatedly surface this framing from working writers: the song tells you when it's done, and "done" sometimes means "this is what it is and I have no more to give it." Honoring that is part of the craft.

### Move 4 — The graveyard-folder practice

Whatever you decide, do not delete the song. Archive it. Create a folder — "graveyard," "shipped — won't release," "ideas," "fragments" — and move the project there with notes:

- One sentence about why you abandoned it
- The core idea you were proud of (if any)
- The next move you couldn't make (if you got that far)
- The date

This practice has two purposes. First, it lowers the stakes of the abandonment decision — you're not destroying the work, you're putting it in a holding pattern. The lower stakes make the decision easier to make honestly. Second, abandoned songs *return*. Working songwriters across the Sodajerker archive describe songs they abandoned for years and then rediscovered as exactly the song they needed for a later project. The graveyard folder is a future asset; deletion forecloses it.

The graveyard practice also produces self-knowledge. Over time, the folder reveals patterns — the kinds of songs you abandon, the stages where you get stuck, the subjects you walk away from. That pattern is information about your craft.

### Move 5 — The opportunity-cost call: finish or fresh

The hardest move and the one that resolves the stall. Take the honest estimate from Move 2 (cost-to-finish times three) and weigh it against the value of the time. Make the call:

**Persist if:**
- You named a core idea you're proud of (Move 1)
- The cost-to-finish is justified by the result you'd ship
- The next concrete move is namable and you don't dread it
- You haven't been on the song so long that finishing it is mostly habit
- The subject of the song still matters to you

**Abandon if:**
- No core idea passes Move 1
- The realistic cost-to-finish exceeds the value of the result
- You can't name a specific next move
- The song's subject no longer feels true to your current life
- Multiple sessions have produced no forward movement and the moves you have tried have all failed
- You have other songs in flight or queued up that would benefit from the redirected time

The honest acknowledgment: writers will get this call wrong both ways. Some abandoned songs would have been great if persisted. Some persisted songs were always sketches. The framework doesn't eliminate the error; it makes the error legible, so the writer can learn from it.

The Sodajerker archive contains many writers describing both regrets — songs they abandoned that they wish they'd finished, and songs they finished that they wish they'd abandoned. Both errors are normal. The framework reduces the rate of error; it doesn't eliminate it.

## Socratic prompts ARLO uses verbatim

When ARLO detects the abandoning-vs-persisting decision:

1. "Name the one element in this song you're proud of. One concrete thing — a chord move, a line, a moment. Just one."
2. "If you finished this song, who would notice it exists? Name three people — not just 'fans.'"
3. "Realistically, how many more sessions to finish? Now triple it. Is that still worth it?"
4. "What's the next concrete move on the song? Can you name it?"
5. "If you couldn't work on this song for a month, would you miss it? Or be relieved?"
6. "What other song could you start instead? What would you write?"
7. "Are you avoiding finishing this song because it's failing, or because you're afraid to finish?"
8. "If you had to put this song in a folder called 'graveyard, may return,' could you actually walk away — or would you keep opening it?"
9. "Is the subject of this song still true to you? Does the writer-you-are-now still want to say what the song was saying?"
10. "If you fast-forward a year — would you rather have finished this one song, or written three new ones?"

## When to abandon and re-arrange

This file IS the framework for when to abandon. The "when to abandon" is not a separate decision from the moves above — it's the output of the framework when the moves point to abandonment.

The clearest abandonment signals, restated:

- No core idea passes Move 1 (you can't name one element you're proud of)
- The cost-to-finish estimate, tripled, is not justified by the value of the result
- You cannot name a specific next move
- Multiple sessions have produced no forward movement
- Other songs are queued up that would benefit from the redirected time
- The subject of the song no longer feels true to your current life

The clearest persistence signals:

- You named a core idea you're proud of
- The next move is namable and not dread-inducing
- You have time and the cost-to-finish is reasonable
- The subject of the song still matters
- You've had one or two sessions of momentum recently — the stall is recent

**The cross-stall pattern:** every other stall in this bucket eventually points back here. The bridge problem that can't be solved (B1), the second verse that won't come (B2), the chorus that won't lift (B3), the demo that won't ship (B4), the arrangement that won't cohere (B5), the lyric that won't mean (B6), the song you hate (B7) — each of these stalls has its own moves first, but when those moves are exhausted, the decision returns here. The "when to abandon" sections of every other file in this bucket cross-link to this file because this is where the abandonment call gets made.

Do not abandon impulsively. Do not persist out of guilt. The framework above is what the call looks like when made honestly. The graveyard folder is where the abandoned songs live; some of them come back. The next song is always available. Both finishing songs and abandoning songs are part of the craft; doing either well requires the framework, not the mood.
