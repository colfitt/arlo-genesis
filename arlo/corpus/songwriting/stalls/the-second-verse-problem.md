# Stall: The Second Verse Problem

## Why it stalls

The second verse is the most-feared lyric assignment in songwriting. Pat Pattison's blunt diagnosis in *Writing Better Lyrics* is that verse 2 is where amateur songs collapse — the writer used everything they had in verse 1, the chorus paid it off, and now there is nothing left to say. The cure is structural, not lyrical: a second verse must *develop* the song, not restate it.

The mistake almost every writer makes is treating verse 2 as a parallel of verse 1 — same situation, same scope, same emotional register, just different rhymes. That works in folk and traditional ballads (where the song advances by accumulating verses of similar shape), but it fails in contemporary pop, where the listener has heard the chorus and expects verse 2 to escalate, complicate, or recontextualize what the chorus said.

Jimmy Webb's *Tunesmith* frames the verse 2 problem as a narrative problem: verse 1 sets up the situation, the chorus declares the emotional response, and verse 2 has to make the listener understand *more* — more about why the singer feels this way, more about the consequences, more about the other person, more about the world the song lives in. If verse 2 doesn't add information, the song flatlines.

Sheila Davis in *The Craft of Lyric Writing* offers the simplest test: after the chorus, ask, "What does the listener now want to know?" That question is verse 2. If you cannot name the question, you do not have verse 2 yet — and trying to write it from a blank page is the stall.

The deeper trap: songwriters often *start* a song without knowing what verse 2 will be, then trap themselves into a chorus and verse 1 that pre-spent the song's material. Cave, Cohen, Dylan, and Pattison all describe drafting verse 2 *first* or alongside verse 1 to avoid this.

## The 6 recovery moves

### Move 1 — Time shift: change when verse 2 takes place

Verse 1 is the present; verse 2 is the past or the future of the same situation. This is the most-used move in Nashville and in the Sodajerker interview canon — it gives you new material without changing the song's center of gravity. Examples: verse 1 is the morning of the fight; verse 2 is the morning after. Verse 1 is the first kiss; verse 2 is the divorce. Verse 1 is the phone call; verse 2 is the drive home. The chorus stays as the emotional response that binds both moments.

Concrete recipe: write down what's happening in verse 1 as a one-sentence scene. Now write the same sentence shifted to a different point in time — five minutes later, five years later, the night before, the funeral. That's the seed of verse 2.

### Move 2 — POV shift: change whose eyes the verse is in

Verse 1 is from the singer's perspective; verse 2 is from the other person's, or from a third party who witnessed the situation. Pattison teaches this as the "shift the narrator" move — risky if mishandled but powerful when it lands. Sheila Davis warns that the listener has to be able to follow the shift; if the POV change is unclear, the song reads as confused. A common safe version: keep the same singer but have them imagine what the other person is thinking ("She probably tells her friends...").

The risk: POV switches that aren't earned read as gimmicky. Test by reading both verses aloud — does the listener immediately know who is speaking in each?

### Move 3 — Zoom: change the lens width

Verse 1 is the close-up; verse 2 is the wide shot — or the reverse. Verse 1 is the specific detail (the coffee cup on the table, the song on the radio); verse 2 is the larger context (the city, the year, the cultural moment). Or verse 1 names the world and verse 2 zooms into the room. This is the move when the song's material is about the relationship between the personal and the universal — Aimee Mann songs do this frequently, as does Bruce Springsteen's *Nebraska* catalog.

The discipline: each verse owns its lens. Don't switch lenses mid-verse; the listener needs to feel the camera move *between* sections, not within them.

### Move 4 — Consequence: verse 1 is the choice, verse 2 is what happened

Verse 1 sets up an action; verse 2 reveals its result. This is the narrative-logic move and the most Nashville-friendly of the six. Verse 1: "I told you I was leaving / packed the bag, paid the rent." Chorus: "and I never looked back." Verse 2: "Now I sit in the kitchen / of a house I don't recognize." The consequence-move forces the second verse to *advance* the story rather than restate it, and it gives the final chorus weight by anchoring it in earned material.

Jimmy Webb's *Tunesmith* describes this as the "what happened next" question — the same one a screenwriter asks at every act break. If verse 2 doesn't answer it, verse 2 doesn't exist yet.

### Move 5 — Verse-1 shape with new images (the swap)

Keep verse 1's syntactic structure — line lengths, rhyme scheme, prosodic phrasing — but swap every concrete noun and verb for new ones. The song's structure does the work of feeling familiar; the new images do the work of feeling new. Pattison teaches a version of this in his Berklee lectures: the syntactic shape of verse 1 *is* the song's lyric form, and verse 2 inhabits that form with fresh material. This move is especially useful when you love verse 1's prosody and don't want to lose its momentum.

The danger: if the new images don't connect to the chorus's emotional center, you've written two parallel verses that don't *develop*. Combine this move with Move 1 or Move 4 to give verse 2 a directional motion.

### Move 6 — The cut option: there shouldn't be a verse 2

Some songs are one-verse songs. The song says what it has to say in one verse, the chorus pays it off, and a second verse would dilute the emotional concentration. Modern pop has many of these — the post-chorus has absorbed some of the function the second verse used to perform, and song lengths have shortened. If every attempted second verse is filler, the song may want a structure that goes verse-chorus-instrumental-chorus, or verse-chorus-bridge-chorus, with no second verse at all.

The test: write the song without a second verse. If the song is missing something, write verse 2. If the song is complete, verse 2 was always procrastination.

## Socratic prompts ARLO uses verbatim

When ARLO detects the second-verse stall, it pastes these:

1. "What does the chorus tell us happens emotionally? Now — what does the listener want to know *after* hearing that chorus?"
2. "Verse 1 takes place when? If it's the morning, what's the night? If it's the present, what's the past?"
3. "Whose voice is verse 1 in? Whose voice would verse 2 be in if you switched narrators?"
4. "Read me verse 1 out loud. What are the three most specific images in it? Now name three different specific images from the *same situation*."
5. "If the song stopped after the first chorus, what would feel missing? That missing thing is verse 2."
6. "If you couldn't have a verse 2 — only a bridge or instrumental — would the song be worse? Be honest."
7. "What's the consequence of what verse 1 described? If verse 1 is the choice, verse 2 is what happened next."
8. "Sing me verse 1 and the chorus. Now hum the verse 2 melody and tell me — what does the *music* want to say that the words don't yet?"

## When to abandon and re-arrange

If you've tried all six moves and verse 2 still feels grafted-on, the song may want a different form. The signals:

- Verse 2 attempts all sound like watered-down versions of verse 1
- The chorus says everything the song has to say; verse 2 is structural padding
- The song's emotional arc is one verse and one chorus — adding more dilutes it
- You can't articulate what the listener doesn't yet know after the first chorus

The move: cut verse 2 entirely. Structure as verse-chorus-bridge-chorus, or verse-chorus-instrumental-chorus, or verse-prechorus-chorus-postchorus-chorus. Many of the strongest modern pop songs do not have a traditional second verse — they replace it with a post-chorus hook (Eilish, Charli XCX) or a beat-switch second half (Travis Scott, hip-hop conventions).

If verse 1 itself is the problem — if it's too long, too dense, or burns through all the song's material — consider splitting it into two shorter verses with the chorus between, so the song goes verse-chorus-verse-chorus and verse 2 is the *second half* of what was originally one long verse.

When to walk away from the song: see [the abandoning-vs-persisting framework](abandoning-vs-persisting.md). If the second-verse problem persists for more than two sessions and none of the six moves opens a door, the song may be a sketch that has reached its natural length. Archive it; the verse-1-and-chorus may seed a different song later.
