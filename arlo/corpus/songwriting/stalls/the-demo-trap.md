# Stall: The Demo Trap — Polish Killing Momentum

**Scope:** songwriting
**Source:** Deep-research synthesis (2026-05) — Songwriting Craft — verify before treating as authoritative

## Why it stalls

The demo trap is the most-pervasive stall in the bedroom-producer era. The writer has a song that is structurally finished — verses, chorus, bridge, arrangement — and is now in a months-long loop of opening the session, tweaking the kick EQ, automating the vocal rider, A/B-ing the bass with three other plugins, bouncing, listening, finding one more thing to fix, and repeating. Forward progress on the song is approximately zero. Forward progress on the *body of work* is negative, because the writer is not starting new songs.

Dennis DeSantis in *Making Music* names this directly: polish is comfortable. Polish has a clear interface (knobs, faders, plugins). Polish gives the dopamine of small wins. Writing the next song requires a blank page, a confrontation with the taste gap (Ira Glass's term — your taste is ahead of your skills, so every new song initially disappoints you), and the social risk of a song no one has heard yet.

Steven Pressfield's *The War of Art* frames this as Resistance — the force inside the writer that pushes against the work that matters. The demo trap is Resistance dressed up as professionalism. The writer can point to hours of "work" — they're in the studio, they're on the session — but the work isn't moving anything forward.

Mixerman's *Zen and the Art of Producing* offers the operational version: the demo is done when the demo is done. The writer's job is to ship and start the next one, not to perfect the current one. Mix engineers and producers describe a known threshold — the point at which additional tweaking neither improves nor degrades the mix, but consumes time. Past that threshold, you're in the trap.

The honest caveat: the demo trap is sometimes real fear of judgment ("if I never finish, no one can reject it"), sometimes real perfectionism (the taste gap), and sometimes a legitimate need for the mix to be better. Diagnosing which one matters because the move differs.

## The 6 recovery moves

### Move 1 — Bounce and walk away: 48-hour separation

The first move is the cheapest. Bounce the current mix as it stands — even mid-tweak — to WAV or MP3. Close the session. Do not open it for 48 hours. Listen to the bounce on your phone, in the car, on a friend's speakers, anywhere outside the room you mixed in. The point is to break the polish loop and force fresh ears.

After 48 hours, listen to the bounce once. Decide: is the song's *song* there — the melody, the lyric, the arrangement, the feel? If yes, the song is done as a demo, even if the mix is imperfect. If specific mix issues are loud and unignorable on fresh listen (the vocal is buried, the low end is muddy, the chorus doesn't pop), fix those *specific* issues in one focused session — not a return to the open-ended polish loop.

Mike Senior's *Mixing Secrets for the Small Studio* describes this as the "consumer-systems rotation" — listening on every playback system you can to reveal what the studio hides. The same principle applies to the demo trap: distance reveals what proximity buries.

### Move 2 — Start the next song before finishing this one

The Spoon discipline (Britt Daniel's "two demos a week" approach), the Robert Pollard volume school, the Guided By Voices ethos: forward momentum is created by starting new songs, not by perfecting old ones. When you have three or four demos in flight, no single one carries the weight of being The One. The stuck song becomes one of many, and the pressure on it drops to a level where you can finish it without precious-ness.

Concrete recipe: pick a writing morning this week. Do not open the stuck session. Write a new song — verse and chorus, ugly, fast, an hour. Then go back to the stuck song the next day with the new one already in the queue behind it. Often the stuck song finishes itself once it isn't the only thing on the workbench.

### Move 3 — The ship-in-24-hours rule

Declare a hard ship date. Twenty-four hours from now, the song is done. Whatever state it's in at the deadline is the final mix. No exceptions. Tell someone the deadline so it has social weight — your collaborator, your group chat, a person who will ask.

This is a forced-function move. The artificial deadline removes the option to keep tweaking, which is the only option that was keeping you stuck. DeSantis describes this as the "decide once, move on" principle. The decision to ship is not a decision about the mix; it's a decision to *be a person who finishes things*. The mix is what it is.

The honest risk: a 24-hour-shipped mix is sometimes worse than a 1-week-shipped mix. The trade is that you get to start the next song. Over a year, the writer who ships ten 24-hour mixes outpaces the writer who ships one 1-month mix.

### Move 4 — Get an outside ear

The demo trap is invisible from inside the session. Send the current bounce to one trusted person — another writer, a producer friend, a musician whose taste you respect. Ask one question: "Is this done?" Not "what should I fix" — "is this done?"

The outside ear hears the song. You hear the session. The outside ear is almost always closer to right than the inside ear, because they're listening for whether the *song* works, not whether the kick is at exactly -7 dB. Mixerman warns that the writer-mixer is the least-objective person in the room — the work needs an ear that hasn't been close to it.

If the outside ear says it's done, you're done. If they name two specific issues, fix those two and only those two, then ship.

### Move 5 — Distinguish mix decisions from non-mix decisions

The diagnostic move: list every change you've made or considered making in the last two weeks. Classify each:

- **Mix decisions** (EQ, compression, levels, panning, automation): the demo trap lives here
- **Non-mix decisions** (lyric edits, arrangement changes, replacing a part, rerecording a vocal): these are legitimate, but they're *songwriting* decisions, not polish

If your list is 95% mix decisions, you're in the trap. If your list contains real non-mix decisions, you have legitimate work — but separate the two sessions. Do the songwriting decisions in a writing session, not a mix session. The mode switch is part of escaping the loop.

DeSantis frames this as the separation of composing, arranging, and mixing modes — when they blur, the writer mixes when they should be arranging and arranges when they should be writing, and nothing finishes.

### Move 6 — The graveyard-folder option: declare it done, archive it, start fresh

The hardest move. Declare the song done in its current state, even if you don't love it. Bounce the master. Archive the session. Move it to a folder called "finished — may revisit" or "shipped demos." The point is not that you love the mix; the point is that the song is no longer on your active workbench.

Some songs never reach the version of "done" the writer was holding out for. The graveyard option acknowledges this honestly: the song is done because *you're done with it*, even if the mix isn't where you wanted. The alternative is months more of polish that won't change the song.

The discipline Dennis DeSantis names: a song you've decided is done frees the mental space for the next song. A song you can't decide on consumes the mental space and prevents you from writing anything new. The graveyard folder is the operational version of the decision.

## Socratic prompts ARLO uses verbatim

When ARLO detects the demo-trap stall, it pastes these:

1. "Have you written a new song this week? When was the last time you wrote a new song?"
2. "What are you going to change in the session today that you haven't already tried? Be specific."
3. "When did you last bounce the mix and listen on your phone? In your car? On someone else's speakers?"
4. "If you had to ship this song in 24 hours, would the version you bounce be better or worse than what you'd ship in a month? Be honest."
5. "Name one person who would tell you the truth if this song is done. Are you willing to send it to them today?"
6. "Of the changes you've made in the last week, how many were mix decisions and how many were songwriting decisions? Count them."
7. "What would you write next if you couldn't open this session? What's the next song?"
8. "If this song never got mixed any further than right now, would it still be worth the work you've put in? If yes, ship it."
9. "What are you afraid will happen when the song is done?"

## When to abandon and re-arrange

The demo trap is itself a form of abandonment-avoidance — the writer is keeping the song "alive" by not finishing it, because finishing means the song can be judged and the writer can fail. The real abandonment move is not throwing the song out; it's shipping the song and starting the next one.

Signals that you should ship and move on, even if you don't love the mix:

- You've been mixing for more than two weeks without structural changes
- Every change is undone within a day or two
- The outside ear has said "it's done" and you're still tweaking
- You haven't started a new song in a month
- The session takes ten minutes to load and you spend forty minutes on each EQ decision

The move: ship the song. Use Move 3 (24-hour rule) or Move 6 (graveyard folder). The song is done. The next song is waiting.

When to actually abandon the song entirely — to delete the project rather than ship it — see [abandoning-vs-persisting](abandoning-vs-persisting.md). The criteria are different: the demo trap is about a song that *is* finishable that you won't finish; abandonment is about a song that isn't worth finishing. Don't confuse the two. Most demo-trap songs are worth shipping; the trap is shipping them.

If you suspect the demo-trap is paired with broader avoidance — months without a new song, no recent writing, dread on opening any session — see also [why-songs-don't-get-finished](#) and the F-bucket finishing-psychology files (forthcoming) for the diagnostic tree.
