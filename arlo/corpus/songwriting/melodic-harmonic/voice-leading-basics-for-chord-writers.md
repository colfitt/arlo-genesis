# Voice Leading Basics — For Chord-Writers, Not Composition Students

## Why this file exists

The general-corpus file `corpus/harmony/voice-leading-for-songwriters.md` covers the foundations. This file is the *practical, no-jargon* version for a songwriter who writes by chords — on a guitar, on a keyboard, or by dragging chord blocks in a DAW — and has heard "your chords sound choppy" or "the change is clunky" without knowing the fix. Voice leading is the difference between chords that *flow* into each other and chords that *jump* awkwardly. None of this requires choir-class rules; it's a handful of moves you can apply to chords you've already written. Mission-fit: smoother voice leading makes a progression sound finished and professional without changing a single chord.

## The one principle that matters most

If you remember nothing else: **move the fewest notes the smallest distance between chords.** When you go from one chord to the next, keep any shared notes where they are and move the rest by the smallest possible step. The reason is simple — small movements sound smooth and connected; big jumps in every voice sound choppy and disjointed. Most "clunky chord change" problems are just chords played in positions that force every note to leap. The fix is to *revoice* one of the chords so the notes are closer together. Same chords, smoother result.

## Chords are stacks of voices

The mental shift that unlocks voice leading is hearing a chord not as a single block but as a **stack of independent voices** — a top note, a middle note (or two), and a bass note — each of which moves to the next chord on its own. When you think this way, you stop asking "what's the next chord?" and start asking "where does each note go?" The top voice is the one the ear tracks most (it's effectively a melody line of its own), and the bass is the second-most-tracked. Keep those two outer voices moving smoothly and the chords will connect, even if the inner voices are doing something rougher.

## Common-tone connection: hold the shared note

The most useful move is the **common tone**: when two chords share a note, *keep that note in the same voice* and move only the others. For example, C major (C–E–G) to A minor (A–C–E) shares C and E — hold those two notes still and just move G down to A. The result is barely any motion at all, and it sounds seamless. Most chords a step or a third apart share one or two notes; finding the shared note and holding it is the single fastest way to smooth a change. When a progression sounds choppy, look for the common tones you're failing to hold.

## Contrary motion between the outer voices

When the top voice (melody) and the bass move in **opposite directions** — one up, one down — the progression sounds strong and balanced. This is contrary motion, and it's why a bass line that walks down while the melody climbs feels so satisfying. The opposite — top and bass moving in **parallel**, both rising or both falling in lockstep — sounds thinner and can feel like the whole chord is just sliding. You don't need contrary motion everywhere, but when a section feels weak or one-dimensional, try moving the bass against the melody rather than with it. A descending bass under a rising vocal is one of the most reliable "strong" sounds in songwriting.

## Avoid parallel fifths and octaves between the outer voices

The classic "rule" that scares songwriters: avoid **parallel fifths and parallel octaves** between the outer voices. The practical reason — stripped of choir-class dogma — is that when two voices move in parallel octaves or fifths, they stop sounding like two independent voices and start sounding like *one voice doubled*, which thins out the harmony and collapses the sense of independent movement. You don't have to treat this as an iron law (plenty of rock and pop deliberately uses parallel fifths — power chords are parallel fifths). But if a progression sounds oddly hollow or like the parts aren't independent, parallels between the outer voices are a likely culprit, and breaking them by moving one voice differently usually fixes it.

## Smoothing inversions to minimize jumps

The fastest practical tool is **inversions** — playing a chord with a note other than the root in the bass. If going from one root-position chord to another forces a big jump, an inversion of the second chord often puts its notes right next to where the first chord's notes were, eliminating the leap. For example, C major to F major in root position jumps the bass up a fourth; using F in first inversion (with A in the bass) can keep things closer. In a DAW or on keys, the move is: when a change sounds choppy, try inverting the target chord until its notes sit nearest the previous chord's notes. The bass line you get from choosing inversions is itself a melodic line worth shaping.

## Voice leading on guitar: the "shape adjacent to the shape"

Guitarists can voice-lead without theory by finding the **chord shape that sits closest to the shape they're already holding.** Instead of jumping the hand to a distant open-chord shape, look for a voicing of the next chord up or down the neck near your current hand position — often this means using partial chords, triads on a few strings, or moving one or two fingers while leaving others planted. Holding a finger on a common tone while the others move is the guitar version of common-tone connection. Many of the smoothest-sounding guitar progressions come from small hand movements between adjacent voicings, not from cycling through textbook open chords. Capo positions and altered tunings (see `arlo/workflows/joni-mitchell-alternate-tuning-first.md`) open up even more close-voiced options.

## Voice leading in pad and synth arrangements

In electronic and produced music, voice leading shows up as the **smoothness of the top line of a pad or synth chord.** When chords are played as block pads, the top note of each chord forms a line the ear follows; if that top line jumps around, the pad sounds restless, and if it moves stepwise or holds, the pad sounds settled and intentional. The producer's move is to voice pad chords so the top notes connect smoothly — often by inverting chords so the highest note stays put or steps to a neighbor. The same applies to any sustained harmonic layer: shape the top voice as if it were a melody, because to the listener, it is.

## The bass line is a melody too

A consequence of thinking in voices: the **bass line is its own melodic line**, and shaping it deliberately improves the whole progression. A bass that walks stepwise (the descending "lament" bass, or a rising stepwise climb) gives a progression direction and momentum that a bass jumping between roots doesn't. When you choose inversions, you're really choosing the bass line — so choose it for shape, not just to support the chords. A strong, smooth, intentional bass line will make an ordinary chord progression sound composed. When a progression feels static, look at whether the bass is just hammering roots and give it somewhere to walk.

## What you can ignore as a songwriter

Honesty about scope: most of the formal voice-leading curriculum (resolving every tendency tone, four-part chorale spacing rules, exhaustive parallel-motion prohibitions) is aimed at composition and theory students, not songwriters, and you can largely ignore it. The high-value subset for a chord-writer is small: hold common tones, use inversions to keep chords close, shape the bass and the top voice, and watch for parallels only when something sounds hollow. Pop, rock, and electronic music break the "rules" constantly and on purpose. The goal isn't correctness — it's *smoothness when you want smoothness*, which is most of the time, and the four moves above deliver almost all of it.

## The diagnostic, when a change sounds clunky

If a chord change sounds choppy or "wrong," work through this before changing the chords. (1) Do the two chords share any notes? Hold the shared notes still and move only the rest. (2) Is the change forcing a big bass jump? Invert the target chord so its notes sit closer to the previous chord's. (3) Is the top line jumping? Revoice so the top note holds or steps. (4) Do the bass and melody move in parallel? Try moving them in contrary motion instead. (5) Does it sound hollow? Check for parallel fifths/octaves between the outer voices and break them. The chords are usually fine — it's almost always the *voicing* of the chords that's clunky, and revoicing is a free fix.
