## Why this file exists

The general-corpus file `corpus/melody/melodic-contour-and-motif-development.md` establishes what contour is and how motifs develop. This file is the *planning* layer: it treats contour as something you decide section by section, deliberately, so that the song's melodic shapes contrast and build rather than blur together. Mission-fit: a song where every section has the same melodic shape feels flat and samey even when the individual melodies are good. This file gives you four named shapes and a planning discipline for distributing them across a song — a fast way to diagnose and fix "all my sections sound the same."

## Contour, defined simply

**Contour** is the shape a melody traces in pitch over time — the up-and-down silhouette of the tune, independent of the exact notes. You can hum a contour without knowing the pitches; it's the gesture. Perricone and others treat contour as a primary expressive tool because the *shape* of a melodic line carries emotional meaning before the listener processes the specific intervals. Rising shapes feel like building, opening, questioning; falling shapes feel like settling, releasing, concluding. Planning contour means deciding the *gesture* of each section before fussing over individual notes.

## The four archetypes

There are four basic contour shapes worth keeping in your toolkit:

- **Arch** — the line rises to a peak then falls, like a hill. The most common and satisfying shape because it has a built-in climax and resolution. The peak is where the section's most important word or moment should land.
- **Descent** — the line generally falls from a high start to a low end. Reads as release, resignation, settling, conclusion.
- **Ascent** — the line generally rises from a low start to a high end. Reads as building, intensifying, reaching, unresolved.
- **Plateau** — the line stays mostly level, hovering around a register without large rises or falls. Reads as steady, conversational, hypnotic, or insistent depending on context.

Most real melodies are combinations and variations of these, but naming the dominant shape of a section is enough to plan with.

## Verse contour conventions

Verses commonly use **descending** or **arched** contours. A descending verse settles the listener in, conversational and relaxed, and conveniently ends low — which leaves maximum room to climb into the chorus. An arched verse gives the verse its own small internal peak and resolution without committing to the chorus's altitude. The tendency, not a rule: verses often live lower and shape-wise calmer than choruses, both to carry the lyric conversationally and to preserve contrast. If a verse already peaks high and arches dramatically, the chorus has nowhere to go.

## Chorus contour conventions

Choruses commonly use **arched** or **plateau-high** contours sitting *above* the verse's register. An arched chorus has a clear peak — the emotional high point of the song, usually on the title or the key word. A plateau-high chorus hovers at an elevated register, insistent and anthemic, the way a chant or a big sing-along hook does. The unifying principle is that the chorus contour sits higher and/or peaks higher than the verse contour; the register lift plus the shape together signal *arrival*. When a chorus feels flat, check whether its contour is genuinely shaped differently and placed higher than the verse's, or whether it's just the verse shape repeated.

## The planning principle: contrast between sections

The core discipline is **deliberate contrast** between adjacent sections' contours. If the verse descends, let the chorus arch upward. If the verse is a low plateau, let the chorus rise. The contrast is what makes each section feel like a distinct place and gives the song a sense of motion. The failure mode — covered below — is unconscious repetition, where every section traces the same shape and the song feels monotonous despite having "different" melodies. Plan the song's contour as a *sequence of contrasting gestures*: this section settles, the next builds, the next peaks. The shapes should tell the emotional story even with the lyrics muted.

## Where does the highest note live?

A single high-level decision shapes the whole melodic plan: **where is the highest note of the entire song, and what happens on it?** Most strong songs have one melodic peak — usually in the final chorus or the bridge — and the rest of the melody is built so that peak feels earned (this dovetails with high-note placement in `corpus/songwriting/melodic-harmonic/vocal-range-management.md`). Deciding the peak first lets you keep earlier sections deliberately below it, so the song has somewhere to climb. If your melody hits its top note early and often, there's no peak to build toward — pull the early sections down and reserve the top for one moment.

## Step vs. leap: the intervallic layer

Underneath the large-scale contour is the question of *how* the line moves between notes. **Stepwise motion** (moving to adjacent scale notes) feels smooth, singable, and conjunct — the default for conversational verses and easily-hummed hooks. A **leap** (a jump of a third or more) creates drama and tension, and the larger the leap, the bigger the gesture. A useful working principle, common in melody pedagogy: tension via leap, release via step — reach up with a leap, then resolve down by step. Choruses often open with a leap (the lift into the hook) then move stepwise. When a melody feels boring, it may be all steps and no leaps; when it feels jagged and unsingable, it may be all leaps and no steps.

## The answering-phrase contour

A melody often works as a question-and-answer pair: a **rising "question" phrase** that feels open and unresolved, answered by a **descending "answer" phrase** that resolves. This rising-then-falling pairing is a miniature arch spread across two phrases and is one of the most natural-feeling melodic structures in songwriting. If a melodic phrase "hangs" and feels incomplete, it may be a question that never got its answering descent — adding a falling answer phrase completes the gesture. Conversely, two descending phrases in a row can feel like the melody gives up; pairing a rising phrase with a falling one keeps the energy alive.

## The trap: unconscious contour repetition

The most common contour failure is repeating the same shape across every section without noticing. Writers tend to have a default melodic gesture — many people unconsciously end every phrase with a descent, or arch every section identically — and a whole song built on one repeated shape feels samey no matter how the lyrics or chords change. The diagnostic: **hum each section with no words and ask whether the shapes are actually different.** If the verse, pre-chorus, and chorus all trace the same silhouette, that's the problem. The fix is to consciously assign a *different* archetype to a section and rewrite its melody to that shape.

## Plateau as a deliberate choice

The plateau shape is easy to dismiss as "boring," but a deliberate plateau is a powerful tool. A low, level verse plateau can feel intimate and spoken, making the chorus's rise hit harder by contrast. A high, level chorus plateau can feel anthemic and chant-like — a melody that refuses to fall, holding the energy up. Rap and much modern pop use plateau contours deliberately, sitting in a narrow pitch band for rhythmic and lyrical reasons. The plateau only fails when it's *accidental* — when you meant to write a shaped melody and it came out flat. Used on purpose, against a contrasting section, it's as effective as any arch.

## The diagnostic, when sections blur together

If a song feels monotonous or "all the same" despite having distinct sections, run this contour check. (1) Hum each section with no words; sketch its shape (arch / descent / ascent / plateau). (2) Are adjacent sections actually different shapes? If two in a row trace the same silhouette, reassign one. (3) Where is the song's single highest note, and is everything else built below it? If the peak comes too early, lower the earlier sections. (4) Does the chorus sit higher and peak higher than the verse? If not, lift it. (5) Are there any leaps, or is it all stepwise? Add a leap into the hook for drama. Contour planning is fast, costs nothing, and fixes the "samey" problem that rewriting individual notes usually won't.
