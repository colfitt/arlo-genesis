## Why this file exists

This file builds on two general-corpus files — `corpus/harmony/modal-interchange-and-borrowed-chords.md` and `corpus/harmony/secondary-dominants-and-applied-chords.md` — and applies their theory to a single practical job: **shifting the mood of a moment by swapping one chord for a more colorful one.** These are jazz-derived substitutions used in pop, presented move-by-move with the feeling each produces. Mission-fit: when a progression is correct but emotionally flat, a single substitution can add sophistication or drama without rewriting the section. The honest framing: these are advanced colors, easy to overuse, and many great songs need none of them.

## What a substitution is

A **chord substitution** replaces an expected chord with a different one that serves a similar function but carries a different color. The substitute usually shares notes or function with the original, so the progression still "works," but it sounds richer, more surprising, or more sophisticated. Jazz developed a deep vocabulary of these, and pop has borrowed selectively from it. The key discipline: a substitution should *do a job* — add a specific feeling at a specific moment — not just decorate. Used with intent, one substitution can lift a whole section; used as wallpaper, they muddy a song.

## Move: IV becomes iv (the canonical sad turn)

The most-used jazz-adjacent substitution in all of pop is replacing the major **IV** with the minor **iv**, almost always right before returning to I. It's technically modal interchange (covered in `corpus/songwriting/melodic-harmonic/modal-interchange-by-mood.md`), but it functions as the go-to *substitution* for adding bittersweet ache: the IV opens bright, the iv darkens that opening into longing, and the resolution lands wistful. It's a one-note change — the third of the IV drops a half step — with an outsized emotional payoff. **Radiohead's "Creep"** and **The Beatles' "In My Life"** both put IV→iv exactly where the song aches. When a turnaround or chorus feels too sweet, this is the first substitution to reach for.

## Move: the tritone substitution (♭II7 for V7)

The signature jazz substitution is the **tritone sub**: replace the dominant **V7** with a dominant seventh chord a tritone away — the **♭II7**. In C major, that means replacing G7 with D♭7. It works because both chords contain the same tritone (the interval between the 3rd and ♭7), so the part that creates the pull toward home is identical — only the root changes ([Jazz-Library, *Tritone Substitution*](https://jazz-library.com/articles/tritone-substitution/); [Tritone substitution, Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tritone_substitution)). The payoff is a smooth chromatic bass line: instead of G→C, the bass slides D♭→C, a half step into home. The mood it creates is a sophisticated, slightly unexpected resolution — "lounge-y," noir, jazz-ballad color. In pop it's rare and special; drop it on a single cadence where you want the resolution to feel chromatic and elegant rather than bright and obvious.

## Move: the secondary dominant (V/x for forward drive)

A **secondary dominant** is a dominant chord borrowed from another key to point strongly at a chord that isn't the tonic — written "V/V" ("five of five"), "V/vi," "V/IV," etc. It creates a brief, urgent pull toward the target chord, adding forward momentum and a "leaving the key for a second" brightness. The classic is **V/V** — the dominant of the dominant — which drives hard into the V before the chorus. Dolly Parton's "9 to 5" uses a V/V in its chorus ([Popgrammar, *Secondary Dominants*](https://popgrammar.com/secondary-dominants/)). The mood: energy, lift, forward motion, a momentary harmonic surprise. Use a secondary dominant when a progression feels static and you want to inject drive and pull toward a specific chord. (Full treatment in `corpus/harmony/secondary-dominants-and-applied-chords.md`.)

## Move: the deceptive resolution (V to vi)

Instead of resolving the dominant home to **I**, resolve it deceptively to **vi**. The ear expects the bright tonic arrival and instead gets the relative minor — a "we didn't land where we expected" feeling that reads as bittersweet, unresolved, or emotionally expansive. A practical application: if a chorus feels like it ends too soon, replace the final V–I with **V–vi** and add another phrase; the listener thought the section was ending, and now it continues, creating a sense of emotional expansion ([Disc Makers Blog, *Secondary Dominant Chords*](https://blog.discmakers.com/2023/02/secondary-dominant-chords/)). The mood: surprise, melancholy, "not quite home yet." Use it to extend a section or to color a cadence that would otherwise resolve too cleanly.

## Move: the diminished passing chord (♭II°7 and friends)

Diminished seventh chords make excellent **dramatic passing chords** between two diatonic chords, adding tension and chromatic motion. A diminished chord a half step below a target chord (a leading-tone diminished) pulls into it strongly; placed as a passing chord it creates a brief, suspenseful lean before the resolution. The mood is dramatic, suspenseful, slightly anxious — the "something's about to happen" sound. In pop this often appears as a chromatic passing chord between I and ii, or pushing into V. Use a diminished passing chord when a smooth diatonic step feels too plain and you want a beat of tension between two chords.

## Move: the iim7♭5 (jazz minor predominant)

In a minor key (or to darken a major-key predominant), the **iim7♭5** — the half-diminished "two" chord — is the jazz predominant that leads into the dominant with a darker, more sophisticated color than a plain minor ii. It's the front half of the minor ii–V–i, the most fundamental jazz cadence. In pop, substituting a half-diminished ii for a plain ii adds a noir, jazzy, melancholic shading to the approach to the dominant. The mood: sophisticated melancholy, a "torch song" color. Use it when a predominant chord feels too plain and you want the approach to the V to carry weight and shadow.

## Move: extended and altered dominants (color on the V)

Rather than substituting the V entirely, you can *enrich* it — adding a 9th, 13th, or altering it (♭9, ♯9, ♭5, ♯5) for jazz color. A dominant with tensions sounds lusher and more sophisticated; an altered dominant (the "♭9" sound especially) sounds tense and dramatic, pulling hard to resolve. In a pop ballad, a single 9th or 13th added to the dominant before the chorus can add a cocktail-jazz warmth without changing the function. The mood ranges from lush (added tensions) to dramatic and unstable (alterations). Use this as the lowest-commitment jazz move — you keep the chord and the function, you just color it.

## Stacking substitutions: the secondary-dominant chain

You can chain secondary dominants to create a long pull of forward motion — **V/V → V → I**, or longer "back-cycling" chains where each dominant resolves to another dominant before finally reaching home. Each link adds drive and a sense of inevitability building toward the resolution. The mood is relentless forward momentum — the harmony pulling the listener toward home through a series of leaning chords. Use a short chain (V/V→V→I) to make a chorus arrival feel powerfully prepared; use a longer chain sparingly, because too much back-cycling starts to sound like a jazz exercise rather than a song.

## Matching the substitution to the mood

A quick map, mood in / move out: **bittersweet ache** → IV becomes iv. **Sophisticated, chromatic resolution** → tritone sub (♭II7 for V7). **Forward drive / energy** → secondary dominant (V/V). **Surprise, expansion, "not home yet"** → deceptive resolution (V→vi). **Drama, suspense between two chords** → diminished passing chord. **Noir / torch-song melancholy on the approach to V** → iim7♭5. **Lush or tense color without changing function** → extended/altered dominant. Name the feeling first, then pick the single move that delivers it — and try one at a time.

## The trap: move-shopping

The defining failure with substitutions is reaching for them because they're *interesting*, not because the song wants them. A pop song with a clear, simple emotional message often needs zero substitutions — its power is its directness, and a tritone sub dropped into it sounds like showing off. The discipline (echoing the modal-interchange "earn it" rule): a substitution should serve the lyric and the section's emotional job at that exact moment. If you can't say in one sentence *what feeling this substitution adds and why this moment needs it*, you're move-shopping. Pull it out. The most sophisticated choice is sometimes the plain diatonic chord that lets the song speak clearly.

## The diagnostic, when a progression feels flat but "correct"

If a section's chords are technically fine but emotionally inert, try this before rewriting. (1) Name the feeling the section is reaching for in one word. (2) Map it to a single move above. (3) Substitute *one* chord and play the section back. (4) If it lifts, keep it; if it sounds clever-but-wrong, the song probably wanted the plain chord — put it back. (5) Resist stacking multiple substitutions in one pass; each one should earn its place individually. A single well-placed substitution can transform a flat progression; four of them usually just bury the song.
