# Vocal Range Management — Writing for the Singer's Actual Range

## Why this file exists

A song can be harmonically perfect and lyrically true and still fail at the microphone because it sits in the wrong part of the singer's voice. This file is about the practical discipline of **writing for the range that will actually sing it** — yours, or someone else's. Mission-fit: most "the chorus feels weak" or "the vocal sounds strained" problems are range-and-register problems with a thirty-minute fix (transpose, re-place the high note, re-pitch the verse). The framing comes substantially from Jimmy Webb's *Tunesmith*, which treats range as a craft constraint rather than an afterthought.

## Know the singer first

The foundational move — before writing a melody — is knowing the singer's voice. Three facts matter: the **range** (lowest to highest note they can hit at all), the **tessitura** (where their voice sits comfortably and sounds best — the notes they can sustain without strain), and the **break** (the passaggio, the register transition where the tone can crack or shift). Range is "the tonal distance of notes from the lowest to the highest," while tessitura is "where most of the vocal line or notes lie" — the comfortable working zone ([John Henny, *Range vs. Tessitura*](https://johnhenny.com/difference-range-tessitura/)). A useful image: range is how tall the ladder is; tessitura is the rung you stand on most of the time. Write the bulk of the song in the tessitura and save the range extremes for moments that earn them.

## The verse as conversation range

A verse usually wants to live in the **speaking-voice register** — the comfortable lower-to-middle part of the singer's tessitura where the voice is relaxed and the lyric is intelligible. Verses carry the most words and the most narrative, so they need a register where the singer can articulate clearly and conversationally without effort. Writing a verse too high makes it sound effortful and leaves nowhere to go for the chorus; writing it in the conversational zone keeps it intimate *and* preserves headroom. When a verse feels strained or shouty, the first check is whether it's sitting above the singer's natural speaking register.

## The chorus as lift

If the verse lives in the conversational register, the **chorus can lift above it** — and that register jump is one of the most reliable ways to make a chorus feel like arrival (see `corpus/songwriting/stalls/the-chorus-isnt-lifting.md`). The physical reality of a higher tessitura — more breath, more body, more apparent effort — reads to the listener as emotional intensity. The chorus doesn't have to be screamingly high; it just needs to sit *higher than the verse* within the comfortable range. The contrast does the work. When a chorus feels flat and the lyric and hook are fine, raising the chorus melody's center of gravity relative to the verse is often the fix.

## The pre-chorus as range-launcher

The pre-chorus has a specific melodic job: **climb**. A well-built pre-chorus rises from the verse's register toward the chorus's, so that when the chorus hits, the voice has already been lifted partway and the chorus's higher tessitura feels prepared rather than abrupt. This is the melodic equivalent of a runway. If a chorus jumps register too suddenly and feels jarring, inserting or rewriting a pre-chorus to ascend into it can smooth the launch and increase the payoff. The pre-chorus is where you spend the climb so the chorus can spend the arrival.

## The bridge as departure

The bridge contrasts (its whole job — see `corpus/structure/section-function-verse-prechorus-chorus-bridge.md`), and register is one way to deliver that contrast. A bridge can depart **upward** (the highest, most exposed moment of the song — the emotional peak) or **downward** (a sudden drop to a low, intimate register that resets the ear before the final chorus). Either departure makes the bridge feel like a different place. When a bridge feels like a third verse, moving its register sharply — higher for intensity, lower for intimacy — is a fast way to give it the contrast it's missing.

## Placing the highest note

A common amateur mistake is using the singer's top notes constantly, which both fatigues the voice and squanders the drama. Webb's discipline (and standard practice among working writers) is to **save the highest note for the one moment that matters** — typically once or twice in a song, at the emotional climax. The high note lands hardest when the surrounding melody has stayed lower, so the reach is genuinely a reach. If a song spends most of its time near the singer's ceiling, there's no room to climax. The move: identify the single most important word or moment in the song and let *that* be where the highest note lives — and keep the rest below it.

## The singer's break: avoid it or feature it

Every voice has a **passaggio** — the register transition where the tone can crack or shift noticeably ([Passaggio, Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Passaggio)). A melody that repeatedly sits *on* the break, or crosses it on sustained or important notes, will sound unstable unless the singer has trained to smooth it. There are two strategies. The default: **route the melody around the break** — keep sustained and exposed notes clearly below or above it, crossing it only on quick passing notes. The deliberate alternative: **feature the break** — many singers use the crack or flip at the passaggio as an expressive device (the catch in the voice, the yodel-like break). The choice is stylistic, but it should be a choice, not an accident. If a particular line consistently sounds unstable, check whether it's parked on the break.

## Transposition is the cheapest fix

When a song fights the singer, the first and cheapest intervention is **changing the key**. A melody that's beautiful but sits too high for comfort, or too low to project, often just needs to be transposed up or down a few semitones to land in the tessitura. This costs nothing and changes nothing about the writing — it just relocates the whole thing into the right part of the voice. Before rewriting a melody you like, transpose it through a few keys and sing each one; the right key is the one where the verse is conversational and the chorus lifts without strain. For guitarists, a capo does this instantly; for producers, it's a key change in the DAW.

## Writing for yourself vs. writing for someone else

There's a real difference between writing for your *own* range and writing a "cut" for another singer. Writing for yourself, you can write to your comfortable zone by feel — but the trap is writing *everything* in your single most comfortable five notes, producing melodically flat songs. Writing for someone else, you must learn their range and tessitura first, ideally from recordings of them singing, and resist writing to *your* comfortable zone. Even singers with huge ranges choose keys so the tessitura "sits where their voice sounds best" ([SingWise, *How to Determine Singing Range and Vocal Fach*](https://www.singwise.com/articles/how-to-determine-singing-range-and-vocal-fach-voice-type)). If you're demoing a song for another artist, demo it in *their* likely key, not yours.

## The conversational-test diagnostic

A quick test for whether a verse is in the right register: **can you say the lyric, out loud, at the pitch you've written it?** If the written verse pitch is roughly where your speaking voice sits, the verse will feel natural and the words will be intelligible. If you have to reach up or push down to speak it, the verse is mis-registered. This test is fast and works for any singer — speak the lyric, find the pitch your speaking voice naturally lands on, and check whether the verse melody is near it. Verses near the speaking register almost always feel more honest and carry lyrics better.

## The strain diagnostic

If a vocal sounds strained on the recording, work through this before assuming the singer needs to "sing better." (1) Is the verse above the speaking register? Lower it or transpose down. (2) Does the chorus sit at the very top of the range for its whole duration? Pull the sustained notes down and save the top note for one climax. (3) Does any exposed note sit on the break? Route around it or commit to featuring it. (4) Is the whole song in the wrong key? Transpose through a few keys and pick the one where the tessitura sits best. Strain is almost always a placement problem, and placement is almost always fixable without rewriting the melody.

## Range as a writing constraint, not a limit

Webb's larger point in *Tunesmith* is that a singer's range is a *constraint that focuses the writing*, not a limitation to resent. A defined range forces decisions — where the climax goes, where the verse rests, how the sections contrast — that an unlimited range would let you dodge. Writing to a real voice, with its real top, its real bottom, and its real break, tends to produce more shaped, more deliberate melodies than writing in the abstract. When you know exactly where the ceiling is, you know exactly how to build toward it.
