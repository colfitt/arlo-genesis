## Why this file exists

The general-corpus file `corpus/melody/hook-construction.md` covers what a hook is and the types it comes in. This file goes deeper into the *construction patterns* — the repeatable shapes a writer builds a hook out of when a section needs one and nothing is sticking. If the complaint is "my chorus doesn't have a hook" or "it's catchy in my head but not on the recording," these are the moves to try. Mission-fit: each pattern below is something you can build in one sitting and hum-test immediately.

## The four hook types (quick recap)

A hook is the most memorable, repeated element of a song, and it can live in four domains: **melodic** (a tune you hum), **lyrical** (a phrase you quote), **rhythmic** (a rhythm you can clap), and **production** (a sound — a synth riff, a vocal chop, a sample). The strongest hooks usually combine two or three: a melodic-and-lyrical hook (the sung title) is the pop default. When a section feels flat, the diagnostic question is *which domain is the hook supposed to live in here?* — because the fix for a missing melodic hook is different from the fix for a missing production hook.

## Pattern: the short, repeated phrase as the unit

Most hooks are short — typically two or four bars — and built to repeat. The brevity is the point: a hook the listener can grasp and reproduce after one or two passes is a hook that works. A useful constraint is to write the hook so it fits in a single breath and uses a small handful of pitches. Long, wandering, wide-range melodies are harder to remember and harder to sing along to. When your chorus tune isn't sticking, the first move is often to *shorten* it — find the four bars that carry the weight and let them repeat, rather than writing eight bars of continuously new melody.

## Pattern: repetition with one-note variation (the second-iteration trick)

The most reliable melodic-hook pattern is **repeat the phrase, change one thing.** State a two- or four-bar melodic idea, then state it again nearly identically but alter a single element — usually one note at the end, or the harmony underneath. The repetition makes it memorable; the small variation keeps it from being monotonous and often supplies the "lift" on the second pass. This is the engine of countless choruses: the first half plants the phrase, the second half answers it with a tweak. If a hook feels repetitive in a bad way, you've usually under-varied; if it feels forgettable, you've usually over-varied. The sweet spot is *one* change.

## Pattern: the call-and-answer phrase (within the hook)

A hook can contain its own internal call-and-response: a rising "question" phrase answered by a falling "answer" phrase, or a sung line answered by an instrumental or backing-vocal response. This builds a complete little arc inside the hook — tension and release in four bars — which feels satisfying and quotable. The answer can be lyrical (a backing vocal echoing or completing the lead) or purely melodic (a descending resolution after an ascending reach). When a hook feels like it "doesn't finish," try giving the first phrase an answering second phrase rather than just repeating it.

## Pattern: title-as-hook (placement matters)

In a huge share of pop, the **title is the hook**, and *where* it lands inside the hook determines its impact. The most common placement is the title hitting in the first bar of the chorus — the listener gets the payoff immediately and the rest of the chorus develops it. The alternative is the title as the *last* line, the chorus building toward it as a punchline. Both work; the choice depends on whether the title is an arrival you want to reach or a banner you want to plant up front. If you have a great title but the chorus isn't landing, audition both placements — moving the title from the end to the front (or vice versa) can flip a flat chorus into a memorable one. (See `corpus/songwriting/lyric/refrain-construction-earning-the-title.md`.)

## Pattern: the melodic hook without a title

Not every hook is a sung phrase. A hook can be a wordless melodic figure — a "na-na-na," a whistled line, a vocal "oh-oh-oh" — that carries no lyric at all but is the most memorable thing in the song. These thrive because they're trivially easy to reproduce; you don't have to remember words, just a shape. When a chorus lyric feels clunky and won't sing well, consider whether the hook should actually be wordless, with the lyric living in the verses and the chorus carrying a pure melodic figure.

## Pattern: the post-chorus hook (modern pop)

A relatively recent structural pattern is the **post-chorus** — a short section after the chorus that maintains or increases the song's energy and carries its own melodic hook, vocal or instrumental ([Post-chorus, Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Post-chorus); [Top40Theory, *Everything You Need to Know About the Postchorus*](https://www.top40theory.com/blog/everything-you-need-to-know-about-the-postchorus)). Post-choruses often use few or no words — "oh"s, "ooh"s, and vocables are common — and frequently feature an instrumental hook that the chorus's vocal didn't include. Verified examples cited in the literature include the repeated vocable hooks in **Ed Sheeran's "Shape of You"** and the layered "oh-oh-oh" post-chorus in **Katy Perry's "Roar."** If your chorus is good but the song still feels like it's missing a "moment," a post-chorus is a place to add a second, complementary hook without crowding the chorus itself.

## The hum-test

The single most useful diagnostic for any hook is the **hum-test**: can you hum it back after one listen? If you (or better, someone who hasn't heard the song) can't reproduce the hook after a single pass, it isn't doing its job yet — regardless of how clever it is. The hum-test is brutally honest because memorability is the entire function of a hook. When you've been staring at a chorus for an hour, play it once for someone, then ask them to sing it back. Whatever they sing back *is* your hook; whatever they drop wasn't pulling its weight.

## The trap: too many competing hooks

A common failure is stuffing a section with multiple hooks that fight each other. The principle most writers converge on is **one section, one hook** — give each section a single most-memorable element and let it dominate. If the verse, the pre-chorus, and the chorus all have equally strong hooks, none of them gets the foreground and the song reads as busy rather than catchy. The hierarchy matters: the chorus hook should be the biggest, and the other sections' hooks should support, contrast, or set it up — not compete with it. When a song feels cluttered, the fix is often to *demote* a hook (simplify the verse melody) so the chorus hook has room to be the star.

## Matching the hook to the section's job

Different sections want different hook treatments. A **verse** can carry a conversational, lower-energy melodic figure that leaves room for the lyric. A **pre-chorus** often wants a rising, tension-building figure that hands off to the chorus rather than a self-contained hook. The **chorus** wants the biggest, simplest, most repeatable hook — the one the song is named for. The **post-chorus** wants a complementary hook that extends the energy. Building hooks section-by-section, with the chorus as the apex, keeps the energy contour intact (cross-link `corpus/songwriting/melodic-harmonic/melodic-contour-shapes.md` for the contour-planning view).

## The rhythmic and production hook routes

When melody isn't where the hook wants to live, the other two domains are open. A **rhythmic hook** is a distinctive rhythmic figure — a syncopated vocal cadence, a clapped pattern, a stuttered phrase — memorable for its *placement in time* rather than its pitch. A **production hook** is a sonic signature — a synth riff, a pitched vocal chop, a recognizable sample or sound — that becomes the song's identity (think of riffs that are more famous than the vocal). If a song's vocal melody refuses to become a hook, ask whether the hook should be an instrumental riff or a rhythmic figure instead. Many modern records hang their identity on a production hook with a deliberately understated vocal.

## The diagnostic, when you're stuck

If a chorus has no hook, work through this. (1) Decide which domain the hook should live in — melodic, lyrical, rhythmic, or production. (2) If melodic: shorten the phrase, then repeat-with-one-change. (3) If lyrical: audition the title in bar one vs. as the last line. (4) Run the hum-test on a real person, not yourself. (5) If the section feels busy, demote competing hooks so the chorus hook leads. (6) If the vocal still won't hook, build a post-chorus or a production hook to carry the "moment" the chorus can't. One strong, repeatable, hummable element beats four clever ones.
