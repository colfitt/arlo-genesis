## Why this file exists

The general-corpus file `corpus/harmony/modal-interchange-and-borrowed-chords.md` establishes *what* modal interchange is and *which* chords get borrowed. This file does the opposite job: it starts from the **feeling you want** and tells you which borrowed chord delivers it. If you're stuck because a section is "too sweet," "too flat," or "not landing," this is a lookup table — a mood goes in, a concrete move comes out, and you can try it in the next ten minutes. The honest caveat up front: these mood associations are *tendencies*, not laws. Context (tempo, register, what came before) shifts how a borrowed chord reads. But the tendencies are reliable enough to be a starting move when you're stuck.

## The thirty-second refresher

Modal interchange means borrowing a chord from a parallel mode — almost always the **parallel minor** of your key — without actually changing keys. In C major you stay in C, but you grab a chord (or its notes) from C minor for a bar or two, then return home. Open Music Theory calls it "mixing the notes from the parallel major and natural-minor modes... to achieve expressive effects" ([Open Music Theory, *Modal Mixture*](https://viva.pressbooks.pub/openmusictheory/chapter/modal-mixture/)). The five workhorse borrows in pop are **♭VII, iv, ♭VI, ♭III, and ii°** — and each one has a recognizable emotional fingerprint. The rest of this file maps those fingerprints.

## Move: "I want bittersweet ache" → IV becomes iv

The single most reliable mood-move in songwriting is swapping the major **IV** for the minor **iv** right before you return to **I**. The IV opens the door (it's bright, lifting), then the iv darkens that same opening into longing, and the resolution home lands wistful instead of triumphant. The change is one note — the third of the IV chord drops a half step — and that one note carries the whole emotional turn. Verified examples: **Radiohead's "Creep"** lands its Cm (iv) exactly on the song's emotional wound, cycling I–III–IV–iv ([Hooktheory analysis](https://www.hooktheory.com/theorytab/view/radiohead/creep)); **The Beatles' "In My Life"** puts IV→iv under the words "life" and "gone" ([Hooktheory analysis](https://www.hooktheory.com/theorytab/view/the-beatles/in-my-life)). If a chorus or a turnaround feels too happy for what the lyric is saying, this is the first move to reach for.

## Move: "I want anthemic, rock, open" → ♭VII

When a major-key song needs grit and lift *without* turning minor, the **♭VII** is the move. It's the most-used borrowing in rock and acts as a softer substitute for V — where V pulls home tightly through the leading tone, ♭VII pulls home open and unresolved-feeling, which reads as anthemic and forward-driving ([Popgrammar, *Borrowed Chords*](https://popgrammar.com/borrowed-chords/)). Verified examples: **Guns N' Roses' "Sweet Child O' Mine"** verse riff (D–C–G–D = I–♭VII–IV–I) ([Hooktheory analysis](https://www.hooktheory.com/theorytab/view/guns-n-roses/sweet-child-o-mine)); **The Beatles' "With a Little Help from My Friends."** The Tom Petty catalog leans on ♭VII for exactly this open, mid-tempo-rock feeling. If your chorus needs to feel like a fist in the air but a straight V sounds too "schooled," try ♭VII.

## Move: "I want uplifting-but-melancholy" → ♭VI (and the ♭VI–♭VII–I climb)

The **♭VI** is the heaviest, most cinematic of the common borrowings. On its own it adds gravity and yearning; chained as **♭VI–♭VII–I** it becomes a signature emotional climb that resolves with a lump-in-the-throat lift. This is the "triumphant sadness" sound — the major-key resolution arrives, but it had to climb through borrowed darkness to get there. Verified examples: **Radiohead's "Creep"** uses ♭VI in the bridge to support its alienated yearning ([Music Theory Alchemy, *Creep bridge analysis*](https://musictheoryalchemy.com/musictheoryanalysis/radiohead-creep-bridge-music-theory-analysis/)); **Led Zeppelin's "Stairway to Heaven"** uses ♭VI on its journey through A. When you want a chorus or final section to feel hard-won — uplifting *because* it earned the lift — reach for the ♭VI–♭VII–I climb.

## Move: "I want a surprise lift mid-phrase" → ♭III

The **♭III** (E♭ in C major) is rarer but striking — a sudden brightening partway through a phrase, often as I → ♭III → IV, which feels like the floor briefly drops then rises. Because ♭III is the relative major of vi, a progression that passes through it can sound like a momentary glimpse of another key without committing. Dominic Pedler's analysis of the Beatles notes that mediant relationships — both chromatic and modal — were one of Lennon-McCartney's most-used color tools ([Beatles modal mixture analysis, DePauw](http://depauwform.blogspot.com/2008/02/beatles-masters-of-mixture.html)). Use ♭III when a phrase feels too predictable and you want one unexpected bright pivot before the line resolves.

## Move: "I want tension feeding into the dominant" → ii°

The diminished **ii°** borrowed from the parallel minor is a tension chord that wants to push hard into V. Where the diatonic ii (minor) is a gentle predominant, the ii° adds a darker, more unstable pull that sharpens the arrival of the dominant and, through it, the return home. It's a smaller, more functional color than the others — you'll feel it as "the chord that makes the V feel inevitable" rather than as a standalone mood. Reach for it when a pre-chorus or a turnaround needs more drive into the chorus and a straight ii feels too soft.

## The minor-key inverse: borrowing from major

Modal interchange runs both directions. In a minor-key song you can borrow from the parallel *major* — the most common being the **Picardy third** (ending a minor passage on a major tonic) and the major **IV** in a minor key (the bright "Dorian" four that lifts an otherwise dark progression). The Beatles used minor-key major-borrowing to differentiate sections emotionally even when the structure was simple, per Pedler. The mood-move: if a minor section feels relentlessly dark and you want a single ray of hope without leaving minor, borrow one chord from the parallel major — most often IV or the final major tonic.

## How long to hold the borrowed chord

The emotional effect depends partly on duration. A borrowed chord held for a single bar reads as *color* — a passing ache, a quick lift — and the ear barely registers leaving the key. Held for several bars, the same chord starts to feel like a genuine departure and can tip toward sounding like a modulation. For a mood-shift inside a section, keep it brief (one bar, sometimes two) and resolve back. If you want the borrowing to *be* the new emotional ground — common in indie and dream-pop loops — build it into the repeating cycle so it never resolves away. Both work; they're different jobs.

## The chord-loop approach (indie / alt-pop)

Modern indie and alt-pop frequently bake borrowings into the *loop itself* rather than using them as one-off color. A cycle like **I–♭VII–IV–iv** carries two borrowings (♭VII and iv) in four bars, so the whole song lives in a not-quite-major, not-quite-minor space — emotionally ambiguous by design. **♭VI–♭VII–I** as a permanent chorus lift is everywhere in 2010s–2020s pop and synthwave. The mood-move here is different from the single-chord swap: instead of a momentary ache, you're choosing a *persistent* emotional ambiguity for the entire song. If you want a loop that refuses to commit to happy or sad, stack two borrowings into the cycle and let it ride.

## The trap: mood-shopping

The most common failure mode is reaching for a borrowed chord *because it sounds cool*, not because the song wants it. A ♭VI dropped into a bright, simple pop song because you read it's "cinematic" will often just sound like a wrong note that wandered in. The borrowing has to serve the lyric and the section's job. Pedler's framing is useful: the Beatles used these moves to differentiate verse from chorus *emotionally* — the harmony was doing narrative work. If you can't say in one sentence *what feeling the borrowed chord is adding and why this moment needs it*, you're mood-shopping. Pull it back out and ask the song what it actually wants.

## The "earn the borrowing" rule

A borrowed chord lands hardest when the listener has been set up for it. The IV→iv ache in "In My Life" works because the IV established brightness first — the darkening has something to darken. The ♭VI–♭VII–I climb works because the climb implies struggle before the lift. As a working rule: **establish the diatonic version of a chord before you borrow its altered version**, and place the borrowing where the lyric's emotional turn actually happens. A borrowed chord on a throwaway word is wasted; a borrowed chord under the line that matters is the whole song.

## The diagnostic, when you're stuck

If a section feels flat and you don't know why, run this quick check before adding instruments or rewriting the melody. (1) Name the feeling the lyric is reaching for in one word. (2) Find that word in the map above — bittersweet → IV becomes iv; anthemic → ♭VII; hard-won lift → ♭VI–♭VII–I; surprise → ♭III; drive into the chorus → ii°. (3) Swap *one* diatonic chord for the borrowed equivalent and play the section back. Most of the time, one swap is enough to add the missing depth without losing the key. If two swaps still don't fix it, the problem is probably melody, rhythm, or arrangement — not harmony — and you should stop borrowing chords and look elsewhere.
