## Why placement beats mic choice

The single biggest tonal decision in any tracking session is where the microphone sits, not which microphone is on the stand. A Shure SM57 moved an inch across a guitar speaker cone produces a more audible change than swapping that SM57 for a vintage ribbon at the same spot. As Sweetwater's tracking guide notes, "with mics like the Shure SM57, you get big sound differences even if you move the mic just a millimeter" ([Sweetwater Insync](https://www.sweetwater.com/insync/importance-mic-placement-guitar-amps/)). Before reaching for EQ in the mix, the cheapest improvement is to move the mic — closer for proximity warmth and isolation, farther for room blend, off-axis to roll off highs, on-axis to brighten. Engineers like Mike Senior advocate auditioning placement by recording 15–30 seconds at several positions and A/B-ing them in the DAW before committing, because ear-on-source predictions are rarely accurate.

## Electric guitar amp — the SM57 starting point

The default close mic on an electric guitar cabinet is a Shure SM57 placed roughly an inch from the grille cloth, aimed where the speaker cone meets the dust cap (the dome in the cone's center). Center-cap aim yields brighter, more aggressive tone with maximum attack; moving the mic outward toward the cone edge darkens the sound and increases low-mid weight ([Sweetwater Insync](https://www.sweetwater.com/insync/importance-mic-placement-guitar-amps/), [Recording Magazine](https://www.recordingmag.com/resources/recording-info/mics-miking/capturing-the-cab/)). Off-axis angles (10–45 degrees tilted away from the cone) progressively roll off high-frequency content — a built-in de-esser for harsh amps. Recording engineers commonly print one cab close mic and adjust from this single variable. The classic position to audition first: SM57, 1 inch from grille, aimed between the dust cap and the cone edge, slightly tilted toward the center. Move in half-inch increments and listen.

## Dual-mic electric guitar: blending ribbon with dynamic

For records, the most-cited pairing is a Shure SM57 plus a Royer R-121 ribbon, both close to the grille cloth. The R-121 captures body and natural midrange ("what the speaker sounds like in the room"), while the SM57 contributes presence and bite ([Royer Labs](https://royerlabs.com/recording-electric-guitar/)). Royer's own AxeMount clip positions the SM57 toward the cone-and-dust-cap junction with the R-121 closer to the dome — a phase-coherent pairing because both capsules sit equidistant from the speaker ([Sweetwater](https://www.sweetwater.com/store/detail/R121-SM57--royer-r121-and-shure-sm57-recording-mic-pack)). A practical blending approach is to start with the SM57 alone, then bring the ribbon up "until the brittleness is gone." If the two mics aren't equidistant from the cone, expect comb-filtering — check by summing to mono and listening for hollow midrange before committing.

## Acoustic guitar — the 12th-fret default

The canonical close-mic position for acoustic guitar is a small-diaphragm condenser aimed at the 12th fret, 6–12 inches away ([Recording Magazine](https://www.recordingmag.com/resources/recording-info/mics-miking/acoustic-guitar-miking/), [Sweetwater](https://www.sweetwater.com/insync/acoustic-guitar-recording-techniques-pros/)). This spot balances string articulation against body resonance: closer to the soundhole boosts boom around 80–120 Hz (often too much for a mix), closer to the bridge thins the sound and emphasizes pick attack. Aiming straight at the 12th fret tends to de-emphasize midrange ([Humbucker Music](https://www.humbuckermusic.com/pages/acguitrectec)). For a denser tone, angle the mic slightly toward the body rather than moving it closer to the soundhole. Sound on Sound and Universal Audio both treat the 12th-fret position as the "first mic you try" reference point, with everything else benchmarked against it.

## Stereo acoustic — spaced pair vs. mid-side

For stereo acoustic guitar, two common setups are: (1) a spaced pair with one small-diaphragm condenser at the 12th fret (6–12 inches) and a second mic aimed at the bridge or over the player's shoulder, observing the 3:1 rule — the distance between the two mics should be at least three times the distance from each mic to the source, which keeps comb-filtering audible only at low levels ([Universal Audio](https://www.uaudio.com/blogs/ua/stereo-miking-acoustic-guitar)); and (2) mid-side, where a cardioid mid mic at the 12th fret is paired with a figure-8 sides mic oriented perpendicular to it. Mid-side is forgiving because the M channel collapses to a perfect mono if the stereo image is unusable ([Sound on Sound](https://www.soundonsound.com/techniques/introduction-mid-sides-recording)). Both techniques benefit from a treated room — close micing wins in an untreated space.

## Vocal mic distance and proximity effect

For a sustained, intimate pop vocal on a large-diaphragm cardioid condenser, the working distance is 6–8 inches from the capsule with a pop filter at 2–3 inches in front of the mic ([Pure Audio Insight](https://pureaudioinsight.com/blogs/microphone-accessories/professional-tips-for-optimizing-your-pop-filter-usage)). Cardioid patterns exhibit pronounced proximity effect — bass boost below roughly 200 Hz that intensifies as the source approaches the capsule ([LEWITT](https://www.lewitt-audio.com/blog/what-proximity-effect)). At 1 inch, low-end thickens to a muddy degree; at 6 inches, proximity effect is mostly neutralized. Rappers and crooners who want chest-tone weight work closer (2–4 inches) and trade pop-filter dependence for tone. Belters back off to 8–12 inches to avoid both proximity overload and plosive overload. The simplest in-session adjustment: if the vocal sounds boxy or boomy, move the singer back two inches before reaching for EQ.

## Kick drum — the inside/outside/sub triad

A modern kick drum recording chain uses up to three mics. The **inside mic** (typically a Shure Beta 52, AKG D12, or similar dynamic) lives inside the shell, roughly halfway between the beater head and the resonant head, aimed at the beater for click or at the shell center for body ([Vintage King](https://vintageking.com/blog/2018/03/miking-kick-drum), [Sweetwater](https://www.sweetwater.com/insync/mic-drum-kit/)). The **outside mic** (often a large-diaphragm condenser like an AKG C414 or a kick-specific FET like the AKG D12 VR) sits 6 inches to 2 feet in front of the resonant head, capturing low-frequency boom and the drum's room interaction. The **sub-kick** — historically a Yamaha NS-10 driver wired in reverse, now sold as a dedicated mic like the Solomon LoFReQ — sits a few inches from the resonant head and captures the 30–80 Hz range that standard dynamics miss ([Drum Microphone](https://www.drummicrophone.com/how-to-use/kick-drum-miking-techniques-inside-vs-outside-the-drum/)). Blend the three to taste; check phase by soloing pairs and flipping polarity.

## Snare drum top and bottom — the polarity question

For a typical snare miked with an SM57 on top (1–2 inches above the head, angled 30–45 degrees toward the center) and a second mic underneath aimed at the snare wires, the top and bottom mics will be out of polarity with each other. When the batter head is struck, it moves down toward the top mic (negative pressure on the top capsule) and the resonant head moves down toward the bottom mic (positive pressure on the bottom capsule). Summing the two without inverting the bottom mic produces a thin, hollow tone with low-mid cancellation ([Sound on Sound](https://www.soundonsound.com/sound-advice/q-why-should-snare-bottom-mic-be-polarity-inverted)). The recipe: solo top and bottom together, flip polarity on the bottom, and keep whichever orientation produces more body and presence ([Sonnox](https://sonnox.com/articles/drum-phase-alignment-when-to-nudge-flip-or-leave-alone)). Most sessions end with the bottom mic polarity-inverted, but not all — confirm by listening.

## Overheads — XY (coincident) for mono safety

The XY (coincident) overhead technique places two cardioid small-diaphragm condensers with capsules nearly touching, angled 90 degrees apart and centered over the kit. Because the capsules are physically coincident, sound waves arrive at both at effectively the same instant, eliminating time-of-arrival differences and yielding bulletproof mono compatibility ([AEA Ribbon Mics](https://aearibbonmics.com/blumlein-vs-spaced-pairs-on-drum-overheads-how-to-choose/), [Shure](https://www.shure.com/en-US/insights/five-techniques-for-stereo-miking-drums)). The trade-off is a narrower stereo image even when panned hard left/right, because the stereo cue is amplitude-only (no inter-channel delay). XY is the safe choice for releases that may be heard on phone speakers, Bluetooth, or club PA where mono summing is unavoidable. Typical height: 36–40 inches above the snare. Aim each capsule outward toward opposite sides of the kit.

## Overheads — spaced pair (A/B) for width

A spaced pair (also called A/B) uses two cardioid or omnidirectional mics separated by 2–4 feet, both pointed down at the kit. The wider physical separation introduces inter-channel time-of-arrival differences that produce a dramatically wider stereo image than XY ([Shure](https://www.shure.com/en-US/insights/five-techniques-for-stereo-miking-drums), [Production Expert](https://www.production-expert.com/production-expert-1/drum-overheads-5-techniques-compared)). The penalty: mono compatibility suffers. When summed to mono, the inter-channel delays cause comb-filtering, audible as a hollow, phasey tone, especially on cymbals. Apply the 3:1 rule — the spacing between the two overheads should be at least three times the distance from each overhead to the snare — to keep mono cancellation tolerable. Spaced pairs reward treated rooms because they capture more ambient information than a coincident pair.

## Overheads — the Glyn Johns technique

Glyn Johns' three-mic drum technique (kick plus two equidistant "overheads") yields a balanced kit picture with minimal mic count. Mic one sits about 36–40 inches directly above the snare, pointed straight down. Mic two sits next to the floor tom, roughly 6 inches above the rim height, aimed across the kit toward the hi-hat ([Produce Like A Pro](https://producelikeapro.com/blog/recording-huge-drums-with-4-mics-the-glyn-johns-method/), [Melodics](https://melodics.com/blog/glyn-johns-method)). The critical constraint is that both mics must be the *same physical distance* from the center of the snare — measured with a tape, a piece of string, or an XLR cable pinched at the snare and stretched to each capsule. Equidistance ensures the snare hits both capsules at the same time, preventing phase cancellation when the two mics are summed. Glyn Johns himself reportedly disputes the precision of the measurement, but the principle (equidistance from the snare) is non-negotiable.

## Overheads — Recorderman variant

The Recorderman technique is a close-overhead variant that places two mics equidistant from *both* the snare and the kick. Mic one sits about two drum-sticks-end-to-end above the snare (~40 inches), pointing straight down at the snare. Mic two sits over the drummer's right shoulder near the ride cymbal, also aimed at the snare ([Hometracked](https://www.hometracked.com/2007/05/12/recorderman-overhead-drum-mic-technique/), [Garnish Music Production](https://ny.garnishmusicproduction.com/drum-miking-techniques/)). To verify positions, secure one end of an XLR cable at the kick beater contact point and the other at the snare center, then stretch the cable up to each overhead — both should be the same number of cable-stretches away. The technique produces a tight, balanced kit picture with strong snare and kick presence and is particularly useful in untreated rooms where wider spaced pairs would over-capture room reflections.

## Hi-hat, toms, and the bleed problem

A close hi-hat mic (typically a small-diaphragm condenser) sits 4–6 inches above the top cymbal, angled away from the snare to minimize bleed. Toms use dynamic mics (Sennheiser MD 421, Shure SM57, Audix D-series) clipped to the rim 1–2 inches above the head, aimed at the head center. The fundamental tension in close-mic'ing every drum is **bleed**: the snare leaks into the hi-hat mic, the hi-hat leaks into the snare mic, the toms catch cymbal wash. Engineers manage bleed through three levers: tighter polar patterns (hypercardioid where leakage is severe), tighter distances (close mics dominate the diffuse field), and gating in the mix. Aim each close mic so its null points at the loudest unwanted source — the hi-hat mic's rear null pointing at the snare, for example.

## Distance microphones and the room blend

Beyond close and overhead mics, many engineers add a pair of room mics 6–12 feet from the kit at ear height, or a single mono "Bonham mic" at the end of the live room. Distance mics capture the room's decay character, transient smear, and the natural compression of a loud kit in a real space. Mike Senior's *Recording Secrets* approach is to commit to one room sound on the way in (often by heavily compressing the room mics during tracking) rather than mixing a clean ambient track later. Distance mics can be inverted in polarity to test which orientation gives the punchier blend with the close mics. In untreated rooms, room mics tend to add boxy low-mid energy around 200–400 Hz; either treat the room, gate the room mics, or skip them.
