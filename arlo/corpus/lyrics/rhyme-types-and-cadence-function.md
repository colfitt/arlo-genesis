## Rhyme as a Stability Tool

Pat Pattison's central reframe of rhyme — laid out across *Songwriting: Essential Guide to Rhyming* and confirmed by reviewers and Berklee Online materials — is that rhyme is not a decorative requirement but a **stability dial**. Different rhyme types create different degrees of closure, ranging from full resolution (the listener feels the section has landed) to deliberate suspension (the listener feels something is still owed). Per the [Berklee Online Take Note article on prosody](https://online.berklee.edu/takenote/prosody-in-music-and-songwriting/), Pattison categorizes rhyme types on a "scale of rhyme" analogous to musical chord stability, and treats stable-vs-unstable as the master organizing axis. Choosing a rhyme type is therefore a section-function decision, not a thesaurus exercise.

## The Six Pattison Rhyme Types

Pattison's published rhyme taxonomy in [*The Essential Guide to Rhyming*](https://www.amazon.com/Pat-Pattisons-Songwriting-Step-Step/dp/0876391501) names six categories. These are the canonical labels; do not invent or rename them.

1. **Perfect rhyme**
2. **Family rhyme**
3. **Additive rhyme**
4. **Subtractive rhyme**
5. **Assonance rhyme**
6. **Consonance rhyme**

Stability ranking from most resolved to least, per the [Andrea Stolpe summary of Pattison's framework](https://www.andreastolpe.com/articles/rhyme-types): perfect > family > additive/subtractive > assonance > consonance. The further down the list, the more forward motion and unresolved feeling the rhyme creates.

## Perfect Rhyme

**Definition:** the stressed vowel sound and every consonant sound after it match exactly, and the consonants before the stressed vowel differ. Examples per [Andrea Stolpe](https://www.andreastolpe.com/articles/rhyme-types): *well/sell*, *chase/face*, *form/dorm*. Per the [Berklee Take Note article](https://online.berklee.edu/takenote/prosody-in-music-and-songwriting/), Pattison cites *fire/desire* and *tears/fears* as canonical resolution-grade pairs. Perfect rhyme is the strongest closure tool in the kit. Used at the end of a chorus, it locks the section. Used everywhere, it makes the whole song feel like a greeting card — tidy in a way that contradicts most lyric content worth writing about. Pattison's standing critique in [Sound on Sound](https://www.soundonsound.com/techniques/pat-pattison-writing-better-lyrics): a song about a broken heart with four perfect-rhymed AABB lines sounds like "you're just telling me facts."

## Family Rhyme

**Definition:** the stressed vowel matches exactly, and the ending consonants belong to the same **phonetic family** but are not identical. The phonetic families, per the [Song Chops summary of Pattison's sound chart](https://songchops.com/rhyme-families-your-secret-weapon/):

- **Plosives** (stop consonants): voiced b/d/g, unvoiced p/t/k
- **Fricatives**: voiced v/z/th(voiced), unvoiced f/s/th(unvoiced)
- **Sibilants**: sh, ch, j, zh
- **Nasals**: m, n, ng

Examples per [Andrea Stolpe](https://www.andreastolpe.com/articles/rhyme-types): *wet/deck*, *dame/grain*, *float/yoke*. Family rhyme is the workhorse of pop and country lyric writing — slightly less resolved than perfect, giving you sonic continuity without forcing the cliché-rich perfect-rhyme dictionary. The [Tunedly rhyme guide](https://www.tunedly.com/blog/understanding-rhyme-types-for-better-songwriting.html) notes the typical move: when *love* needs a rhyme and *above/dove/of* are exhausted, family rhyme opens *touch, judge, blood*.

## Additive Rhyme

**Definition:** matching stressed vowels with a perfect-rhyme front, where the **second word adds an extra consonant sound** that the first lacks. Examples per [Andrea Stolpe](https://www.andreastolpe.com/articles/rhyme-types) and the [Tunedly guide](https://www.tunedly.com/blog/understanding-rhyme-types-for-better-songwriting.html): *stow/hope*, *year/feared*, *down/found*, *try/cried*. Pattison's stability argument, per [Andrea Stolpe](https://www.andreastolpe.com/articles/rhyme-types): adding sound is more stable than subtracting it — "you have a perfect rhyme with an additive rhyme and then some extra." The trailing consonant gives the line a soft landing rather than a hard one.

## Subtractive Rhyme

**Definition:** matching stressed vowels where the **first word ends in a consonant the second word lacks**. Examples per [Andrea Stolpe](https://www.andreastolpe.com/articles/rhyme-types): *bake/stay*, *shout/now*, *roll/know*. This is the asymmetric mirror of additive. It's less resolved because the listener's ear expects the trailing consonant and doesn't get it — a small unresolved tug. Useful inside verses where you want forward motion, less useful at chorus endings where you usually want closure.

## Assonance Rhyme

**Definition:** the stressed **vowel matches** but the ending consonants are different and from different families. Examples per [Andrea Stolpe](https://www.andreastolpe.com/articles/rhyme-types) and confirmed in [Pattison's Berklee teaching](https://online.berklee.edu/takenote/prosody-in-music-and-songwriting/): *rope/known*, *straight/fame*, *life/tide*, *read/cheap*. Assonance creates **sonic relationship without closure** — the ear hears a connection, but the brain registers something still owed. The [Berklee Take Note article](https://online.berklee.edu/takenote/prosody-in-music-and-songwriting/) summarizes Pattison: assonance "supports unstable, unresolved meaning through ambiguity." Heavy use in indie and singer-songwriter writing (Joni Mitchell, Phoebe Bridgers) because it preserves the rhyme's sonic glue while refusing emotional closure.

## Consonance Rhyme

**Definition:** the **ending consonants match**, but the stressed vowels differ. Examples per [Andrea Stolpe](https://www.andreastolpe.com/articles/rhyme-types): *bag/log*, *ground/bond*, *stood/wade*, *sock/back*. This is the weakest rhyme on Pattison's scale — barely audible as rhyme to most listeners, but it does smuggle in sonic continuity. Heavily used in hip-hop alongside multisyllabic and internal rhyme to thicken the texture, less used in melodic pop because it doesn't reliably land.

## Cadential Strength and Section Function

The practical move: **match rhyme stability to section function**. Per Pattison's prosody framework via [Berklee Online](https://online.berklee.edu/takenote/prosody-in-music-and-songwriting/) and the [Songwriting.net study](https://www.songwriting.net/blog/size-matters-a-study-in-prosody):

- **Choruses** typically need stability — perfect or family rhymes at the line-ending key positions, especially on the title or hook line. The chorus is the answer; the answer should sound landed.
- **Verses** can carry less-resolved rhyme types — additive, subtractive, even assonance — because the verse is the question, the setup, the forward-motion phase.
- **Bridges** often deliberately destabilize via assonance, consonance, or odd line counts to make the return to the final chorus feel earned.

This is a section-function principle, not a rule. Plenty of effective verses use perfect rhyme; plenty of effective choruses use assonance for an unresolved emotional payoff. The point is to **make the choice consciously**.

## Rhyme Scheme Stability — AABB, ABAB, ABBA

Beyond individual rhyme type, the **rhyme scheme pattern** also encodes stability. Per the [Berklee Online article](https://online.berklee.edu/takenote/prosody-in-music-and-songwriting/):

- **AABB and ABAB** are stable. Couplets and alternating quatrains feel resolved, predictable, balanced.
- **ABBA** is unstable. The "envelope" pattern leaves the listener mid-thought before returning to the rhyme. Pattison cites James Taylor's *"Sweet Baby James"* as an ABBA scheme that intentionally unsettles.
- **ABAC or AXAY** schemes (where one slot is unrhymed) are deliberately incomplete. Useful when content is itself incomplete or wounded.

Pair scheme with rhyme type: AABB perfect rhyme is the most stable possible structure; ABAC with assonance is among the least.

## Internal Rhyme — Pace Accelerator

**Internal rhyme** places rhymed sounds inside a line rather than at line endings. Per the [Quillbot internal rhyme guide](https://quillbot.com/blog/rhetoric/what-is-internal-rhyme/) and the [LitCharts entry](https://www.litcharts.com/literary-devices-and-terms/internal-rhyme), internal rhyme primarily functions as a **pace accelerator** — it pushes the ear from one rhymed point to the next inside a single line, making the line feel denser and faster. The [Lyric Stories internal rhyme explainer](https://lyricstories.com/internal-rhyme) notes the typical use case: pop and hip-hop verses where forward momentum matters. Examples cited in [Quillbot](https://quillbot.com/blog/rhetoric/what-is-internal-rhyme/): Taylor Swift's *"Romeo, take me somewhere we can be alone"* (Romeo / alone), and various Beatles lines where a mid-line word rhymes with a line-end. Heavy internal rhyme in verses creates the perception of speed and verbal dexterity; sparser internal rhyme in choruses keeps the hook from feeling crowded.

## Mosaic and Multisyllabic Rhyme — Hip-Hop's Backbone

**Multisyllabic rhyme** (or "multis") matches two or more consecutive syllables. **Mosaic rhyme** matches one polysyllabic word against two-or-more shorter words ("poet" / "know it"). Per the [Wikipedia multisyllabic rhymes entry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multisyllabic_rhymes) and the [Simon On Songs mosaic rhyme primer](https://www.simononsongs.com/p/what-is-mosaic-rhyme), these are hallmarks of advanced rap and the central engine of rhyme-density in hip-hop. The [Support Hip Hop rhyme scheme guide](https://supporthiphop.com/blog/rhyming-schemes-for-rap/) documents how artists like Eminem, Kendrick Lamar, and MF DOOM chain multis across bars to create rhyme schemes invisible at the line-end level but dense within. For songwriters working outside hip-hop, occasional mosaic rhymes are a sharp tool for verses where you want a clever, surprising landing — but they read as showy if overused in melodic pop.

## When to Break the Rules

Pattison's prosody framework permits — and sometimes demands — **deliberate violations**. A perfectly stable AABB perfect-rhyme verse about loss feels emotionally false; a deliberately unbalanced ABA assonance verse about loss feels true. The [Songwriting.net prosody analysis](https://www.songwriting.net/blog/size-matters-a-study-in-prosody) summarizes the principle: align rhyme structure to content character, and break alignment only when the break is itself the point (irony, denial, performed cheerfulness over real grief). The [Sound on Sound Pattison interview](https://www.soundonsound.com/techniques/pat-pattison-writing-better-lyrics) records his framing of this calibration: every form choice should be auditable against the song's central feeling.
