## Pattison Background

Berklee professor; began as a guitarist forced into songwriting, then transitioned to teaching. Created Berklee's groundbreaking songwriting course, which evolved into the world's first songwriting major. Students include John Mayer and Gillian Welch.

## Prosody: Form Supporting Content

**Prosody** = alignment between form and content.

Quote: *"Whatever you are saying, all of the elements should support it."*

A song about heartbreak needs structural elements reflecting that emotion, not tidy rhyme schemes suggesting simplicity. Tools for expressing instability:

- Asymmetrical rhyme schemes
- Odd line counts
- Melodic displacement against chord structures

## Action Verbs as Amplifier

Action verbs are the **"power amplifier"** of language. Robert Frost's image of a seedling *"shouldering its way and shedding the earth crumbs"* cited as example.

Warning: *"Be careful what you attach your amplifier to!"* — strength should match the idea's importance. Weak forms of "to be" offer alternatives for amplification.

Spacing matters — place important lines where listeners can absorb them, preventing competition between ideas.

## Cliché vs. Specificity

The most common mistake is **cliché**: prefabricated language reassembled repeatedly. Solution: specificity and sensory detail.

Examples of sense-bound specificity that works:

- Cole Porter, *"You'd be so nice by the fire"* — specificity triggers sense memories
- *"Turn down the lights, turn down the bed"* — concrete imagery

Effective lyrics stimulate listeners' senses early, transforming them from observers into participants.

## Revision

Writing is process; rewriting is *"the gold standard"* for professionals. A first verse needn't remain first — repositioning reveals new structural possibilities.

On writer's block: Pattison rejects the framing. His solution is to **write intentionally bad songs daily**. Around the seventh or eighth attempt, quality emerges naturally.

He advocates **15–20 minutes daily practice** alongside object writing exercises from *Songwriting Without Boundaries*.

## Point of View — Four Approaches

1. **Third-person narrative** — narrator outside the song's world, omniscient. *"Eleanor Rigby"*; John Mayer's *"Walt Grace's Submarine Test, January 1967"*.
2. **Second-person narrative** — narrator tells the "you" character something unknowable to them. *"For No One"*; *"Like A Rolling Stone"*.
3. **First-person narrative** — narrator's personal experience. Should offer listeners meaningful lessons rather than self-indulgence.
4. **Direct address** — most common in pop music, addressing the listener directly (I/you). Warning: easy to overuse, can become static during live performances.

## Final Principle

Write the best song possible rather than chasing market preferences. Excellence — successful or not — represents growth.

Quote: *"If you're writing and trying to write well, and trying to write about things that matter, you're going to grow as a human being."*
