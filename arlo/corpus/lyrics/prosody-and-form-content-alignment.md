## What Prosody Is

Prosody, in Pat Pattison's working definition recorded at [Berklee Online Take Note](https://online.berklee.edu/takenote/prosody-in-music-and-songwriting/), is **"the appropriate relationship between elements"** — words to melody, rhyme scheme to emotion, line length to feeling, chord motion to lyric content. Across his books and Berklee lectures, Pattison treats prosody not as one technique among many but as **the master craft principle**: every formal choice in a song should be auditable against the song's central feeling. The corollary, recorded in the [Sound on Sound Pattison interview](https://www.soundonsound.com/techniques/pat-pattison-writing-better-lyrics): *"Whatever you are saying, all of the elements should support it."* A song about heartbreak with tidy rhyme and balanced quatrains is prosodically false — the form contradicts the content, and the listener feels the contradiction even when they can't name it.

## The Stable / Unstable Axis

Pattison's organizing distinction, repeated in [Berklee Online](https://online.berklee.edu/takenote/prosody-in-music-and-songwriting/) and the [SongPad prosody overview](https://www.songpad.co/blog/the-art-of-prosody-in-songwriting-crafting-emotion-with-precision/), is **stable vs. unstable**. He has said this binary "covers everything" because most lyric content sits somewhere on that axis — resolution or motion, certainty or doubt, closure or wanting. The drafting question becomes: *for this section, is the feeling stable or unstable?* The answer drives every form decision downstream. The [Berklee Online article](https://online.berklee.edu/takenote/prosody-in-music-and-songwriting/) summarizes the process Pattison teaches: identify the idea, characterize it as stable or unstable, then choose the formal tools that match.

## Tool 1 — Line Count

Per the [Songwriting.net "Size Matters" study](https://www.songwriting.net/blog/size-matters-a-study-in-prosody) summarizing Pattison's framework:

- **Even-numbered line counts** (2, 4, 6, 8) feel **stable, resolved, balanced**.
- **Odd-numbered line counts** (3, 5, 7) feel **unstable, incomplete, forward-leaning**.

The cited example: **John Mayer's *"Your Body Is a Wonderland"*** uses a three-line chorus, and the odd count creates "a sense of longing, a desire for more" — the listener feels the chorus wants a fourth line that never arrives. The Beatles' *"Lucy in the Sky with Diamonds"* uses a three-line chorus to reinforce surrealism. Pattison's editorial demonstration on Lana Del Rey's *"Ride,"* documented in the same Songwriting.net article, reduces originally-even sections to three lines to inject motion that the lyric's wandering theme demands.

## Tool 2 — Line Length

**Matched line lengths feel stable.** Unequal line lengths feel unstable. A verse of four 8-syllable lines lands balanced; a verse alternating 8 and 5 syllables creates a tug. Per the [Songwriting.net study](https://www.songwriting.net/blog/size-matters-a-study-in-prosody) and the [SongPad article](https://www.songpad.co/blog/the-art-of-prosody-in-songwriting-crafting-emotion-with-precision/), Pattison's working move is: **for stable content, use matched lines; for unstable content, deliberately break the match.** A long line followed by a short fragment ("and I waited there until morning / for nothing") encodes the imbalance the lyric describes.

## Tool 3 — Rhyme Scheme Stability

Per the companion rhyme-types document and the [Berklee Online article](https://online.berklee.edu/takenote/prosody-in-music-and-songwriting/), rhyme scheme is a stability lever:

- **AABB, ABAB** = stable. Predictable, resolved.
- **ABBA** = unstable. The envelope pattern interrupts the expected return. Pattison cites James Taylor's *"Sweet Baby James"* as deliberate ABBA destabilization.
- **AXAY or open-ended schemes** = most unstable. Leaving slots unrhymed creates explicit incompleteness.

Pair scheme with content character. AABB perfect rhyme about loss is the textbook false-form failure case Pattison critiques in the [Sound on Sound interview](https://www.soundonsound.com/techniques/pat-pattison-writing-better-lyrics): a song with four equal AABB perfect-rhymed lines about heartbreak sounds like "you're just telling me facts," because the form is too tidy to carry the content.

## Tool 4 — Rhyme Type

Stability ranking from the [Andrea Stolpe summary of Pattison's framework](https://www.andreastolpe.com/articles/rhyme-types): perfect > family > additive/subtractive > assonance > consonance. The deeper into the unstable end you go, the more the rhyme connection feels owed-but-not-paid. Use perfect rhyme to land a chorus that needs closure; use assonance to keep a verse open and yearning; use consonance to barely connect a couplet that is more about phonetic glue than rhyme payoff. See the companion document `rhyme-types-and-cadence-function.md` for the full taxonomy.

## Tool 5 — Lyric Placement Within the Bar

Per the [SongPad prosody article](https://www.songpad.co/blog/the-art-of-prosody-in-songwriting-crafting-emotion-with-precision/) summarizing Pattison's teaching: **front-heavy placement** (the stressed syllable lands on or near beat 1 of the bar) reinforces **stability**. **Back-heavy placement** (the line starts late in the bar, with the stress arriving on beat 3, 4, or the "and-of-4") reinforces **instability**. The move is a rhythmic-displacement decision, not a melodic one — even with the same notes, pushing the line later in the bar changes its emotional weight.

## Tool 6 — Linguistic Stress vs. Melodic Stress

This is the central technical alignment in prosody. **Stressed syllables should land on stressed melodic positions; unstressed syllables on unstressed positions.** Per the [Grokipedia prosody article](https://grokipedia.com/page/Prosody_(music)) and the [Ableton "Rhythm of Lyrics" chapter](https://makingmusic.ableton.com/the-rhythm-of-lyrics): when this alignment fails — when an unstressed syllable lands on a strong beat or a stressed syllable on a weak one — the line feels forced, the rhyme feels grudging, and the listener experiences low-grade friction without knowing why.

The textbook example: "PHO-to-graph" is naturally stressed PHO-toh-graph (strong-weak-medium). Setting it to a melody that emphasizes "toh" (pho-TOH-graph) creates an unnatural stress that listeners parse as awkward. The fix, per [Songstuff's lyric rhythm article](https://forums.songstuff.com/blogs/entry/1489-getting-lyric-rhythm-and-stress-right/), is to sing your lyrics aloud against the melody before committing — the ear catches mismatched stress instantly. Pattison's *Songwriting: Essential Guide to Lyric Form and Structure* dedicates substantial real estate to this alignment, per the [Pat Pattison Berklee Press page](https://berkleepress.com/berklee-authors/pat-pattison-2/).

## Tool 7 — Section Function Reinforcement

Per Pattison's frame from the [Berklee Online article](https://online.berklee.edu/takenote/prosody-in-music-and-songwriting/), each section of a pop song has a default stability character:

- **Verses** = relatively stable in delivery but unstable in resolution (telling the story, expecting the chorus to land)
- **Pre-choruses** = explicitly unstable (rising tension, asymmetric lines, often unresolved rhyme)
- **Choruses** = stable (the answer, the title, perfect or family rhyme, matched lines)
- **Bridges** = unstable (the deliberate departure that makes the final chorus feel earned)

Prosodic violations happen when the chosen form contradicts the section's function — a chorus written like a pre-chorus, or a bridge written like a verse. The diagnostic question: does each section's *form* sound like its *function*?

## Tool 8 — Asymmetric Structures for Unstable Feelings

Per the [Sound on Sound Pattison interview](https://www.soundonsound.com/techniques/pat-pattison-writing-better-lyrics): unstable feelings call for **asymmetric rhyme schemes, odd line counts, and melodic displacement against chord structures**. Heartbreak does not arrive in tidy quatrains. The form has to leak. Pattison's example sets — songs where the verse has five lines instead of four, where the rhyme drops out in one position, where the melody pushes through a chord change rather than landing on it — all reflect the principle that **felt instability should be encoded as structural instability**.

## Tool 9 — Action Verbs as Amplification

Per the [Sound on Sound Pattison interview](https://www.soundonsound.com/techniques/pat-pattison-writing-better-lyrics), Pattison treats **action verbs as the "power amplifier" of lyric language**. The example he cites is Robert Frost's image of a seedling *"shouldering its way and shedding the earth crumbs"* — "shouldering" and "shedding" do the imaginative lift that any version of "growing" would not. The prosodic corollary: place action verbs in positions of metrical stress, on important downbeats, on rhymed syllables. Pattison's warning from the same interview: *"Be careful what you attach your amplifier to!"* — using a heavy action verb on a trivial idea creates the opposite of prosody, an over-energized line attached to under-weight content.

## Prosodic Violation as Intentional Craft

The rule is align form to content. **The advanced move is to break alignment deliberately when the break itself is the meaning.** Per the [Sound on Sound interview](https://www.soundonsound.com/techniques/pat-pattison-writing-better-lyrics) and the [SongPad article](https://www.songpad.co/blog/the-art-of-prosody-in-songwriting-crafting-emotion-with-precision/), examples include:

- **Performed cheerfulness over real grief** — tidy AABB perfect-rhyme couplets on devastating content can read as denial (Randy Newman's catalog leans on this, as do certain Morrissey songs)
- **Ironic stability** — the form lies about the content on purpose, and the listener catches the lie
- **Sudden form breaks** — three matched verses followed by an unmatched fourth signals rupture without naming it

The test for whether the violation is craft or accident: can you articulate why the misalignment serves the song? If yes, ship it. If you can only justify it as "it just felt right," check whether the form is doing what you want.

## Revision Through the Prosody Lens

Pattison's revision protocol, recorded in the [Sound on Sound interview](https://www.soundonsound.com/techniques/pat-pattison-writing-better-lyrics): rewriting is *"the gold standard"* and a first verse needn't remain first. The prosody-driven revision pass is: **read each section against its content character.** Where do form and feeling agree? Where do they fight? Where the fight is unintentional, fix the form. Where the fight is the point, leave it. The [Songtown rewriting guide](https://songtown.com/on-songwriting/how-to-master-the-art-of-rewriting-lyrics/) recommends a parallel pass on tense and POV — both are prosodic levers in Pattison's broader sense.

## The One Question That Drives Everything

Per [Berklee Online's summary](https://online.berklee.edu/takenote/prosody-in-music-and-songwriting/), Pattison reduces prosody practice to one diagnostic question: **"How do I feel in this section, stable or unstable?"** Every formal lever — line count, line length, rhyme scheme, rhyme type, lyric placement, stress alignment, action-verb weight — answers to that question. Most amateur lyrics fail prosody not because the writer doesn't know the tools but because they never asked the question. Songs that sound emotionally true almost always have answered it consciously, even if the answer was intuitive.
