## What Object Writing Is

Object writing is a sense-bound free-writing exercise developed by Berklee professor Pat Pattison and introduced in his 1995 book *Writing Better Lyrics*. The mechanic is simple: pick a real, concrete object, then write about it for a fixed window, channeling every sense you can muster. The object is treated as a "diving board to launch you inward to the vaults of your senses," as described in the [iZotope object writing guide](https://www.izotope.com/en/learn/object-writing-your-secret-to-vivid-song-lyrics). The point is not to produce a finished lyric; it is to mine raw sensory material that you later loot for images, lines, and metaphors when a song actually needs them. Pattison popularized the practice through Berklee's songwriting major, where it is taught as foundational daily training — analogous to scales for an instrumentalist, per the [Berklee Online Take Note overview](https://online.berklee.edu/takenote/prosody-in-music-and-songwriting/).

## The Ten-Minute Rule

The discipline is timed: **exactly ten minutes, no more and no less**. Pattison is emphatic about the duration in *Writing Better Lyrics* — the exercise is described as "ten minutes a day," as documented in [Joan Y. Edwards' summary](https://joanyedwards.com/ten-minutes-a-day-to-good-writing-using-seven-senses/) and the [MasterClass article on object writing](https://www.masterclass.com/articles/object-writing). Set a timer. When it sounds, stop mid-sentence. The fixed window does two things: it builds the habit through repetition without inflating it into a daily burden, and it forces you past the conscious editor in the first three or four minutes, into the looser associative territory where the better images live. Pattison recommends practicing this **daily for at least six weeks** to internalize the habit, per [Writer's Digest's excerpt summary](https://www.writersdigest.com/wd-books/writing-better-lyrics).

## The Seven Senses

Pattison expands the standard five senses to seven. The full set, as enumerated across his books and confirmed by the [iZotope guide](https://www.izotope.com/en/learn/object-writing-your-secret-to-vivid-song-lyrics) and the [Soundfly Flypaper article](https://flypaper.soundfly.com/write/how-to-generate-great-songwriting-ideas-with-object-writing-technique/):

1. **Sight** — visual detail, color, shape, light quality, figurative resemblance
2. **Sound** — what the object makes, what surrounds it sonically
3. **Smell** — often the strongest trigger for memory and place
4. **Taste** — flavor, including metaphorical taste ("bitter morning")
5. **Touch** — texture, temperature, pressure, weight
6. **Organic** — internal bodily awareness: heartbeat, breath, muscle tension, nausea, the prickle on the back of your neck
7. **Kinesthetic** — the body's position and movement in space: falling, spinning, leaning, being trapped or weightless

The last two are the differentiators. Most beginners over-rely on sight; Pattison's framework forces you into the body senses where felt experience actually lives.

## How to Write the Ten Minutes

Free-write in sentences or fragments. No rhyme, no meter, no structure, no editing, no crossing out. Follow associations — if writing about "back porch" leads you to your grandmother's perfume, follow it; don't drag yourself back to the porch. Pattison's instruction from *Writing Better Lyrics*, quoted in the [MasterClass summary](https://www.masterclass.com/articles/object-writing): "Anything goes, as long as it is sense-bound." The discipline is **sense-bound**, not topic-bound. If you've drifted to a memory of broken glass on a bathroom floor, write that floor, that glass, that smell. Do not stop to make it pretty. Pretty is the enemy of true.

## Specificity Beats Generality

The reason object writing matters for finished lyrics is that **specific concrete nouns out-perform abstract emotional labels every time**. Pattison's standing example in interviews is Cole Porter's *"You'd be so nice by the fire"* — "by the fire" does work that "with me" cannot, because it cues a listener's own sense memories of warmth, smoke, dim orange light. The [Sound on Sound Pattison interview](https://www.soundonsound.com/techniques/pat-pattison-writing-better-lyrics) records his framing: effective lyrics "stimulate listeners' senses early, transforming them from observers into participants." A line like "I miss you" is a label. A line like "your toothbrush is still in the cup" is a participant ticket.

## Avoiding Cliché Through Sense Detail

Pattison identifies **cliché as the single most common lyric failure** — prefabricated language reassembled because the writer reached for what was nearest. Per the [Sound on Sound interview](https://www.soundonsound.com/techniques/pat-pattison-writing-better-lyrics), the cure is specificity: sense-bound detail that could only come from this writer, this memory, this object. If a line could appear in 300 other songs, it is by definition cliché. Object writing builds the reflex to reach past the prefab — the daily ten minutes trains you to access detail that is yours alone, not detail recycled from the last six pop hits you heard. The seventh-or-eighth-attempt principle Pattison teaches (write deliberately bad songs until something true falls out) is the same instinct applied to whole drafts.

## Show, Don't Tell — In Practical Terms

"Show don't tell" is generic creative-writing advice, but Pattison gives it a concrete operational definition: **replace stated feelings with sensory nouns and action verbs**. Instead of "I was nervous," write "my keys would not fit the lock." Instead of "she was angry," write "she set the cup down hard enough to spill." Object writing builds the inventory of such substitutions. The [SongPad prosody overview](https://www.songpad.co/blog/the-art-of-prosody-in-songwriting-crafting-emotion-with-precision/) reinforces this: emotional words tell, sensory nouns and motion verbs show. The reader/listener does the emotional inference work, and inference is what makes a lyric stick.

## Harvesting Object Writes Into Songs

After the ten minutes, **highlight in three different colors**: the strongest image, the most unexpected association, and any phrase that already sounds like a line. The [iZotope guide](https://www.izotope.com/en/learn/object-writing-your-secret-to-vivid-song-lyrics) recommends keeping a running "songwriting ideas document" that you mine from. Most object writes will go nowhere directly — that is fine; the practice trains the muscle. But every fifth or sixth session typically yields a phrase, an image, or a metaphor that becomes a verse anchor or a title. Treat object writes as a quarry, not as drafts.

## Organic and Kinesthetic — The Underused Senses

Because these two are the least obvious, they are the most differentiating in finished lyrics. **Organic** examples: Fifth Harmony's "Sledgehammer" works the heartbeat-as-organic-sense pulse, per the [iZotope guide](https://www.izotope.com/en/learn/object-writing-your-secret-to-vivid-song-lyrics). **Kinesthetic** examples cited in the same source: Linkin Park's "Crawling" (the verb is the whole image), The Shires' "State Lines" (movement across geography), Faith Hill's "When the Lights Go Down" (positional darkness). When a lyric feels physically inhabited rather than narrated from outside, organic or kinesthetic detail is usually doing the work.

## Daily Practice Cadence

Pattison's recommendation, quoted in the [Sound on Sound interview](https://www.soundonsound.com/techniques/pat-pattison-writing-better-lyrics), is **15–20 minutes of writing practice daily**, of which the ten-minute object write is the core. The [American Blues Scene profile](https://www.americanbluesscene.com/2020/12/pat-pattison-berklees-songwriting-guru/) confirms the same expectation across his Berklee teaching. The cadence matters more than session length — five days a week of ten minutes beats one ninety-minute marathon. The *Songwriting Without Boundaries* book is structured as a 14-week object-writing program with daily prompts, indicating Pattison's belief that the practice has to be habituated, not visited.

## Common Failure Modes

Three failure modes recur with new practitioners:

1. **Editing during the ten minutes.** The mind stays in conscious editor mode and never drops into associative flow. Fix: enforce the no-stopping rule, even when you write nonsense.
2. **Staying visual.** The write reads like a description of a photograph rather than a body in a place. Fix: deliberately rotate through the seven senses; if you reach minute 4 and haven't written a smell, write a smell.
3. **Picking abstractions instead of objects.** "Loneliness" or "regret" are not objects. "The blue armchair where my mother read" is an object. Fix: pick something you could photograph.

The [Songwriter Theory summary of Pattison's three tips](https://www.songwritertheory.com/blog/3-tips-from-writing-better-lyrics-by-pat-pattison) reinforces the diagnostic value of re-reading old object writes — what senses do you skip? Those are your gaps.

## When Not to Object-Write

Object writing is preparation, not composition. **Do not try to write a song through object writing.** Pattison treats it as a separate practice — daily scales — distinct from drafting an actual lyric, per the [Berklee Online Take Note article](https://online.berklee.edu/takenote/prosody-in-music-and-songwriting/) and the framing in *Songwriting Without Boundaries*. When drafting a song, you may pull from your object-write archive, but you should be in composition mode (deciding what serves the song), not free-association mode (going wherever the senses lead). Conflating the two produces bloated, sense-drunk drafts that are vivid but structurally weak.
