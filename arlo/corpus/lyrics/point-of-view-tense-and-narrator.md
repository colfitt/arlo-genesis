# Point of View, Tense, and Narrator

## POV as a Camera

Pat Pattison's framing of point of view, recorded in the [Sound on Sound interview](https://www.soundonsound.com/techniques/pat-pattison-writing-better-lyrics) and reinforced in [iZotope's POV guide](https://www.izotope.com/en/learn/songwriting-basics-choosing-the-right-point-of-view.html), is that **POV controls listener distance from the song's world** the way a camera controls distance from a scene. Long shot, medium shot, close-up — each available position changes how much the listener can see, how much they feel implicated, and how much they project themselves into the song. POV is therefore not a grammatical choice; it is a directorial one. The default for pop is direct address (singer to "you"); the default for narrative folk/country is first or third person. Choosing consciously beats defaulting.

## The Four Pattison POV Options

Pattison's published taxonomy in *Writing Better Lyrics*, summarized in the [Sound on Sound interview](https://www.soundonsound.com/techniques/pat-pattison-writing-better-lyrics):

1. **Third-person narrative** — narrator outside the song, telling about characters
2. **Second-person narrative** — narrator tells the "you" something they could not know
3. **First-person narrative** — narrator inside the song, reporting personal experience
4. **Direct address** — singer ("I") speaks to a present "you"

These are the four canonical options. Some sources collapse #2 and #4 into a single "second person" category — Pattison distinguishes them because they place the narrator at different distances. Stay with the four-way split when working in Pattison's frame.

## Third-Person Narrative

**Definition:** the singer is not in the song. Pronouns are "he, she, they, it" or character names. The narrator can be omniscient (knowing every character's interior) or limited (knowing only what is observable). Per the [Sound on Sound Pattison interview](https://www.soundonsound.com/techniques/pat-pattison-writing-better-lyrics), the canonical examples are the Beatles' *"Eleanor Rigby"* — the first Beatles song to contain none of the words I, me, mine, you, or your, per [Wikipedia's "Eleanor Rigby" entry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eleanor_Rigby) — and John Mayer's *"Walt Grace's Submarine Test, January 1967,"* a third-person character study. Strength: the listener gets to observe and empathize without being directly addressed; the writer can say things about characters they could not say about themselves. Weakness: the most emotionally distant POV; love songs almost never work in pure third person.

## Second-Person Narrative (Pattison's Sense)

**Definition:** the narrator addresses a "you" character with information the "you" could not know about themselves — backstory, motives, fates. The narrator is not the "you," but is intimately aware of them. Per the [Sound on Sound Pattison interview](https://www.soundonsound.com/techniques/pat-pattison-writing-better-lyrics), the standing examples are the Beatles' *"For No One"* (narrating "her" interior life to "her") and Dylan's *"Like A Rolling Stone"* (narrating "you"-the-fallen-aristocrat to that same person). This POV is rare and powerful — it creates the sensation of a god's-eye witness speaking to a subject who cannot answer. Note that this is **distinct from direct address**, where "I" speaks to "you" inside a present relationship.

## First-Person Narrative

**Definition:** the narrator is the protagonist; pronouns are "I, me, my, we, us." Per the [iZotope POV guide](https://www.izotope.com/en/learn/songwriting-basics-choosing-the-right-point-of-view.html), this is the most common POV in modern songwriting because it carries **maximum immediacy** — the listener experiences events through the singer's interior. Examples from the [BMI POV article](https://www.bmi.com/news/entry/which-point-of-view-is-best-for-your-song) include Donna Fargo's *"I'm the Happiest Girl in the Whole U.S.A."* and Camila Cabello's *"Bam Bam."* Pattison's caution from the [Sound on Sound interview](https://www.soundonsound.com/techniques/pat-pattison-writing-better-lyrics): first-person lyrics should offer listeners "meaningful lessons rather than self-indulgence." First person without earned universality is a diary entry the audience does not need to read.

## Direct Address — Pop's Default

**Definition:** "I" speaks to "you" within a present relationship. Both pronouns appear; the conversation is implied. Per the [Sound on Sound Pattison interview](https://www.soundonsound.com/techniques/pat-pattison-writing-better-lyrics) and the [iZotope POV guide](https://www.izotope.com/en/learn/songwriting-basics-choosing-the-right-point-of-view.html), this is **the dominant POV in modern pop** — Dolly Parton's *"I Will Always Love You,"* Stevie Wonder's *"Overjoyed,"* Ariana Grande's *"God is a woman,"* Carole King's *"You've Got a Friend."* The strength is intimacy: the listener overhears or is positioned as the "you." Pattison's warning: direct address is "easy to overuse" and can become static — when every song is "I love you / I miss you / you're the one," the lexical economy collapses. The fix is to make the "I" and "you" specific: who is speaking, who is being addressed, what circumstance.

## Second Person Without "I" — A Variant

A subset of second person uses "you" without a present "I," addressing the listener as universal subject. Per the [BMI POV article](https://www.bmi.com/news/entry/which-point-of-view-is-best-for-your-song), examples include Billy Joel's *"We Didn't Start the Fire"* (addressing collective "we"/"you") and Pink's *"What About Us?"* (rhetorical address). This works well for protest, anthems, and collective grief — but Pattison would categorize most of these as a hybrid rather than pure second person, because the narrator's "I" is usually implicit even when unwritten.

## Tense Decisions — Present, Past, Future

Per the [Sound on Sound "Understanding & Writing Lyrics Part 2" article](https://www.soundonsound.com/techniques/understanding-writing-lyrics-part-2) and the [Tony Conniff present-vs-past article](https://tonyconniff.com/is-your-song-happening-in-the-present-or-the-past/):

- **Present tense** = immediacy. The action is happening now; the listener is in the room. The Beatles' narrative songs largely use present tense to put events in front of the listener.
- **Past tense** = reflection, regret, distance. The events are over; the singer is metabolizing them. Most "she left me" / "we used to" lyrics live here.
- **Future tense** = anticipation, promise, threat. Rarer because it implies the song's events haven't happened yet — useful for love letters, ultimatums, and aspirational songs ("we'll be").

The [Songtown rewriting guide](https://songtown.com/on-songwriting/how-to-master-the-art-of-rewriting-lyrics/) recommends tense as a revision lever: if a draft feels lifeless, try shifting it from past to present and see if it pulls the listener closer. The [thisismywww perspective-and-tense article](https://thisismywww.com/on-songwriting/perspective-time-tense/) reinforces this — tense changes the emotional temperature without requiring rewrite of content.

## Tense Consistency vs. Deliberate Shift

The default is **stay in one tense per section**, ideally one tense per song. Per the [Sound on Sound article](https://www.soundonsound.com/techniques/understanding-writing-lyrics-part-2), accidental tense-switching reads as a writer error and breaks the listener out of the world. But **deliberate** tense-switching is a sharp tool: verse in past tense (looking back), chorus in present tense (the feeling is alive now), bridge in future tense (what comes next). The shift becomes the dramatic motion. The rule of thumb from the [Songtown rewriting article](https://songtown.com/on-songwriting/how-to-master-the-art-of-rewriting-lyrics/): if you switch tense, the switch should be **section-aligned**, not mid-line, and the listener should feel the change as meaningful.

## Switching POV Mid-Song

Per the [BMI POV article](https://www.bmi.com/news/entry/which-point-of-view-is-best-for-your-song), mixing POVs is "not typically" recommended — most songs commit to one and stay. But there are exceptions that work: Charlie Puth's *"Attention"* uses first-person verses and direct-address choruses (singer reports about "you" in the verse, addresses "you" in the chorus). The [Song Chops POV piece](http://songchops.com/2014/07/point-view/) documents the safer rule: switch only between adjacent POV pairs — first-person to direct address, third-person to second-person — not across the full spectrum. Switching from third-person verse to first-person chorus mid-song without a structural reason will read as a writer mistake, not a craft choice.

## Narrator Reliability and Distance

The narrator is a **character** even when invisible. Per the [Berklee Online narrative-gaps article](https://online.berklee.edu/takenote/long-read-writing-songs-with-narrative-gaps/) and the [Substack analysis of Taylor Swift's unreliable narrators](https://aptaylorswift.substack.com/p/taylor-swift-unreliable-narrators-songwriting-evolution), modern songwriting increasingly leans on **unreliable narrators** — first-person speakers who are wrong, in denial, or performing a version of themselves. Swift's *"Blank Space"* is the standard example: the narrator's "boy-crazy" self-portrayal is performed irony, and the song works because the listener recognizes the performance. Frank Ocean and Kendrick Lamar use narrator unreliability extensively to make the listener "an active participant in the story." For the songwriter, the move is to ask: does the narrator know what the listener knows? When the answer is *no*, you have access to dramatic irony.

## Direct Address — The Static Risk

Pattison's specific warning in the [Sound on Sound interview](https://www.soundonsound.com/techniques/pat-pattison-writing-better-lyrics) about direct address: it "can become static during live performances." The reason is that when every song addresses "you," the "you" stops being a specific person and collapses into a generic listener — the dramatic situation flattens. The countermove is **specificity within the address**: describe the room, the moment, the trigger, the physical detail. "I miss you" is static. "Your toothbrush is still in the cup and it's been three weeks" is not. Object writing (see the companion object-writing document in this corpus) is the standard remedy for static direct-address writing.

## When the POV Is the Hook

Sometimes the POV decision *is* the song's core idea. *"Eleanor Rigby"* would not work in first person — the distance is the meaning. *"Like a Rolling Stone"* would not work in third person — the indictment requires that "you" be addressed directly. The [Slate article on "Eleanor Rigby"](https://slate.com/culture/2016/02/the-making-of-eleanor-rigby-the-beatles-most-mysterious-song.html) records Paul McCartney describing the writing as "like writing a short story" — third person was structural, not incidental. When auditioning POVs during drafting, ask: which POV makes the central feeling impossible to write in any other way? That's the right one. The [iZotope POV guide](https://www.izotope.com/en/learn/songwriting-basics-choosing-the-right-point-of-view.html) recommends rewriting the first verse in all four POVs as a diagnostic exercise — the version that holds up best identifies the song's natural perspective.
