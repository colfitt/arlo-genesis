# TPS Vocal Lead — treble booster into an overdriven channel

## Concept
That Pedal Show's key treble-booster insight: a treble booster into a clean amp can be unpleasant, but into a channel that's ALREADY overdriven it comes into its own and takes you to a really vocal lead tone — Mick Ronson / Brian May territory. Engage the booster on Channel 1, push Channel 1 into overdrive/distortion, and lean on the booster for a singing, cutting lead. This is the general lead principle; the rig-specific banjo version lives in Banjo-Lead Cut Recovery.

## How to play it
1. Set Channel 1 to OVERDRIVE (or DISTORTION) and get it clearly into breakup first — the booster is unpleasant into a CLEAN amp but sings into an already-overdriven channel.
2. Engage the treble booster on Channel 1 — DOWN (classic Rangemaster) for the vocal voice, UP for more cut through a dark/humbucker rig.
3. Play leads — you're after the singing, vocal Mick Ronson / Brian May tone.
4. If it sounds closed-in or chewy, bring PRESENCE up (hold the Ch1 footswitch + turn TONE 1) or engage PRES LINK 1 to open it out.

## Notes
- TPS-quoted + PG humbucker-cut note: the "booster into an already-overdriven channel = vocal lead" principle, the presence-up reveal, and the top-end+booster cut for dark humbucker rigs are all quoted; no exact knob numbers exist in any primary source — dial GAIN/VOL/TONE from the recipe.
- Distinct from Banjo-Lead Cut Recovery (which targets banjo articulation at low gain in a specific rig): this is the general lead-voice principle that wants the channel already overdriven.
- Expect a higher noise floor with the booster on — normal when emphasizing upper-mids/treble.
