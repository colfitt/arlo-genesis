Joe Edelmann · Shreddy or Not, Here It Comes: The King of Gear's Oxford Drive v2 · 2021-10-22

Hey friends, Joe Edelmann here with a special-edition gear demo video for a pedal that I absolutely love: this is the newest revision of the Oxford Drive from The King of Gear. If you watch my channel and you're a Radiohead obsessive like myself, you probably already know all about The King of Gear and the wonderful pedals he makes. He releases pedals in small batches, and the latest batch for this new Oxford design should be releasing very soon.

The Oxford Drive is his take on two iconic Marshall distortion pedal designs — the ShredMaster and the Guv'nor — that were most notably put to use by Jonny Greenwood, Ed O'Brien and even Thom Yorke of Radiohead during the band's classic period. While you can still occasionally find used versions of these long-out-of-print pedals separately, the Oxford is a great way to get both of their signature sounds plus some added features in a small footprint for a solid price. The Oxford costs $190 US plus $10 shipping if you're in the US.

I've been using version one of the Oxford Drive since I bought it back in early 2019, and it was later modded by the King to include a few of these latest improvements. You've heard it on nearly all of my Radiohead covers since then, especially ones like "Just" and "My Iron Lung," where it was used in conjunction with a Fender 85 amp and Lace Sensor pickups for Jonny's signature burn. Apart from accomplishing this distinctively massive and smooth high-gain tone, it has a lot of flexibility that comes from its 3-band EQ and the added clipping and voice selection options.

CONTROLS. At the top you have your EQ section: a BASS knob on the left, a TREBLE knob on the right, and what's called a CONTOUR knob in the middle. You'll notice it's not just called a "mids" control, since there's a bit of interactivity with the treble frequencies that goes on when you turn it up or down. When it's turned down, the mids are boosted and the trebles are slightly attenuated; as you turn it up, the pedal scoops mids but it also adds a bit of treble. Also important to note: the BASS knob is logarithmic in its taper, so to actually get more bass cut you're going to need to set it on the low side — between seven and nine o'clock.

On the bottom right you have your GAIN and VOLUME controls. There's a pretty wide range of gain on tap, so with the right EQ and clipping settings you can get into light overdrive territory rather than just straight distortion. For me, when I'm using the basic Jonny ShredMaster style, I keep the GAIN at or past three o'clock and the VOLUME around noon or so. This thing can really get loud and push your amp around if you want it to, but it sounds great even at bedroom volumes.

CLIPPING TOGGLE (three-way). The first is Si or silicon, which is the standard heavily-compressed high-gain distortion sound akin to the ShredMaster. The second is LED clipping, which is more open-sounding, louder and less compressed, and is based on the Guv'nor design. The third is asym, asymmetrical clipping, which uses the silicon and LED in tandem for a best-of-both-worlds that's fairly warm and dynamic — and to my ears maybe the most amp-like of the three.

VOICE TOGGLE (newly added two-way, labeled '91 / '88). The '91 voicing is the same as the previous version of the Oxford Drive, which emulates the ShredMaster's slightly darker profile. The '88 mode is a brighter voicing rooted in the Guv'nor of that same year. The voice switch affects both the pre- and post-gain filters of the pedal, allowing back in more upper frequencies without becoming too harsh. For me, I love the smoothness of the ShredMaster sound for lead playing, but I'm a huge fan of crisp high frequencies, especially in more rhythmic stuff, so I really love the optionality of this extra switch.

OTHER IMPROVEMENTS. The new soft-touch relay-style footswitch replaces the traditional latching switch of the previous version (same switch used on his Mini Glitch pedal). You can achieve flexible bypassing: tap the switch and it turns on/off as expected, but hold the switch and it stays on for as long as you have your foot held down, then instantly bypasses when you lift up. This is a really cool feature for inserting distorted bursts into clean playing, à la Pixies, or in cases where you might not have a noise gate to handle the little bit of hum that comes with higher-gain settings. This version also features improved capacitors that help eliminate unwanted feedback.

You've been hearing the Oxford Drive with a variety of my guitars into my Fender '68 Custom Deluxe Reverb amplifier miked with an SM57. This is just to give you a sense of how the pedal sounds with your garden-variety Fender-voiced tube amplifier.
