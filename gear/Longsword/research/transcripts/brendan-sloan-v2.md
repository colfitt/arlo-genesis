Brendan Sloan · Demo: Electronic Audio Experiments Longsword v2 · 2017-03-19

[NOTE: This is a V2-era demo (2017). The clip-mode labels and the second-channel footswitch described here are the OLD V2 design — LED/germanium/op-amp clipping and a "Gain 1/Gain 2 swap" footswitch — NOT the current V4/V4.5 (MOSFET/none/silicon clipping + a +20dB Boost channel). Kept for version context and the concrete band-fuzz setting Sloan quotes.]

So I thought while I had everything set up — I talked about this for a bit too — this is the Electronic Audio Experiments Longsword, built by the inimitable John Snyder of Electronic Audio Experiments. He's responsible for the feT that I was using as a preamp in my other video, but this is his flagship drive. You've probably seen other versions of it floating around — ones by Quarry or [other] effects pedals, the Bastard Saw, I think, in a bigger enclosure with a different control layout; there's a Vorpal Sword floating around — but this is V2, the currently available version you can get from John's website, and it is an absolute killer. This and the Model feT combine to make up probably 90% of why my guitars sound any good now.

It's pretty simple. Low and high Baxandall shelving EQs; an active midrange switchable between 300Hz and 1kHz as the Q-value, whatever your preference is, low or upper mid; a main out for a Level, pre-gain for a Drive — pretty standard. But there is a second drive inside attached to a trim pot, which is attached to a footswitch, so you can subtract that drive from the circuit and add this one. It's a swap — it's not additive like a fuzz with a boost, it's a direct swap, so you can set really low here, sit really high there, and swap them out super easy. I do it constantly.

And lastly but critically there is the clipping toggle here, which is LEDs on the left, new-old-stock germanium diodes in the center, and op-amp clipping on the right. So it changes the active component generating the gain in the circuit, so each have their own texture, each have their own overall output flavor. Some are splatty, some are crunchy, some of the less overall gain, some have more overall output / more gain, some are fuzzier. There's a lot you can find in this pedal.

I'll set it all to noon for now, on the 300Hz mids and LEDs on the left-hand side clipping, and see how we go. I've got my Harvester with Shiloh Dean P90s built by Anthony Pain (Harvest Guitars, Melbourne), going into an Axe Tommy Encompass combo, tuned to D standard.

[Playing — sweeps through clipping modes and EQ on P90s, D standard]

You can probably tell it's incredibly versatile. The germanium is probably my favorite setting, but it has so much output by comparison to the rest, you have to really do some real adjustment to get them to match up. But it's so versatile — I'm using it in the band as a fuzz right now. So I said it's the germanium, with the lows up, mids at 300 and about there, and the highs here, I can set a low Drive and then kick into like a crazy unhinged fuzz very easily.

[Band-fuzz setting playing — germanium clip, Low boosted, Mid at 300Hz, low Drive into the second-channel swap]

So good. Thank you John.

SETTINGS QUOTED (V2 — translate with care to V4/V4.5):
- Sloan's live band-fuzz: "germanium [clip], lows up, mids at 300 and about there, highs here, low Drive" then footswitch into the higher-gain second channel = "crazy unhinged fuzz." On V4/V4.5 the analog is: silicon clip (max compression/asymmetry, the fuzziest mode), Low boosted, Mid Shift down (300Hz) boosted, Drive low, and kick the Boost footswitch as the intensifier — directly relevant to the rig's "back the Drive off, use Boost as the stomp" approach.
- Confirms the 300Hz/1kHz Shift, the Baxandall Low/High, and that the modes differ markedly in output level (you must rebalance Level when switching clip modes — matches the V4.5 manual's warning).
