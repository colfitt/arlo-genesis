EAE's own positioning of the Longsword vs Dagger vs Halberd — the maker's intent in his own words.

**Longsword (the right EAE drive for this rig):**
- "an open, clear distortion character with lots of thickness," "incredibly versatile thanks to its hybrid active/passive EQ."
- Honest about lineage: it's "a member of a big family of op-amp drives, including the Distortion+, Rat, OCD, even the Klon KTR." Wears its influences on its sleeve.
- **The headline pitch:** "The thicker low midrange content of the Longsword fills out bright guitars, and its EQ allows for matching to a specific amp." — the single most relevant line for a bright banjo/baritone source.
- **Who it's for (EAE's words):** players who want "medium to high gain tones, versatility, twiddling EQ knobs, cutting through a mix with a **blade of midrange**." That "blade of midrange" is the intended use of the Mid + SHIFT control.
- Use range: "any setup from indie rock to death metal." The Boost channel "adds extra front-end gain with tightened midrange for two distinct distortion levels."

**Dagger** — "scaled-down Longsword," germanium clipping, more compressed, "chimey at low gain, fuzzier as Drive increases," sizzly treble, *less low-mid punch*. Wrong direction for a rig that needs more low-mid mass.

**Halberd** — not op-amp at all; 24V transistor OD, "soft transition into clipping," dynamics-first, lower gain. The opposite job from "the wall."

Takeaway for this rig: EAE itself confirms the Longsword is the gain-ceiling + low-mid-mass choice, and frames the Mid control as the "blade" you cut through a dense mix with — exactly the carve move used when stacking on the fuzz wall.
