The in-house zoning question — when to reach for the Apollo (x8) vs the RME Babyface Pro FS. (Article compares the Twin X; the logic applies to the x8, which only widens the Apollo's lead on I/O and preamp count.)

ONBOARD DSP / PLUGINS:
- Apollo: onboard DSP runs UAD plugins at near-zero-latency tracking; UNISON makes "the hardware preamp physically interact with the software plugin" — impedance and clipping "respond like the real hardware."
- RME Babyface: NO onboard DSP. Offers TotalMix FX for routing/monitoring, no plugin ecosystem — and no lock-in.

PREAMP CHARACTER:
- Apollo: Unison vintage emulations (Neve 1073, API Vision) — color baked in at the source.
- RME: "clean, neutral preamps designed to capture your source without adding any coloration."

PORTABILITY / POWER (RME wins decisively):
- RME: USB-C bus-powered, under one pound, fits a laptop bag, "no power adapter, no wall outlet required."
- Apollo: Thunderbolt 3 + separate power supply — "not a portable device in any meaningful sense."

DRIVER STABILITY:
- RME: "famous for supporting products long after other manufacturers have moved on"; consistent low-latency across Windows and macOS.
- Apollo: Thunderbolt "well-supported on modern Macs but can be genuinely problematic on Windows."

VERDICT:
- Apollo: fixed home/Mac studio wanting vintage character "baked into your recordings at the source."
- Babyface: mobile engineers, Windows users, or those prioritizing driver reliability and transparency.

FOR THE RIG: x8 = the fixed recording terminus (DSP, 4 Unison preamps, 8 line ins + ADAT, monitor control, reamp loop). Babyface = the go-bag / location rig and the pristine, totally-transparent clean backup. They're zoned, not competing.
