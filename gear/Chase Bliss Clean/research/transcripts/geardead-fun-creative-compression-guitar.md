# FUN and Creative Compression — Gear Dead (guitarist always-on perspective)

A guitarist's "few days in" take. Useful for the always-on framing and a guitar-first read on the EQ-tremolo, Swell, and Dusty modes (demos a saxophone through Dusty at the end). Distilled.

## Framing
"Incredibly practical but also incredibly creative." It injects a good amount of character, but if you don't need that character it also serves as a great compressor. Maps the controls: Dynamics = ratio, Sensitivity = threshold, plus Attack and the Release toggle (quick / custom / slow — he leaves it on fast). EQ knob: middle toggle position = leave it straight up for no EQ, right of noon rolls off lows, left of noon rolls off highs. Wet/Dry are straightforward. "Where things get interesting is the Mode knob and the Physics knob."

## Demonstrated behavior
- As a plain comp: fast attack + fast release + Sensitivity up = audible pumping (watch the LED brightening = more compression). Turn Dynamics up further to feel more pumping; or just turn Wet up if you like the compressed sound. Backing the Attack down a little gives more room before the compressor kicks in — "a little more lively," you get the transient of the attack.
- **Past noon on Dynamics things get aggressive:** ~12:00–3:00 is a limiter, then **Sag** at the end — "emulating when a tube starts to go." Past the threshold your uncompressed signal effectively becomes louder than the compressed signal; pushed hard it's almost like a gate ("you don't even hear it at all"). Turn Sensitivity down in Sag and it behaves almost like a swell — add reverb over it.
- **EQ left (manual low cut):** rolling off lows makes the uncompressed signal dark; combined with heavy comp it acts almost like a gate.
- **EQ right = tremolo (his favorite little mode):** the EQ flutters the signal at a rate set by Attack/Release. Turn Attack down for a faster flutter — "sounds like a tremolo," likes the quick almost-FM character; would map it to a mod source. The other EQ direction gives a tremolo that reduces highs.
- **Physics** sets how vintage-y the compressor acts. Left = subtle (even the LED dims in and out) — "not really a tremolo, just a little flutter." Right = "where it gets really Chase-Blissy," a more aggressive glitch flutter on the attack/release.
- **Swell** — acknowledges it's there; he already uses a volume pedal but nice to have.
- **Dusty** — turns off the second limiter so the saturation comes through; demos it as the only effect on a guitar, then on a saxophone (dry vs. Clean+Dusty) — adds beef/vintage saturation to a thin pickup/source.

## Verdict (relevant to always-on use)
"Vintage-y, light compression that's not trying to be anything else — its own thing," with a really cool take on tremolo, swelling, and overdrive/glitchiness (the sense of a broken compressor circuit feeding character into your sound). He loves that it's **a practical pedal he can leave on** without having to be "playing weird ambient music" — more traditional than typical Chase Bliss delay/pitch/sampler boxes because it's a purely analog circuit with the quirky CB character on top. "One of my favorite Chase Bliss things to come out. I'll keep it."
