# Dialing Clean in for everyday / always-on compression (concrete how-to)

A distilled, practical setup procedure for using Clean as a transparent always-on leveler, pulled from the compressor-specialist review and corroborated by the official video manual (see the daily- video-manual transcript in this gear folder). SOURCED unless marked.

## The six controls that matter for plain compression
For bread-and-butter always-on use you only touch six things — ignore the dips/modes:
- **Dynamics** = compression ratio/amount
- **Sensitivity** = threshold (trigger point)
- **Attack** = how fast it clamps
- **Release toggle** = 50 ms (L) / adjustable (mid) / 1.5 s (R)
- **Wet** = compressed-signal output level
- **Dry** = uncompressed-signal output level (blend in for parallel)

## Step-by-step dial-in (SOURCED)
1. **Set Sensitivity first — it's the critical control.** Think of it as a threshold. Clean reacts to how loud you play, so this must be re-set per instrument. **Watch the left LED** at a comfortable playing volume and turn Sensitivity until the LED is clearly moving / lighting on your peaks. *"The brighter the LED, the more compression."* Too high = overwhelmed/over-compressed; too low = barely anything.
2. **Set Dynamics for ratio. Start at ~10:00 ≈ 4:1** — the review's recommended starting position and a great everyday ratio. (Per the maker's map: 7:00 = 1:1, 10:00 = 4:1, noon = ∞:1, past noon = hard limiting, last quarter = Sag.)
3. **Set make-up level with Wet.** Compression makes things quieter; the Wet knob has plenty of gain to bring it back. **Unity is ~noon on Wet; past noon is a healthy boost.** This is how Clean doubles as a level-setter into the next pedal.
4. **Blend Dry for parallel ("New York") compression.** *"Bring in more of your uncompressed signal by rotating the Dry dial more clockwise… very useful when dialing more extreme compression settings to retain some of the original articulation and dynamics."* Dry also has unity at ~noon and boosts past it.
5. **Attack/Release to taste.** Clockwise Attack = slower = lets more transient/pick-attack through before clamping.

## Character notes (SOURCED)
- The compression is **"natural feeling and easy to dial in," with "a nice weight to the tone"** — **not completely transparent**; it adds **"presence and fullness."** Good for an always-on role: it makes playing **"feel more effortless and quicker."**
- **Very quiet / no artifacts** even with Wet and Dry gained up — *"extremely quiet, even when the Wet and Dry controls are gained up,"* with no real distortion. Safe as a permanent front-end boost.
- **Sag zone** (last quarter of Dynamics) simulates an overloaded tube **without distortion** — highly responsive to playing dynamics; a flavor, not a default.
- **Physics** introduces subtle→chaotic instability to the attack/release envelope (models a coil spring), interacting with the Sensitivity threshold — leave centered (stable) for clean always-on use.

## Noise-gate gotcha (SOURCED — owner reports)
The built-in Noise Gate kills single-coil hum, **but you have to set the threshold low or it will swallow lightly fingerpicked notes.** Relevant to a quiet banjo control-band: calibrate the gate threshold carefully or it'll cut soft picking.

## Mini-review caveat
sixstringsensei's "mini" review deliberately gives no specific knob numbers (philosophy over patches) but confirms: the DRY/WET labels make parallel blending obvious, the EQ dial is "very effective," and Clean "does a much better overdrive/distortion" than expected when you gain it up (Dusty/Sag). For clean-boost duty he personally prefers a dedicated boost — i.e. Clean's gain is make-up gain, not a pristine boost circuit (matches the DeepResearch dossier).
