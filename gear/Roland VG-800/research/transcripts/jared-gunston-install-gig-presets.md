## The single most important practical lesson: PICKUP HEIGHT
He installs the GK (mentions both GK-5 and a GK-3 he uses with a converter) using the **strap-button mount** (no drilling) — the bracket attaches via the strap-lock screw. He hit major problems and walks through them honestly:
- **First mount too LOW:** he eyeballed the gap at "about 1 mm, maybe slightly more," but it was too low. Result: sound was "very flubby," bad tone, and he had to crank the per-string gain/SENS to compensate, which introduced **a lot of noise/hiss through the amps.**
- He removed it (tip: **window cleaner / Windolene dissolves the adhesive** to pull a taped pickup off cleanly), added support shims/beams underneath to raise it — and overcorrected to **3 mm, which is way too high:** strings started to buzz against it, and the pickup leaned/tilted as he played, again bad tone.
- **Resolution:** mounting it at the correct **~1 mm** clearance, level and stable, fixed everything — "3 mm versus 1 mm is the difference between great tone and really bad tone, and no noise versus a lot of noise." He'd spent most of a day chasing the bad sound in the pedal and software before realizing it was purely the pickup mount. **Takeaway: if a GK install sounds flubby/noisy, it's almost certainly height/level, not the patch — get it to ~1 mm, dead level, rock-solid (no tilt under playing).**

## The gig rig and MIDI automation
He gigged it as his 2025 board. Layout:
- VG-800 (the "blue guy") for guitar; a TC-Helicon-style **BE-500 for vocal effects** beside it; everything in **stereo**.
- Patch changes are fully automated from an **iPad** sending MIDI program changes via a **CME WIDI** wireless MIDI bridge (he describes the converter/interface as "like a WIDI Jack but with five outputs and one input").
- **Backup plan that worked:** he ran a **GK-3-equipped guitar through the Serial-GK converter box** into the VG-800's input as a backup in case the new GK-5 system failed mid-gig. Result: "no issues, my guitar didn't cut out once, played like a dream." Good corroboration that the converter-box path (older 13-pin GK → Serial GK) is gig-reliable.

## How he organized his patch bank (shared on BOSS Tone Exchange)
He built a tidy, gig-ready bank and put it on the BOSS Tone Exchange (link in his description). Structure:
- **7 tunings:** Standard, −1, −2, −3, −4, Drop D, Drop C — all done in the VG-800's ALT TUNING (no physical retuning).
- **4 sounds per tuning:** Clean, Overdrive, High Gain, Lead/Solo boost — so each "bank" is one tuning's worth of the four core voicings.
- Because all four sounds in a tuning sit adjacent, he just clicks over live when he wants more dirt or wants the solo boost to come in.
- **Clean** = typical chorus + delay + reverb. **Overdrive** = expressive, "bites back," dirty (not edge-of-breakup but dynamic). **High gain** = he notes you could drop your own favorite IR on top and re-EQ, but he built all of these **without impulse responses** specifically so they're shareable. **Lead/solo** = cuts through, expressive, and he engages a **wah pedal** on it for extra expression ("makes you want to burn through the fretboard").

## Framing / lineage observation
He says the VG-800 is "definitely more like a GT-1000 core on the inside" plus the tuning/instrument abilities — exactly the SY-1000 capabilities he wanted, but the SY-1000 was a big pedal he felt wasted board space "just to tune." Now he gets the alt-tuning plus GT-class amp/effects sounds in a tiny enclosure. He's effusive about the size-to-power ratio.

## Net practical advice for a builder
1. Get the GK clearance right first (~1 mm, level, solid) before blaming the patch.
2. The converter-box + older GK-3 backup path is gig-proven reliable.
3. Organize patches as tuning-banks × {clean/OD/high-gain/lead} for fast live switching, driven by external MIDI PC (iPad/WIDI).
4. Build IR-free if you want to share patches; layer your own IR locally.
