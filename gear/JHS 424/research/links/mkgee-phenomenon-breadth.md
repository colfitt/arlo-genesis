# The Mk.gee tone-chasing phenomenon — why the 424's fame is borrowed

Context for the "honest about fame" framing: an entire micro-market now exists to recreate ONE artist's Tascam preamp tone, and the JHS 424 is one entry among many.

## Multiple products chase the same tone
- Plugins (Audio Hertz **Mk.pre**), the JHS Pedals **424 Gain Stage**, and various boutique pedals all aim to replicate the Tascam Portastudio 424 preamp sound.
- "Before Mk.pre, the only way to get this tone was by chasing down old hardware or buying boutique pedals." Audio Hertz jabs: "Some of those pedals run over $250 and still miss the mark" (a pointed nod at the JHS's $249 price).

## How Mk.gee actually achieves it (hardware, not a pedal)
- Michael Todd Gordon "connects his guitar straight into the Portastudio's preamp... pushing hard into the Tascam's preamp to create a distinctively lo-fi sound." The fame source is the **real cassette four-track hardware**, used as a preamp/overdrive — there is no public evidence he uses any of the recreations.

## Takeaway for the dossier
The JHS 424's mythology is **borrowed twice over**: from Mk.gee (who uses the hardware, not the pedal) and from the Tascam Portastudio lineage. The pedal is too new (Aug 2025) for its own user roster — its fame is the demand-side phenomenon of everyone trying to bottle the same Tascam preamp. Treat the JHS as one (well-supported, XLR-equipped) option in a crowded recreate-the-424 field, not as a pedal with an established artist following.
