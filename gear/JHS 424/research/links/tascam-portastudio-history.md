# Tascam/TEAC Portastudio history — the borrowed mythology

The 424's fame is inherited from the cassette four-track lineage, not earned by the pedal. Core history facts:

## The home-recording revolution
- **1979: TEAC 144 Portastudio** — the first four-track multitrack recorder on a standard cassette, $899 (~$4,000 today). It "triggered a home recording revolution," democratizing multitrack recording out of pro studios and into bedrooms. (TEAC's recording division became Tascam.)
- Cultural reach: the machine was featured on BBC's *Tomorrow's World*.

## Springsteen's Nebraska (the keystone record)
- **1982:** Bruce Springsteen recorded *Nebraska* on a **TEAC 144 Portastudio** with "little more than a 144, some Shure SM57 mics, a Gibson J-200 acoustic guitar, a mandolin and a glockenspiel." Note: this is the earlier 144, NOT a 424 — the 424 is the later MKI cassette unit the JHS pedal models. The lineage, not the exact machine, is what's inherited.
- The stark lo-fi character (voice against tape hiss, sparse arrangements) validated home recording at the highest artistic level.

## The wider lineage (80s–90s lo-fi / DIY)
- Sting cut Police demos on his 144 (sold for £3,200 at a 2022 auction). Mac DeMarco recorded his 2012 debut on a 244 Portastudio. Cassette Portastudios saw renewed lo-fi popularity from the 1990s; the aesthetic now lives on in DAW emulations (SketchCassette, etc.).

## Honest note for the dossier
The pedal models the **424 MKI specifically**; the famous Springsteen record used the **144**. Both are Tascam/TEAC Portastudios in the same family, so the mythology carries — but keep the model distinction accurate.
