PREMISE: This is about getting guitar sounds WITHOUT a guitar amp — not even a device pretending to be one. Just plugging into a channel strip and driving it. Prince did this back in the day (straight into the desk = very funky). Hence JHS also making the Colour Box. The 424 Gain Stage recreates the gain stage of an old Tascam 424 Portastudio.

THE MK.GEE THING (Michael Gordon): plugs guitar into his Tascam Portastudio, cranks the channel strip a little, and takes that sound DI'd — that's his sound. A "fat sound with occasionally these slightly... clipped, distorted" moments. You layer it with dreamy modulation, a bit of compression, interesting guitar parts → the Mk.gee vibe. KEY: "the Mk.gee thing is very much about triggering that clipping" — fairly infrequently during a passage, by digging in dynamically.

SIGNAL CHAIN USED IN THE VIDEO (note for this rig!):
- MXR Rockman X100 on its clean sound with chorus ("definitely adds a thing" — Mk.gee's single "Rockman" was a clue to his effects).
- JHS 3 Series: oil can delay, octave, reverb ("great value pedals").
- 424 Gain Stage: JACK output → looper → DI box → recorded on one track.
- 424 XLR output → straight into the interface = "Apollo, from Universal Audio."
- No compressor anywhere in the chain — all the compression/clipping comes from the pedal.

CONTROL MAPPING (read from the box):
- Volume = the MASTER fader on the Portastudio.
- Gain 1 = the input TRIM knob on the channel.
- Gain 2 = the CHANNEL fader.
- Bass + Treble EQ.
- "The volume, gain 1, and gain 2 controls work together to give you versatile tone shaping."

KEY DEMONSTRATED BEHAVIORS:
- Plain guitar → DI (pedal off) = "about as sterile a guitar sound as you can make," slightly laggy.
- VOLUME (master) does NOT add distortion: with Volume cranked all the way (and gains low) there's "not really any distortion as such coming by just driving the master fader." Volume is purely output level. [This is the crucial gain-staging point: drive comes from Gain 1/Gain 2, loudness from Volume.]
- The clipping = a COMPONENT distorting — "unpleasant clipping but it did a thing." It IS clipping by design.
- DYNAMIC TRIGGERING: "I'm playing it softly now, but as soon as I dig in..." → that's the lo-fi crack/snap/clipping you're looking for. Lower-output pickups make it more controllable; harder/hotter pickups push it more.
- Add delay + reverb on top and the DI sound becomes "a great sounding DI sound" despite NO speaker emulation ("it's just straight in").
- Into a Fender Deluxe Reverb (amp) it's "so compressed" — works like an "EP Booster / Dallas Rangemaster"-style always-on fattening preamp when gain is below clipping. "Just have it on all the time."
- Big caveat they stress: distortion sounds VERY different through a guitar speaker vs a monitor/interface. This pedal is voiced for the DI/monitor path (no cab sim), so judge it there — which is exactly how Mk.gee uses it and how you'd run it into an Apollo.

WRAP: $249, "not too bad." They found the sounds by spending 30–40 min hunting before recording — expect to do the same; "when you get there it's a juicy thing." Mk.gee's album is "Two Star and the Dream Police"; they compare the vibe to Andy Summers / early-Police dreamy jazzy stuff.

KEY TAKEAWAYS FOR THE RIG:
- This demo IS the rig's use case: 424 XLR → Apollo, no amp, no cab sim, drive from the gains and level from Volume.
- Volume = clean master level (no added grit) — set it last to hit the converter; push Gain 1/Gain 2 for dirt.
- Mk.gee tone = set gains near the clipping threshold and TRIGGER the clip dynamically by digging in; keep it mostly clean-ish, let occasional notes "crack."
- Lower-output pickups = more control over when it clips; hotter pickups clip more readily (relevant for the VG-800/banjo near-line-level output — back Gain 1 off).
- Pairs shown: MXR Rockman (clean+chorus) and JHS 3 Series oil-can delay/octave/reverb on top of the 424.
