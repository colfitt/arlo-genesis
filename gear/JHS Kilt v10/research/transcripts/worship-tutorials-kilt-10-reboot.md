This is the Kilt 10 — a collaboration with JHS and Stu G. Bradford has the V2 for reference (not the V1). The Kilt starts as a Bixonic Expandora — cool round/circular pedals you can't get anymore, which is why Stu and JHS wanted to make a Kilt. The Kilt 10 exists for the 10-year anniversary but also to address common issues people had with the pedal.

Comparing V2 and Kilt 10: they are very, very similar — you can get them to sound very similar, especially into a clean amp. A common problem people had with the V2: there's not a lot of output, and it's a little dark when you run the distortion clipping (the G2 switch up). The 10 brightens it up quite a bit and fixes that.

From the one-pager: "After 10 years, the Kilt gets a refreshed voice and reimagined aesthetic. An updated clipping section delivers more headroom, smoother attack, and a richer, more dynamically useful range than ever before. You'll find transparent overdrive textures that were never before possible, as well as the familiar and much-loved gating fuzz abilities in the last 25% of the range." The volume is way more usable; the gain is way more usable. Bradford likes running the gain very low and pushing the drive — you get more transparent sounds, like you're beefing the amp up a little. Good for getting an edge-of-breakup amp tone out of a clean amp at low volume.

RIG: Cordoba/Certelli tele-style guitar, standard tele pickups, straight into Quad Cortex, switching between a free Korg 2002 AC30 capture (with Anniversary Blue speakers) and a '67 Deluxe Reverb capture.

SETTINGS / MODES WALKED THROUGH:

- Base tone: tone knob straight up (noon), all dip switches down. "That sounds like the Kilt and that sounds like an AC30 getting pushed into more drive." Shows the just-barely-pushed sound — running a dirtier pedal into a cleaner amp so the delays/reverbs after it don't get washed out. Then brightens it up a touch.

- Low gain: with gain all the way down you have to turn the volume up (which is why the extra headroom matters). Demonstrates how much more usable the early gain sweep is than the V2.

- Low-Cut switch: "I don't like how it sounds — I feel like it takes away the body." But it's a cool effect to take all the body from a sound, like a filter-sweep thing you hear on records, especially if you can switch it back to get the low end back. (This is both gain switches down.)

- G1 up (both gain switches: G1 up): a thicker drive — "a little extra saturation and compression under my fingers." Good for leads; gets a little fizzier.

- G2 up: "gives you more fuzz," but at lower-to-noon gain it sounds more like a distortion than a fuzz. Shows the distortion, then brightens it up.

- Both up / full gated fuzz ("the danger zone"): "Full-on fuzz. Gated fuzz at that. It's a delicious sound." Then, instead of brightening it, he lops the low end off with the Low-Cut because the fuzz is pretty woolly and beefy.

WHY HE LIKES IT: an extra drive that gives you everything in one pedal — a lighter overdrive (in the transparent vein, though he "personally wouldn't necessarily say it's transparent"), a bigger distortion, and fuzz as an option. He mostly uses it as a mid-gain thing and boosts it with a clean Achilles Katana if he needs more.

Into the '67 Deluxe Reverb: the moderate-gain sound he likes (same settings as the AC30 clip but turned up) "brings it alive" — the Deluxe normally sounds scooped to him for worship, but the Kilt fixes that. Shows G2 (more fuzz / more distortion) and the full fuzz into the Deluxe too.

KILT 10 vs KILT V2 A/B (into a Tonex capture of a Marshall 1959 Superlead Plexi, Tyler Studio Elite guitar, comp + Oceans Abyss reverb):
- "You can hear there's just more push out of the 10." You can push the V2's level and gain to get close, but it's still not quite there.
- "More bottom end on the 10, but not flubby or warm — the Kilt 10 sounds a little bigger and has a little more gain on tap."
- Driving them harder: "the V2 actually lowers the level — it's driven but quieter."
- Conclusion: you CAN get very similar sounds out of the V2; the knobs land in different places, but the Kilt 10 just sounds bigger, fuller, with a more usable gain range.

STU G INTERVIEW (history, in his own words):
- The Kilt came from a conversation with Josh Scott at an AB show in Nashville ~12 years ago. Josh said they hadn't done an artist pedal yet. Stu asked if the Expandora was still being manufactured; Bixonic wasn't making it.
- The Expandora: Delirious was touring the US Midwest, went into a music store, saw "really cool round silver dirt boxes," picked a couple up. It became the sound of the record Mezzamorphis — and on tour for that record Stu wore a black kilt. (That's where the name comes from.)
- Stu ended up with FOUR Expandoras, kept at least two on his board at all times — "one that was more of a first-stage gain, and then one that was full-on distortion." The Expandora had dip switches on the inside; you had to take the battery cover off to change the gain structure.
- V1 had a boost you could move in front or behind. Because Stu used a 4x12 / Marshall half-stack, the thuddy bottom end sometimes needed cutting — so they put a high-pass / low-cut switch on it, and put the gain switches on the OUTSIDE, "which makes it one of the most diverse overdrives there is — you can go from breaking up the cleans to full-on fuzz." Released 2015.
- V2: smaller footprint, dropped the boost, added the Red Remote so you can switch G2 on at any point.
- Kilt 10 mods (Stu's summary): voiced a little more germanium-diode-sounding (warmer, more saturated); increased the frequency on the Low-Cut so it's more of an effect (thinning it out, toward — but not fully — "telephone guitar"); and it's louder (with both gain switches off you used to have to pump the volume up to match your gain stages — fixed).
- "It's like we've created the vintage Kilt — a little bit more old-sounding in some ways."
- How Stu uses it: he likes it with the two switches down, but also likes the fuzz, and likes G2 up "because that's more hard-clipping distortion." He always uses the Red Remote to send it into fuzz — "it's not as finicky as an actual fuzz pedal; you can put it anywhere in your chain and it'll sound the same. Always so gnarly and spitty in the best way." He also loves keeping the gain really low and barely having it do something — "it just lifts the sound." "Probably one of the most versatile drive pedals I've ever played — you could have three on your board and get three completely different sounds."
