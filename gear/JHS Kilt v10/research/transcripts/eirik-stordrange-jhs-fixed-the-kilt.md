This is the new Kilt 10 from JHS Pedals with updated features. The intro song uses it as an overdrive, then flicks a switch mid-song to a fantastic fuzz.

It's been 10 years since JHS released the Kilt, a Stu G signature pedal based on the Expandora circuit. It's still a Stu G signature pedal; Eirik isn't sure how close it is to the original Expandora circuit now with the new features.

From the copy: "An updated clipping section delivers more headroom, smoother attack, and a richer, more dynamically useful drive range than ever before." That means:
- More headroom on the volume knob — you don't have to turn it up that much to get a volume increase.
- More usable gain in the first 25% of the gain range, AND the last 25%.
- The tone knob has enhanced clarity and improved amp compatibility.
- The Low-Cut knob cuts bass if you want.

THE FOUR MODES (his summary): "G1 and G2 work together to give you up to four different resistance clipping options. Both down — your lowest gain option. G1 up — more overdrive. G2 up — more fuzz. Both up — that's a lot of overdrive and fuzz together." G2 can also be controlled with the Red Remote (he doesn't have it, so in the intro song he just flicks the toggle by hand to turn it on).

RIG: Shaw Mod Songstress amp, really clean. Shows his clean tone, then replays the intro song to demonstrate the different sounds.

ON THE FUZZ: "A really good example of the fuzz tone from this pedal is this particular riff — I remember playing this riff in my first Kilt demo 10 years ago." What he remembers about the Kilt is the insane fuzz tones — "they are so fun to play with, huge and spitty, and there's just the right amount of mid-range in the fuzz tone (which can be tricky with certain fuzz pedals, but it has the perfect amount). So it's easy to play with and it sits really well in the mix."

ON THE OVERDRIVE: the overdrive tones are fantastic, especially with the updated features. He remembers the original being tricky, especially in the first 25%. "It's a really good transparent overdrive — not as transparent as the Morning Glory [he just did a Morning Glory video for JHS], but still really good."

TWO CONS:
1. The Cut knob is "too aggressive, way too aggressive — it removes too much bass from the signal." But that's in his studio setting where he hears it clearly. It might be good live or at band practice where there's more low end to compete with — it's probably made for that. In the studio it's almost lo-fi-sounding; might also be good for a solo. He prefers to control the low end a different way.
2. Nitpick: when playing the intro song live, he set the tone knob for the overdrive part, but flicking the G2 switch to fuzz "sounds a bit darker, and it's hard to control that on the fly" since he played it live (overdrive verse → fuzz chorus) without re-tweaking tone.

VERDICT: an amazing gain pedal from JHS. It reminded him he has a tendency to sell pedals he likes — he used the first Kilt a lot for recordings (the fuzz tone is so unique) and has no idea why he sold both the original and the V2. "Note to self: keep this one — it's unique and you have a lot of different tones in it. A really good add to your gain vocabulary." A great way to explain having lots of tonal options in one pedal.
