Chuck Bailey with The Bass Channel checking out the Kilt 10 from JHS. Background: this isn't the original intro for the pedal — it came out 10 years ago as "The Kilt," a meeting of the minds between Stu G (UK band Delirious) and JHS. Back in 2015 Stu needed a pedal to meet all his extensive traveling and studio needs. It's a dirt pedal by design, but that undersells it — from a distortion/dirt perspective it's pretty incredible.

A lot of pedals designed for guitar don't translate to bass, but this one was a pleasant surprise — it sounds phenomenal on bass.

THE GAIN-KNOB POINT (his headline): JHS's attention to the gain knob makes it way more usable texturally. In a lot of past pedals there's a dead zone from 0 to about 9 o'clock / 25% — not really a noticeable change, almost absent. With the Kilt 10 you get very nuanced, noticeable, subtle sonic differences in that first 25%. A lot of pedals are "0, and as soon as you get past 25%/26% it's BOOM, hits really forward with no in-between." JHS accounted for that and gave the gain knob functionality from 0 through that 25% gap.

BASS DISCLAIMER: it sounds really good on bass, but it was designed for guitar — so you'll probably want to run some kind of parallel clean low end (a clean blend from your bass signal alongside the drive) to fatten it up and keep it usable.

CONTROLS (his rundown): tone knob, volume knob, gain knob. Three diagonal switches: "cut/flat," then G1 and G2.

THE FOUR MODES (quoted from JHS B-roll narration):
"The G1 and G2 toggles work together to give you up to four different resistance clipping options. Both toggles down is the lowest gain setting. G1 up gives you more overdrive. G2 up gives you more fuzz. Both toggles up give you the heaviest gated fuzz that the Kilt pedal has ever been able to offer."

TRANSPARENCY POINT: the pedal is "very transparent in the character of the bass you're using" — it doesn't color your tone beyond the dirt's job. It shows the difference between a jazz bass and a P-bass (they overlapped the different basses to demonstrate). Different pickups/models come through inherently — no sterile, same-sounding delivery.

CLOSING THOUGHTS:
- Loved it on the 4-string jazz and the 4-string P-bass.
- On the 5-string, the lower-end character was "still usable, still cool, but it did kind of start to get a little wonky." Mileage may vary; could be pick attack. They collectively decided the 4-string bass was the way for this pedal, so most examples were geared toward that.
- Aesthetics: normally dislikes polished-mirror finishes ("gaudy"), but the simplicity here works for him; likes the X / kilt nod to the Scottish flag.
