Hey everyone, it's Josh here with Stu G. We're showing off a brand new 10-year anniversary Stu G signature Kilt pedal. For the 10-year anniversary, Stu has done some adjustments, some mods to it. The Kilt is our take on the vintage MXR Expander [Bixonic Expandora], and it is essentially an overdrive, distortion, or a fuzz, all in one small package. Once you play the Kilt — the regular V2, and especially this new 10-year — it's a pretty wild thing that one pedal can swing that far.

One of Stu's biggest loves of this pedal is the crazy gated fuzz that's highly controllable. On the old Expandora, each pedal sounded a little bit different. What I love about this is it's consistently rude. It's wild to see it on so many different boards — country players, a jazz guy that loves it, Nine Inch Nails, engineers that use it on a drum bus in the studio.

With the 10-year anniversary, we can now go completely the opposite end of the scale and clean it up really nice, but still be loud and warm. It was a sliding scale when modifying it — we found a perfectly sweet spot. We just lost the unusable, ridiculous side of the fuzz, but we gained the low-gain stuff even more than the V2.

The origin story: sometime in 2014 Josh and Stu met in Nashville. Josh asked, "Stu, if we were to do a signature pedal with you, what would that be?" Stu said the Expandora was no longer being made, and there was the frustration of having the dip switches on the inside — Stu used to have two or three because they all sounded a little bit different in the fuzz mode. This is JHS's first artist pedal; Stu is their first signature artist ever. They launched the V1 in a castle in Napa Valley.

The Red Remote function is really key to how such a small pedal can do all these different disciplines of a dirt pedal. You have a jack with a red ring. You plug in a mono patch cable, and JHS sells a little red box (you can use any latching footswitch, it doesn't have to be JHS). Because you have staging in the pedal — the G1 and G2 toggles (gain one, gain two — conveniently "Stu G," "Stu gain") — when you tap the footswitch, G2's light goes red and G2 is internally flipped up. That lets you have all these combinations to alter and make different drive sounds. You can go G1 up, G1 down, G2 up while G1 is up — a lot of combinations, especially when you start rotating the gain control. The way Josh likes it, and how Stu uses it: G1 on, and you hit that footswitch and go from a drive pedal to a raging gated fuzz.

[On the low noise floor:] "I was noticing how, for a high-gain distortion pedal, how quiet it is when you're not playing." Josh jokes it's "magic stuff I do at night, like at my cauldron — a de-noise potion."

THE THREE MAJOR CIRCUIT CHANGES:

1. New clipping / germanium-style diodes. The heart of the pedal and how it clips the signal has been changed. The original Expandora uses silicon-style diodes. The lower the forward voltage, the harder and quicker it distorts — germanium is the lowest (less headroom, clips earlier, like a small tube amp turned up), silicon is in the middle (more headroom, like a higher-wattage amp), and LED clipping is all the way up. Josh put germanium-style clipping into the Kilt 10 to lower the voltage — similar to the way a Klon Centaur and an MXR Distortion+ clip. They use a more modern, reliable component that gets the old-school sound with modern technology. This makes it naturally more warm, more saturated — which can feel a little darker, so there's a compensating brightness adjustment.

2. Usable low gain. If you turn the gain down, it actually does low-gain stuff — Josh had never thought of an Expandora as low gain. The old problem: when you went low gain, you lost volume, and you almost couldn't get a usable sound with the gain really low. On the 10-year they fixed that — the way more usable first 25% of the gain range.

3. The Low-Cut ("flat/cut") toggle actually works now. JHS got the most emails from Kilt buyers about the flat switch working. On the original it was felt more on a big cabinet (a 4x12) — with modern small/modeled rigs you could be in spots where you'd ask, "is that working?" They made the cut more audible and more impactful. Stu has used it in the studio as an effect, almost a telephone-voice thing.

PLUS: more overall volume / headroom. JHS pedals are always loud; this one originally slipped through quieter. More available volume makes the pedal feel more powerful, and it tends to clean up a little better with the volume control. It also has more presence / a little more crispness (not harsh) to compensate for the warmer germanium clipping.

Stu's description: "It's like having a vintage Kilt — it feels like an older piece of gear because of the germanium clipping. It's like a future-vintage Kilt." Josh: "non-plastic-couch distortion pedal."

THE JAMS / SETTINGS SHOWN:
