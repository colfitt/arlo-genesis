# JHS Kilt 10 — Deep Research

A working dossier for a pedal that currently lives **on the bench**, not on a board. In this rig the Kilt 10 is a benched drive — the rig sheet's stated reason is blunt: *"Drive coverage full — Brothers + Longsword + Oxford."* The on-board dirt stack (CB Brothers AM as the always-on platform, EAE Longsword as the high-gain wall, TKOG Oxford Drive as the Marshall-voiced shredder, plus the MXR 108 Fuzz and EQD Hizumitas for fuzz) already spans clean-boost to gated-fuzz, so the Kilt — a *one-pedal* version of exactly that arc — has nothing unique to do in the live chain. This document documents what it is, honestly assesses the bench call, and identifies where it would actually earn a slot (spoiler: studio re-amp and travel, not Board 1).

## 1. Lineage: Bixonic Expandora → JHS Kilt → Kilt 10

The brief hypothesized a Lovepedal/Crowther Hot Cake heritage. That is **wrong, and worth correcting up front**: the Kilt is JHS's take on the **Bixonic Expandora**, the cult Japanese boutique distortion released circa 1995. JHS, Guitar World, and Guitar Pedal X all describe the Kilt as "JHS's Expandora take" ([Guitar World](https://www.guitarworld.com/gear/guitar-pedals/jhs-kilt-10-overdrive), [Guitar Pedal X](https://www.guitarpedalx.com/news/gpx-blog/jhs-pedals-fixes-its-expandora-take-with-a-new-and-much-improved-kilt-10-anniversary-edition-in-shiny-polished-aluminium), [gearnews](https://www.gearnews.com/jhs-pedals-kilt-10/)). The JHS product copy calls the V2 "a heavily tweaked Expandora style dirt box" with a hand-picked low-noise op-amp ([JHS Kilt V2 page](https://jhspedals.info/products/kilt-v2)).

The Expandora's own circuit is the interesting part, because it explains everything the Kilt does. Per [Aion FX's teardown](https://aionfx.com/project/dynamo-reactive-distortion/), the Expandora "originated from an experiment in putting two RAT Distortion pedals in series, which you can see in the two cascaded LM308 or OP07 gain stages." It adds one trick a RAT doesn't have: "a side-chain envelope detector engages an opto-FET... that serves as the op-amp's gain control, meaning **the gain itself varies with the signal strength**." That reactive, touch-sensitive behavior is the Expandora's signature, and it's why the Kilt 10 marketing keeps using the words "touch-sensitive feel" and "smoother attack."

The other inherited feature is the toggle-selectable clipping. On the Expandora a switch disengages the series resistance in the feedback loop to reach a notorious extreme-gain mode — its "Forbidden" setting ([Aion FX](https://aionfx.com/project/dynamo-reactive-distortion/), [Mitch Akes' "Forbidden Fuzz" writeup](https://www.mitchakesmusic.com/post/the-forbidden-fuzz-setting-the-secret-mode-inside-the-bixonic-expandora)). The Kilt's G1/G2 toggles are JHS's reimplementation of that idea: they "select various forms of distortion via **resistance values in the clipping section**, a method very different from typical diode clipping selectors" ([Guitar Sanctuary / JHS](https://www.guitarsanctuary.com/jhs-kilt-v2-overdrive-distortion-fuzz-pedal/)). So when the manual says G1/G2 give "up to 4 different resistance clipping options," that is the Expandora's clipping-resistance trick, two-switch-mapped.

**Version history** (from the manual's own table): Kilt **V1** (2015) — large enclosure, two footswitches, an independent boost on the right side, four knobs/four toggles. Kilt **V2** — smaller box, three knobs/three toggles, the proprietary **Red Remote** added. Kilt **10** (2025) — the 10-year anniversary special edition. Mechanically and sonically the Kilt 10 "functions exactly like the Kilt V2" per JHS; it is a re-voiced V2, not a new circuit.

**What the Kilt 10 actually changes** (manual + [Guitar World](https://www.guitarworld.com/gear/guitar-pedals/jhs-kilt-10-overdrive)): a new clipping section with **more headroom, smoother attack, more compression** ("Think Kilt V2 but bigger"); a re-mapped gain pot where the **first 0–25% is finally usable** (the old Kilt had a low-gain dead zone — its biggest complaint); **enhanced high-end clarity** to cut a mix; a **more amp-friendly Low-Cut toggle** that now plays nicely with modelers and software; and cosmetics — polished aluminum (a nod to the V1 finish), Expandora-style knobs, and a Scottish-flag/Roman-numeral-X icon. Guitar Pedal X, who calls the original compact Kilt "my least favourite Expandora take... somewhat cold and dense, and not nearly bright enough," says the 10 is "brighter, louder, better sounding," now "more in line with the warm and expansive nature of JHS's Morning Glory."

## 2. Controls

Three knobs, three toggles, and the Red Remote jack — straight from the manual ([JHS Kilt 10 page](https://jhspedals.info/products/kilt-10)):

- **Volume.** Output level, left = less. The Kilt 10's added headroom is the headline: JHS notes "you no longer have to turn your volume up past noon," leaving plenty of range to use it as a clean boost into the next stage or the amp front end.
- **Gain.** Drive amount, left = less. The re-mapped sweep is the marquee improvement — "never before has the first 25% of the gain range been so usable," delivering transparent low-gain textures, with "the familiar and much loved gating fuzz abilities" living in the top 25%.
- **Tone.** Brightness — left darker, right brighter. A single high-frequency tilt; not a Muff-style inverted/bilateral control. The 10 widened its amp compatibility here.
- **G1 / G2 toggles.** Together they select up to four resistance-clipping voicings:
  - **Both down** — lowest gain, chimey amp-like crunch / edge-of-breakup.
  - **G1 up** — more overdrive (riffy grit).
  - **G2 up** — more fuzz / heavier saturation and sustain for leads.
  - **Both up** — the heaviest **gated fuzz** the Kilt offers (the Expandora "Forbidden"-style mode — spitty, sputtery, gated).
  - The manual also flags the useful **G1 down / G2 up = high-gain stage boost**, ideal for pushing an amp front end or stacking into a fuzz.
- **Low-Cut toggle.** Down = flat EQ; up = "cut" mode that reduces lows so the pedal cuts a mix. The 10 made this far more amp- and modeler-friendly than the V2's version (a frequent V2 complaint was that the toggle barely did anything).
- **Red Remote jack** (upper right, red washer). JHS's proprietary latching-footswitch input: it flips the **G2 toggle on the fly**, letting you jump between, e.g., overdrive↔distortion or distortion↔fuzz hands-free without a second footswitch. The remote is a $46 accessory ([gearnews](https://www.gearnews.com/jhs-pedals-kilt-10/)).

## 3. Sonic Character vs Named Competitors — and vs the Owner's On-Board Drives

The Kilt's identity is **versatility from one box**: low-gain transparent OD → mid-gain crunch → thick distortion → gated "Forbidden" fuzz, all reactive to pick attack thanks to the Expandora's envelope-controlled gain. The 10 re-voicing pushes it brighter and warmer, closer to JHS's own Morning Glory than the old "cold and dense" Kilt.

Against the field:

- **Bixonic Expandora (the source):** The Kilt is the modern, quiet, board-friendly translation. The real Expandora has the opto-FET reactivity and the genuine "Forbidden" mode; the Kilt approximates both with lower noise and a cleaner low end. If you want the literal ZZ Top / Thom Yorke distortion, the Expandora is the artifact; the Kilt is the reliable stand-in.
- **ProCo RAT:** The Expandora — and therefore the Kilt — *is* essentially two RATs in series with reactive gain. Versus a single RAT, the Kilt is more compressed, more touch-sensitive, and reaches further into fuzz. A RAT is the tighter, more focused single-voice tool.
- **JHS Morning Glory / Bluesbreaker family:** GPX explicitly maps the Kilt 10 toward Morning Glory's "warm and expansive" character at low gain, but the Kilt covers far more range and gets genuinely heavy where the Morning Glory stays transparent.

Now the part that benches it — vs the **on-board drives this owner already runs**:

- **vs CB Brothers AM (the always-on platform, Channel A):** Brothers is two independent gain channels (boost / OD / fuzz-ish) with stereo and ramping — a more flexible, MIDI-controllable platform that already owns the "do-everything drive" job in this rig. The Kilt is the *single-knob-set* version of the same idea, and Brothers does it with more routing control.
- **vs EAE Longsword (the "Wall"):** Longsword is a dedicated high-gain, mid-rich distortion built for saturated walls. The Kilt's "both toggles up" gated fuzz overlaps the Longsword's territory but is spittier and less controlled — Longsword is the better wall.
- **vs TKOG Oxford Drive (ShredMaster/Guv'nor-voiced):** Oxford is a Marshall-in-a-box British distortion with a fixed, focused voice. The Kilt's G1-up overdrive and G2-up distortion land in adjacent British-drive territory, but Oxford's specific ShredMaster mids are a flavor the Kilt doesn't replicate. They overlap on *coverage*, not on *character*.

The honest read: the Kilt is a superb generalist, and this board has no room for a generalist because three specialists already cover the same arc with more control.

## 4. Dynamic Behavior — Cleanup & Stacking

This is where the Expandora DNA actually shines and where the Kilt is *better* than its bench status implies. The opto-FET reactive gain makes it unusually **touch-sensitive**: pick softer and the gain backs off, dig in and it blooms. The Kilt 10's added compression and "smoother attack" lean into this — it tracks guitar-volume rolloff and pick dynamics well, cleaning toward edge-of-breakup at low gain.

**As a stacker** it's genuinely strong (this is the case *for* keeping it around): the manual's recommended **G1 down / G2 up = high-gain stage boost** is purpose-built to push an amp or slam the front of a fuzz. Stacked *into* the Brothers AM or *before* the Hizumitas, the Kilt is a flexible front-end gain shaper. Past noon on gain, the top of the range gets gated and spitty by design — the decay sputters out rather than sustaining smoothly, which suits this owner's degraded-texture aesthetic.

## 5. Bench Placement — Why It's Off the Board, and Where It Would Slot

**Why it's benched (and the call is correct):** The rig sheet says it plainly — *"Drive coverage full."* Board 1 already runs five dirt boxes spanning the entire range the Kilt covers single-handedly: Brothers AM (boost/OD platform, always-on), Longsword (high-gain wall), Oxford (British distortion), plus 108 Fuzz and Hizumitas for fuzz. The Kilt's whole pitch is *"one pedal that goes from transparent OD to gated fuzz"* — but a rig that already has dedicated, more-controllable pedals at every point on that arc gains nothing from a generalist. It would be redundant occupying scarce real estate on a board that's already a dense stereo-out chain feeding the Middle/End boards. Benching it is the right organizational call, not a knock on the pedal.

**Where it WOULD earn a slot:**

1. **Studio / re-amp duty.** This is its real home. Via the EHX Effects Interface (also benched) and the Radial X-Amp, the Kilt 10 is an ideal *single-pedal drive-coverage tool* for re-amping a printed track — dial OD, distortion, or Forbidden-fuzz from one box without rebuilding the live board. Its modeler-friendly Low-Cut and amp-friendly 10 voicing make it well-suited to re-amping into Iridium or VG-800 models.
2. **Travel / fly board.** The Del-Verb is already benched as "travel board only." The Kilt 10 belongs on that same minimal board: one drive that does everything from one footswitch (with Red Remote for hands-free OD↔fuzz) replaces three specialist pedals when weight matters.
3. **Sub-in if a specialist dies.** If the Oxford or Longsword fails, the Kilt 10 covers that ground immediately — same logic the rig sheet applies to the benched Big Sky ("Sub-in if H90 down").
4. **A genuine board case:** if the owner ever wanted *one* hands-free pedal that morphs OD→distortion→fuzz via Red Remote inside a song, the Kilt could replace two of the three on-board drives and reclaim space — but only at the cost of the per-pedal control the current three give.

## 6. Source-Specific Notes

- **Gold Tone EBM-5 banjo (GK-5 → VG-800):** Extremely bright, transient, low-mass signal. The Kilt's single Tone knob is a blunter brightness control than the Hizumitas's filter-like sweep, so it's less surgical at taming banjo ice-pick. But its **reactive gain** evens out the banjo's attack/decay imbalance nicely, and the Low-Cut toggle (in flat, *not* cut) keeps what little low end exists. Use modest gain (G1 up only) and roll Tone left.
- **Baritone Jazzmaster:** Home territory for an Expandora-style drive. The reactive gain loves the baritone's fat low strings; G2-up distortion and the Low-Cut toggle (engage cut) keep the low end from going to mud. This is the source the Kilt sounds best on in this rig.
- **VG-800 modeled signal:** The Kilt 10's explicitly modeler-friendly Low-Cut and "works with software/modelers" voicing make it a clean match for the VG-800's line/amp models — it'll add reactive grit on top of a modeled amp without the harshness the old Kilt had. Synth/COSM patches will gate hard at high gain (good for drone textures).
- **Fender Jazz Bass:** Multiple JHS users report buying a second Kilt specifically for bass ([JHS](https://jhspedals.info/products/kilt-v2)). The Low-Cut "flat" position preserves bass low end; the reactive gain handles bass dynamics well. A legitimate use the live board doesn't otherwise cover — none of Brothers/Longsword/Oxford is set up for bass.

## 7. Famous Users (Honest)

The Kilt's *signature* artist is **Stu G** (Stuart Garrard) — guitarist/producer and founding member of UK band **Delirious?** — who co-developed it in 2015 for studio and touring versatility (manual; [Sweetwater](https://www.sweetwater.com/store/detail/KiltPedalV2--jhs-the-kilt-v2-overdrive-pedal)). That's the honest direct-user story for the Kilt itself.

The *more interesting* lineage users belong to the **Expandora**, the circuit the Kilt clones — and one is directly relevant to this owner's repertoire:

- **Billy Gibbons (ZZ Top)** — the Expandora's most famous user; audible on *Rhythmeen* (1996), rumored to stack multiple units for his tone ([Guitar Pedal X](https://www.guitarpedalx.com/news/gpx-blog/17-of-the-best-billy-gibbons-style-overdrives-and-soundalikes)).
- **Thom Yorke (Radiohead)** — replaced his Turbo Rat with a **Bixonic Expandora around 2008** and used it for all of Radiohead's 2008–2009 shows ([The King of Gear](https://thekingofgear.com/thom/effects)). Given this repo's Radiohead song folders (Creep, Let Down, Full Stop), the Kilt 10 is the closest current-production stand-in for Thom's distortion tone of that era — a real reason to keep it.
- **St. Vincent (Annie Clark)** — reported Expandora user ([gearnews](https://www.gearnews.com/jhs-pedals-kilt-10/)).

Flagging as **unverified**: I could not confirm Jonny Greenwood used an Expandora (searches returned nothing); treat the Yorke connection as the Radiohead tie, not Greenwood.

## 8. Live / Power / I/O

- **Power:** 9V DC, center-negative, 2.1mm barrel. **64 mA** current draw (manual) — modest but ~6x the Hizumitas; budget for it on an isolated supply.
- **Do not exceed 9VDC** — the manual explicitly warns damage will occur and the warranty is voided. No 18V trick.
- **I/O:** Mono in (lower-right jack), mono out (left jack), plus the **Red Remote** jack (upper-right, red washer) for the latching-footswitch G2 switch.
- **Bypass:** Standard JHS footswitch bypass (not specified as relay in the manual).
- **Dimensions:** 2.6" × 4.8" × 1.6" — standard compact JHS B-size enclosure; small footprint.
- **Mono only:** worth noting for *this* rig — Board 1 goes stereo at the CE-2W, and the Kilt is mono. It would live early in the chain (pre-stereo), which is fine, but it's another reason it isn't a natural drop-in late on the board.

## 9. Pairing Recommendations (by name)

- **Into CB Brothers AM:** the strongest pairing. Run the Kilt as a low/mid-gain reactive front-end (G1 up, gain ~9–11 o'clock) feeding Brothers' clean-ish Channel A — the Kilt adds Expandora touch-sensitivity, Brothers shapes and boosts. If boarded, this is where it goes: *before* Brothers.
- **Before the MXR 108 / Hizumitas:** the manual's G1-down/G2-up "high-gain stage boost" is built to slam a fuzz front end. Kilt → 108 gives a thicker, more sustained fuzz than the 108 alone.
- **vs / not-with Longsword & Oxford:** don't stack the Kilt's distortion into Longsword or Oxford — you'd just be piling redundant distortion. Pick one. The Kilt's role, if any, is the *reactive low/mid-gain stage that feeds them*, not a parallel wall.
- **Studio re-amp via EHX Effects Interface + X-Amp:** the cleanest pairing for its benched life — one drive box covering OD→fuzz for printed tracks.
- **After it (downstream):** as with any drive in this rig, the time/texture happens later on the Middle and End boards (Microcosm, Dark Star, H90, Generation Loss). The Kilt just needs to deliver a stable, articulate dirty signal — which it does.

## 11. Mods / Quirks / Known Issues

- **The old Kilt's low-gain dead zone** was its single biggest complaint — the bottom of the gain pot did little. The Kilt 10's whole reason for existing is fixing this (re-mapped 0–25%). If comparing against an older Kilt, this is the audible difference.
- **The V2 Low-Cut toggle was widely felt to be too subtle.** The 10 explicitly addresses it ("now far more amp-friendly"). Players wanting an audible bass cut should prefer the 10.
- **Red Remote requires the separate accessory** (~$46) and only switches G2 — it's not a general remote, just the OD↔fuzz jump. Don't expect MIDI or multi-parameter control; the Kilt has none.
- **Mono only, no MIDI, no expression** — confirmed by the GearProfile. In a heavily-MIDI rig (Brothers, Microcosm, H90, etc. are all MIDI) the Kilt is an analog island, another reason it doesn't integrate as tightly as the boarded drives.
- **64 mA draw** is unremarkable but not trivial — six benched pedals shouldn't all return at once without checking supply headroom.
- No widely reported reliability issues; standard JHS build quality.

## 13. Starting-Point Settings

Knob positions clock-face, viewed from above.

**(a) Stu G transparent rhythm** — articulate low-gain crunch
- Volume: 12 · Gain: 9 · Tone: 12
- Toggles: both down (lowest/crunch); Low-Cut: flat
- The 10's fixed-dead-zone low gain shines here. Use for chord work into a clean amp/Iridium model.

**(b) British distortion (vs Oxford)** — riffy, focused
- Volume: 11 · Gain: 1 · Tone: 1
- Toggles: G1 up (overdrive); Low-Cut: cut
- Tighter, mix-cutting voice; the cut toggle keeps it from mudding the baritone.

**(c) ZZ Top / Thom Yorke distortion** — thick, reactive, sustaining
- Volume: 12 · Gain: 2 · Tone: 12
- Toggles: G2 up (fuzz/saturation); Low-Cut: flat
- The Expandora-leaning lead voice. Dig in for bloom, back off the guitar volume to clean up.

**(d) Forbidden gated fuzz** — spitty, sputtering, broken
- Volume: 2 · Gain: 4 · Tone: 1
- Toggles: both up (heaviest gated fuzz); Low-Cut: cut
- The "Forbidden" mode. Feed it into the Middle board's granular/smear pedals for the drone-doom degraded wall this rig is built around.

## 14. Versus Alternatives — and the Bench Verdict

- **vs the on-board CB Brothers AM:** Brothers wins the *platform* job (two channels, MIDI, stereo, ramping). The Kilt is the simpler, mono, single-set version. No reason to add it where Brothers lives.
- **vs the on-board EAE Longsword:** Longsword is the dedicated high-gain wall; the Kilt's gated-fuzz mode overlaps but is spittier and less controlled. Longsword wins the wall.
- **vs the on-board TKOG Oxford:** Oxford has a specific ShredMaster/Guv'nor British voice the Kilt approximates but doesn't replicate. Different character, overlapping coverage.
- **vs a real Bixonic Expandora:** the Expandora has the literal opto-FET reactivity and Forbidden mode; the Kilt 10 is the quiet, board-friendly, in-production translation. For the *Thom Yorke 2008 tone* the Kilt is the practical modern answer.
- **vs JHS Morning Glory:** Morning Glory is the cleaner transparent-OD specialist; the Kilt covers vastly more range including fuzz.

**Verdict on the bench decision: correct, and unambiguously so.** The Kilt 10 is an excellent, improved, versatile drive — but its core value proposition is *"one box that covers everything from OD to gated fuzz,"* and this rig already deploys three more-controllable specialists (Brothers, Longsword, Oxford) plus two fuzzes across that exact range. A generalist on a board full of specialists is redundant. The Kilt earns its keep **off the board**: as the single-pedal drive for **studio re-amping** (via the also-benched EHX Effects Interface + X-Amp), as the **travel/fly-board** drive, as a **bass-board** drive (a gap the live board doesn't fill), and as the **Thom-Yorke-era Radiohead distortion** stand-in for tracking this repo's Radiohead material. Keep it benched, but keep it close — it's the most useful pedal in the bench drawer.
