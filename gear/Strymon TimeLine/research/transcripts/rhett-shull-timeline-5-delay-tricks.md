Delay is my favorite effect. Five tips and tricks to get the most out of a delay pedal — beefing up rhythm parts, beefing up leads, getting a chorus with no chorus pedal, and some crazy out-there sounds.

## Tip 1 — Dotted-eighth on RHYTHM (not just leads)
Everyone knows the U2/Edge dotted-eighth lead sound. But use a **dotted-eighth delay under rhythm guitar** (verse or chorus) to add weight — it makes a single guitar sound almost **double-tracked**. To add even more width, bring up the **modulation** so the pitch of the repeats wobbles slightly sharp/flat by a few cents — that detune adds a lot of width. For a more upbeat driving section, add some dirt, turn the delay back on, and pull the **mix, repeats, and modulation depth down** a bit so it thickens without washing out.

## Tip 2 — Sub-audible eighth-note under SOLOS
Take a standard **eighth-note** delay and bring **mix and repeats down until it's barely audible** — layered *under* your solo tone rather than heard as distinct repeats. Adds an extra element that's great on recordings (this is a lot of what John Mayer does live and in the studio). He uses a **tape delay** for it because the repeats degrade over time (less high and low end as they trail off), so it blends into the mix. Only **two or three repeats** — you don't want repeats stepping on fast lead lines. It fills the space between notes "almost like a medium reverb."

## Tip 3 — Reverse delay for rhythmic interest on simple parts
Use a **reverse delay** to add a cool rhythmic element to otherwise simple chord progressions or simple lead lines. Done tastefully it's incredible, and it gets even better blended with a **washy reverb**.

## Tip 4 — Chorus with no chorus pedal
On a delay with a modulation control: he uses the **Digital** machine. **Max out the TIME** control (all the way up) and bring **REPEATS all the way down**. Now it behaves like a chorus — the three controls that matter are **mod depth, mod speed, and mix**. He sets **mod depth full up**, **mod speed ~11 o'clock**, and leaves the mix. You still get a quick subtle slap-back, but it nails the 80s chorus-y tone. Adding a washy reverb smooths out the initial slap-back.

## Tip 5 — Self-oscillation as an instrument
A total character effect. He uses the **tape** delay: set TIME to a random value, bring **MIX up** and **REPEATS way up to about 3 o'clock**. Play a single note and push the repeats a touch — there's a tipping point where it **self-oscillates**, and you can put the guitar down and use the pedal as its own instrument. Then sweep the **TIME** control to send the oscillation "into outer space" (the classic pitch-bend dive/rise of changing delay time at high feedback).
