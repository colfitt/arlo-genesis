The TimeLine has been out over a decade and is still one of the most viable delays out there. Common question: which delay types do you actually *need* for a Sunday morning?

## Preset philosophy — flexible, not song-specific
Lots of people save one preset per song. He doesn't — he likes to stay flexible and have more options at his disposal, so he can pull up a sound the instant inspiration strikes or a down moment calls for it, rather than being locked into song-specific presets.

## The workhorse: a small / medium / large TAPE ladder
For a Sunday morning you need a standard delay type — analog, **tape**, or digital. Tape is his go-to: it sits in a nice place where you hear the brightness of the repeat but it degrades enough to sit *underneath* your playing and create ambience. Mirroring his Big Sky workflow, he dials **three tape presets — low / medium / high** — differing in how **bright and present** the repeat is, because different songs (and lead vs. rhythm) call for a more or less present delay. That trio covers whatever ground you need.

## Subdivision strategy — dotted-eighth AND eighth copies
All three tape presets default to a **dotted-eighth** subdivision (popular in worship). He **copies the same three presets over as eighth-note** versions too — because some songs swap mid-song from a dotted-eighth to an eighth. So he has quick low/med/high options in *both* subdivisions, recallable at a moment's notice. (He offers the preset pack for download in the video description.)

## Other staples
- **Analog (dBucket):** darker and more warbly, modeled to mirror a Deluxe Memory Man. Blends into your playing — you don't hear the repeat distinctly, it just adds depth.
- **Digital:** for something really bright, clean, and pristine.
- **Dual:** his "Phil Wickham sound" — dialed bright and fairly in-your-face; you don't hear dual delays often but it has a distinctive crazy effect.

## Ambient textures
For swells and down moments he leans mostly on the Big Sky's washy reverb, but the TimeLine can make interesting textures too. He keeps a few ambient presets that sound very different from each other yet "fill a similar hole" — rhythmic or textured beds he'd use sparingly in down moments, usually layered with heavier reverb.
