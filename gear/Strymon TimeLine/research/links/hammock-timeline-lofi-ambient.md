strymon.net · Strymon (artist feature) · Hammock (ambient/post-rock duo, Marc Byrd & Andrew Thompson)

Hammock is the on-aesthetic gold standard here — ambient walls, textural decay, degraded-on-purpose. Their TimeLine technique is the single most rig-relevant artist find.

## How Hammock use the TimeLine
- Primary machine for the album *Mysterium*: the **Lo-Fi** machine.
- Quote: "It was great for creating textural decaying type sounds, usually created by one of us manually turning the knobs while the other played guitar or unplugged cables."
  → The TECHNIQUE is performative real-time knob manipulation (one person plays, the other warps the Lo-Fi parameters) and even **unplugging cables** mid-decay to mangle the tail. This is exactly the "recorded-wrong" / sustained-wall approach.
- They credit **Matthew Kidd (Slow Meadow)** for showing them the Lo-Fi machine's versatility.

## Signal chain (TimeLine sits at the very END, post-reverb)
Volume → Tuner → Keeley Comp → ProCo Rat → Expandora → Strymon Deco → custom OD → Boss Octave → Fulltone Choral Flange → Strymon Flint → Line 6 DL4 → Boss RV3 → **Strymon BigSky → Strymon TimeLine**
- Note: TimeLine is placed AFTER the BigSky reverb, so it delays/degrades already-reverberant signal — a wash-on-wash approach. (Inverse of putting delay before reverb.)

## Board philosophy quote
"Our pedal boards are almost always in flux. We are constantly changing pedals to accommodate whatever sound might be needed for a particular track." — TimeLine as a per-song textural tool, not a fixed delay.

## Rig fit
Two-person knob-riding is hard solo, but the takeaway transfers: assign Lo-Fi's Sample Rate / Bits / Vinyl to the EXP pedal (EP SET allows multiple knobs at once) to perform the decay-warp live, and consider running the TimeLine post-reverb (post-H90/BigSky in this rig) for the wash-on-wash Hammock placement.
