loopop · Novation SL MkIII MIDI Keyboard Review: Is it a gamechanging controller? (49SL MK3/61SL MK3) · 2018-10-10

THE canonical deep-dive review. Loopop frames the SL MkIII as three things behind one interface — a DAW controller, a hardware controller (templates), and a standalone sequencer — and tests whether merging them "changes the game." Honest pros/cons throughout (this is the 1.0 firmware era, so some gripes were later fixed).

## The three pillars (bird's-eye view)
- Swap between controlling **hardware** and the **DAW** with the InControl button.
- 8 endless encoders + 16 top-row buttons (toggle multiple function rows with the up/down arrows) + transport + 8 faders.
- Keyboard has two modes: dedicated entirely to the active part/hardware, OR split into **Zones** (play two-plus instruments at once — he demos strings low, piano high, both soft synths in the DAW).
- 16 pads are context-dependent: in DAW mode = Ableton session grid (toggle to pads); in sequencer = either the 16 steps of one pattern, the 8 patterns per part, or the Sessions.

## Sequencer workflow (his emphasis: ease-of-use as much as power)
- Basic unit = a **pattern** of up to 16 steps. Per part you see that part's pattern (e.g. one for the virus, one for the prophet, one for the subharmonicon).
- **Preview notes** by pressing pads — lights the corresponding key LED; works for chords too.
- **Add steps**: hold a step pad in and play notes. Change notes the same way.
- **Per-step fine-tune**: hit Options → velocity + gate **per note** (each note on a step can have its own values).
- **Pattern features (Options → Pattern)**: change start/end position; set **direction** = forward / backward / ping-pong / **random**; and the standout — **per-pattern sync rate** (one pattern at 8th triplets, another at quarters, another at 16ths). In his intro demo the prophet pattern was 4 bars of music shown in one 16-step pattern because its sync rate was set to 1/4 (four times slower).
- **Parameter / motion sequencing**: while playing, hit record and move an encoder to bake the change in as automation. Useful for the Virus (few physical knobs). You can motion-sequence live or step-by-step. You can automate the 16 encoders, the faders, buttons, mod wheel, even pedals — but only **up to 8 automation lanes** per part (visible in Pattern Options).
- **Patterns view (two layouts)**: default = each pad row = one of the 8 patterns of a part; scroll parts with arrows; tap to **chain/link** patterns. **Expand view** (Shift + Patterns) = each column is a different part, scroll up/down to choose patterns within parts.
- **Sessions**: up to **64**, save to new slots, color-code. Can't rename on hardware (use Components).

## Templates (hardware control mappings)
- Select: take an unused part → **Shift + Sessions** (templates page) → scroll the knob through the ~20 onboard templates (he loads a Digitakt template). Up to **64** templates stored.
- Knob functions show on-screen, two rows per knob; fader mappings show when moved; pads + controller inputs are all mappable.
- **Cannot create/edit/rename templates on hardware** — must use Novation Components. His firmware wish: on-board MIDI-learn template creation + renaming.
- Per loaded template you still choose output routing: USB / which DIN port / both, and MIDI channel.

## DAW control (InControl, Ableton focus)
- Hit InControl: top section becomes DAW control; keyboard mapping stays tied to whichever part/output you set.
- Faders = track levels; mute/solo/monitor/arm buttons work as expected; pad grid = session view (scroll the grid); device params on the encoder row; Options switches between devices on a track.
- **Two transports caveat**: InControl-mode buttons drive the DAW transport; sequencer-mode buttons drive the sequencer transport. Sync clock in/out is "pretty tight." In 1.0, Ableton-controls-MkIII was solid but MkIII-controls-Ableton was "a little spotty" — he advised running transports independently or making Ableton master / MkIII slave.

## Arpeggiator
- Built-in, with direction, octave range, sync rate, and per-step rhythm (you can turn arp steps on/off). Routable to the selected part or fixed to one of the 8 parts. Latch available. (His wishlist: more than one arp page / one arp per part.)

## Zones
- Set a region's destination part (he stacks piano / strings / subharmonicon / prophet / virus). Zones can overlap. Activate any time with **Shift + Zones**.

## Scales
- **Shift + (scale button)** to enable. Modes: **Snap** (forces wrong notes to scale), **Filter** (mutes wrong notes), **Display-only** (LED guide). Choose scale type, root, and transpose. Applies to keys and sequencer.

## Global settings
- MIDI settings + analog settings. Analog side: clock PPQN, per-output voltage levels, assign which CC converts to voltage out the back, and microtonal calibration.

## Cons / wishlist (1.0 era)
- Wants a smaller (mini-key) version. Wants: sequence transpose, on-board MIDI-learn template creation + rename, **non-quantized live recording**, **micro-timing of step start points** (in 1.0 you were locked to the grid), and DAW mixer level metering on the screens.
- NOTE: several of these (micro-steps / off-grid placement, 32-step gates, aftertouch→any CC) arrived in **firmware 1.2**, after this review.

## Verdict
For a hybrid setup mixing hardware electronic instruments AND a DAW in one central place, the SL MkIII "does indeed change the game for controllers and sets a new benchmark." Compare against Komplete Kontrol / Arturia KeyLab / Ableton Push (tighter software-suite integration) — but those lack the standalone sequencer + hardware templates.
