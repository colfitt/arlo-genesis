Molten Music Technology (Robin Vincent) · Hologram Chroma Console (mostly modular) Review · 2024-05-11 (1:06:35)

The deepest single source: a 66-minute hands-on review driving Chroma Console mostly from a Eurorack modular (Befaco VCO/filter loop and an Algo oscillator) through a DPW guitar-effects-loop module, plus guitar at the end. Honest about strengths and limits.

FRAMING: Robin loves the Microcosm's sounds but its interface defeated him ("I never worked it out... accidentally move something and can't get the nice sound back"). The Chroma Console is the opposite — same gorgeous degraded/saturated vintage character, but it "unfolds like a tulip": simple interface, clear LED indication of what's on, almost no menu-diving. The whole pedal's engine is "drift, saturation, vintage-ness, and joy."

THE FOUR MODULES (synth pass):
- CHARACTER (drive): DRIVE (TILT acts like a filter/EQ — brighter/darker, very easy and good), SWEETEN (more of a compression vibe), FUZZ, HOWL, SWELL. SWELL is the mysterious one — it wants strong, choppy, percussive input (and a VCA/envelope) to produce reverse-swell fades; it's "much more useful on guitar." He doesn't envelope it so it doesn't shine here.
- MOVEMENT: DOUBLER (detune via top knob), VIBRATO (he uses this a lot), PHASER, TREMOLO, PITCH (right = up, left = down, mixable). Workflow note: Hologram suggests MIX full up, then use the four AMOUNT knobs as per-effect levels.
- DIFFUSION (delays + reverbs): CASCADE (a nice delay; time-based effects follow the internal tempo set by tap), REELS (TAPE-style delay), SPACE (reverb), COLLAGE ("more of a Microcosm-type effect — grabs a chunk, runs off with it, brings it back," and its secondary runs the grab through a quick burst of tape), REVERSE (can set AMOUNT full so you only hear the reverse). Limitation he flags: only ONE effect per module, so you can't have a delay AND a reverb from Diffusion at once.
- TEXTURE (the destruction stage on the end): FILTER (HPF/LPF/tilt — "why is this here? but it's handy"), SQUASH (serious console-style compression, fattens a filter-thinned sound back up — "would work well with a bit of drive"), CASSETTE (his favorite — "like running your sound through a Portastudio," wow & flutter, slowing/speeding, magnetic-tape degrade that worsens as you turn it up; "use a little to introduce that sense of deterioration"), BROKEN (digital/databender glitch, "decided things are not well," more intentional than cassette), INTERFERENCE (pushes further into databender glitch, "all coming apart").

DRIFT (the heart of the pedal):
- Secondary function (press the two secondary buttons). Most relevant on MOVEMENT and DIFFUSION (Character's secondary is SENSITIVITY, "who cares"). Turn DRIFT up and "more of the weird stuff" appears — unsettling movement, shifting, plus stereo interest. The bottom LEDs indicate how much you've dialed; when you exit the secondary, the indicator FLASHES to tell you a secondary value is active (so you can always find your way back / zero it). He praises this UI a lot — it's why the multi-level design doesn't get you lost. Demoed on vibrato (gorgeous), phaser (cracks it up), tremolo (breaks it a bit), pitch ("really makes it rubbish" — in a good way), and on the delays/reverbs/reverse.

GESTURE (knob automation):
- Last two buttons (C+D) enter gesture record. From the MOMENT you press, move a control (e.g. push REELS feedback up then back), and exit — it loops that motion by itself. (He fails the first time by taking too long before moving.) Remove a gesture by moving that control. "Beautiful — but hard to do with your feet; very much a desktop thing."

CAPTURE (looper):
- Hold the left button to capture audio and loop it; simple looper. Great over an arpeggiator/bassline — and because your loop length won't match the synth's, the two drift out of phase against each other, which he finds delightful. Can be routed PRE or POST effects.

PRESETS / BYPASS (honest live critique):
- Save: press the two middle buttons, pick a slot, press again. Recall: hold bypass to enter preset mode, step up/down. White slot = empty.
- BUT preset changes have a fade/think gap (it auditions then loads), so it's NOT good for swapping configurations mid-solo/mid-song. Best for between-songs or to bring in a fresh effect after a bypass. Use it for storage, not real-time switching.
- DUAL BYPASS partly compensates: in EFFECT SETUP (also where you reorder modules), hold bypass; the flashing modules are the ones that get bypassed — toggle which modules respond to the footswitch so you can add e.g. a delay on top of a running sound with your foot. "Really a guitar feature." You get ONE such configuration — turn that subset on/off, no further nuance, and you can't reach the four modules individually with your feet.

OVERALL VERDICT: A self-contained "house of vintage fun" — one consistent workflow across all four modules so you don't learn four interfaces. Limitations: can't footswitch the four modules independently (must step through; choose your effects and play, rather than swap constantly); only one effect per module; preset switching has a load gap; no CV mod (gestures/MIDI only). For guitar he loves just the modulation + delays + a dual-bypass "add" module. "Simple, straightforward, sounds phenomenal, get lost in it for days." Ends with guitar through it (drive/sweeten/fuzz/howl/swell, doubler, vibrato, and a looped capture routed post-effects for sustain).
