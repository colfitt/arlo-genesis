# Rival Consoles (Ryan Lee West) — Chroma Console as a texture/finishing layer

The most instructive, on-aesthetic user found for THIS rig's intended use of the Chroma (morph
textures over a source, don't be the primary voice). UK electronic producer; used Chroma heavily on
the 2025 album *Landscape from Memory*.

## Signal path
- **Sequential Prophet Rev2 → Chroma Console.** Other desk gear: OTO BAM, Elektron Analog Heat MkII,
  Memory Boy delay. Everything mixed live in Ableton "like a traditional mixer."

## Concrete technique (verbatim quotes)
- Used it far more than expected: *"I used the Chroma Console a lot on this album, which I didn't
  expect to."*
- **Subtle, layered, multi-module "finishing" approach** — the key quote, and exactly the dossier's
  "morph the textures" philosophy:
  > "It's quite a nice way of adding **10% of this, 5% of that, 20% of something else**, and you end
  > up with a **much more coloured, enhanced and 'finished' sound** versus if you bypass it."
  (i.e. small AMOUNTs across the 4 modules, used as a glue/color stage — not one big effect.)
- **"Recorded-wrong" / degraded aesthetic** on the track *Nocturne*: took a *"beautiful melodic synth
  sound that's really falling apart"* — an otherwise *"absolutely boring"* three-note line — and made
  it *"interesting through this malfunctioning effect."* Using the Chroma to make a clean source
  sound broken/falling-apart is precisely the rig's TAPE-RUINED / degraded-texture goal.

## Why it's the gold-standard reference for this rig
- He treats the 4 modules as a **stacked light-touch color/finishing chain over a synth/pad source** —
  directly transferable to the modeled VG-800 pads and baritone/banjo beds in this rig.
- "Malfunctioning effect" on a falling-apart melodic line = the drone/doom/shoegaze, banjo-as-lead,
  recorded-wrong target sound. Lean on small AMOUNTs across Character/Movement/Diffusion/Texture
  rather than one cranked module.
