Alex Messano Music · 5 Cool Ways to Use The Boss BF-3 Flanger...In Mono and Stereo · 2024-09-11

Unleashed upon the guitar-playing public many many eons ago back in the good old days of 2002, the Boss BF-3 is another highly underrated Boss pedal that I think outdoes its predecessor, the classic BF-2. Why do I say this? Well, let's take a listen and find out. Here are five ways that I like to use this classic pedal.

First, let's go over the number-one thing that every great flanger must do: that classic jet-plane swooping, whooshing sound — or whatever it's called. This is the sound that I think most people are going to go for when they first plug into one of these pedals. To achieve this sound with this pedal it's very super easy: all the knobs are basically going to be set at noon — that's MANUAL, DEPTH, RATE, and then the MODE — all set straight at noon. And then the tiny RESolution knob is going to be cranked to about 3 or 4 o'clock.

[Demo plays — classic jet flange in Standard mode.]

One great improvement that this pedal has over the BF-2 is that it's got this great Momentary mode. So if you wanted to use that crazy flanger effect, say for just a specific part of a song — like maybe a player like EVH would do on "Unchained" or "Talking About Love" — this pedal makes it super easy: just put it into Momentary mode and then hold the pedal down and release, so you won't have to click it on and off twice.

[Demo plays — Momentary-mode flange bursts.]

The second way that I like to use this pedal is in its Gate mode, which is basically like an extreme tremolo sound with a very hard cutoff at the end. To achieve this sound we're going to set the MODE, RATE, and DEPTH knobs to about 9 o'clock, and then the MANUAL and RESolution knobs are going to be turned way down to about 6 or 7 o'clock.

[Demo plays — gated slicer tremolo in mono.]

Now we really get into what makes this pedal a lot of fun, which is stereo applications. So we're going to leave it in the Gate mode, and when it's hooked up in stereo that Gate mode functions as a panning mode, which is cool because it creates a lot of really lush swirling flanger and chorus sounds. So let's set it back to that extreme jet-plane kind of sound and see how that sounds in stereo with the panning mode happening.

[Demo plays — jet flange in stereo with Gate/Pan panning.]

And the fourth way that I like to use this pedal is as a chorus. So we're going to go ahead and set the DEPTH knob to 10 o'clock, the RATE knob to about 1 o'clock, and then the RESonance and MANUAL down low to about 7 o'clock. And first we're going to hear it in mono in the Standard mode, and then I'm going to hook it up in stereo and you'll hear it with the panning mode happening.

[Demo plays — chorus-like setting, mono then stereo.]

And the fifth and final way that I like to use this pedal is as a rotary-speaker kind of simulator. Just like the previous example we'll hear it first in mono and then in stereo.

[Demo plays — rotary/Leslie-style spin, mono then stereo.]

So those are five ways that I like to use this guy, the Boss BF-3 flanger (or flanger — I'm still not sure how to pronounce it). Let me know which was your favorite sound in the comments and we'll get a little discussion happening. Thanks a lot, I'll see you next time.
