# Anatomy of Tone — "What You Need to Know" (best owner-perspective gig workflow)

A practical, honest owner write-up. Strongest source on the live-workflow reality.

## The key gig insight — "pick a world and stick to it"
- Because voicings AREN'T swappable live without the app, the author's gig strategy: *"I picked a world I wanted to explore and stuck to it for the gig."* I.e., load ONE delay voicing + ONE reverb voicing that define your sound, set the knobs, and play the show within that palette — don't treat it as a multi-preset switcher.
- Confirms the limitation plainly: *"The 'voicings,' as they call them, aren't really interchangeable on the fly unless you have your iPhone out."* WORKAROUND offered = pre-load preferred combos before the gig (matches the manual's "voicing saved per effect" behavior).

## Favorite models (owner preference)
- Reverb: gravitates to **Plate** — *"The Del-Verb impressively captures some serious EMT 140 flavors."*
- Delay: *"I'm consistently drawn to the Tape and Analog Delay settings"* for character; notes Precision for pitch-shift/glide.

## Studio framing
- Explicitly endorses it as *"an outboard stereo delay and reverb for your studio"* — i.e., the same use-case as this rig's Apollo synergy: a stereo send/insert ambience box.
- Demos pairing with an Effectrode Tube Drive in front (drive → Del-Verb), consistent with "drive first, Del-Verb last."

## Tone-tracks-the-source observation (from companion review coverage)
- Del-Verb is transparent: a bright guitar makes the reverbs/delays bright, a dark guitar makes them dark ("some have commented the Del-Verb is too 'bright'" with bright sources). Relevant to this rig: the banjo (bright, 2–4 kHz spikes) will make Spring '65 splashy — prefer Plate 140 to tame it (matches dossier §6). A dark baritone neck-pickup will sit better in Hall 224.
