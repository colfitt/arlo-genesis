Mark Johnston · The Dark Star Stereo is larger than life. | Secret Weapons · 2024-12-13

The deepest single video on the V3 (~48 min). Detailed walkthrough of every control, the true-stereo Spread behavior (with a backwards-loop demo), Aux/freeze assignment, and a practical expression setup. Used in a studio post position.

OVERVIEW. At its core this is a proper stereo reverb with Volume, Mix, and a Spread control that you can set at noon for mono reverb in a stereo field, fully clockwise for a very wide stereo image, or fully counterclockwise for an inverted version of that image. The bottom row gives you Decay time, Multiply (a built-in feedback loop), and Lag (effectively pre-delay). Where the previous Dark Star made you choose between three modes, here ALL of it is available at the same time: a Crush knob that dials from noon to either warm overdriven sounds or bit-crushed digital artifacting; two Pitch controls now applied to the left and right channels of the reverb; and a Filter that can high- or low-pass at a fairly synthy resonant filter, opening and closing from noon. Plus three onboard presets, a multifaceted Aux, stereo in/out, MIDI in/thru, USB, and an expression input applicable to basically every feature at once. At its most basic it's a really good, really wide pad reverb — slow onset, drifty, skittery, very wide — but it becomes deeply complex as you add effects.

SIGNAL CHAIN / CONTEXT. Music Man St. Vincent into compressor, boost, and other pedals, into a Morgan PR12 amp, UA Ox Box for cabinets. The Dark Star runs in the effects loop of a Warm Audio WA273-EQ preamp in a POST position — processing the guitar through amp and cabinets first, then sending to the Dark Star so it functions as a studio reverb tool with full stereo width despite a real tube amp.

CONTROL CLUSTERS. Think of the layout as three groups. TOP ROW = creative effects (Crush, two Pitch, Filter); all notch at noon (pops into place where it's not affecting the signal) and apply a different version of the effect counterclockwise vs clockwise. MIDDLE ROW = the shape of the reverb (Lag, Multiply, Decay), minimum to maximum. LEFT SIDE = utility (Volume, Mix minimum-to-max; Spread is mono at noon, panning out to L/R channels turning one way and the opposite turning the other). Note: Volume and Spread do NOT notch at noon, which he wishes they did for finding unity quickly.

VOLUME affects both dry and wet; kept at unity for the video. In a post context use it to avoid preamp clipping; ahead of an amp use it to cut for headroom or boost for additional gain/compression to mangle the reverb. MIX goes full dry to full wet; you start to lose dry volume at about just past noon.

DECAY is straightforward — never that small (Dark Star isn't designed to be small), quickly becomes cavernous and cascades into infinity. "If this was the whole thing it'd still be a pretty cool pedal" — already a really good stereo ambient pad reverb on its own.

MULTIPLY adds a feedback loop to everything, extending the decay in a different way and giving the pedal proper self-oscillation. "It's basically a delay feedback loop inside of the reverb trail." LAG is tied into Multiply but functions as your pre-delay.

FILTER is a high-pass or low-pass depending on counterclockwise vs clockwise, with a very synthy quality; it closes completely (full silence). Handy to dampen harsh crush, clean up thunderous low end on octave-down pitch, or keep mud out when palm-muting on a neck pickup.

PITCH 1 / PITCH 2 are unquantized except at the far extremes: a fluid sweep from unity to an octave down, then a hard jump to the second octave (same going up). Identical to each other. You can do unity-and-two-octaves-down and spread them out, or exaggerate everything. Because the sweep is unquantized you can dial in fourths, seconds, fifths by ear for seasick imperfect pitch jumps. Multiply reprocesses the reverb through itself over and over, giving cascading pitch jumps (Blade Runner / Rainbow Machine territory) that get softer and "aren't even reverb anymore."

CRUSH notches at noon: counterclockwise = digital bit-crushy sample-rate-reduction distortion; clockwise = a warmer, more organic overdrive/distortion in the reverb.

TRUE-STEREO SPREAD (the key section). Left-channel input affects reverb on the left channel; right-channel input affects the right — a genuine true-stereo reverb. The Spread control inverts the left and right channels' WET signals but NOT the dry signals. He demonstrates with the Mood Mk II playing a simple long loop: forward on the left side, backwards on the right, often not at the same time. Fully clockwise Spread: right-channel info plays reverb on the right, left on the left. To the middle: pan that info toward center (all reverb in the middle channel even though dry info is still on L/R). Fully counterclockwise: reverses which channel OUTPUTS reverb without reversing which channel outputs the dry sound — so the backwards loop's notes on the right channel produce reverb on the LEFT channel. Result: dry signal plays on one side while its wet plays on the other. Practical use case: with a ping-pong delay feeding a stereo guitar rig (center guitar, taps on L/R), inverting Spread means every time the right delay pings you get reverb on the left and vice versa — an even wider, more complex stereo bed.

AUX SETUP. Press and hold Aux, then move Pitch, Filter, or Decay to assign the Aux switch to that parameter. Assigning it to Decay gives a freeze/hold function (latching or press-and-hold). Assigning it to Filter opens/closes the filter. Assigning it to Pitch jumps both pitch controls up an octave. Only those three parameters can be accessed via Aux, with pre-assigned extreme ends (filter closes entirely, Decay goes infinite, pitch goes octave up).

EXPRESSION SETUP. Press and hold the right (bypass) footswitch; the current knob settings become heel-down, and any knobs you move become the toe-down position. Knobs not moved while bypass is held are unaffected. Practical example he builds: a slightly high-pass filter with an octave-down reverb as heel, and the toe set to reverb back at unity pitch with the filter at a warmer/darker low-pass. He assigns Aux to infinite Decay so he can find unity pitch effectively, then holds bypass and sets the heel. Essentially every parameter can be assigned to expression simultaneously.

VERDICT. The strongest stereo reverb — possibly the strongest reverb overall — from Old Blood so far. Gives both conventionally nice consistent stereo reverb and very ambitious asymmetrical sound-design when you want it.
