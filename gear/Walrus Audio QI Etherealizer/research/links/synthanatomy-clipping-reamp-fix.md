The single most rig-relevant source for the clipping/level problem, because it tests the Qi with SYNTHS (hot signal) and surfaces Walrus's own recommended fix in the comments.

## The clipping / no-level-switch problem (THE big gotcha)
- The review states flatly: there is "no option to switch the inputs between the line and the instrument level," which is a problem because "synthesizers have a substantially higher output level than guitars" — so "you never really know how the engine will handle the signal."
- Verbatim behavior: the pedal "clips like crazy with a hot synth signal."

## Walrus's OWN recommended workaround (key find)
- In the comments (user **"Todd," Feb 5, 2026**): he obtained the pedal early, hit the clipping, and **asked Walrus directly what to do. Walrus's only recommendation was to REAMP** — i.e. pad the hot signal down with a reamp/attenuator box before the Qi.
- Todd's caveat: a reamper helps but is "limited in utility," and a **stereo reamp box can cost $300+ used** — a real cost to solve a problem that a 6 dB input pad switch would have fixed. So: the fix exists (attenuate the input), but Walrus ships no onboard pad.
- Practical takeaway for a hot/line source (VG-800): put a passive attenuator / reamp / level pad in front of the Qi's input, or pull the upstream patch/boost level down until the clip LED stops. An EHX-style effects-interface or any inline pad does the job of the "missing" instrument/line switch.

## Series vs Parallel
- This reviewer actually PREFERS Series ("produces nicer, richer results," fits a traditional pedalboard workflow) — note this is the opposite of the guitar.com/Guitar World lean toward Parallel for taming the reverb. So community opinion is genuinely split: Series = richer/cascading, Parallel = engines stay discrete + reverb tamed.

## Reverb dominance
- "The reverb has a tendency to eat everything you put in front of it – even in the middle section, but it's what brings all the grains to sparkle." Confirms the dominant-reverb behavior is intrinsic, not a bug — it's also what makes the grain shimmer.

## Granular workflow tip
- "Granular alone sounds a bit weird. Combine it with delay or reverb, and you'll unleash the engine's true beauty." I.e. never run Grain naked — always glue it into delay/reverb.
