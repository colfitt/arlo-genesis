Jackson Brooksby · Lo-Fi Vibrato to 80s Chorus in One! | Caroline Guitar Co. Somersault · 2021-08-11

Hey guys, welcome back to [the channel] — my name is Jackson, and today we are checking out the Caroline Guitar Company Somersault lo-fi modulator.

[Music demo]

The truth is, I have a massive love-hate relationship with modulation — all types of modulation. I'm always switching between chorus, vibrato, flanger, phaser, anything, and I'm always switching between different pedals; I can never quite settle on one. Sometimes I refuse to use modulation at all. So I'm always on the hunt for the perfect one. The Somersault came up on my Reverb feed for a pretty reasonable price and I thought, let's go for it — I know Caroline make good stuff.

My understanding of the Somersault is that it's a half-digital, half-analog chorus pedal. The digital part controls the delay on the wet signal, and the analog part pretty much does everything else. But what drew me to this pedal in particular was this word here — four letters: "lo-fi." What I'm used to when it comes to chorus is super-pristine hi-fi 80s chorus tones, and having a chorus that's lo-fi is something new. This pedal can absolutely do those lo-fi underwater vinyl wobbly tones, better than anything else I've tried in that category.

Those lo-fi tones are helped along by this little switch here, which is my favorite control on the whole pedal — it's basically a low-pass filter or high-cut on the wet signal. Say you're running this pedal 50/50 wet/dry: that switch tames up the vibrato signal and makes it a lot darker and a little less prominent. But when you run this pedal 100% wet, you have that overall darker, warmer sound, and it is SO lo-fi — so reminiscent for me of soundtracks from the Bioshock video games. To be fair, the Somersault can still do those pristine 80s chorus tones, but for me the lo-fi tones are where it's at.

[Music demo]

Let's quickly talk about the controls. Top left we have our wet/dry MIX — this is how we go from a chorus tone to a fully vibrato tone if you go 100% wet. The OFFSET is the most interesting control on the whole pedal, and this essentially changes the delay time for the wet signal — we'll talk more about that in a minute. The two knobs here are SPEED and DEPTH (self-explanatory). In the middle, along with that dark lo-fi switch, we also have a wave-shape switch, switching between a triangle wave and a square wave for weirder tones. Then a bypass footswitch and a HAVOC footswitch, which is on a lot of Caroline's pedals — essentially on this pedal it maxes out the rate, giving you almost a faux-rotary sound on a momentary switch, which is really cool.

[Music demo]

So let's talk a little about this OFFSET. It's taken me a minute to understand and get used to it. Above maybe about 9:00 there is a quite audible and noticeable delay between your dry signal and the wet signal; increasing it basically increases the intensity of the chorusing effect, and it gets weirder and weirder as you increase that knob. Below 9:00, that delay is still there but it's not so audible — you just feel it under the fingers, and it's actually quite a weird sensation having that delay there. In the past I've really struggled with chorus/modulation pedals that embrace that delayed-wet-signal effect, but there's something about the way the Somersault executes it that's easier to get along with — not too gimmicky, doesn't sound too cheesy. Admittedly I'd still prefer if you could turn that delay off, or really minimize it to almost unnoticeable, but it is what it is, and it adds to the character of the pedal.

[Music demo]

So has the Somersault solved all of my modulation needs? No — arguably no pedal ever will. But is it one of my favorite chorus pedals now, and am I glad I picked it up? Absolutely yes. It's taught me more about a different method, a different approach to chorus that I've really enjoyed. And for weirder, darker, more lo-fi tones, this is 100% now my go-to.
