Zachari Smith · Spotlight: Caroline Guitar Company Somersault - Lo-fi Modulator (review and demo) · 2020-09-07

Hello, welcome to Spotlight — my name is Zachari Smith. Today we're going to be talking about the Caroline Guitar Company Somersault; they call it a lo-fi modulator. We'll go through some of the settings, demo it up, and I'll give you my pros and cons.

It does a lot of really cool chorus sounds, some vibrato sounds, some rotary sounds, and then kind of makes a lot of weird noises as well. I'll quickly go through the knobs, because they're labeled with just these logos and it's a bit tricky to figure out what they mean. The first knob is the MIX, this is the OFFSET, this is the DEPTH, and this is the SPEED. Then two switches: one is a brighter mode and one is a dark mode, and then two different waveforms — the standard chorus stuff is generally the triangle wave, and the square wave is where your weirder sounds come from. You have two footswitches: a regular on/off, and their HAVOC switch (which I think they have on a few of their pedals) — this switch takes the rate from wherever it is to max, so that can be fun if you just want to hit one note where the rate goes to max and then you're back to whatever you had.

Overall I found this pedal pretty cool. I picked it up locally online for $220 Canadian. It gives you a really great chorus sound, and from there I've been able to have it do some weird things. I really like the adjustability you can get with it.

[Demo — using a G&L ASAT.]

I just have a little loop going, and we'll engage the pedal — everything at 12 o'clock to start, put it on the bright mode. [Demonstrates.] So that's your DEPTH, this is the OFFSET. Here's kind of what happens with the right knob [SPEED] — that's a really slow rate — and if we max it out with the HAVOC... So that gives you an idea of what it can do as a really adjustable chorus. The OFFSET is kind of how far the delayed signal is away from your original, and so if you max out the MIX then I think you're getting more of a vibrato sound — but that can feel pretty weird if the OFFSET is too high, because everything you play is just a little bit delayed. SPEED and DEPTH are pretty self-explanatory.

The dark sound is basically everything we just heard but a little bit less high frequency. [Plays at 100%.] For me I often use the dark side because it's a little bit subtler, especially if you want a nice chorus that's supporting the sound.

Let's hear the crazy stuff with the square wave — I'll play a single-note thing. We'll start everything at 12, in the bright mode, with the square wave now. [Demonstrates.] You can hear it starts to modulate and almost self-oscillate a bit, something weird going on in that mode where you can almost get some synth-like effects. It can get pretty weird, kind of unpredictable — I don't really know how to control it that well, but usually I'll go to that mode if I just want something a bit crazy. It's always in that vein of "bleep bloop" sounds.

Pros: it's pretty easy to use, and it's more than a chorus pedal — you get great chorus sounds but also the ability to get weird, ambient noises. I really like the build quality, the bright/dark switch, and this HAVOC button is really handy when you're playing along and just want a little something for a second. Cons: it feels a little bit limited in terms of what it can do — it looks like you can do more than it actually can (four knobs, two switches, two footswitches). I've basically shown you everything from a normal usable chorus all the way to a crazy uncontrollable bleep-bloop noise maker. It needs other effects around it to sound cool, and it doesn't go all the way of being something like a Rainbow Machine — it's really still very much a chorus with some extended features. But overall a really cool pedal, and I've liked everything I've tried from Caroline.
