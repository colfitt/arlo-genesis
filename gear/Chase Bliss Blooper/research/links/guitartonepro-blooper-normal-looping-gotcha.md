Focused explainer on whether the Blooper can do "normal" looping and the one gotcha everyone hits.

## THE big gotcha (community-confirmed, most-asked question about Blooper)
- "The length of the loop doesn't change even though it may seem like it temporarily." When you slow playback (e.g. half-speed), the whole loop plays at half speed / an octave down — but **the loop CONTAINER stays the original length.**
- > "if you then try to 'record' that effect permanently into the loop it will only record to the length of the original loop, giving you only half of the slower loop."
- Verdict: "Confusing to describe, confusing the first time you do it, not too hard to work around once you know what's going on." This is the single biggest conceptual hurdle for new owners and the #1 forum question.

## Using it as a standard looper
- Yes, it works as a normal looper — in NORM mode it behaves like a "standard" looper, with modifiers and Stability treated as effects sitting AFTER the loop, not baked in and not affecting the dry signal. You can ignore all the modifier/sound-design machinery if you just want clean overdubs.
- Caveat the reviewer stresses: it's designed as a "creative fully featured looping pedal," not a quick grab-and-go loop station — the $500 price is justified by MIDI, flexibility, presets, and USB updateability rather than by being a better plain recorder.

## Creative workaround the reviewer calls out
- The REPEATS knob in ADD mode reapplies modifiers per repeat cycle, turning standard looping into a customizable delay. Effects compound each repetition: slowdowns get progressively slower, volume decays per the knob setting. (This is the "Blooper as delay" behavior.)
