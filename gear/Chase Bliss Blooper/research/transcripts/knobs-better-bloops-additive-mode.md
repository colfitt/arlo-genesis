**What Additive mode is.** This is the creative heart of Blooper and the original idea behind the whole thing. Additive mode lets you **record the modifiers (and Stability, and your knob movements) as if they were input audio**. Simplest way to think of it: whatever you hear at the output becomes your new layer. Use it for editing loops, or as a continuous process — leave it recording and it passes through effects again and again, becoming many things (including horrifying/weird ones).

**Basic workflow — printing effects.** KNOBs applies a touch of Filter and a touch of Stability "to give it personality," then records those into the loop. He uses **one-shot recording mode** (hold the left footswitch) to input the effects and "paste them over top" for exactly one pass. Then he turns the modifiers off — and they're baked in. (A/B comparison: before vs after the print; the printed version is gentler/more analog and tape-like.)

**The load-bearing concept — the measure (loop length) never changes.** This applies across all of Blooper. Once you set the initial loop length, it is fixed. In Additive mode you can record speed changes — push/pull and mold the loop as you record, hearing it all in real time — but the loop's measure does not expand/contract. This is the least intuitive part of Additive: once you mess with time, pieces of your loop might go away (pushed past the boundaries if you slow down) or you might hear sections repeat (if you speed up, the loop restarts and that's baked into the recording).

**Why — the steady record head.** Blooper is coded like a strange tape machine. The **record head always moves at a steady pace**; the **playhead can do all kinds of weird stuff**, and the record head just listens and lays down whatever it hears wherever it happens to be — always respecting the original measure boundaries. So if Scrambler is jumping around the loop, the record head still places that information wherever it currently is.

**Precise placement — recording a glitch into a specific spot.** Because the measure is fixed and the record head is respected, you can plot any additive material at any specific part of the loop. KNOBs demonstrates replacing one specific note with a glitch: start recording at the moment that note plays, make the glitch, and when it comes back around the glitch is permanently in that exact spot.

**Creative use — bring a simple loop to life.** Record some note information / chords first, then use Additive mode to make it less conventional (record reverse, speed up, brief moments of Stability "really falling apart," more Trimmer to exploit its time-stretch quirk). KNOBs notes that using only the **Stepped Speed (quantized) modifier means everything stays in the original key**. Then overdub standard guitar on top. End the loop with a custom tape delay using Stability.

**Takeaway:** input note/chord material → use Additive to transform/mangle it → overdub clean playing over top. Or just let Additive run continuously and let the loop evolve (or fall apart) on its own.
