**Overview.** Modifiers change the fundamental sound or behavior of your loop. There are two channels of modifiers that can run simultaneously; each channel holds three modifiers. Each has a button to turn it on/off and a knob to control it. (KNOBs records a loop and applies each modifier so you can hear what it does.)

**A1 — Smooth Speed.** A fluid, tape-machine-style speed control. Some people get tripped up trying to stay in pitch/key while changing speed — but that's not really what it's for. What KNOBs enjoys is the tape effect: tape starts, tape stops, slowing things down. You can crawl almost to a stop and explore the textures in between, or get even slower.
- Tape-stop trick: set the knob fairly close to real-time speed, then engage the modifier to trigger a tape-stop. Same idea for a tape-start. You can also ramp it to create modulation (he approximates the ramp with his hand).
- Neutral zone: every modifier has a neutral zone at noon (changes per modifier, keeps things consistent). For Smooth Speed, **middle (noon) = stopped**; counterclockwise from there is **reverse**; turning it all the way to either extreme is an **octave up** — so there is an in-pitch octave interval at the extremes if you want to go up an octave.

**A2 — Dropper.** Omits / fails to play pieces of your loop.
- **Clockwise** = drops in the exact same place with the exact same amount, consistently — a repeating pattern of drops.
- **Counterclockwise** = random drops, won't be consistent.
- The pattern (size of drop, smoothness, location) is calculated each time you turn the modifier on/off — toggle off/on to recalculate an entirely new pattern. Sometimes you get stuttery/glitchy effects, other times smooth descents.
- At max it becomes essentially granular.
- Combines really well with Stability for broken/aged sounds — like a needle skipping on a record player.
- Built specifically to exploit Blooper's additive recording: set it mild, press record, and let it go — the loop slowly disintegrates and falls into silence over time. (An alternative to using Repeats to fade material: use Dropper to crumble instead.)
- Note: if you continue recording new layers on top of a crumbled loop, your new layers won't be crumbled.

**A3 — Stepped Trimmer.** Shortens your loop; sounds glitchy when you move it.
- At noon = no trim, whole loop plays.
- **Counterclockwise** brings the end of the loop toward the beginning (shortening from one direction).
- **Clockwise** = the opposite, brings the beginning toward the end. Great for discovering "loops within your loops."
- Quirk left in deliberately: turning the knob **clockwise gives a time-stretching effect** (a byproduct of how it was coded; they liked it and kept it). You can ramp/expression-control it. Counterclockwise is more abrasive ("those crumbs").

**B4 — Stepped Speed.** Another speed modifier, but locked to musical steps — the speed equivalent of octaves or fifths up/down. Controls work the same as Smooth Speed (clockwise = forward, counterclockwise = reverse) but it jumps in steps instead of a smooth tape sound. Easy to find useful harmonic pitches in key with what you originally recorded.

**B5 — Scrambler** (KNOBs' favorite). Creates jump points in your loop, breaking it into a pattern (random or consistent, like Dropper).
- The knob controls the **amount of jump points**: turn way down and you mostly get your loop with the occasional event; turn up for heavy chopping.
- **Clockwise** = consistent/repeating pattern; **counterclockwise** = random jumps (not consistent).
- Patterns are regenerated every time you turn it on — toggle off/on for a completely different pattern.
- Jump points fall on a **grid: the loop is sliced into 16 equal parts**, so if you play a rhythmic loop in, the scrambles support and move along with that rhythm (funkier/quirkier but rhythmically locked).

**B6 — Filter.** Simple dual-mode filter.
- Noon = no filter; **counterclockwise = low-pass**; **clockwise = high-pass**.
- KNOBs argues the **high-pass is the more useful one for a looper** (it was the last modifier they finished). Busy loops with lots of layers almost always benefit from just a touch of high-pass — it "opens things up." The low-pass contains shrill/webby material.

**Closing note.** Recording these modifiers (additively, through themselves or through each other) was essentially "idea one" that started Blooper — many of the modifiers come to life or get unlocked once you start recording them.
