**What Stability is.** Blooper's multi-effect that gives your loop a more analog, tape-like feeling. It adds **mild filtering, noise, and several kinds of modulation (some random, some periodic)** — wow/flutter-style movement to your loop.

**The knob is broken into quadrants/sections.** When coding it, the designers wanted different parts of the Stability range to have different functions.
- **Very early stage (just off minimum):** more or less too subtle to hear on its own. This region exists specifically for **additive recording** — so you can record Stability again and again and again, and it will *very slowly* wear out your loop, gradually losing frequencies and gaining an aged feel (especially combined with Dropper). It takes ages to demonstrate because it's meant to be cumulative.
- **Moderate:** even without additive recording, a little filtering + a little noise makes a digital loop sound more like it's in the analog domain.
- **Maxed + additive:** "your broken tape machine sound." Crank it as high as it goes and overdub for full degradation.

**Signature additive technique — "mixed eras."** If you continuously record layers with Stability on, **each layer has more stability/aging than the last**. This places each pass in a different era/time: the original notes become quite vintage-sounding, while each layer added on top is a little more modern, clearer, and in the forefront. Result is a "mixed eras" sound.
- Pair with low Stability + the Repeats knob (so old layers fade out) and you get a continuous time shift: old material is both aged and fading away, while your new notes sit on top clean and forward.

**Reference workflow shown:** record clean (Stability off) as a reference point → bring Stability up over successive additive passes → hear the loop progressively age and break down.
