chase bliss (official) · Onward – Walkthrough · 2024-05-14

Tom, Zack, Joel, and Cooper (the dog) walk through the Onward at launch. This is the only first-party video; it is conceptual rather than parametric, but it nails the design intent.

[Music intro — "I really liked that, I've never heard that sound before — that's cool, because the only thing I played was this..."]

Hi, we're here to talk to you today about Onward. It's a new pedal from Chase Bliss, and this is Tom, that is Zack, this is Joel, there's Cooper — he's a dog, the goodest boy. Please enjoy our program.

What's the difference between Onward and Outward? These are two versions of a pedal that Tom made, both called Outward. Onward is kind of like a zoomed-in, focused version of the enveloping modes on those pedals, which dynamically sample. Onward just zooms in on that and explores that effect a little more. Since that was your favorite thing about Outward, you decided that's something you really wanted to explore. The other parts of the pedal are kind of represented elsewhere, either in our pedals or in the greater pedal universe.

What in your view is the one-to-three-sentence summary of Onward? I would say it's just dynamic sampling. That's the method of least resistance with sampling on a guitar — you're doing something, it's triggering it and recording it. You don't have to think about pressing a footswitch at the right time or timing it up perfectly with an LFO or something. It just tracks what you're doing. A DJ would have other tools at his disposal that a guitarist wouldn't — cutting up samples and putting them in a DAW. This gives that opportunity to guitar players in a pretty immediate way.

Onward and upward — there are two channels. There's a Glitch side and a Freeze side, and they're in parallel. But you can lock this, and your Freeze will follow what you're playing. It analyzes your playing over a period of time, so it adapts: if you're playing hard, it'll be triggering normally; then you switch to playing soft and it'll still trigger if you want it to.

The Freeze is like a smooth, indistinct pad — it doesn't even sound like a guitar anymore. (Like 2001: A Space Odyssey.)

Let's do the Glitch side. This will be a short snippet of your sound. The Fade dictates how long it takes for the sample to fade in. Then Sustain says "I'll hold for this long," and the Fade toggle also dictates how long it takes to fade out. So it transforms your sound into something with synth characteristics — and that works for both the Glitch and the Freeze side.

What is Error? It introduces instability to your samples. Instead of being steady, you can make it more organic. The left Error mode messes with your timing — it randomly changes a subdivision, so it'll be in time, just a different, shorter length of your base sample. You can play along with it, and that'll change based on the Size knob — it's synced to the time the Size knob sets. Error mode two makes little defects in the quality of your audio, either dropping pieces of it or making it more digital and crunchy. The last one is my favorite: it introduces variation in your playback speed (pitch) but also the direction, so it'll randomly reverse it. All these errors happen based on the time / the Size knob.

If we do stereo, it'll do that differently on each side of the effect, which is cool.

There's one other block called the effects block: some pitch-shifty stuff (Octave), some grit (distortion and sample-rate reduction via Texture), and modulation (Animate) you can add for variance and movement. At noon for both Octave and Texture, the effect is off — you just get your normal sample. The Animate toggle adds modulation.

Should we show a secret? Right now you just have this one type of Error mode. What if we did this — there's a secret, unlisted mode that'll let you blend in all of them at once.

We had an absolute blast talking to you about Onward. Thanks for tuning in. We love you. What's for dinner?
