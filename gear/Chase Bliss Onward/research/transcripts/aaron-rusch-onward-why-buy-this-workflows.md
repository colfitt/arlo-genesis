Aaron Rusch Guitar · Chase Bliss Onward Demo 5: Why Buy This? · 2024-11-14

Part 5 (final) of Aaron Rusch's series — the "how do you actually use it musically" video. Less parametric, but the most useful for WORKFLOW: what role the pedal plays and the two ways he actually uses it. (He skips a dedicated dip-switch video and folds LATCH usage in here.)

Framing: after living with it, it doesn't replace a delay or the (EHX) Super Ego. Some viewers said it's expensive ($400) and "not particularly musical/useful" — fair, depending on who you are and how you play.

WORKFLOW 1 — Freeze one channel, play live over the other (like a mini multi-effect). He has the pedal set to LATCHING mode (a dip switch) so you can hold the button to grab and it latches/holds. Freeze a chord (he uses B minor) to hold a drone, then play something glitchy on top. Can go extreme/layered or more restrained — e.g. freeze a pad and play a lead part over it with a cool texture.

WORKFLOW 2 (his favorite) — use it as a "warping" / sound-design pedal, similar to a Hologram Microcosm or a Boss/EHX-style warp. Get a loop going, then slowly warp it over time. He considers this a sound-design tool more than a musical instrument; "this thing is a layer maker." You run stuff through it and it destroys/transforms your sound into a cool vibe or layer. He uses it with his big pedalboard where he builds a bunch of layers — not on his practice board.

Verdict / buying advice: is it worth $400? Depends who you are — watch a lot of videos, listen to a lot of sounds, and if you can, play one. Consider it an experimental sound-warping pedal; if that's foundational to how you want to make music, great. But if your guitar, amp, or other foundational gear isn't where you want it, put money there first before any experimental pedal. He really loves it, but wants buyers to be clear-eyed about the role it fills.
