## Wide Stereo Mode (a.k.a. Split Mode)

Another trick engineers used was to send each deck individually to the left and right channels of the stereo field — the **reference deck goes Left, the lag deck goes Right**. That's Wide Stereo Mode, which Strymon also calls **split mode**.

**Wide slapback:** with a slapback echo set, split mode makes the echo actually go left/right — a very wide slapback echo, a nice effect.

**Stereo chorus:** going back to the knobs at the 12 o'clock position in split mode creates a stereo chorus where the reference signal is to the left and the lag signal to the right; with a bit of **Wobble** this creates a really nice natural-sounding chorus. Increase the Lag Time for a really wide chorus that's just short of being a slap.

**Randomly-panning movement:** by setting **Lag Time to minimum** in split mode and adding **Wobble**, you're moving the lag deck from ahead of the reference deck to behind it, and when the signal is split you get a randomly moving panning effect. It creates a nice stereo effect with a sense of movement but doesn't really have any signature sound attached to it. With enough Wobble you'd get pitch effect in there, but keeping Wobble low gives you panning and movement of the signal in the stereo field.

**Split vs. non-split contrast:** in non-split (normal) mode, the decks are summed electronically into each speaker, so you get the benefit of the **comb filtering**. In split mode you instead hear the **movement of the signals in stereo** (no combing). That's the core trade-off: comb-filter tonality (summed) vs. stereo motion/width (split).
