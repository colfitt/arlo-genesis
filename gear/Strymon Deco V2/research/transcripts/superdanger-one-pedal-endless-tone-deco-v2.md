## Framing: not a guitar pedal, a "vibe machine"

"This is not a guitar pedal. Okay, it is — but really it's a time machine, a vibe machine." History: Ken Townsend, the Abbey Road engineer who pioneered automatic double-tracking, manipulated the tape playback heads to create saturation, compression, flanging, chorus, doublers — "all the things we use are because this guy was messing with these play heads, and that's what this pedal is. It's not a distortion pedal, it's not a chorus pedal — it is the **father / progenitor of those effects.** That's why the tone that comes out of it is unlike any other." After a month, he's in the **always-on** camp.

## Rig used

Clean into a Fender **Blues Junior**; guitars are a PRS Custom 24 S2 (single-coil neck) and (for the stereo section) a PRS SE Silver Sky. For stereo he runs into a **Strymon Iridium** (round channel, York Audio "Basement" IR) in stereo, **with the Deco placed last in the chain.**

## Saturation as a single-coil boost / tone tool

- **Classic** voice tape saturation = "a great tool for boosting a single coil."
- **Cassette** voice = "adds a lot more grit, a lot more low end, compresses a lot more."
- His default boost recipe: **Cassette mode, knobs roughly at noon** — clean boost that's "fine." Stack a little drive (an OCD) in front and "it sounds so good." If the cassette voice is too much with the drive, switch to **Classic.** Works great as a solo boost.

## The Lag Time sweep (his description)

"Once you get past 12:00 on Lag Time you actually start to get a **chorus** — it shifts from flanger into chorus, and that's where you get a beautiful chorus sound. As you keep shifting through, the chorus gets so much further away that it morphs from chorus into a **slapback**, all the way to a full slapback — that's where you probably want to **pull the Blend back a bit.** All the way up on Lag Time it's a **legit delay** (500 ms at max), and on **Bounce** you get the original signal plus a bounce repeat."

- **Wobble:** "Alters the signal, detunes it, pushes it forward and pulls it back randomly." Easiest way to hear it in isolation: **Blend full up** (only the affected signal, no dry) — "very cool vintage sounds."
- **Invert + slapback:** "When you have slapback on Invert it legit sounds like you're in a room."

## Type switch (Sum / Invert / Bounce)

"Sum, Inverse, and Bounce — you've got to figure out which works best for you. They all affect the phase relationship differently and consequently affect the **EQ** of what's coming out." Bounce (the bottom setting) is where the "crazy wide swirls" live, and it really shines in stereo. The types are **much more pronounced in stereo than in mono** — "when I had it on the Blues Junior you couldn't really tell."

## His signature use: end of the chain, "the meat grinder"

His favorite, non-traditional approach: **put the Deco LAST in the chain, the last thing before the amp.** "All of your modulation, all your reverbs — everything gets thrown into this meat grinder that is the Deco. It chews it up, spits it out, and gives it a vintage vibe." Specific tricks:

- **Reverb → Deco:** running a reverb *into* the Deco "saturates and compresses your reverb — that long reverb tail is now being compressed and distorted. Very cool when you hold out a note."
- **Reverb → Deco delay-bounce (post-reverb):** "If you really want to get weird, delay-bounce post-reverb so the reverb is getting bounced into each other — you get a weird feedback of reverb getting delayed; compress it even more, throw it on the cassette."
- **Strymon Flint hall → Deco (stereo):** "A really long decay on the hall reverb on the Strymon Flint, going into the Deco, so that reverb is getting saturated, and then it's getting bounced back and forth, Wide Stereo." (Direct Flint-into-Deco ambient pairing.)
- **After an overdrive:** "Where I like it a lot is actually **after a distortion** (after the OCD) — that's so cool. If it's too much, just dial the Blend back for a subtle effect."
- **Chorus trick:** "Throw a **compressor in front of it** — very interesting-sounding chorus, not your normal chorus flavors."

The constraint *is* the feature: "You don't have the fine tuning a dedicated phaser/flanger/chorus/slapback would have, but I don't think that's what this pedal is for. The constraints force you to become more creative — to think about EQ differently."

## Wide Stereo + Auto-Flange (he calls out the held-footswitch moves)

- **Wide Stereo:** "Hold down Tape Saturation, turn Wobble" (the Live-Edit secondary). The lag track goes to one side, the reference track to the other, repeating only on the one side. Used on a song intro/middle section: **"I panned them hard right/left and it gives a super-swirly sound."**
- **Auto-Flange:** "If you hold your foot down it gives you a flanger of whatever setting you can set up ahead of time."

## On MIDI / plugin-controller use (V2)

- **Line-level / plugin controller:** "You can also use this pedal in line — go out of your DAW and use the Deco as a plug-in controller, get all these effects and put it on any track you want at line level. Is it easier to just use the Waves J37 plugin? Yeah. But is it cool that you can do it? Sure."
- **MIDI:** "If you are a MIDI player this might be the most versatile pedal on your board. It can do so many sounds and different things that you really need a MIDI controller and set up all these presets to get the full use of it live — it's too bad you can only do [one setting at a time] without MIDI." (V2-specific endorsement of the preset/MIDI workflow.)
