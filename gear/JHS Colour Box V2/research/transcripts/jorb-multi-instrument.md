Hello, my name is DB, I love gear, and this is the JHS Colour Box — a special colorway of the Colour Box V2 to celebrate 10 years since the original pedal came out. It's a Neve 1073-style preamp in a pedal format with a bunch of smart features that make it really useful in a lot of different scenarios. It can be a loud and clean microphone preamp, a character-rich overdrive, a slightly quirky replacement for an amp sim, and the EQ is strong enough for it to just be a mixing utility. Having it in pedal format (as opposed to a rack unit) invites you to experiment with so many more sound sources and in more environments than just a studio. There's a rich history of classic direct-into-a-mixing-desk guitar sounds and that's what most people would be interested in this for — but not me. I'm going to use it as a preamp for synths, electric pianos, maybe a drum machine. (JHS sponsored the video.)

CONTROLS / GAIN STAGING: The red section is the preamp gain section, blue is the EQ, white is the high-pass filter. I'm plugging into the combo jack with an XLR — it could also be quarter-inch — and coming out with quarter-inch (could also be XLR). Either XLR or quarter-inch in, either XLR or quarter-inch out — that honestly is a big deal. The gain section is MASTER, PRE-VOLUME and STEP. STEP is detented — one, two, three, four, five slots. MASTER is at the very end (output). PRE-VOLUME is the level between two preamp stages in series — how much you're driving the second one. STEP increases the gain of both stages in even increments. So think of STEP and PRE-VOLUME as your gain and MASTER as your volume, if you want to think of it as a simple distortion pedal. It can get pretty gainy and it's really reactive to volume.

DEMO — SYNTH (Roland synth → Colour Box → TC Electronic Hall of Fame): Turning the Colour Box on, immediately there's coloring — the EQ is different. This is supposed to be like plugging into a characterful preamp, so I want it to color the sound. At about maximum gain it gets dusty and broken up because that's what cranked preamps sound like. But so much of what's interesting about the Colour Box is not the high-gain stuff — having access to it is huge but it's indicative of the control range of the EQ as well. You have more than you'd ever want on every control.

EQ EXPLAINED: Noon is flat / no effect. Up is a boost, below noon is a cut. TREBLE and BASS are shelves. The SHIFT knob sets where the shelf starts: turn TREBLE SHIFT all the way up and only the highest highs are pushed; turn it down and everything from the mids upward is affected. For TREBLE the greatest range of frequencies is affected with SHIFT all the way down. BASS is the same idea — SHIFT all the way down affects the least (only the lowest), SHIFT up reaches up toward the mids. The MID band is not a shelf — it's a lump/bell, and these are all pretty wide. For synthesizer I'd leave the MID about where it sounds good and boost the treble.

HIGH-PASS: As a synth/mixing person you think of it as a utility, but even here it makes a big difference — great as a mixing utility or a live "clean up your low end, let somebody else handle that part of the spectrum." The available range is good enough that it can act almost like a performance filter.

DEMO — WURLITZER as MIC PREAMP (SM57 on the speaker → Colour Box): Bypassed first, then Colour Box on — a very healthy, relatively clean signal boost, running a microphone now through quarter-inch effects. It's actually quite high-gain right now just because the 57 isn't very hot. I use the high-pass to get rid of low end, cut a lot of the mids (not too much), and use the treble to get rid of some higher-end buzz, plus cut some really low stuff separate to the high-pass. Relatively high gain but still a pretty clean sound because the 57 isn't very hot. As an EQ and high-quality preamp I really like it — honestly sounding better to me than running the direct out of the Wurlitzer; I might do it this way from now on.

DEMO — LAP STEEL / AMP-SIM PRINT STAGE: A pretty normal pedalboard for me — compressor, Blues Driver, Digidelay, a bit of reverb, UA Dream 65 for an amp sim, volume pedal for dynamics. Since the Dream 65 is emulating an amp and a microphone, I can simulate an additional step: what if that amp-and-mic is running into a mixing board? Add some EQ changes, high-pass, get it crispy — subtle is the way to go. Right on the attack you really notice that direct sound, the "unplugged" sound. Since the MID is so wide, the upper range on the SHIFT is very audible. I could even turn off the amp sim and treat this just like running direct into a mixing board. I'm not using it as an overdrive or using much EQ — just treating it like a final gain stage. If we're emulating the amp and the speaker, why not emulate running into the board at the end as well? I'm liking this better than just running a DI, and I've got an additional gain stage and EQ.
