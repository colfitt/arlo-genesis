# The genuine "direct-into-Neve/EMI" recording history the pedal emulates

This is the documented studio TECHNIQUE — guitar (or DI) straight into a Neve/EMI console channel, overdriving the preamp itself, no amp. The Colour Box recreates *this*, and the history is real and well-attributed (separate from the artist marketing list — see neve-direct-in-artist-vs-technique.md).

## The canonical direct-in records (engineer-verified, not marketing fluff)
Premier Guitar and JHS both cite the same set of documented direct-into-console guitar tones:
- **The Beatles — "Revolution"** (the headline example; "guitar straight into the desk" overdriven preamp). Premier Guitar / Tape Op both say the Colour Box nails it: *"Yep — that's the sound. Right there."*
- **Led Zeppelin — "Black Dog"** (direct-in rhythm guitar).
- **The Byrds — "Mr. Tambourine Man."**
- **Chic — "Le Freak."**
- **Motown** rhythm-section direct tones.
- Plus the broader lineage the pedal's marketing leans on: **Steely Dan, Tom Petty, Nirvana** all feature direct-into-console guitar somewhere.

Premier Guitar frames the method plainly: the **"guitar → direct box → mixing desk"** path became an alternative to amplification for capturing the console color engineers prized.

## What the technique actually sounds like (so the pedal makes sense)
- Clean-ish: transformer-fattened, mid-forward, "recorded" rather than "plugged-in."
- Pushed: Premier Guitar describes the V1's extreme as **"blistering solid-state fuzz of a type we usually avoid,"** with **"anarchic pumping/gulping effects"** at max gain — i.e. genuine *broken-console* distortion, not amp distortion. That ugliness is the point in a doom/shoegaze context.
- The **Lundahl transformer** is the magic ingredient: per JHS it "fattens the lows, adds harmonic complexity to the midrange, and smooths/rounds the highs."

## The Tape Op engineer's-eye take
Tape Op reviewed it as **real outboard**, not a toy: "This thing is worth the price alone just as a DI." They tracked **bass DI** ("a beautiful mid-'70s rock way"), ran a **Beyerdynamic M160 ribbon for live vocals** ("reminiscent of something off Gene Clark's *No Other*"), and pushed **Moog, percussion, even a tabla machine** through it — it "lent a real legitimacy to the sound" of unconventional sources. On tour it solved gain-staging for a dynamic vocalist (no fader-riding). Topology note: JHS says it uses "the same discrete gain stage found in the Neve 1073, but with two gain stages in series instead of one."
