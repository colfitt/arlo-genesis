True Cuckoo (Cuckoo) · OP-1 drumkit tutorial - CUCKOO · 2013-04-29

The definitive "build a drum kit entirely on-box, then sample it into the drum sampler" tutorial by the OP-1's defining educator. This is the ORIGINAL OP-1 (2013), but the workflow is identical on the Field except the Field's sample memory is 20 s (vs ~12 s here) and everything is stereo/32-bit. The technique — synthesize each drum to tape, then resample the whole row into the drum sampler via the EAR — is exactly the rig-relevant approach.

## Core idea
The OP-1 lets you do the entire drum-kit creation in-box — no exporting to a computer. Synthesize each percussion sound, record them in a tight row on tape, then sample that row into the drum sampler and map across the keys.

## Building each sound

KICK:
- PHASE engine. Make the sound very SHORT (a snap firing all frequencies).
- Turn on the NITRO filter (a multi-filter — choose where in the spectrum to resonate). Pull the frequency down to the bass region.
- Switch from poly to UNISON (fires the note across ~6 channels at once = fatter, more audio/frequencies). Result: a nice fat bass drum.
- Record it to tape at a fresh in-point.

SNARE:
- Same PHASE setup / same noise snap; just shift the NITRO FILTER up (the filter is doing most of the work). Tweak to taste. Hold [Shift] while turning for FINE-TUNING.
- Record onto tape directly AFTER the kick.

HI-HAT / SHAKER:
- DIGITAL engine — it has a noise channel good for noisy hat/shaker sounds.
- Play it in the 4TH OCTAVE (high pitch) so the tonality disappears and it reads as a shaker. Keep it SHORT.
- Record tight, right after the previous sound.

PERCUSSION / HAND DRUM (tuned):
- FACE engine — can emulate a hand drum (conga-ish); also good for tuned/melodic percussion. Record a few tuned tones so you can play notes, not just one pitch.

METALLIC / GONG (gamelan-ish, tuned):
- FACE engine again, NOT in unison this time; record a couple of tuned tones for a metallic/gamelan voice.

BASS (tuned):
- CLUSTER engine for a bass voice; record a couple of tones.

EXTRA SNARE / SHAKER from the STRING engine's noise:
- Tip: the source noise matters a lot (digital vs pink vs white changes the result). The STRING synth has a very USEFUL, acoustic-quality noise: put String in UNISON, tighten the strings (bow-like), tune it up — that noise works as a shaker, or filter it (Nitro) into a small snare.

## Critical gotchas while building
- COMPRESSION DOUBLES: the sounds are recorded WITH the current master-level/master-compressor settings, and when later played back through the sampler they hit the compressor AGAIN. So watch master levels — don't compress too hard, or it gets over-snappy / pumped.
- Keep everything TIGHT and packed together on tape, because sample memory is limited (~12 s on the OG; 20 s on the Field). The row used here was ~12 seconds.

## Sampling the row into the drum sampler
1. Before sampling, make sure FADE is OFF.
2. Set the record source to the EAR icon (samples everything happening inside the synth back into itself) — NOT the microphone (that would feed back).
3. Press and HOLD the sample button and play the tape back exactly when you press it, capturing the whole row.
4. Go to [Drum]; the row is now in the sampler. Space the slices out across the keys.

## Mapping and cleaning up slices
- Find the ZERO CROSSING for each slice — the OP-1's low levels aren't dead silent (especially after heavy compression and the Nitro filter, which is noisy), so trimming to zero crossings avoids snaps/clicks.
- Hold [Shift] for fine adjustment of start/end points.
- COPY a slice: hold the KEY + press [Lift] (under the mirror icon). PASTE: hold the key + press [Drop]. Very useful for drums — e.g., copy a slice to another key and REVERSE it.
- Keep the kit DYNAMIC: not everything should be loud — some sounds loud, some tiny — so it feels alive when played.
- "Don't stop being creative once it's in the sampler" — reverse, re-pitch, rearrange slices.

## Takeaway for this rig
This is the canonical method to turn the OP-1's own synth engines (or, by extension, any sampled source like the banjo or the fuzz wall) into a playable, chromatic/percussive drum kit entirely on the device, then chop, reverse, and re-pitch the slices — all before it ever leaves the box.
