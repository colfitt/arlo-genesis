Gavin Vickery · 3 ways to bounce and merge tracks on the OP-1 · 2022-03-30

Three methods to bounce the four tape tracks down to a single track (to free up the other three for melodic elements). Demoed on a pre-made drum loop with EQ/volume already balanced.

## Key prerequisite: the record/sample source icon
On the record (sample) screen there is a source icon: the EAR icon = "record what the OP-1 is playing" (internal resampling / loopback). Make sure it is NOT the purple X. When you intend to resample the OP-1's own output, set it to the EAR. When you DON'T want to accidentally resample, turn the ear OFF.

## Method 1 — Resample down to a tape track (the presenter's favorite, easiest)
1. While in loop mode, hold [Shift]+[Lift] to lift ALL four tracks. IMPORTANT: immediately DROP them back (jump to the next bar and drop) so you don't lose them — this also gives you a backup copy.
2. Choose a destination track (he uses track 4). MUTE that track so it doesn't play back — otherwise you'd double its volume into the resample.
3. Set the record source to the EAR icon (records what the OP-1 plays).
4. Hit [Record] + [Play] to resample. Trim the end.
5. Lift the new bounce, drop a copy, unmute — you now have a single combined track on 4.
- Limitation: no backup of the original separated tracks beyond the temporary lift buffer; you must remember/re-lift the source.

## Method 2 — Lift and sample down to a DRUM track (his second favorite; gives a backup)
1. Make sure the EAR is OFF (so you don't accidentally resample).
2. [Shift]+[Lift] all four tracks; drop a copy back.
3. TRICK for a clean break/loop point: extend the loop OUT point into an EMPTY bar so the lifted audio includes a blank tail. (When you drop a sample shorter than the slot length, it includes old sample data — the blank bar makes the loop end easy to find.)
4. Re-lift with that empty bar included.
5. Go to a DRUM track / sample slot (reset/clear the slot first if it already has a sample, e.g. to "acetate"), make sure you're on the FIRST key, and DROP. The blank tail marks where it ends — leave a small gap for an easy loop point.
6. Set it to one-shot mode and play it back. (Watch out for leftover effects/filter/LFO or PATTERN mode being on — a common gotcha that makes it sound wrong.)
- VOLUME NOTE: the drum-sampled bounce comes out noticeably QUIETER than the Method-1 tape resample. Fix: hold [Shift] so the orange knob becomes volume, and bump the SAMPLE VOLUME by about +8 to match.
- BACKUP: hold down the sample pad and it shows "saving" — this backs up the sample, so even if you overwrite the slot you can recover the original.
- Limitation: sample length cap (~16 seconds, he says "14 or 16, I think 16").

## Method 3 — Album / record (good for very long bounces, but fiddly timing)
1. Hit the [Com] button to reach the album/record. Record what the OP-1 plays back.
2. IMPORTANT: turn DOWN your overdrive/compressor before resampling this way, because it stacks (you record an already-compressed waveform then resample again, making it louder). (Same caution applies to Method 1/2 resampling.)
3. Give yourself ~1 second, hit play, then record to the album.
4. To get it back onto a tape track: set the OP-1 to record itself (EAR on), start recording, then play back the album.
5. Timing is the hard part — you must scrub to find where the loop started (watch for the waveform / time signature), cut there, lift the blank head, drop on a bar boundary, count the bars (e.g., two bars), cut, lift the tail.
- Best for long material (e.g., a minute of chord progression or ambience). Not great for drums because aligning the timing is very hard.

## Summary of trade-offs
- Method 1 (resample to tape track): easiest, most common; no easy backup.
- Method 2 (lift → drum sampler): ~16 s limit, but lets you drop the bounce anywhere on the tape and gives a pseudo-backup of the sample.
- Method 3 (album): handles long samples/ambience; timing alignment is painful.
