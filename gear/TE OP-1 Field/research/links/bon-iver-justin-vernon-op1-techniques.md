# Justin Vernon / Bon Iver — the canonical OP-1 user, concrete techniques

The OP-1 is the documented creative kickstarter for Bon Iver's *22, A Million* (2016). This is the original OP-1, not the Field, but the philosophy and techniques transfer directly. The honest takeaway: Vernon used it as a **fast capture-and-mangle sketchpad** — exactly the rig role in this studio.

## Origin / mindset
- Vernon discovered it during a low period in Greece, sang an improvisation into it, and when he **chopped the sample it sounded like "two, two"** → the album's "22" theme. Quote: "Many of the moments on the record came from using the OP-1, which samples a bit like the Casio SK-1."
- Why it changed the writing: "You can sit anywhere and sample the radio, your breathing, whatever, and make a song out of it." And: "Once I had enough of that going on, it wasn't really obvious any more to pick up an acoustic guitar as often." It pushed him off guitar-based songwriting.

## Concrete techniques (the useful part)
- **Radio sampling.** The album literally samples FM radio captured on the OP-1 — a muffled station callsign from **105.7 FM WCFW (Chippewa Falls, WI)** is layered into the vocal manipulations. (The Field has an FM RX/TX radio built in — same trick, native.)
- **Sample a percussive gesture, then play it chromatically.** On **"33 'GOD'"**, percussionist **Joe Westerlund "took an OP-1 and sampled the sound of scraping the tip of a stick along a cymbal, which gets it to sing. He then played that at different pitches."** → i.e. one weird one-shot into the sampler engine, played melodically across the keys. This is exactly the "banjo-roll-into-Drum-Sampler" move in this rig's workflows.
- **Sample a choir and varispeed it.** On "33 'GOD'", **"the reverb wash at the end was a sampled choir from The Staves, played and sped up in the OP1."** → record a vocal/choir sample, speed it up on tape/sampler for the texture.
- **"FindGod is a sample that came in from the OP1"** — whole song-seed sounds originated as OP-1 sketches that the team then built around.
- **Build around "cruddy-sounding samples."** Engineer Andrew Fitzpatrick used the OP-1 in his "The Box" setup for "cool things… weird little glitschy things" deliberately contrasted against the organic instrumentation: "one of many things that take you out of the organic nature of everything for a moment." The lo-fi character is the point, not a flaw.

## Rig-relevant translation
This is the blueprint for the OP-1's role here: sample the banjo/baritone/fuzz wall or even ambient/radio, chop it, play it chromatically as a "singing" one-shot, speed/slow it for texture, and let its crudded-up character be a deliberate counterweight to the cleaner sources — then hand the sketch off to the bigger boxes.
