## Architecture / character insights (highest signal)

- The defining, oft-missed point about what makes Big Time *different* from any other delay, paraphrasing a poster: **"very few of us have a limiter and bias controls in the delay feedback loop."** This is exactly Big Time's hook — the analog limiter + misbias (STATE) operating *inside* the feedback loop, not just at the input. This is the feature to lean on; ordinary delays don't have it.

- A vivid description of the official CB demo's best moment (user "cogsy", May 1): "In the main Chase Bliss demo, they had a sound where they **cranked the feedback and drive, so that the limiter chewed up the sound with every repeat, resulting in these beautiful drop-outs. It sounded like a recording on dissolving tape in a failing tape deck.** It sounded crazy good." — This is precisely the rig's degrade-the-repeats aesthetic; the recipe is high FEEDBACK + high COLOR so the limiter "eats" each repeat.

- OTO BIM is repeatedly named as the closest architectural cousin. "Flipsos" (May 1): **"the OTO BIM ... from a hardware perspective is the closest to the Big Time with all the analog components mixed with a digital delay path."** "chungapalm": **"Bim is the same architecture, analog preamp into a digital delay."** Useful mental model: Big Time ≈ a BIM-style analog-front/digital-core delay, but with the EAE limiter/bias in the loop.

## Usage / workflow opinions

- On what to FEED it: "Azzarole" (Apr 30) argued Big Time has **"way too much (analog) character to be fed with a characterful (analog) synth"** — an open saw into the drive "sounded awful." His takeaway: it shines fed something **sterile/tame (e.g. an FM synth) that the pedal then colors**, rather than an already-saturated source. (Relevance to this rig: Big Time sits AFTER Generation Loss, i.e. an already-degraded source — so keep COLOR modest, consistent with the dossier's §5 warning about double-saturation.)
- Counterpoint from "GovernorSilver": good input sources = EHX POG3, DAW stems "not trying to sound like analog synths," and acoustic-y/electric-piano patches (Osmose), i.e. cleaner sources.
- General CB philosophy (LyingDalai, May 1, widely liked): **"with every Chase Bliss pedal: given what they do, you'd better be minimal enough so that you have a chance to let them shine. When I jam with friends, the subtle textures are often lost in the mix."** Feed one source, not a wall.
- "Azzarole" agrees: send ONE element to the CB chain and record that, rather than slamming everything through it — "would be complete overkill sonically and also start to sound samey."
- Routing opinion ("oldbills"): **"best served as sort of an insert/direct/buss situation and not as an aux send on a mixer."** (LyingDalai partly disagrees — depends if you have downstream pedals to refocus the parallel chain.)

## Format / UX gotchas (no screen)

- "tendingtropic": **"Marks video is great. but it also shows how this is a complicated pedal. for instance how you change the divisions, seems like quite a hassle; and they are not labeled on the pedal."** — i.e. the no-screen Automatone format makes deep editing (e.g. setting delay divisions) fiddly and unlabeled. Echoes the GPX critique in the dossier.
- "Otto-Otosan" (positive on format): **"the motorized faders are great compared to something like the Lost & Found when it comes to navigating secondary parameters."** All the options "make the pedal seem more worth the price tag... than the cxm." First "big box" CBA he's tempted by.

## Reception (honest)

Mixed-to-skeptical. The #!&% (misbias) mode drew the most criticism — "Azzarole": **"the #%$? mode sounded like unuseable mess in every demo"**; "square_wave" joked back **"I mean, to be fair, it is called #%$?"**. Several posters were unimpressed by the demos ("regretfully not impressed," "Meh"). Heavy price/value debate (a poster built a chart pricing other gear in "Big Time price units" at €1500-ish street). Pre-release skepticism, not a verdict on the pedal in use.
