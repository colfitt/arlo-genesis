---

Hello and welcome to our walk-through on Big Time. Big Time is a delay — and it's a lot of other things, too.

Why did we love the sound of 80s rack technology so much? All the ways to store sound and then pick it back up later to get a delay didn't work very well, and so the digital circuitry needed some help in the form of preamps and compression and limiting and filters. So we took a lot of those ideas and we remixed them with various lessons that we've learned over the years making cool gear, and we put it all into this thing.

**The input architecture.** At the input of the delay line there is a 4x2 matrix mixer. That matrix mixer is voltage controlled — the same technology that's used in Cleen [EAE Clean]. So you can get compression, you can get all kinds of fun automations. Then we extended it by taking a little bit of the soul of the Halberd [EAE Halberd], and then we added a variable bias circuit to that, too. So you can kind of crunch it up in all sorts of strange ways. You can make it sound kind of rich and asymmetrical, or you can make it sound broken. The interplay between all those has led to a lot of really fun places that we didn't expect.

**Signal trajectory — COLOR / FEEDBACK.** When you're thinking about the trajectory of sound traveling through this pedal: COLOR is going to set how hot your input signal is and how hard it's initially interacting with whatever your compression / limiting / saturation mode (STATE) is. Then FEEDBACK is going to say how hard it hits those things over time. [Demo: brighten up, turn FEEDBACK up — you hear the compression being used to hold it all together.]

**The misbias / broken sound.** It's almost like when the bias fully cuts off and fully gates out the signal, it's like you're burning a hole in the loop — which is very evocative for what this pedal is trying to do.

**As a standalone tone / dirt pedal.** All the classic delays we know and love have this sonic character or footprint even outside of the delay. I love that it makes your dry signal sound good, too. You can use this thing as a dirt pedal if you want to. That preamp sound is stuck at the front of everything — it gives you saturation character, color, a bit of volume boost, and that volume boost is what interacts with the stuff downstream. The preamp is fully stereo — there are two of them, very well matched. There's also a +12dB switch; kick it on and you can get a lot of juice out of this thing. We spent a lot of time making sure this preamp sounds good on guitar, bass, synthesizers, drum machines, everything — it's not just guitar dirt, it's more like a studio saturator circuit.

**Delay ranges / MODE.** We have different delay ranges. In the "off" (MOD) state you'll be in the modulation delay ranges, which can get very, very short. You can have almost all this stuff in real time.

**VOICING vs TILT EQ.** The VOICING selects a filter for everything; TILT EQ selects a filter for your repeats.

**SCALE.** [In response to "how are you doing that?"] Scale quantizes the delay time to musical intervals.

**MOTION + the Alt menu.** The way we set up modulation invites a discussion about the alt menu we've been hopping into continuously: at any time you hold the SHIFT button and you enter the alt menu — indicated by the flashing "A" — and all the sliders jump to whatever the alt-panel settings are. The RATE and DEPTH for the modulation live there.

**CLUSTER.** The cluster slider has a few zones: it'll add one head, then a few more heads, and then it'll start to modulate and diffuse those into something more washy. Give it something sparse and slowly turn up CLUSTER to hear that effect.

**The looper.** When you're overdubbing, the whole pedal is active as it would be in a normal delay mode. So if FEEDBACK is low, things fade out a lot like repeats on Blooper. Everything in here is doing its job when you're in overdub. But then you can always just come out and it uses DSP feedback — it locks it in place and it will no longer degrade. It'll just hold it forever.

**0.5X mode.** 0.5X reduces all the clock speed by half, so you get twice as much delay time and half as much bandwidth. It also reduces the bit depth of the delay so you get some of those nice artifacts as well. In this mode we're more faithfully trying to get at some of the digital flaws of the units that inspired it.

**Closing.** Big Time can do a lot of stuff and we did our best to anticipate all the different use cases — to cover those bases we have a deeper configuration menu. We hope we got you started in the right direction and you can continue to explore with the materials we provide. Hope you have a Big Time.
