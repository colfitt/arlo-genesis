That Pedal Show (Dan Steinhardt / Mick Taylor) · Brothers AM vs Prince/Duke of Tone · 2025-04-25

Player-shared settings and A/B observations from That Pedal Show's deep King-of-Tone-family comparison. Dan owns an Oct-2007 King of Tone and uses it as a gig staple, so his "favorite setting" is a real, road-tested reference.

## Dan's favorite King of Tone / Brothers AM setting (quoted)
- "I've always used **side one and side two together — OVERDRIVE on side one, BOOST on side two.** I just use the boost to add a bit more harmonics." Kicking side two in: "you hear the mids — it's not a massive mid jump, but it just lifts a little." Summed up as **"overdrive boost."**
- Caveat he flags: expect "huge level changes as you go from distortion / overdrive / boost in each side because the nature of the clipping is fundamentally different — distortion will naturally be louder [quieter, in fact]. So dial that into your presets."

## The presence reveal (quoted, important)
- They started with **presence at zero (down)** — sounded "more closed-in, a bit more mid-focused, chewy." Then Dan "brought the presence up quite considerably on the Brothers AM — that changed the game totally... as soon as you open that presence up, it really opened out." (Mick noted Dan probably went a touch too far, but the point stands: presence-up is the fast fix for a closed/chewy tone.)
- Mick's own preference: "I always have mine at minimum [presence] because I don't like the extra presence." So: down for thicker/mid-focus, up for open/clear — taste, easily accessible here vs internal trim on the real KOT.

## Treble booster usage (quoted)
- "A treble booster into a clean amp can be quite an unpleasant thing. But where it really comes into its own is where you use it **into a pedal that's already overdriven** — that will take you to your really vocal sound" (Mick Ronson / Brian May territory).
- Two positions on the AM booster: a **brighter** position (up) and a **less bright** position; **middle = off = the original/classic King of Tone with no Rangemaster.** The "less bright" voice has "a lot more low mids."

## Boost-stacking another drive (quoted)
- Famous KOT trick: use the **BOOST circuit after another overdrive.** They set a Bluesbreaker dirty, then put the AM's boost after it: "the boost after the Bluesbreaker just lifts it and gives it all the love — it has that frequency that's going to get you heard, 100%." Note "there's more bottom end in the Brothers than in the Prince of Tone."

## Voicing notes (quoted)
- "What it doesn't have at all is anything in the way of scoopy mids — it's always nicely mid-present. It's a thing where you'll be heard, in a big way."
- Mick's verdict: prefers the Brothers AM to the King of Tone for "the range of sounds... the way it's EQ'd, where the mids hit." Six modes (3 per side × 2) vs three on a Prince/Duke = "colossal" scope.

## External control (quoted)
- Assign expression to any knob (or all at once) via the right dip bank, with settable min/max. "You can have the gain control of side one and control the master of side two — so you can clean up: turn the gain down but adjust the volume of side two."
- The **MIDI jack doubles as an external footswitch input** to bypass Channel 2: plug a normally-open momentary footswitch with a TS (mono) cable and it takes control automatically — handy for burying side two's switching elsewhere on a board.
- Treble booster can be toggled via MIDI as part of a saved preset (not via a latching expression pedal).
