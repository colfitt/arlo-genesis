# Premier Guitar review — treble booster + high-gain caveat

## Concrete tips distilled
- **Treble booster = "addictive, dynamite."** Especially valuable at **low/bedroom volumes** — delivers "full-bodied, sparkling sounds" off a 35-watt amp while leaving room for acoustic instruments and vocals. (In this rig: the same upper-mid lift is the banjo-cut recovery tool.)
- **High-gain compression caveat:** "heavily colored compression at the highest gain levels." Saufley's own workaround: you don't need it — "you don't need maximum gain to make your tone as hot as the hinges of Hades." Practical takeaway: keep GAIN modest; the pedal's sweet spot is below max, and max-gain compounds with an already-compressed Muff signal upstream.
- **Touch response / low-volume strength:** "range and interactivity between the tone and gain controls enables hot, lively tones and touch-responsive playing dynamics" — excels at bedroom/home-studio levels where many ODs fail. Cleans up well with picking/volume rolls in BOOST/OD.
- **Voicing vs. Tube Screamer:** "less cramped in the midrange" than vintage TS9 — deliberately *not* mid-honky. This is why it works as a transparent shaper after a mid-forward Muff rather than stacking another mid hump.
- **Stacking range:** dual-circuit design moves "from very subtle to very vicious" by configuring each circuit independently.

## Rig relevance
Confirms the dossier's core thesis: use it as a low-gain transparent tightener (sweet spot below max gain), and lean on the treble booster for cut. The "heavily colored compression" caveat is the explicit reason to avoid DIST + HI-GAIN stacked on top of the Hizumitas.
