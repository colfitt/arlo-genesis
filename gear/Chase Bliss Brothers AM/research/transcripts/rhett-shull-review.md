So this is Brothers AM, a collaboration between Chase Bliss and Analog Man. Essentially what we have here is the famous Analog Man King of Tone in a Chase Bliss box with a few extra features, a few extra controls here and there. This is the collaboration I did not see coming, but after spending a few days with it, I actually think it makes a lot of sense. What I want to get down to in this video is: what is the big deal with the King of Tone? Players will wait years and years on a wait list to get one, or pay astronomical prices on the used market. So why that pedal specifically?

[Chase Bliss did send this pedal for free; not sponsored, not requested.] Josh Scott (JHS) joins to discuss the King of Tone. Analog Man dropped it around 2003. It's a variant modification to the Marshall Bluesbreaker — no one had ever done that. This is before John Mayer, back in the day, and it was an alternative to the Tube Screamer in the big boutique era. The most interesting thing about the King of Tone is how it has not changed. The way Mike does it has not changed. He has a principle there — he enjoys building them a certain way and he's stuck to it. If you want to go get one, the wait list is almost eight years now. How many people have got one and sold it, because the love of it is far outweighed by the worth of it? It gets into this weird category — scarcity driving supply and demand. The King of Tone is the king of scarcity. Scarcity drives FOMO, and FOMO makes people feel they don't have something that would fix their life, and then they go spend absurd money. It's Pappy Van Winkle, it's Leica, it's Rolex. It is an overdrive pedal — it's wonderful, but it's an overdrive pedal. You skip the line and use money to feed the FOMO. That's where we get "blues lawyers" — lawyers have money and they skip the line.

How much does the aesthetic of Analog Man play into this — they're still doing it the exact same way, the hand-stamped enclosures, the surface-mount components. He has stayed his course so well. He did the MXR version (Duke of Tone) to his specs, but no one believes it's the same — they're not going to believe it because the FOMO is actually what people want to buy.

What do I think about the Chase Bliss one and what it'll do to the secondary market? I don't think it'll affect that wait list at all. Chase Bliss customers versus Analog Man customers are a different world — there's a tiny Venn-diagram overlap (the big-pedalboard ambient players who'll have a King of Tone occasionally), but for the most part you're in two different pools of customer, which means scarcity still exists in the original.

What's funny is that this is kind of the least Chase-Blissy Chase Bliss pedal to exist. Yes, it has the dip switches, but the dip switches are basically taking all of the internal controls on the real King of Tone and putting them on the outside, which is objectively better in my opinion. It doesn't do any of the typical Chase Bliss LFOs and modulating and controlling things — there's a little bit of that under the hood, but for the most part it's a Chase Bliss dual-stage overdrive pedal.

This pedal has survived the entire climate change of guitar with zero changes as a stubborn, countercultural artifact. I've never owned an Analog Man pedal, and quite frankly I don't really understand the King of Tone hype — it's a fantastic pedal, but I don't think it's worth the eight-year wait or paying $700–$1,000 on the used market. So the fact that the Brothers AM exists is really cool. It makes improvements on the original in form factor — this is more pedalboard-friendly while still being usable. If you're typically not a Chase Bliss fan because of all the dip switches, you really don't have to worry, because there's nothing really weird happening here — it's all pretty straight-ahead controls that normally you'd find on the inside of a pedal like the King of Tone, just put on the outside. Plus the addition of the treble boost circuit with its two settings means you've got kind of three pedals in one.

If, like me, you're not really into the lore of the King of Tone but you want something that is undeniably great on your board, I think this is a really good option. It's a pedal that can meet you where you are. If you want something simple — just a dual-stage overdrive, or boost, or distortion — it can be that. But for more adventurous or advanced players using expression control or CV control, you've got options for that as well; you can patch it into a modular or synthesizer setup and add LFOs, gates, and triggers to do all kinds of interesting stuff with a really beautiful classic analog overdrive. I think this is a beautifully done collaboration. Analog Man created one of the best-sounding, most sought-after overdrives ever, and Chase Bliss does uniqueness, control, great form factor. Brothers AM — I dig it.
