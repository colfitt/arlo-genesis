Hi, it's Rabea — we're nearing the end of the Big Sky demonstrations. For this video we're looking at the **Shimmer reverb**.

The Shimmer reverb is basically **two tunable voices**. The most common shimmer verb people know (e.g. the EHX/Eventide-style) just gives you a root and a fifth, but what's really sweet about the Big Sky Shimmer is you can **tune the pitches of both voices** — so you can create your own harmonies and intervals. Depending on the section of the song you're writing, you could set up a fifth and use the **Freeze function to hold it as a fifth drone** — it's great. The general reverb sound is warm and filling.

**Parameter mapping for Shimmer** (everything starting at 12:00): Param 1 = one voice (on one side of the dry signal). Scrolling Param 1 moves through the intervals — minor 2nd, major 2nd, minor 3rd, major 3rd, tritone, up to an octave, octave + 5th, two octaves above; and you can go down an octave too. That's a big range. I set voice 1 to an octave down; voice 2 (Param 2) I bumped up to an octave-and-a-fifth.

Inside the parameter menu: **Amount** = how much of the shifted voices is dialed into the signal (I put it on full). **Mode** = Input / Regeneration / Input+Regeneration. Regeneration regenerates the initial shimmer and tries to find the harmonies you're playing, so it sounds like a synth with a pitch bend. For this demo I left it on **Input** so you can hear the exact characteristics of the reverb.

One thing to bear in mind: because you set fixed harmonies in the pedal, you have to be specific about the part you play — otherwise it can sound pretty horrible (unless you *want* weird and twisted). It's a very specific tool, a real asset, and great in post-production for adding extra layers and harmonies behind something you've already written.
