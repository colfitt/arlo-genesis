## Global config first (the boring-but-mandatory step)
Hold the **Shift/Subdivision button + the Phrase Looper button** for a few seconds to enter global configuration. The lights animate. To change a setting: **wiggle a knob** to jump to that option, then **scroll the selector dial** through its choices. Two inputs matter:
- **Activity knob = mono/stereo input.** One light (left) = mono, two lights (right) = stereo. (Jorb runs the mono Minilogue, so he leaves it mono — for a stereo rig you'd pick the two-light option.)
- **Mix knob = instrument vs. line level.** Leftmost LED flashing = instrument level (guitar/EP pickups); one to the right = line level (synths). Pick the right one or you fight the Mix knob constantly.
Hit **Bypass** to exit back to where you were.

## How modes are organized
Four categories — Micro Loop, Granules, Glitch, Multi-Delay — each containing multiple effects (e.g. Micro Loop = Mosaic, Seq, Glide), and each effect has four modes shown by which of the four LEDs is lit (A/B/C/D).

**Critical mental model:** A/B/C/D are NOT presets (same sound, different knob settings). Your **knob positions persist as you scroll through modes** (until you hit the User preset bank). A/B/C/D are genuinely different *behaviors* of the effect — different octaves on the micro-loops, etc. And the *effects* differ from each other far more than the A/B/C/D modes within one effect. So: think of A/B/C/D as "modes for an effect," and expect huge jumps between effects.

## The knobs in three sections

### Section 1 — the three left knobs (Activity, Time, Repeats): "what the effect is doing"
Demonstrated using **Pattern A in Multi-Delay** because it's a plain delay:
- **Time** = the frequency/rate of the effect. Speeds up / slows down the delay. Can be subdivisions of tap tempo, OR (when the light is on the right side) a free-running tempo. **Can sync to MIDI clock** (Jorb does this, then switches it back to subdivision for the demo).
- **Repeats** = think of it like feedback. In any mode, anything that gets caught in the effect/buffer/loop will repeat longer and fade out over time.
- **Activity** = "more of whatever the effect is doing." Generic by design. Example in Mosaic D with Repeats middling and Activity at zero: turn Activity up and you get more loops, specifically more of the pitch-shifted ones.
- **Don't forget REVERSE:** press in on the mode-selector knob to reverse the entire buffer. Dramatically changes the sound; very easy to forget it exists.

### Section 2 — Shape, Filter, Space: "getting what you cooked up to blend with the dry signal"
- **Shape** = the amp envelope of the micro-loops/grains as they come in. It's effectively a **4-way switch, not a continuous knob** (Jorb wishes it were continuous). The four shapes: (1) abrupt-in, fade-out like a **sawtooth**; (2) **triangle** — fade in and fade out; (3) **ramp** — ramp up then fade away; (4) **square** — in and out abruptly (with a little release/attack). Has a substantial effect on how sounds blend.
- **Filter** = low-pass. Fully right = open. Can fully close off the effect, or slowly take out high end so the effect sits underneath the dry signal.
- **Space** = reverb. Increasing it raises the reverb mix; fully right = only the reverb signal. Combined with the Filter you get a "moving, shifting ambient wash."

### Section 3 — Mix and Loop Level (far right): mixing the three sources
- **Mix:** left of center = dry signal, right of center = affected signal. (Loop Level below = the built-in looper's playback volume.)

## How to explore an unfamiliar mode (Jorb's method, demonstrated live on WARP)
Starting point he uses: **Mix ~50/50, the three "effect" knobs (Activity/Time/Repeats) all at noon, Shape fully right, Filter fully right (open), Space fully left (no reverb)** — so whatever grain/delay starts abruptly and you can hear it clearly. Then:
1. **Play, and listen.** Don't just change pedal settings — change what you put INTO it. He found Warp wanted live playing, not a sequence, and wanted free tempo (he pulled the MIDI clock out for it).
2. **Freely move between A/B/C/D** — you understand what's changing by hearing the differences, and may find a mode that connects. (Warp: A = envelope-controlled filter per tap, B = resonant bandpass, C = adds pitch shifting to taps.)
3. **Grab the manual** and read the one-line description for the mode you're in.
4. **Watch the four LEDs** — they give cryptic but real feedback (e.g. orange while delays are active; the taps showed orange / lighter white / very light as they faded).

## Closing thesis
If you just want "spacey/ambient," a good reverb might serve you better — the Microcosm's controls and modes are idiosyncratic. It earns its place if (a) you specifically want granular AS A PEDAL effect, or (b) you want to *explore* and be surprised by what comes back given what you play in. "It is an explorer's pedal." How to actually use it: understand the controls, grab the manual, and explore.
