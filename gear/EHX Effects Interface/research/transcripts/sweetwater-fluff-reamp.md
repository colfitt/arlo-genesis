Sweetwater Soundcheck · Electro-Harmonix Effects Interface Hardware Plug-in Overview ft. Fluff · 2026-01-13

Ryan "Fluff" Bruce walks through the Effects Interface on Sweetwater's channel, focused on the re-amp (Hardware Plugin) workflow in Pro Tools.

WHAT IT IS. A hardware unit that can act as an audio interface (light, travel, USB-C power), OR a bridge to use audio plugins in your DAW with your pedalboard (Pedalboard Mode), OR the inverse — use beloved hardware pedals in the context of your DAW (Hardware Plugin Mode), which is the focus here. It is a STEREO unit: two sets of sliders (output side and input side), stereo I/O, a headphone out for direct monitoring, and USB-C connectivity that also feeds power. If you're not using a computer, use the included power adapter. Part two is the included audio plugin, which works in "any recording software — Logic, Pro Tools, Reaper, Cubase, all of them," and is what lets you use the hardware in the context of the DAW.

PHYSICAL CONNECTIONS for the re-amp loop. Powered by USB-C (no separate power needed). Two pedals hooked up: an EVH 5150 chorus and a vintage 1980 Boss PH-1 phaser. Signal chain: mono out via 1/4" patch from the Effects Interface OUTPUT → input of the phaser → out of phaser → into the EVH 5150 chorus → out of chorus → back into the Effects Interface INPUT. "This is just a mono loop, as if it were in the effects loop of an actual guitar amp." (Demonstrates the basic OUTPUT → pedals → INPUT insert loop.)

IN THE DAW. The accompanying plugin is inserted; the foot control button turns it on/off. Settings → device menu → select MONO LEFT to tell it you're in mono. "Full control over basically everything." Crucially: in the hardware/Device Options menu you set the BUFFER. "I typically am running a very, very small buffer." Be mindful that converting analog↔digital introduces a little latency. On a fast system he reports a ROUND-TRIP DELAY OF ABOUT 30 SAMPLES — "very, very low latency," not a concern in the context of a mix.

KEY ADVANTAGE over patching a pedal normally — the WET/DRY MIX. A Boss PH-1 hooked up normally is only on or off. Run through the Effects Interface, anything has a wet/dry mix knob, so you can dial an effect back just a little. "That has a lot of implications sonically… huge implications for synth and keyboards and modular stuff, especially in stereo." (Even though his demo is a simplified mono setup.)

RE-AMP WORKFLOW (the payoff). On the LEFT DI track for a melody guitar there are two DI tracks (left and right) feeding a '77 Marshall JMP. "We're going to reamp like normal, however we have this effects insert on the outgoing DI" — i.e., the Effects Interface loop is added onto the outgoing DI signal so the real pedals process it on the way to the amp/return. Engages the insert (no audible change until pedals are on). Tries the Boss PH-1 first (too subtle a churn), moves to the 5150 chorus (slightly more than wanted), then uses the MIX knob to fine-tune the wet amount BEFORE committing/printing. Records (re-amps) the part.

DOUBLE-TRACK / STEREO TIP. To match the other side, he drags the effect insert down to the right-side DI, solos it, arms the applicable return track, and runs it again — keeping the two sides identical. In context the result adds "just a little bit of coloration to give it some cut in the context of a mix."

Takeaway emphasis: the value is the per-track wet/dry blend and recallable plugin control over real outboard pedals, with negligible latency on a fast system.
