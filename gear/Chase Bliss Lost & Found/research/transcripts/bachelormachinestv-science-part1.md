This is a Chase Bliss Lost and Found pedal — what they refer to as a "collection of curiosities." It is a multi-effect pedal: a pedal that lets you choose from a bunch of different effects you can mix and match. Historically multi-effects pedals are a one-stop "pedalboard in a box." Lost and Found is kind of that and kind of isn't. It has 12 different FX algorithms; some are bread-and-butter (a delay, a chorus) but most are weird, uncommon stuff. The point seems not to be a jack-of-all-trades box so much as a chemistry set — you combine two chemicals and see what happens.

The pedal has two positions, a left side and a right side, and each can contain any of the 12 algorithms. The two algorithms can run in parallel, in series (either running into the other), or a blend of both. There's also another algorithm sitting at the end of the chain called GLUE — a compressor/saturator. So the pedal makes 12 × 12 = 144 FX combinations (not counting series/parallel or adding glue). One minute per combination would be a 2h24m video, so this video instead investigates each algorithm: what it does, its settings, solo, plus a couple of combinations.

## Interface
Six knobs, two footswitches, four toggle switches, two indicator lights. The bottom toggle selects presets (saved for the end). Two FX slots, left and right. Turn a slot on/off by stomping its footswitch. You select that slot's FX *category* with the slot's toggle (six categories, two effects each). You select *which* of the two effects in the category using the MODIFY knob:
- **MODIFY at noon = NEITHER effect (no effect).** Left of noon = first effect; right of noon = second effect. The farther from noon, the higher the MODIFY value for that effect.

Each effect has four controls: **MODIFY, TIME, ALT, EQ.**
- **MODIFY** — also selects the A/B effect; what it does past selection varies per effect.
- **TIME** — upper knob; every L+F effect has a time-based component (less time → more time). Meaning varies per effect.
- **ALT** — secondary control: hold BOTH footswitches and turn the upper knob for that slot. Function varies per effect.
- **EQ** — hold BOTH footswitches and turn the bottom knob. EQ is a non-resonant filter: filters out HIGHS as you go LEFT of noon, filters out LOWS as you go RIGHT of noon. (Very similar to the Chase Bliss Clean pedal's EQ, except this is digital and has no LFO/tremolo on it.)

The toggle has only three positions (three categories per side) — so how do you reach all six categories on one slot? **Dip switches on the back.** There's a SPREAD control (mono→stereo, left ON for the whole video). There are two SWAP switches: **SWAP L** makes the left slot use the algorithms normally on the right; **SWAP R** makes the right slot use the algorithms normally on the left. So you can access any algorithm from either slot, and even run the same algorithm on both sides. The two middle knobs control how the slots mix together (covered when he reaches the second slot).

## Category 1 — REVERB: Slow-verb (L) / Useful Ambience (R)
Toggle position 1 = reverb. MODIFY left = Slow-verb, right = Useful Ambience.

**Slow-verb** — shoegaze-inspired (manual); seems based in part on the reverse reverbs of the Yamaha SPX90 and Alesis MidiVerb 2 (80s reverbs used on classic shoegaze records). Both Slow-verb and Useful Ambience are a **delay component followed by a diffusion network** (a network of all-pass filters / feedback delay lines that mixes out-of-phase versions of the delayed signal back in, smearing the sound). Knob mapping for Slow-verb:
- **MODIFY = feedback of the delay before the diffusion network.**
- **TIME = the delay time of that delay.**
- **ALT = relative levels in the diffusion network (i.e. diffusion amount).**

Demo findings: with diffusion (ALT) at zero you just hear a single delay; raising MODIFY adds feedback (max MODIFY = infinite delay loop). Raising the diffusion network turns up the gain of the echo and smears it. Distinctive trait: that **second/diffused delay is LOUDER than the dry signal** even with MIX turned fairly down — so you get a louder-than-dry, diffused wash. It DOES shoegaze: nice viby delay even without diffusion, lush with diffusion up. Use the EQ to remove mud. **Slow-verb syncs to MIDI clock** great as a delay; once you add diffusion the hit-point shifts so it sounds less strictly in sync, but you can still play with TIME for fun.

**Useful Ambience** — no insider info, but it sounds a lot like the reverb on Chase Bliss's own **MOOD Mk2**. Where Slow-verb is one big delay you smear, Useful Ambience is a **multitap delay** (a bunch of little delays at different times) smeared by the diffusion network. Same control mapping as Slow-verb: **MODIFY = feedback in the delay loop, TIME = duration of the delay loop, ALT = strength of the diffusion network.** With diffusion down it's clearly a stereo multitap; with diffusion up it's a nice regular reverb. Turn SIZE (TIME) all the way DOWN and it's a great naturalistic **room reverb** ("useful ambience" in the room-tone sense) — turns a dead clean guitar into something recorded in a nice stereo room. Like MOOD, at very long delay times it gets a hairy/aliased lo-fi quality in the tails (low-quality-clock character, sounds nice on guitars and synths). With feedback maxed it does an interesting thing: when it hears a new loud chord that crosses a threshold and is long enough, **it ditches its existing delay buffer and starts over** so chromatically-clashing chords don't pile into mud. **MIDI clock caveat:** Useful Ambience has NO useful musical relationship to clock — its multitap delays are deliberately not musically related (for a more random reverb), so they can't beat-sync. Also, with clock attached the shortest delay gets much longer, so you LOSE the really short ambient/room reverb. Watch out for that.

## Category 2 — PITCH: Orchestral Swell (L) / Pitch Repeater (R)
**Orchestral Swell** — seems based on the synth patches in the Digitech XP300 Space Station ('90s). A delay feedback loop (like the reverbs) but the delayed signals go through a **pitch shifter** that (1) shifts pitch by a user-selectable amount — down one octave, unison, up a 4th, up a 5th, or up an octave — and (2) applies a slow LFO that varies the pitch slightly over time, creating a chorus when blended with the input. Control mapping (NOT the same as reverb):
- **MODIFY = pitch-shift amount (oct down / unison / 4th / 5th / oct up).**
- **ALT = feedback amount.**
- **TIME = swell time** — an attack envelope for the delay line (NOT the feedback network). If swell time is high you can play one chord, let it swell, then play another and it changes pitch to the new chord right away; but if feedback is up, the feedback network won't ditch the old signal and you'll hear both chords blended.

Demo: at unison with wet up you see the partials waver from the chorus LFO. Up a 4th adds the 4th; up a 5th; octave up = shimmer-reverb-like; octave down adds a strong sub. (He notes it appears to pitch-shift again on the feedback, so a "4th" actually hides a 9th too, etc.) The swell removes attack then brings in the orchestral swell — best heard on a short impulsive electric-piano sound. Presenter's taste: prefers unison + octave-down over octave-up. **Syncs to MIDI clock** (surprisingly yes); with feedback and swell-time down it becomes like an auto-quantized tremolo/twinkle.

**Pitch Repeater** — "what you get when the SLIP algorithm from MOOD's wet side meets the multitap delay from a Stereo Memory Man with Hazarai and they start a family." It's a delay with a single repeat you can pitch-shift (the MOOD-SLIP part). **MODIFY = pitch shift, TIME = delay time.** With MIDI clock present, **TIME selects a multiple of the clock: noon = a beat, left = subdivisions of a beat, right = multiples of a beat.** The Memory-Man part: **ALT divides the delay into multiple taps** — sounds a lot like an early Electribe delay, including the classic Electribe move of sweeping the TIME knob for sequence-like effects. (Ramping and MIDI/expression control could automate this but are out of scope.) Demo (clock left on throughout): you get two repeats not one because SPREAD is on (left + right). ALT up chops the repeat into finer taps; TIME changes the subdivision (quarter → eighth, faster, etc.); MODIFY pitches it (octave down sounds cool with GLUE on; up a 5th gives an airy quality; chopped = glassier).

## Category 3 — WARP: Pinging Phaser (L) / Spectral Modulator (R)
**Pinging Phaser** — a phaser with anywhere from **2 to 64 poles**. (He explains all-pass-filter phasing; each all-pass copy is a "pole," two per notch, hence min 2 poles; the out-of-phase frequency rides an LFO for the whoosh; more poles = more notches on the same LFO; feedback increases the in/out-of-phase amplitude difference.) Control mapping:
- **TIME = LFO speed.**
- **MODIFY = number of poles.**
- **ALT = feedback (output of the all-pass filters fed back to the input).**
- **HOLD (the hold/latch function) freezes the LFO in place** — useful for setting the number of poles by hand and seeing/hearing them.
Findings: you really need some feedback (ALT) for it to make much sound. Max MODIFY ≈ 64 poles ≈ 32 notches → a bank of parallel sine waves moving up/down; max rate = chaos; few poles + low feedback = classic '70s phaser. Practical taste: on CLEAN sounds it sounds good when ONE setting is cranked (e.g. low feedback, min poles, high speed — especially nice with SPREAD on); on LEAD sounds it sounds better with everything around noon. **MIDI clock:** the LFO does sync, but only its fastest setting maps to once-per-beat, which is slower than the fastest free LFO — so syncing the phaser to clock isn't very useful.

**Spectral Modulator** — no known hardware precedent; closest analogy is a filter bank à la Moog Spectra. Manual says it "constructs a synthesized replica of your dry signal and modulates it." It seems to split the audio into many frequency bands and change their amplitude as a group, sweeping across them in batches — effectively a giant moving notch filter; slow enough and you hear adjacent bands switch off one by one (a deliberately digital, un-analog sound). Control mapping:
- **MODIFY = depth of the notch filter.**
- **TIME = rate of a sawtooth LFO governing the notch frequency.**
- **ALT = resonance + clipping on the digital filter bands** (a kind of digital distortion; effectively normalizes every partial band to the same energy/volume).
Demo: input gets "digitized"; ALT (resonance) up makes every band the same normalized level (pushes up upper partials even where there's no content, by normalizing noise there); the notch then sweeps through. On drums even with the notch off it does something wonderful — a special digital distortion. SPREAD puts the left/right spectral processing on two opposed LFOs. Best followed by a delay/reverb to make it shinier. **Syncs to MIDI clock** — the LFOs sync; e.g. ~3 o'clock ≈ one LFO cycle per beat, noon ≈ once per bar per side. There's a little latency audible only with resonance fully down; with resonance up it's smashed enough not to matter.
