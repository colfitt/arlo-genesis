PedalScapes · OBNE / Emily Hopkins - Parting Glitch pedal - Unboxing / 1st Play - Ambient Dream pedal · 2026-03-07
(Mostly unboxing + an unscripted ~40-min ambient jam; limited spoken walkthrough — distilled notes below. Auto-captions during the music are unreliable.)

UNBOXING / IMPRESSIONS. Host Matt impulse-bought Parting after seeing the Andertons demo (Pete playing while Lee adjusted) — "that one pedal sounds like a Pedalscape." It's Emily Hopkins's (the Harp Lady's) signature pedal with Old Blood Noise Endeavors — "a whole mixture of goodness." He notes he's long liked OBNE reverbs, delays, and the Signal Blender Stereo. Bought the Stone (silver) colorway specifically so the panel writing is readable (the Andertons unit was hard to read). It ships in a cotton bag with stickers, pins, and an OBNE pick; no printed manual, just a QR code to the manual.

PANEL TOUR. Top row = Rate, Depth, Shape on the Modulation section. Then Chance, Smear, Glitch for the delay/reverb, with Dissolve in the middle ("kind of giving you all your glitchy stuff"). Three presets on the left; buttons and two footswitches on the right. The smaller buttons cover Mod, Time, Filter, and Mix. Smear "goes from definitely separate delays to smoothing them out until it begins to be more reverb-like." I/O: stereo in and out, MIDI in and out, USB-C, 9V, and expression. He calls it "the Emily Hopkins OBNE Parting glitch machine."

FIRST PLAY (electric guitar, with a clean-with-a-lift tone). "It's an adventure — I have no idea what I'm doing. Just going to push buttons and make sounds." He found the Aux footswitch can do tap tempo or other features, and tried setting it to half-time. Started with everything at 12 o'clock and wasn't sure it was doing much; then engaged a preset and "it all started happening." He copied some of the preset knob configurations, then went between the presets and his own settings, switching back and forth. Long ambient/glitch jam follows (the engine clearly self-generating washes and reverse/glitch textures).

CLOSING TAKEAWAY. "Well, blimey, that was pleasantly insane." Notes a learning curve — the Andertons demo "went straight to the presets," and he couldn't make head or tail of it from the panel alone, but the presets unlocked the sound. His verdict: "it really does stand on its own — you could have that and maybe a distortion pedal and a reverb, and that could be your ambient pedalboard once you know what you're doing." Honest about not fully understanding it yet ("I haven't got a blooming clue what I just did — I had fun") and asks viewers who own one to explain how it works. Credits Andertons for the demo and Emily Hopkins for inspiring it.
