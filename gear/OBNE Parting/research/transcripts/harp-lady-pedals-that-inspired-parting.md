Harp Lady (Emily Hopkins) · All of the pedals that inspired Parting · 2026-03-10

Parting is a pedal I developed in collaboration with Old Blood Noise Endeavors. It's probably the best project I've ever worked on, and it's the culmination of all the knowledge that Russ and I have from trying hundreds, if not thousands, of effect pedals. When we designed Parting, it wasn't like we wanted to make a new version of an already existing pedal — it was a combination of elements that I really love, and once we put them all together it was a lot of effort to make them work well with one another and have its own personality. But I'd be lying if I said I didn't have direct inspiration from other pedals — certain sounds, details, and conceptual ideas. So I want to go through some of that and give credit where credit is due. Parting doesn't make any of these obsolete to me; at most it applies small ideas from each and modifies them to work within Parting.

BIT-CRUSHED REVERB (the starting point). The starting point for all of this was our love for bit-crushed reverb. (Technically it's sample-rate reduction, not bit crushing, but everyone calls it bit crushing.) Three pedals were the first reference points:
- The Unusual EAS (discontinued) — I have two so I can run them in stereo. A really distinct, creepy sound; I love using these in horror soundtracks and unsettling pieces. Parting can get pretty creepy in its own way.
- The AC Noises Arma — an incredible ambient reverb and bit crusher, more peaceful and ambient than the EAS.
- Dark Star (also Old Blood Noise) — a pad reverb with bit crushing; its crushing is probably the most similar to Parting's, but it's not the same algorithm. It wasn't purposefully similar — we tried a lot of different bit-crushing sounds, and the one that worked best combined with everything else happened to be somewhat similar to Dark Star's crushing.

DISSOLVE / REVERSE (the central idea). The central idea for Parting from the start was the Dissolve knob — a duality of destroying your signal in a beautiful way. Center = clean; turn left = lowers the sample rate (bit crush). We weren't sure what should happen when you turn it right — it started as a lo-fi thing, was tape saturation at one point, but we love reverse sounds, so we made turning right a reverse delay.
- Avalanche Run has always been my go-to for reverse delay; to this day I haven't found one I like better.
- The Old Blood Noise BL44 — a clock-controlled reverse pedal. Turning Dissolve right is the same concept as BL44, except Parting has a much cleaner-sounding version. BL44 does a weird clock thing with the FV-1 chip where it slows down, degrades, and gets really dirty/noisy. On Parting, turning Dissolve right starts with a clean reverse delay and continuously divides the clock to sound more degraded and crushed — same concept as BL44, but less noisy / different sound.

[Mid-design candid clip: discussing a mix-control bug on a prototype, debating whether the reverse direction should be a clean reverse first and then "everything from there is just clocked down," rather than a weird mix control where dry and wet cancel each other out.]

GLITCH (chance-based delay + octave doubling). We wanted a glitch element but didn't know what it meant. We didn't reference our glitch pedals much; the only similar element is the way the sample rate jumps around in the Chase Bliss Warped — but that was a detail, not a focal point. Dan from Old Blood suggested the glitch could be a chance-based delay buffer, which sounded cool but wasn't glitchy enough, so we added more.
- We tried pitch shifting but aren't fans of cheesy digital pitch-shift sounds. We eventually used our favorite pitch-shifting method — uncommon, happens in analog bucket-brigade pedals. Parting's delay is digital and not modeled on BBDs, but it borrows the element where turning the time knob causes octave-up/-down jumps.
- The Flower (Flancher) pedals: turning a delay's time knob gives crazy modulation as a byproduct; Flancher has a "double" button that instantly doubles/halves the delay time, so you hear it shift up or down an octave momentarily. Flancher's CV input can automatically double/halve via a modular LFO. This is the main element that inspired Parting's glitch sound.
- Chase Bliss Thermae uses this mechanic for all different pitch intervals; we only wanted octave up/down. The Bertonix BBD (came out late in development) is another bucket-brigade pedal that does this.
- Parting's Glitch knob controls the chance of the doubling/halving happening (originally called "Doubles"). You can also use the Aux footswitch to double/halve. Parting takes it further: since it's digital, you can Smear the glitch delay into a reverb, so the delay-time pitch shifts become part of the reverb — something I don't think any other pedal does. It also doubles/halves the modulation speed at the same time, which makes everything feel more in sync while still being glitchy.

ENVELOPE MODE. We wanted an Envelope mode so people could have more control over the glitch and make it less chance-based. It was one of the last things coded in and we didn't spend too much time on it.

TIME KNOB (no modulation artifact). Parting's delay has a Time knob, but we didn't think the usual modulation-when-you-turn-it was appropriate. We knew it might be removable because the Mood MkII's delay-time knob doesn't do that. We asked Dan to remove it; his first attempt made a cool glitchy sound when turning Time (we loved it), but it caused MIDI issues so we abandoned it. The best we could do was make it go silent when you turn it — which I love, because it's a way to quickly reset the delay buffer when things get out of hand.

TREMOLO. Parting originally had a lot of modulation elements, but the one we cared about most was tremolo. We wanted a selection of waveforms (reverse sawtooth, random sine, square) and ping-pong tremolo. But ping-pong tremolo with choppy waveforms and high depth can be disorienting on headphones (we got that complaint on an Arboretum video).
- We helped test/develop the Intrepid Audio Crossflow, and our feedback included controlling stereo width to avoid that. We implemented a similar concept: one of Parting's secondary controls adjusts the stereo width of the tremolo (made a secondary control since most people won't use it often).

VIBRATO. The Mod button changes tremolo to vibrato; straightforward, we didn't put much time into it. It behaves most like the EarthQuaker Aqueduct and the LCM (Leclercut) Nostalgia — chorus/vibrato with unusual waveforms.

FILTER. A later addition. We didn't want it super resonant — it's just there to remove certain frequencies. We love the low-pass filter from Vongon, which was somewhere in the back of our minds.

TWO MIX MODES (unique to Parting). One element I've never found in another pedal is that it has two mix modes. Once we discovered Parting's sound, the mix control had become really complicated — it had bit crushing in it, but no way to make it JUST a bit crusher (same for the tremolo and reverse sounds). Adding an alternate mix mode opened up those possibilities and made the pedal much more flexible and versatile. These different mix modes are really what lets Parting have a strong personality but also be extremely versatile at the same time.

We referenced concepts from all these pedals to communicate to Old Blood Noise how Parting should work — they coded it based on our feedback. If a company can drop another Tube Screamer clone with a slight variation, this whole process is even more ethical than that.
