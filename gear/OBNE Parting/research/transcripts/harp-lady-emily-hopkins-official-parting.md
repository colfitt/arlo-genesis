Harp Lady (Emily Hopkins) · Old Blood Noise x Emily Hopkins - Parting · 2026-01-21

This is Parting, a pedal I designed in collaboration with Old Blood Noise Endeavors. We made it together from the ground up, based on a concept that Russ and I came up with, and it came out better than I could have ever imagined. Parting is the culmination of researching and trying out almost every effect pedal that's come out over the past few years and taking that information and creating something truly special that does things no other effect pedal does.

This pedal can do a lot. It can do simple, very practical things, some fun glitchy things, and it can also create beautiful ambient soundscapes that no other single pedal can do. Before we break it all down, I want to show you what the true sound and personality of Parting is to me.

[Opening musical demonstration — harp through Parting.]

I wanted Parting to feel like something living and breathing, something you can have a conversation with as you play. Parting can be a really versatile tremolo, a lo-fi sounding verb, a bit crusher, a reverse delay, a reverse bit crusher, a glitchy delay, a glitchy reverb, a glitchy dynamic thing, and any combination of those things. It has three presets that you can change between instantly, or a lot more with MIDI — so it could probably replace quite a few pedals on your board.

We worked really hard to give Parting a distinct personality but also have it be extremely versatile, so it wasn't just an ambient soundscape machine, because it can do a lot more than that. Parting was designed and tested on guitar, bass, synths, voice, and obviously harp. But Parting is not a harp pedal — it's just that harp is my primary instrument.

This pedal can feel overwhelming and complex, but if you break it down, it's really not so bad. This section over here is for the tremolo and verb [Modulation]. This is for the glitchy delay and the reverb. And this knob over here [Dissolve] is really the heart of Parting. And this section over here is a bunch of miscellaneous stuff we'll get into later. All of these elements are connected in a really beautiful way.

We're going to start with the modulation. It could be a tremolo or vibrato, and this button switches between them. When this knob is lit up, it's vibrato; when it's not, it's a tremolo. The Shape knob chooses the waveform. This knob controls the Rate, and Depth is the intensity. Just for reference, this section is almost like an entire tremolo pedal and an entire vibrato pedal, but in stereo — not exactly the same, but the function is similar.

This light shows you how the modulation is moving, and its brightness shows the depth. Every time the tremolo or vibrato goes up and back down, this light blinks — even when the modulation is off. Every time it blinks, the pedal rolls the dice to see if a glitch delay will happen. So the more you turn up the Chance knob, the more likely the delay will happen. This grabs a random bit of your signal and plays it through a delay buffer. All the way down is never; when it's at noon, it happens constantly. Past noon, it controls the chance it'll feed back into itself, and it can get pretty cool.

The Glitch knob lets you divide the delay and modulation time. It also messes with the sample rate. Past noon, it controls the chance that it automatically doubles or halves the time of the delay and modulation, creating some really beautiful octave-up and -down sounds. And this is where the pedal gets truly glitchy.

This knob [Smear] smears the delay into a reverb as you turn it up, and all of these sounds start to feel a lot bigger.

So Parting can really force you to embrace randomness, but you can also have complete control over when these things happen if you want to. Envelope mode lets your playing control when the delay happens — the delay will only grab your notes when you play loudly, and the Chance knob controls the sensitivity. You can also set it so that the glitch only happens when you step on this footswitch, so you can have complete control over the glitch element. And you could set the aux footswitch to do either tap tempo or switch between presets.

But the heart of Parting is really this Dissolve knob. This was the main idea behind the very first concept that Russ and I ever showed Old Blood Noise. When it's in the center, everything is clean. As you turn it to the left, it reduces the sample rate for a bit-crushing sound. To the right, it's a reverse delay. As you turn it further to the right, the clock continues to divide for this reverse bit-crusher sound. And now I'm going to combine these with some reverb.

The middle knob and button in this section is for a filter, and you can choose either high pass or low pass.

[Demonstration sending a drum machine through a second (black) Parting, connected with MIDI so the tempos are synced, then adding the filter while Parting glitches it out.]

This button over here lights up the Mix knob. When it's not lit up, the bit crusher and modulation apply to the wet signal. When the Mix knob is lit up, the modulation and bit crushing apply to your dry signal instead. These different mix modes are really what lets Parting have a strong personality but also be extremely versatile at the same time.

You can control pretty much anything on Parting using an expression pedal, or even use it to control multiple things at the same time. They also make a matching expression ramper, so I'm going to use that to automatically move the sample rate.

I like to leave a lot of space when playing through Parting, but you don't have to, because the glitching and the ambience can add a lot to more dense playing. Another favorite sound of mine from Parting is one that kind of sounds like a broken tape machine came alive only to tell you that it's sad. Parting can also take just one note and turn it into a weird percussive soundscape. And it can also get pretty creepy sounding.

Parting is a stereo pedal and the routing is really flexible. If you're just plugging an instrument like guitar or a mono synth into it, you can make it mono-to-stereo by holding this switch and pressing this button so that the middle LED lights up. If you're sending a stereo signal into it — like a synth, drum machine, or putting a stereo pedal before it — tap the button until the third one lights up. If you just want it to be mono, light up the first one. You can cycle through all these pretty quickly and it's really flexible.

Parting shows us that there are beautiful ways to destroy something. It's a metaphor for the beauty of death, which is why we decided to call it Parting. You can get it in black, purple, or green. This wasn't just something where I said "it's already done, slap my name on it, slap the Emily Hopkins brand on it" — no. We worked really hard on it.
