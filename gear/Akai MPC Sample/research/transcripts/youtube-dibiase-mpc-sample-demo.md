DIBIA$E (Steven Brandon) is an LA beat-scene producer/finger-drummer out of the Project Blowed / Good Life / Low End Theory lineage — exactly the "swing-forward, hands-on chop" tradition the dossier maps the MPC Sample onto. This is one of the only sources of a *named, respected MPC artist demoing this specific box (AC50)*, so it's load-bearing for the artist facet. Distilled prose below; concrete technique quoted.

## Who he is / context
- "My history with the MPC goes back to 1997. I got my first MPC 2000 at Guitar Center... that's how I pretty much learned." Came up making beats around Project Blowed/Good Life open mics, "hearing a lot of experimental hip-hop there" — Freestyle Fellowship, the Nonce, etc.; grew up on Tribe Called Quest, Pharcyde, Wu-Tang.
- Frames the Sample as "my little miniature MPC. But the portability is crazy... it packs a punch." Praises 5-hr battery, micro SD (he runs a 512 GB card, says you can go to terabytes), effects, color-compressor, Flex Beat, resample, and the recall feature.
- "First day I made like eight beats straight out the box. Didn't have the manual yet. If you come from that MPC ecosystem, you're going to catch on to it quick. It's not a crazy learning curve." (Confirms the dossier's "immediacy" thesis.)

## Concrete techniques he demos (his "favorite features")
- **Pad Link + Offset for swing (an old-school trick).** "With offset, you can take a note and add some silence to it... basically like adding some silence in front of the one shot. That was like an old trick we would do to add some swing." He then *Pad-Links* two hi-hats (Shift → Pad Link, "I'mma link pad four to pad three"): "I'm basically just tapping one pad, but it's triggering two hi-hats at once. Then you can adjust the velocity on the second hi-hat" — manual, humanized hat swing without quantize.
- **Tempo / boom-bap BPM.** "I'mma just get a tempo around 80–85. It's like a go-to BPM if I'm doing some boom bap."
- **Lazy Chop.** Chop types = transient, 4/8/16 auto chops, "or my favorite is the lazy chop." Fine-tune: "if you hold shift and this encoder, you can zoom in on your actual chops... so it can land right on the transient."
- **Warp locks to BPM.** Turning Warp on "locks to the BPM"; also semitone-tunes chops.
- **Build sequences fast.** Shift → Double Sequence to duplicate a 1-bar loop up to 128 bars; "I just made eight copies that quick," then add variations per pattern, chain in Song Mode.
- **Side-chain / "ducking" pump effect.** In Pad FX, assign the pumper to a pad: "It ducks wherever the kick drum is hitting and it adds a vibe... gives the sample more space." (NOTE: the loopop review below clarifies this is the *pumper*, not true side-chain — same destination, different mechanism.)
- **Color-Compressor.** Shift + Pad 5 — "adds a little punch."
- **Fader-mapped FX + automation (his standout).** Shift + Pad 9 assigns the 30 mm fader to pad volume, pan, tune, attack, decay, filter cutoff, or whole-kit volume. "All that filtering I was doing live, I can automate it" — record a dry sequence, copy it, then perform/record fader filter sweeps in real time. He notes his MPC 2000 fader broke early so he never used one — this box's fader+automation is "super clutch."
- **Pad FX for texture (on-aesthetic).** 16 pad FX: beat repeat, delays, reverb, Color (cassette/vinyl/saturation), LoFi, flangers, phasers. "The effect that I will latch is something like the lo-fi and the Color to add some texture... you can get some real good dirty textures with this MPC Sample." Can latch multiple FX at once.

## His "beat set" workflow (transferable, multi-beat in one project)
- Multiple beats per project: "bank A and B with like eight different kits. And then bank C and D, I'll have like 32 different sample chops" — samples-per-project is effectively unlimited ("it's not a limit").
- **Mute/choke groups to stop overlap.** When stacking samples on pads: "you want to set it to the same choke group, the mute group. So now they'll cut off each other... every time I add another sample, I'm going to assign it to mute group two."
- **Set-building by tempo.** "I might make like eight beats inside of one project and it's all cohesive... I know they all [are] 85. I might do a whole other project 87 BPMs with eight beats, then... 89 BPMs... So when I'm building a set I work my way up to 120 BPMs and basically structuring the set at the same time while making a beat."

## Sampling sources he names
Line inputs (plug in a turntable or Walkman), built-in mic, and USB-C straight from an iPad/iPhone — "drag and drop to the folders directly to the MPC sample from your phone. You don't even need to go to your computer."

## Studio relevance
Pad-Link-plus-velocity for swung hats, Offset-silence for human feel, and Lazy Chop + latched LoFi/Color FX are the exact moves to flip the rig's fuzz-wall and banjo samples into broken, swung, "recorded-wrong" beats. His mute-group discipline is how to keep stacked drone slices from smearing.
