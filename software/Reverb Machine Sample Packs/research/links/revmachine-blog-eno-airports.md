# How Brian Eno Created "Music for Airports" — the incommensurable-loops drone method

THE single most useful article for a drone/ambient rig. Free recreated WAV loop files
at the article's end.

## The core technique: incommensurable tape loops
Eno layered multiple sung-note tape loops of **non-syncing lengths** (e.g. 23.5 s,
25.875 s) so they "are not likely to come back into sync again" — producing endlessly
evolving phasing with no mechanical repetition. This is the foundational drone/wall
technique: stack a few sustained loops of slightly different lengths and let them drift.

## Track-by-track techniques
- **1/1**: Robert Wyatt piano → echo units → looped → **played back at half-speed on
  reel-to-reel** (drops an octave, diffuse texture). Modal D Mixolydian for emotional
  ambiguity.
- **2/1**: Seven sung-note loops, durations within ~0.5 s of each other (20.1 vs 19.6 s)
  so they phase slowly toward/away from unison; D♭ on the longest loop for semitone
  dissonance.
- **1/2**: Eight piano-phrase loops + one arpeggiated chord intersecting freely over 11 min.

## DAW recreation (copyable)
1. **Disable the grid** (Ableton ⌘+4 / Logic: ignore tempo / free clips) so loops are
   un-quantized.
2. Record clips in **seconds, not bars** — pick durations that don't divide evenly.
3. Loop several different-length clips simultaneously; record the emergent combination.
4. Half-speed a recorded loop for the octave-down diffuse texture.
Also recommends VCV Rack / Bitwig's The Grid for phase-looping.

## Rig application
This is the blueprint for "sustained walls" with the user's gear: sample held banjo/
guitar notes (or a CB Big Time / Blooper loop) into Kontakt or Logic, make 3–5 loops of
slightly different bar-lengths, let them drift. The Eno half-speed trick maps to Logic
Flex/Varispeed or sampling a note an octave down.
