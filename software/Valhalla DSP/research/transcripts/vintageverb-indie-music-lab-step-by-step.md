The Indie Music Lab (Marcus) · Valhalla VintageVerb: A Complete Step-by-Step Guide · 2023-12-14

Beginner-to-intermediate practical workflow, demoed on a Lana Del Rey-style indie mix. Strong on the return-track method, preset-first workflow, and the EQ moves that turn VintageVerb into a warm, vintage-sounding verb — directly relevant to lo-fi/ambient use.

---

You've got Valhalla VintageVerb — now what? This shows how to get started and make the most of it for incredible-sounding reverb on vocals and any other tracks.

**Demo context**: a remake of Lana Del Rey's "Chemtrails Over the Country Club" — VintageVerb is used heavily across the mix.

**Mix knob & return tracks**: The Mix knob sets how strong vs. subtle the reverb is. But the way I almost always work is on **return/aux tracks** rather than inserting directly. When a reverb is on a return track, **Mix stays at 100%** — you blend with the send level instead. You only use the Mix knob when the reverb is inserted directly on a track (then dial back to blend with the dry). Tip: search "[your DAW] return tracks" if unfamiliar — e.g. "Logic return tracks."

**Preset library** (the fast way in): I love presets — find something close, then adjust. Walking through folders on the lead vocal:
- **Ambiences**: very short, small-room sounds.
- **Chambers**: a nice almost-sheen, brighter, close to a room.
- **Gated reverbs**: more chaotic/crazy, abrupt.
- **Halls**: huge spaces.
- **Plates**: a go-to — "Dark Plate" preset is cool.
- Plus Rooms, Sanctuary, etc. Explore them — they're like having another person in the studio helping mix. The plug-in's preset library is excellent.

**What I let the preset handle vs. what I tweak**: I let the preset largely set the Damping / Shape / Diffusion / Modulation boxes — I don't like fiddling those knobs; if I don't like the sound I'd rather switch presets than perfect every knob. The things I DO adjust after loading a preset:

1. **Decay** — short or long tail. Make it long (e.g. 15 s) and it goes on forever; make it very short for a tight sound.
2. **Predelay** — at 0, the dry vocal and reverb hit basically together. Turn it up and the dry vocal pops through first, then the reverb follows after a gap — separation between the source and the tail. Extreme values are obviously too much; a little predelay is great for longer, spacious sounds. Watch what value presets land on (0, 20, 50…).
3. **EQ — high cut and low cut** (adjusted all the time):
   - **High cut**: roll it down to "put a blanket over" the reverb for a warm sound. Even down to ~1,000 Hz (1k) fully blankets it — but it's only the reverb signal, not the lead vocal, so the dry vocal stays bright. Even for a fairly bright reverb I'll often roll high cut to ~2–3 kHz and plenty of highs still pop through.
   - **Low cut**: almost by rule at least **200 Hz**, often landing around **300–400 Hz** for reverbs, to clear the low-frequency buildup.

**Mode & Color** (treated like extensions of the preset menu): Mode usually mirrors the preset (a hall preset → a hall mode). Color goes from a 70s sound to 80s to a more modern "Now" sound — I don't tweak it much; the changes are subtle but worth testing by flipping between 70s / 80s / Now.

**The two reverbs actually used in this mix** (the takeaway recipe):
- Reverb 1 — **"Fat Plate" preset, in Chamber mode**, ~**2.5 s decay**, **high cut rolled to ~2,000 Hz**. A slightly-longer, warm plate/chamber.
- Reverb 2 — a short room: **"Ambience Tiled Room" preset** (sounds like a bathroom). 
- These two return tracks served the whole mix — lead vocal, strings (sent to the short one + the plate), guitars, and all background vocals bussed through. One beauty of return tracks: many sources share one reverb instance.
