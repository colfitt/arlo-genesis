# Free shimmer on clean guitar with Supermassive (real workflow)

A guitarist's lived recipe for getting a Shimmer-like ambient tone out of the FREE
Supermassive on clean guitar — directly relevant to the banjo/baritone/clean-guitar rig.

## The setup
- Clean guitar through Neural DSP **Archetype: Nolly** (first amp = great clean) — a
  Forester Savelle-based preset with compression, EQ, stock cab IR.
- **Crucially: there's delay + reverb in the amp BEFORE Supermassive.** *"Without this
  the Supermassive plugin tends to kind of fade in in a way that's not very pleasing."*

## The Supermassive patch
- Built from Supermassive's factory **"Swelling Synth Verb"** preset — *"that was pretty
  intense but I dialed it back... so it sounded quite musical."* (A **late-reverb
  shimmer** character.)
- Describes it as *"quite a late reverb shimmer"* — the slow-swelling, pitched-feeling
  wash, not literal pitch-shift (Supermassive has no pitch shifter — the "shimmer" is the
  dense modulated long tail; see roster note that real shimmer = paid Shimmer/H90).

## The two key takeaways (transferable)
1. **The fade-in problem.** A swelling/slow-attack Supermassive patch fades in unpleasantly
   on sharp, stabbing, high-attack notes when fed a *dry* signal. **Fix: put a short
   delay + reverb BEFORE Supermassive** so the source is already "wet/sustained" going in —
   the swell then has something to grab and the attack is smoothed.
2. **Dial back for the mix.** The intense solo-instrument shimmer patch is *"a little bit
   intense in the context of a full mix"* — he made a **second, dialed-back preset** (less
   Feedback/Mix) for mix use. Lesson: keep two versions — a lush solo one and a restrained
   mix one.

Honest flag: the video doesn't read out numeric Supermassive values (he says he won't
"talk too much about Supermassive itself") — the technique (front-end wetting + dial-back)
is the takeaway, not exact knob numbers.
