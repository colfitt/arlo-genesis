## Method
"If you want to know what an effect is doing, put the Mix at 100% and mess with the settings — that's usually going to show you how it's affecting the sound."

## The five main controls
**Mix** — 100% = whole signal is reverb, 50% = half wet/half dry. **Predelay** — lags the reverb so a drum/transient comes through hard before the reverb kicks in; useful on bass and transient sounds to preserve the attack. **Decay** — how long the reverb sticks around (it acts mostly on the LATE reverb). **High Cut** — a lowpass; use it because with a long decay and a lot of high-frequency content (hats, leads) you get a "ringing noise" you don't want. (Note: it's a high cut, not a low cut — "you can always use an EQ after the reverb on a return bus" for more control.) **Depth** — the one that throws people.

## Depth = early/late crossfader
"Depth is pretty much like a crossfader — you're crossfading between early and late style reverb. At 50% you have a bit of early and late. **Early stands for the reflections** (clap in a bathroom/tunnel — those early reflections), **late is the body** of the reverb. At **100% you get only late** reverb; at **0% you get only early** reverb (the reflection points)."

## Early section
- **Early Size** — the size of your reflections; "think of it like a decay time for those reflection points."
- **Early Cross** — reverb is a stereo effect; turn it toward 1 and the left/right channels transfer energy (transfer reflection points). Subtle, hard to grasp; not a big deal for most EDM producers.
- **Mod Rate / Mod Depth** — "they don't tell you, but the reverb's going to have a chorus on it." Higher Depth = more chorus applied; higher Rate = faster modulation. Think of it like an LFO / chorus effect.
- **Early Send** — how much of the early gets sent to the late.
- **Diffusion** — "a really cool one." It makes the sound seem foggy/spread out instead of hitting hard — "like someone came with an eraser and erased it a bit." Real-world analogy: studio diffuser panels spread the sound so it doesn't reflect straight back at you. Add diffusion when you want a smoother, spread-out reverb instead of distinct slaps.

## Late section ("where the action happens — my favorite")
"What I like to do when I create atmosphere or cinematic sounds is apply 100% reverb and mess with the Late section to get really cool sounds." (He used this to build atmospheres/sounds to load into rompler instruments.)
- **Late Size** — controls the size of the room = the BODY of the reverb. How big do you want the body to sound.
- **Late Cross** — same as early cross but for the tail: at 0 left/right keep their own channels; at 1 they spread/intermingle.
- **Late Mod Rate / Depth** — another chorus effect on the late reverb; higher Mod Rate = more modulation of the late tail.

## The bottom row — frequency-dependent decay (the part that confused him)
This is the key copyable insight. The **Bass Xover** is the frequency that splits low from mid: set it to e.g. 540 Hz and "anything below 540 gets affected by the Bass Mult." The **High Xover** sets where the highs split: set it to e.g. 10,000 Hz and "anything above that gets affected by the High Mult."

**The multipliers scale the Decay time per band:**
"Let's say our Decay time is 10 seconds and our Bass Mult is 0.5×. This means only **5 seconds** of decay applies to the low frequencies (below the Bass Xover). If the Bass Mult is at 2, the lows get ~**20 seconds** (10 × 2)." So: **Bass Mult < 1 = shorter low-frequency tail (keeps the wash out of the mud); Bass Mult > 1 = longer, blooming low tail.** Same logic for High Mult on the highs.

"The problem with reverb is if you have a bass sound you don't want the low end to get really big — you want the reverb more in the mid-range. So set the Bass Xover and pull the Bass Mult below 1." Great for advanced sound design; you can also automate these bands (e.g. Ableton automation).

## Modes
"Change the Reverb mode = change the algorithm. A **Dark Room** is not going to have as-high frequencies; **LV-426** is more of that vintage style of reverb — for lo-fi hip hop or very old-school stuff, try LV-426, it sounds very nice and very retro." Find your settings first, then audition modes to see which fits.

## Takeaway honesty
He admits the plugin is "a little too free for my taste — I like plugins that are just mix/decay/room-size" — i.e., ValhallaRoom's depth of control is a double-edged sword for someone who wants to move fast.
