VintageVerb vs the hardware reverbs (Big Sky etc.) — when to reach for which
Compiled from KVR/Gearspace/MOD WIGGLER "Big Sky vs Valhalla" threads (search-surfaced; several

## Consensus
- **Both are "absolutely gorgeous."** Big Sky has more modes and a wider palette; its **Cloud**
  mode is singled out for a "lush grandeur / magic" that software rarely matches.
- **VintageVerb wins on value + speed:** ~19 algorithms for $50, "intuitive interface ... tune
  reverbs to fit your music quickly, even with a mouse." (Big Sky = 12 algos for ~$199.)

## When to reach for which (this rig: Big Sky + H90 + Microcosm + VintageVerb in-box)
- **VintageVerb (in the box):** recallable per-track, automatable, run multiple instances cheaply
  (Tycho-style inserts, reverb-into-reverb), total recall with the project, vintage/lo-fi color.
  The default when the reverb needs to be IN the session and saved with it.
- **Strymon Big Sky (hardware):** when you want the **Cloud** lushness, real **FREEZE** (held
  infinite drones — VintageVerb has none), and to play the reverb as an instrument / reamp a
  pedalboard wall. Best for performed/captured ambient beds.
- **Hologram Microcosm:** granular/glitch/loop reverb-ish textures VintageVerb can't do —
  reach for it for rhythmic, mangled, generative beds, not natural space.
- **Eventide H90:** the deep, dual-algorithm, modulated/pitched reverbs (Blackhole etc.) and
  routing complexity — when you want algorithmic reverb AS a sound-design instrument with
  pitch/mod, and MIDI-controllable.

## Practical division of labour
Capture/reamp the moody played beds through Big Sky / H90 / Microcosm → then sit them in the mix
and add cheap recallable air, vintage color, and reverb-into-reverb depth with VintageVerb. Use
VintageVerb's 70 s decay for "almost frozen"; use Big Sky's actual Freeze for truly held drones.
