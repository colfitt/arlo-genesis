The "adaptive shimmer" mode — a pitch-shifting reverb-delay that gets out of the source's
way. Directly useful for shimmer walls under banjo/baritone in this rig.

## What Pitch Duck does
A Pitch-mode delay where **the input signal continuously controls the feedback gain and
output volume** (smooth ducking). So the shimmer wash ducks down while you play and blooms
up when you stop — the wall stays under the source, then rises in the gaps.

## The three documented dialing approaches
1. **Adaptive shimmer (lush):** Pitch +12 (or your interval) · high Feedback for a long
   tail · Diffusion up for the wash · then set **Ducking amount** to taste — higher Ducking
   makes the shimmer "more precise and less dissonant with the input" as notes sustain (it
   gets out of the way, then returns).
2. **Clarity/unobtrusive tail:** **Pitch Shift 0** · modest **Detune** · boost Feedback for
   tail length · use Ducking to "add clarity" — a wide, long, slightly-detuned reverb-delay
   that never masks the dry signal.
3. **Performance (live banjo/baritone):** turn Feedback up to the sustain you want, then
   ride Ducking for "the clarity you want while playing" — the wall follows your dynamics.

## Why it matters for this rig
This solves the #1 ambient problem: a 100%-feedback shimmer wall normally buries the
played source. Pitch Duck keeps the picked banjo/baritone legible (ducks the wall while
you play) and lets the shimmer bloom into the silences = a breathing, self-managing wall.
No external sidechain needed.

⚠️ The post gives conceptual guidance (Threshold/Ratio behaviour), not exact numeric
Ducking values — dial by ear to the source.
