Rachel K Collier · Electronic Music Production Using Harmony Engine · n.d.

Producer/artist (electronic, sync/campaign work) demo of Harmony Engine in **Ableton Live**, recorded live through an SM7B. Notable because it's a working-producer's real use, not a manual tour. Distilled:

- Pitches it as both "a really cool modern sound" texture generator AND "a straight-up really good harmonizer." The modern-pop/electronic angle (Bon Iver-adjacent lush texture under a lead) is the use she keeps coming back to.
- Live workflow: arm the vocal track in Ableton, hear the harmonizer working in real time. Set the **key** (she's in C). Sees the **lead vocal channel + the four harmony channels**.
- Per-voice moves she calls out: **mute** voices to drop from 4 down to 2; **pan** each voice differently; **change the Interval** per voice — "put it up an octave, put it up another octave." So octave-stacked intervals (not just 3rds/5ths) for the wide modern stack.
- MIDI: "a harmonizer wouldn't be a harmonizer if you couldn't send MIDI to it." Set up a MIDI channel in the DAW, route MIDI to Harmony Engine, switch Harmony Source to **Chord Via MIDI** — "does what it says on the tin" — arm the track and play chords while singing; the voices follow the played chord. Notes there are "loads of other cool little ones to play with" (the other modes).
- Adds a "little bit of **reverb**" right at the plugin/return for the lush wash — reverb is part of the sound, not an afterthought.
- Real productions cited: a track for **Mary** (subtle — "creates this really lush kind of modern texture underneath Mary's vocal") and an outro she produced for **Anna Akana** for a Fabletics campaign — "using the harmony engine at the end of Anna's track really gave that big bold pop sound." So: subtle-bed use AND big-bold-stacked-climax use, both from the same plugin.

Rig takeaway: the live-armed + Chord-Via-MIDI "play the chords while the source sounds" workflow is exactly the played-choral-bed move; octave-up/octave-up interval stacking + reverb-at-the-plugin is the wide-wall recipe. Confirms it works armed/live (latency permitting) as well as printed.
