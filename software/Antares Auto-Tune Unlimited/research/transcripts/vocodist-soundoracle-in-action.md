## Positioning
He uses it as a **background synth / texture-layering** tool, not just a lead — "almost using this
more as a production thing." Calls it a beefed-up iZotope VocalSynth; the differentiator is the
**real Auto-Tune algorithm** built in. Note: he likes the extra scales (e.g. **harmonic minor**)
not in Auto-Tune Pro. Subscription-only gripe (wishes it were sold standalone).

## Three carrier/control modes — shown live
The Quick Start window lets you pick the mode up front; or change it later in the Synth section.
1. **Internal synth pitch-tracked from the voice** (default) — slap on a vocal, the synth follows
   the vocal. He starts here.
2. **Side-chain carrier** — uses **another instrument as the carrier**. In his demo he routes a
   **guitar** track via the **key/side-chain input**, then sets **Synth Source = Side Chain**.
   Result: the vocoder output now follows the guitar's rhythm but carries the voice. Notes a
   **synth or drums** as the side-chain carrier could give a "really rhythmic pattern" /
   "computerized drum sound."
3. **MIDI chords** — Synth Source = Oscillator, Pitch Track = MIDI. He plays a chord progression
   matching the song's guitar; the vocoded voice now follows the **chords** ("a really traditional
   vocoder thing"). Can be performed **live** (play chords while singing).
4. **Key (drone)** mode — Pitch Track = Key; a one-octave mini-keyboard sets a single drone pitch.
   Song is in F minor, so he sets the drone to **F** (then tries **C**, the dominant). The
   **Octave** knob extends the limited one-octave keyboard up/down.

## Settings he reaches for
- **Retune Speed**: since it's already a synth/digital sound, **faster retune speeds sound better**
  here (vs. slow for natural vocals). Humanize relaxes it on sustained notes.
- **Bands**: fewer = vintage vocoder; more = clarity. Q + slope options change the character.
- **Min/Max Hz** set the split range; **Stretch/Slide** are formant-style moves.
- **Play (pink-noise audition)** buttons in both Filter and Envelope to dial filters by ear.
- **Envelope band sliders** used like an EQ — pull out muddy bands. Attack/Release like a comp.
- **Voices**: 1 = single; up to **8 = "more like a crowd," a wider layered stack** sound.
- **Output+FX**: Spread (stereo widen, like Harmony Engine's), **Warmth** = tube saturation,
  Voice Mix to blend the dry vocal, **Chorus 1/2/3** ("classic vocoder sound — choruses are found
  on a lot of vocoders"), the right-arrow sends the dry source through the chorus too, **Voice Mod**
  = ring mod to make the source "less real / more synth."
- **XY pad** like EFX+, parameters assignable.

## Rig-relevant takeaways (mine, from his demo)
- His **guitar-as-side-chain-carrier** is the exact shape of the rig's *banjo/baritone as carrier*
  idea — except here the guitar is the carrier and the voice the modulator. For the rig you'd
  typically want the inverse (sustained synth/drone = carrier, banjo/voice = modulator), which the
  same Side-Chain routing supports — you just choose which source is on the insert track vs. the
  side-chain bus.
- The **Key drone mode** is the no-routing way to get a sustained machine-choir wall: drop on a
  played/voice track, Pitch Track = Key, pick the drone note = instant talking-pad on one pitch.
- He confirms it works great on **non-vocal instruments** (synths, pianos, drums) — supports the
  rig's "use it on banjo/baritone/drone" approach.
