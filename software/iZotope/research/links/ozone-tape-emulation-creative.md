izotope.com (Blog) · "From subtle to saturated: how to use tape emulation in your mix" · iZotope

Frames tape emulation (incl. Ozone's Vintage Tape) by how hard you drive it. Useful map
for using it as a CHARACTER tool, not a master polish.

## Three intensities
1. **Reactive glue (subtle)** — musical harmonic saturation + tiny zero-crossing
   smoothing. Makes a mix "more cohesive" without obvious compression or transient
   change. (The default move on a degraded master bus.)
2. **Saturation (moderate)** — noticeable color: low-end lift, softened highs, new
   harmonics, "dynamic rounding and loudness" that pushes RMS up while staying natural.
3. **All-out distortion (extreme)** — slam it for "crushed, aggressive, completely
   transformed" sound; great on drum buses + creative color. (The lo-fi/"recorded-wrong"
   move.)

## What tape adds
- Subtle harmonic distortion / overtones
- Gentle dynamic shaping (evens peaks/troughs)
- "Tape bump" low-frequency lift
- Natural high-end softening (less harsh than EQ)
- Zero-crossing smoothness

## Where to put it
- **Individual tracks:** roundness on bass/vocals, natural high-end taming that sounds
  "less tweaked" than EQ.
- **Mix bus (primary use):** A/B emulations until one adds the right saturation + gentle
  compression + vibe for THIS mix.

### Rig note
For drone/shoegaze: drive Vintage Tape from "glue" toward "saturation" — the low-end bump
+ softened highs are exactly the analog-warmth the digital wall is missing. Pair/contrast
with the hardware tape (Big Time, Generation Loss / Chase Bliss) — use the pedals for
character in tracking, Ozone Tape for the final glue.
