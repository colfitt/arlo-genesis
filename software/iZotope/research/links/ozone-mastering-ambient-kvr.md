KVR Audio Forum · "Your mastering techniques for ambient music?" · Production Techniques

The honest-niche source: how ambient/drone people actually approach "mastering" — mostly
restraint. Directly applicable to whether/how to use Ozone on this rig.

## Loudness philosophy
- "The same as for all other genres, just make it sound good! Although I personally prefer
  far less limiting with ambient music."
- Ambient typically lands around **-14 LUFS**, but: "Stop chasing the numbers! Every track
  is different and has its own loudness potential."
- Drones/noise aren't dynamic in the pop sense (no big peaks/troughs), so loudness-
  normalization math barely applies — leave the dynamics.

## Cautions
- Bass drones are where limiting distortion shows FIRST — be careful with limiting/clipping
  the extended low end.
- One workflow: add a tape-saturation plugin with MINIMAL input gain + set mixer levels so
  peaks stay under ~-6 dB → keeps dynamics while making a release consistent.

## The real job = consistency, not loudness
- For a multi-track release/album: "Line up the tracks and gently use EQ to bring them into
  the same tonal space without messing up their individual characters."
- Limit only to tame wayward peaks. If a processor has to work hard, the MIX needs more
  headroom — go back, don't crush at the master.
- Several voices: if the mix already sounds good, mastering may be unnecessary. Avoid heavy
  compression chains; let it breathe.

### Takeaway for Ozone on this rig
Ozone's value here is NOT loudness — it's (1) tonal/level consistency across a release
(Tonal Balance Control, gentle EQ, Match EQ), (2) gentle glue + analog color (Vintage Tape,
Vintage Limiter), (3) cleaning a muddy low end (Low End Focus). The Maximizer is the LEAST
useful module for drone; use it last, lightly, only to catch peaks.
