Arturia's sound-designer breakdowns of how factory Mellotron V presets are
built. These are directly copyable ambient/shoegaze recipes.

## "Final Frontier" — by Boele Gerkes (ambient wine-glass pad)
- Built on the **Wine Glass** tape, layered **three times** with different pan
  positions (uses the three A/B/C tracks spread in stereo).
- Effect pedals + VCA envelope shaping for movement — both things impossible on
  vintage hardware.
- Result: an evolving ambient texture from a single delicate tape.

## "Big Stereo Choir" — by Paul Schilling (orchestral choir wall)
- Pairs **two choir tapes** (Mixed Choir + Male Choir) **panned hard L/R** to
  simulate an orchestral arrangement / wide stereo field.
- **Sample looping → infinite sustain** (overcomes the original Mellotron's
  ~8-second tape limit).
- **Soft attack / long release** envelopes, subtle EQ + reverb.

## Named tapes referenced
- **Wine Glass** (glassy, delicate — great for shoegaze shimmer)
- **Mixed Choir**, **Male Choir** (the choir wall)
- (plus the broader palette: 3-violins strings, flute, brass, cello, organ,
  mandolin across the 65 racks)

## Design philosophy (the "embrace the flaws" approach)
Arturia's designers explicitly say to lean into the machine's character:
"pitch fluctuations, audio dropouts on worn-out tapes, the weird frequency"
response and distortion are what make it special. Recommended approaches:
- **Multi-tape layering** with differential panning (wide stereo).
- **Sample looping** for sustained atmospheric pads (kills the 8s limit).
- Tasteful **stompbox FX** (especially reverbs).
- **Tape flutter + saturation** for organic instability.
- **Minimalist editing** — don't over-process; let the tape character show.
