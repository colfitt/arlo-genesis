A full architecture overview with honest strengths/weaknesses for ambient work. Distilled.

## Sound generation
- **2 wavetable oscillators**, each with multiple modes (Standard, Gorilla, etc.); the main knob (Wavetable Position) morphs between frames. (Fewer than original Massive's 3 oscillators but deeper per-osc controls.)
- **2 Phase-Modulation oscillators (PM1, PM2)** → FM-style synthesis.
- **2 Noise layers** — "work especially well on pads"; great for atmospheric texture.

## Processing
- **9 filter types**. The SVF variants are the cleanest — good for precise shaping of ambient textures.
- **Effects: 3 insert slots (A, B, C)** + **3 stereo slots (X, Y, Z)**. Routing is customizable — "you can draw new connections" between components → creative signal flow for evolving soundscapes.

## Modulation
- **Three Performers** — step-based sequencers for complex modulation patterns; ambient motion without melodic constraint.
- **9 assignable modulators** — Modulation Envelopes (with extended Hold), Exciter Envelopes, Switcher LFOs (16 preset shapes), Random LFOs.
- **4 Keyboard Trackers** — filter evolution across the keyboard range; useful for sustained pads.
- **Unison** has a "Scale mode" for harmonic richness.

## Honest weaknesses for ambient
- "The lack of any visual representation is a bit annoying" — painful when designing slow, sustained modulations where visual feedback helps.
- "LFOs in Massive X are not easily customizable" → Performers often serve ambient design better.
- "Architecture is quite inflexible" for effect ORDER without going into the Routing tab.

## Takeaway
Massive X's routing flexibility + Performers + two noise layers make it strong for evolving textures; budget time in the Routing tab and lean on Performers (not LFOs) for the slow, non-repeating moves. The missing visual feedback on slow modulation is the main friction for drone work.
