## Framing
The five styles are **fundamentally different saturation behaviors, not subtle tweaks**: "You
are not designing saturation. You are choosing a behavior." Pick the style (harmonic response)
FIRST, then adjust everything else. Core strength = **speed**: results in seconds, supports
quick intentional decisions over deep parameter tweaking.

## Source-specific
- **Drums:** small amounts enhance punch; too much drive softens transients even as loudness
  rises.
- **Bass:** one of its strongest uses — harmonics help low frequencies translate across
  playback systems without relying on volume.
- **Vocals:** use subtly for clarity/presence; the **Tone control is critical** to avoid
  harshness, which develops quickly at aggressive settings.
- **Mix bus:** requires restraint; **parallel** recommended for subtle coloration.

## Honest limitations (the "when not to use it")
- **"Falls short when you need precision"** — no multiband, no surgical shaping.
- **"Easy to overuse."**
- **"Always introduces noticeable character"** → unsuitable for *transparent* enhancement.
- Not ideal for dynamic saturation changes across different mix elements.

(This squares with Nathan Nyquist's note: for poking specific frequencies out in a mix, reach
for a **multiband distortion** like Trash 2 / FabFilter Saturn instead — Decapitator is
broadband character.)
