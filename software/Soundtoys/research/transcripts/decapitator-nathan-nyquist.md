Decapitator is a deceptively simple tool but it can be confusing if you don't read
the manual. I'll explain it the way I use it. Up front: I think it's exceptional
for very aggressive saturation AND for subtle saturation — great all around.

**The drive stage.** The first stage of Decapitator is the drive stage, controlled
by the big knob, and there are different saturation curves denoted by the A / E /
N / T / P style buttons. You hit the drive stage and as you turn it up you get more
distortion. **Punish is a multiplier** — if I had drive at 5, with Punish on it
sounds twice as intense. It just lets you drive it even harder. If you want to
punish the god of your sound and make it a dirty, nutty sound, that's what you do.

**Signal flow (important).** The low cut actually happens BEFORE your drive stage,
and the high cut happens AFTER your drive stage. The reason it's set up this way:
typically your low frequencies wreak havoc on the responsiveness and predictability
of your saturation, which is very important to control because distortion gets out
of hand very quick. So with Punish on, turning the low-cut up to remove low
frequencies changes the whole shape of the sound — a fun, simple move. The high cut
is basically a low-pass filter, probably 60 dB/oct [NOTE: manual says High Cut is
6 dB/oct, 30 dB/oct with Steep — Nyquist's eyeballed figure is off], darkens the
sound after the distortion.

**The tone control is a tilt shelf.** If you'd let me redesign the UI I'd put the
tone control after the drive (it happens post-drive). A tilt shelf means turning
toward bright gives an EQ curve where the top band boosts and the low band cuts —
it changes the balance of the sound. This is a very important EQ maneuver: super
powerful, super lazy, extremely effective at shaping sounds. I'll use at least half
of it all the time. Do not forget the power of the shelf — it's probably the easiest
way to get what you're after with little confusion. It happens post the filter
section, after the drive stage.

**Output / Auto.** The output stage has auto-gain attached via the Auto switch — it
gain-matches the sound to stay at relatively the same volume. It does its best;
really it just prevents it from getting too loud as you crank distortion. Keep it on.
Once you've done heavy distortion, drive up with Punish and it can make the sound too
quiet — at that point I turn Auto off and set the output myself.

**The mix knob.** A surprising number of people don't use this enough. Sometimes you
want to color a sound but not change it too much, so it's fun to get really aggressive
with the distortion and then turn it off and gradually mix it back in — see what
warmth and character you can subtly add. The way I think about the mix knob: zero it
out, then ask "how much of that original in-your-face character do I want to add — how
much icing on the cake?" The original sound is the cake; this is the icing. At 100%
it becomes a paper shredder / meat grinder — it takes things in their original form
and applies entropy, ripping them apart. That actually gives them a purpose in your
track where before they were lifeless and boring.

**The styles (how he hears them).** E is the one he uses most — he describes it as
mimicking a triode tube/transistor [NOTE: manual actually assigns triode to the **T**
style; **E** = Chandler/EMI TG. Trust the manual — this is Nyquist's loose
recollection]. It gives a very pleasing amount of distortion. Each setting is a
different tone control AND a different transient behavior, because **saturation is
also a form of compression** — your attack/release timings effectively change per
style. With E you get a fast attack and a fast release on the top frequencies
(1 kHz-plus gets squashed). T and P are lower, more gentle saturation curves — you
have to drive harder to get similar distortion; they get a bit thinner when driven
hard but are warmer and gentler at lower drive. When you drive them all really hard
they get pretty similar. His honest take: he just uses E almost all the time. His
workflow: pick a saturation you like (usually E), set the drive, then flip through
the styles to see if one is ~30-40% better — don't try to re-dial drive for each
style, that's confusing. At high drive (10) he sometimes prefers A — "very natural,
high levels of distortion." Between N / T / P / A it's a toss-up; it depends on the
sound you want in the moment.

**Philosophy.** Distortion is the most imperfect, inaccurate kind of sound design
there is. You're really just going for a randomization of the tonal balance of your
sound — rolling the dice and hoping for the best. If you try to be a wizard with it
you'll have a bad time. It's really fun to use.

**Honest limit.** For mix work where you want specific frequencies poking out (e.g.
saturate just the top of a piano/pluck/lead synth), a **multiband distortion unit
(he names Trash 2)** gives more control — Decapitator is more heavy-handed broadband
distortion, or at low drive a gentle whole-sound warmer.

His full workflow: distort with E → set drive → shape with low-cut → bring up high-cut
only if needed (rare) → use the tone control more often to go darker/brighter → if too
quiet, Auto off and set output by hand → flip through styles to find the favorite →
maybe re-shape low-cut/tone once. Designed to be a very simple tool — most of the time
in production he just flips on drive, maybe Punish, and goes.
