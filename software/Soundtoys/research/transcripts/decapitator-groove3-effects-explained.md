Decapitator is an emulation of pushing hardware into saturation — it's NOT just an
overdrive or a distortion plugin; it reacts and is dynamic to whatever you send it. The
big knob on the left (deliberately the biggest, most fun control) is **Drive** — turning it
up on a drum stem instantly gives the drums some love with one parameter. **Punish** kicks
the gain up in one big step so you can absolutely destroy the audio. **Auto** is on, so
raising drive lowers the output automatically. **Mix** blends the dry signal back in to
preserve transients and original audio.

**Tone section / signal flow:** The **Low Cut and Tone are PRE-drive** — they reduce the
lows or change the EQ before it hits the drive section. The **High Cut is AFTER the drive**,
so you can roll off the harmonics the drive creates. **Thump** boosts around the low-cut
frequency (set low cut to ~130 Hz with Thump on → a small boost there). **Tone** turns up
highs / down lows toward bright, and vice versa toward dark. High Cut is a standard low-pass;
**Steep** OFF = 6 dB/oct, ON = 30 dB/oct.

**"Use it as just an EQ" trick:** You can turn the **drive down** and sculpt sounds with just
the EQ section (low cut / tone / high cut / thump). On a drum stem he works the kick low end
and the beater, then mixes some dry back in.

**The styles (5 hardware emulations):**
- A = Ampex 350 tape machine preamp (popular in the '50s/'60s).
- E = EMI TG channel (an EQ + preamp piece).
- N = Neve 1057.
- T = Thermionic Culture Vulture in **triode mode → even harmonics**.
- P = the same Culture Vulture in **pentode mode → odd harmonics**, a different flavor.
Audition the styles — each affects the tone/quality/flavor; e.g. switch from a starting
style to **E for the EMI** color on drums.

**The delay-return Decapitator trick (very useful):** He puts a Decapitator on a **delay
return/aux** (a simple, clean slapback delay). On a lead line, Decapitator on the dry track
brings clarity with the EQ section; then sending to the delay, switching the Decapitator on
the delay return **sculpts the highs and lows of the delay and pushes the delay forward so it
has its own place in the mix** — without it, the delay gets buried under the guitars and
drums. A clean way to make an effect-return cut through.

He also runs it on a Rhodes software synth to add **dirt and grit, "old, like it's coming
through an old preamp."** Closing point: it reacts to the dynamic range you send it — try it
on vocals, individual drum tracks, even the master track, to bring warmth and clarity.
