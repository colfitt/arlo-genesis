Thomann Bedroom Producers (Sensho) · 5 Great Ways To Use DS-10 Drum Shaper | In The Beat | Sensho | Thomann · 2023-07-06

Today we're talking about one of my favorite plugins, the DS-10 Drum Shaper by XLN Audio. It's super versatile even though it's called "Drum Shaper" — don't let that fool you. I want to look at some less-obvious ideas for how to use it.

This is what the DS-10 looks like when you first open it. At the top there are three tabs for three modes for three different source materials — but you do NOT have to follow this religiously. It's just a little better geared for that type of material via this third special knob. This is one of the things that makes it unique from other transient designers: with a Kick you get a Tightness control, with a Snare you get a Body control, and with the Bus you get a Presence control. But you can use any of these modes for anything depending on what sounds best.

SCENARIO 1 — chopping up a drum break. When you first add it to a drum break, you can hear all that room sound and beautiful imperfections of a live recording (especially an older one). We can make it sound a little drier just by pulling the Sustain down. Once you've got the other instruments / a rapper on top and you like the groove the drummer's already playing, you can dry it up a little so it doesn't have to be so live-sounding.
What I prefer: chop each hit. In Ableton, right-click and "Slice to New MIDI Track" — you get a drum rack. I like to add the Drum Shaper on top of the whole drum rack (or whichever sampler) and dry it up from there, because you get even more control over how dry — or how roomy if you go the other direction.
Then bring Sustain DOWN if you want more attack / wider dynamic range — or push it for a sort of parallel-compression "New York style" feel taken to the extreme. If we RAISE the Sustain up, it increases the gain AFTER the initial hit. Get creative with whatever sound you're after.

CPU: This doesn't require much processing power. In this session I have like 10 instances going and it's barely touching CPU usage — nice not to worry about that.

SCENARIO 2 — non-drum use (a plucky synth). I use it outside of drums most of the time, honestly. Plucks can be harsh and hard to sit in the mix. Keeping it on Bus mode, reduce the Attack a little until it's curbing that harshness. You can bring Sustain up a little so it emphasizes the body of the sound rather than the transient.
The Attack ↔ Mojo (Presence) relationship: I treat these two as almost INVERSE for best results. If you bring Attack DOWN, it's nice to bring Presence UP — because you don't want it too dark, since the transient is usually the brightest part of the sound. Conversely, if you push Attack UP, bring Presence DOWN a little.

REVERB-SEND TRICK: It changes the relationship between the source and the reverb (I'm using DS-10 on a return). If you INCREASE the transients, more of the initial sound is sent to the reverb and it "sparks" more reverb in the background. Increase Attack and the reverb goes harder; the other way softens the send. Another idea: put the Drum Shaper BEFORE the reverb in the return track and increase the Attack — you extract more reverb out of a signal without just turning the send up, and without affecting the dry signal.

SCENARIO 3 — plucky bass. Same technique: accentuate the Attack to complement an existing pluck, or dial it back to soften and make room for vocals/drums. I love this on plucky bass. Turn ON the Soft Clipping so it adds a little nice saturation when you push into the non-linear zone by adding more Attack. Don't forget the other direction — add Sustain so the note rises in volume as it goes by, accentuating the low-end of the chord.

SCENARIO 4 — taming a harsh perc loop. A piercing triangle/perc loop: you could turn it down or low-pass it, but I don't want it darker, just more controlled. Bring the Attack DOWN — it dials back the harshness. And on a busier loop, push Sustain all the way UP and Attack DOWN to emphasize every aspect EXCEPT the transient — pulls textural/room elements already inside the loop forward, adding dimension.

Not an exhaustive list — we discovered a couple of new uses mid-video. Get creative with the relationship between Attack and Sustain on all kinds of material.
