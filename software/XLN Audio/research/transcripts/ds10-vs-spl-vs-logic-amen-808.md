A direct A/B shootout of three transient shapers on two source types relevant to this rig:
- An **Amen break** (the classic sampled drum loop — exactly the "chop a break" material the owner works with on the MPC Sample / Digitakt).
- An **808** (sub/long-decay percussive material).

Three tools compared:
1. **XLN DS-10 Drum Shaper** — the dedicated drum transient shaper with Kick/Snare/Bus modes + Classic/Natural/Smooth algorithms + Mojo.
2. **SPL Transient Designer** — the classic two-knob (Attack/Sustain) hardware-modeled shaper; adds a consistent "character" to most material.
3. **Logic Pro stock Transient Shaper** (an Audio Unit, runs native in Logic) — the free in-box option.

Takeaways (triangulated with the KVR/Gearspace consensus captured in this folder, since captions were unavailable):
- All three do the core job: pull Sustain down on a break to "dry it out" / tighten room, push Attack to recover knock. On a busy Amen break the differences are subtle but audible.
- **DS-10's edge** is the per-source modes and three algorithms — it has more shaping options than SPL's two knobs, plus Mojo frequency-band focus and built-in soft clipping. The community view: DS-10 is the most feature-rich of the three.
- **SPL** imparts its own consistent analog character/color regardless of source — some prefer that "vibe," DS-10's Natural algo is cleaner/more transparent.
- **Logic's stock Transient Shaper** is a legitimate free option and gets you most of the way on simple tightening; DS-10 wins on drum-specific tuning, the Mojo frequency focus, and the Smooth (parallel-comp-like) algorithm.
- On an **808**, transient shaping is about restoring the click/attack vs controlling the boomy sustain tail — DS-10's Kick mode + Mojo "Tightness" is purpose-built for exactly this (boomy ↔ tight).
