The Indie Music Lab · Getting Started With Addictive Drums 2 (Crash-Course) · 2022-08-01

So you've just purchased Addictive Drums 2 — now what? I'll walk you through step by step. (Demo is in Studio One, but you can do this in any DAW — it doesn't matter.) Drag in an instance of AD2.

**Preset browser (top left).** Any packs you have are listed here — click Fairfax Volume 1 and these are all the presets from that drum kit; then Fairfax Volume 2, United Pop, etc. You might have more or fewer packs. Next to it is where you **save any presets you make** — they get added to your library inside AD2.

**Gallery vs Explore.** We're in Gallery mode; hop over to **Explore**. This is where you explore the packs you have — or ones you haven't purchased yet. Even if you're not a drummer and not good yet at creating your own patterns, this gives you a beautiful starting point because each preset has demo grooves. Say I like one — I can **drag it into my session and it automatically brings the MIDI** with it. When I was browsing, it automatically switched the preset to the one listed. Then I can **adjust the tempo** (it's MIDI, so it follows). So I discovered the sound and the beat in the Explore window and dragged it straight into my DAW — boom, there it is. Then duplicate it, edit anything, and build the rest of your song.

**Kit window.** This is where you customize. Say I like a kit but want to switch out the kick — go to the kick slot and there's a list of all the other kicks you have inside the software. Browse until you find one you like. Same for snares, cymbals, rides; you can add extra sounds — claps, bongos, you name it. As you select a different preset from any pack (e.g. the 80s LinnDrum preset), it automatically changes this.

**Edit window.** This is where you do fine-tuning and really customize the sound — EQ, compression, tape saturation, all those. **My suggested workflow: start with a preset, browse until you find a sound close to what you want, then fine-tune on the periphery rather than building from scratch.** It's faster, and usually a more interesting sound because really good drummers/musicians spent hours developing these presets — no shame in using them.

If I want to adjust the kick: there's an **EQ** (make it boxier, less high end), you can adjust the **beater** — there's a beater mic and a front mic, so you blend between them or go right in the middle. You can even add **Noise** here in AD2 — tube noise, DC, muff, vinyl — and adjust its level. And there's **compression**.

Important tip: I don't recommend a ton of adjusting on individual tracks/faders. You get the **most bang for your buck by putting effects on the Master fader** — it affects the entire drum track, and it usually sounds better/more glued to process the whole drum bus together. On the Master you've got **compression, distortion, EQ**, and **Tape/Shape** (one of my favorite features) — add tape, beef up the bottom end, add saturation, and there's a **Cut** (filter) you can automate from your DAW. Plus **Overheads**, **Room** (at the Bus). (I honestly don't pay much attention to the Bus button — it's probably parallel processing — wouldn't be too concerned about it.)

**Send Effects.** Say you want reverb on the snare. You could add it in your DAW, but it can be efficient to keep everything in AD2. In the LA Rock preset, they already have an **Ambience** and a **Hall** reverb on the two effects blocks. Turn the snare's **Send 1** (ambience) and **Send 2** (hall) on — there's the big hall sound. You're also hearing it because the overheads and room are already sending to both effects. Under Effects you can switch ambience → room → hall → plate, adjust the **EQ of just the reverb signal** (useful for a warmer reverb), and adjust overall effect level.

**Panning / width.** You can pan things — hi-hat panned left doesn't move much because most of the hat comes through the overheads, so pan the overheads left and the room right for an interesting stereo image.

**Beats section.** If you want inspiration and good-sounding grooves but aren't confident producing your own yet, go to Beats and browse. Drag one in and it drags the MIDI — a long drum performance you can chop, move to a different section, or focus on one part. Every pack you buy comes with its own preset beats made specifically for that pack (e.g. Real Machines → its 80s LinnDrum beats). Within each beat there are **variants** you can drag — Main, alternatives, claps, etc.

**Map window (MIDI mapping).** Hit the question mark → Map window. This is where you assign which sound each pad/note triggers. They have defaults for popular pads/keyboards (Roland, Yamaha, etc.). I had to do my ATOM pad (Studio One) manually — for each note, drag the desired kit piece (kick, snare open hit, hats, toms, cymbals) onto it, then **Save** the preset. If you're using a larger keyboard you're probably good to go and can just record in.

That's how to get started maximizing Addictive Drums 2.
