# "Reverb FOR a Spitfire orchestra" — how much (if any) you add on top of the wet hall

The companion question to the baked-in thread. Triangulated from snippets.

**Consensus: for a Spitfire-only mix you often need little or NO extra reverb.**
- "You may not need much or **any** additional reverb because having ambient, tree micings
  and outriggers provides enough ambience; adding more doesn't necessarily improve the
  already great sound." Spitfire's whole design goal = "a natural and believable space
  through its micings."
- For an SF-only mix: "**not using additional reverb**"; only when *combining* SF with OTHER
  (dry) libraries does "a nice algorithmic reverb work as a smooth tail over everything" — a
  glue tail, not a space.

**The two named pro approaches (signal in the thread):**
- **Christian Henson** (Spitfire founder, scores almost entirely on Spitfire/AIR samples) —
  "just uses an **algorithmic** reverb."
- **Jake Jackson** (engineer/mixer for real AIR Lyndhurst sessions + Spitfire) — "always uses
  a **convolution** reverb, often **in conjunction with an algorithmic** one."

**The "feather reverb" rule (state-of-the-art Lyndhurst thread):**
- "These samples are **really wet by nature.** You really have to **tame the room** a bit more
  and make use of a **feather reverb** instead of painting over with additional reverb." →
  i.e. add only a whisper of new space, or you turn the hall to mush.
- "**There is no way to match the reverb settings baked into the Spitfire libraries**" — the
  Lyndhurst character is embedded in the samples; you can't dial in a matching IR after.

**Width trick that preserves the close sound:** "Spitfire **hard-pans their close mics**, so
route the close mics to their own output and send them to an instance of reverb — a great way
to get width and depth whilst keeping that close sound."

**Rig takeaway:** SSO does NOT want a big Valhalla space stacked on the front of it (double-
hall mush). For the degrade aesthetic, *replace/recolour* the existing hall rather than add a
second one — see the degrade-on-a-wet-bed capture.
