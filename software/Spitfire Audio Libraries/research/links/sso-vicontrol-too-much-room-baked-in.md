# "Too much room baked in?" — the core wet-Lyndhurst problem (THE key SSO source)

The defining difference between SSO and the dry BBC SO. Triangulated from search snippets
(VI-Control returns 403 to all crawlers).

**The problem (quoted from the thread):**
- "The close micings of Spitfire Orchestra are more direct but yet you can hear the
  **relatively dominant room sphere over the samples and the tails.**" → i.e. even the
  CLOSE mic is wet. You cannot get a dry signal out of SSO the way you can out of BBC SO.
- "The library is **wet out of the box because of the room it's recorded in.** It's not a
  bad thing because it sounds great, but depending on what you are going for, **you are more
  or less stuck with it.**"

**What people actually do about it (the practical menu):**
1. **Use mostly the close mics** to *reduce* (not remove) hall — accepting that close still
   has a noticeable tail. This is the best you get; SSO has no truly dry signal.
2. **Accept and process** — "you can move a lot of different things with enough post
   processing." AI EQs / dynamic EQ + MIDI envelope shaping to dampen the reverb impression,
   "though these have limitations."
3. **Reach for a different library** if you actually want a dry/intimate scoring-stage sound:
   thread names **Spitfire Studio Strings** (Spitfire's *dry* sibling — "more control of the
   room," easier to blend into any soundstage), plus CSS / Sample Modeling / Berlin Winds.

**Takeaway for this rig:** SSO's wet Lyndhurst hall IS the sound — embrace it, don't fight
it. If you need a *dry* orchestra to invent your own space, that's BBC SO (Maida Vale) or
Studio Strings, NOT SSO. SSO wins precisely when you *want* the gorgeous baked hall.
