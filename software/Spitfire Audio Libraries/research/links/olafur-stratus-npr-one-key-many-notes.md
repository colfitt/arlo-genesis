NPR · "One Key, Many Notes: Ólafur Arnalds' Piano Rig Fuses Technology And Musicality" · 2018

The clearest technical explanation of the Stratus self-playing-piano system — the generative philosophy behind the whole Cells/Evolutions world.

## How Stratus works
- "I will play a chord. That chord goes into the software, which manipulates it and then sends it out as rhythmical textures to these two pianos." A single chord triggers "a multitude of unanticipated notes" across two player pianos behind him.
- **Euclidean rhythms** (complex mathematical calculations) + randomness. "It always plays something different. It's never the same twice."
- A **randomness slider** controls whether the pianos emphasize **upper or lower octaves** (octave relocation of the repeated note).
- Deliberately dumb tech: "It's like AI that people who don't really know AI make… There's no real AI here. There's no real learning here. It's just an instrument."

## Why he built it
"I found myself in a rut. Like, I'm composing the same pieces of music over and over again." He wanted instrumental responses he "couldn't anticipate." (Note: this NPR piece does NOT mention the hand/nerve-damage origin story that some other sources cite — flagged as unconfirmed across sources.)

## Generative philosophy
"We're all watching a concert that will never be replicated exactly the same. We're all living in this unique moment in time which will never happen again."

→ This is the conceptual engine behind Cells (the same one-note-becomes-many, key-constrained-randomness idea, packaged as a plugin).
