Spitfire Audio (Paul Thompson) · "Walkthrough: Ólafur Arnalds Chamber Evolutions" · 2018-01-19 (13:17)

The official walkthrough for Chamber Evolutions (NOT confirmed owned — the installed library is Cells; this is general technique for the Evolutions line). Explains the Evo Grid, the chamber section, and Ólafur's two key innovations: "feathering" and "waves."

---

Following on from the successful Symphonic Evolutions library, it was the obvious thing to collaborate with Ólafur on Chamber Evolutions. He worked with his partner in sound, Viktor Orri Árnason (who worked on his previous string evolutions library), to create a tool that lets you easily build sophisticated, dynamic-sounding string arrangements very simply — using just a few sparse notes. It's also a great way to add complexity and depth to existing string arrangements.

**The chamber section.** The library contains the **chamber grid + waves** and the **basses grid + waves**. The chamber group is **4/3/3/3** — violins 1, violins 2, viola, cello — plus **3 basses**, recorded separately so you can have the basses at pitch *or* with the octave below. This matches the existing Spitfire Chamber Strings library exactly, so it locks in incredibly well. Recorded in Hall One, AIR Lyndhurst (the room used for Dunkirk, Beauty and the Beast, Wonder Woman, The Dark Knight, Harry Potter and the Philosopher's Stone).

**The categories.** Evolutions are organized into **subtle**, **thrills**, **episodic**, and **dissonance** — some familiar from previous Evolutions, but dissonance is added here. These are represented by different-colored pegboards on the UI. You can **randomize within different sections or across the whole board**.

**Individual evolutions (advanced folder).** Examples shown:
- #6 "detached waves" — evolves even within one section.
- #10 "episodic trems" — sul tasto, very delicate, individual little trems happening as you move through.
- #13 "sudden molto vib" (in the thrills section) — slightly more extreme, a wide range of possibilities.
- #17 "episodic pitch" — hold an octave and you hear the pitch movement clearly; ranges from gentle beautiful murmurings to "queasy, unsettling" textures.

**How you place them.** You feather them into the Evo Grid as much as you want — or use just one. You could play a part out and then pick one note you want to be slightly edgy, and just stick the peg in the right place for that one note. Or just randomize and use it for inspiration — very quick to write that way. Paul's favorite use: keep a traditional string arrangement, then add elements of evolution underneath to give the arrangement more interest and texture — lots of little things happening in the background that keep the listener engaged without distracting from a picture you're scoring, giving a feeling of momentum and dynamism as the score progresses.

**Innovation 1 — Feathering.** With the same number of players as Chamber Strings, Ólafur designed the sound (with Viktor) so the **players feather in and out of each other**. The advantage: you get a really *clear* sound — you can hear individual players coming and going — but the texture isn't too dense. You still get the feel and weight of the strings. Demonstrated with the most vanilla sound, Evolution 1 (simple sul tasto, no vibrato, long): it's evolving but doesn't sound like a tiny number of players — it still sounds like a full chamber section.

**Innovation 2 — Waves.** A very characteristic sound from Ólafur's scores — "one of my favorite parts, incredibly simple but really effective." There are **four different lengths** (shorter / slightly longer with half-notes / all minim notes / the longest), all also recorded **with trem** and again **with vibrato**. These are pre-recorded crescendo-diminuendo phrases — instant dynamic expression. A really beautiful, intense, close, rich sound.

**Mic positions.** Close (a stereo mic pair) · Decca tree · ambient mics. Use these to tailor the sound and make it your own; with all in, it's beautiful, intense, close and rich.

**Basses.** Seven essential selections; a rich bottom end; also the "southern/sul tasto molto" bed. With these bass extensions you have everything for the bottom end, whether or not you want to go below the cellos. There are also bass waves (shown with close mics).

**Combining.** Load the chamber waves and the bass waves, set both to the same MIDI channel — and they play together. Then set up a quick mix on the chamber grid with all the mics in.
