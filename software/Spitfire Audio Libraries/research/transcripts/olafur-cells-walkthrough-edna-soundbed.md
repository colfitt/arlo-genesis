Soundbed Videos · "Spitfire: Ólafur Arnalds Cells - Walkthrough, eDNA" · 2024-11-26 (29:01)

A hands-on, player's-feel walkthrough of the installed instrument (Ólafur Arnalds Cells). Distinct from Spitfire's own product video — this one focuses on how Cells feels under the fingers and how to actually make use of it, plus a deep dig into the eDNA synth engine inside it.

---

Spitfire Audio has released another unusual and lovely instrument — Ólafur Arnalds Cells. It traces its genesis back to pre-2018. The idea comes across as slightly aleatoric: instructions given to the players based on intervals. It's going to feel slightly unpredictable the first time you play it — or until you get used to it — but it's absolutely *predictable* once you understand it. Once you play a note you'll always get that note, so when you play back you'll always get the same result, and you can edit it in the MIDI so it's always generating a predictable outcome. But it'll be a little surprising unless you've memorized every single pattern, which is going to be very difficult.

It's the kind of thing you really just want to explore and play and play and play — but the real question is *what are you going to make with it*, and how are you going to put it to use.

**The two harmony modes (upper right).** There are two different modes of working with the concept of "the scale":

- **Scale mode** — a set of notes. When they're on, they make sound; when they're off they're silent. If I play a C that's not in the scale/set, nothing sounds. As soon as I add it to the set, I hear it. You can actually use a set scale and turn *on* the notes that are out of scale.
- **Played mode** — hold down some notes, e.g. an F minor chord, and you'll notice some of those notes have a "+2," "+3," etc. on them. "+3" means it'll play the note you're playing *and* the note three semitones above it (the A-flat) — because in a modified C-major tonality, modified to be more like F minor, that's where the harmony sits.

**Velocity drives the intervals.** The amount it goes up is how hard you hit the key. Hit it very gently and it won't change at all (0). Play a little harder and you reach +2 (the G). Harder still, +3 (the A-flat, the minor third). Then the fourth (5 semitones), then a fifth (7 semitones). So the *cell* — what it plays — is determined both by velocity and by the played/set of notes configured up top. It takes a couple of tries to get your velocity sensitivity dialed in where it should be.

**Key center & tonality.** You pick your key center, then pick from the dominant-7 / minor / major-7 / sharp-11 / major-sharp tonal varieties. "Out of scale" off = the note won't play when it's out of scale; turn it on to use out-of-scale notes (good for tension).

**Sound sources.** There are synthesizers; a separate bass; and all the other strings (including a string that sounds like a bass). "Full band" includes the bass. The synths have been run through hardware. All the sounds are generally calm and peaceful — even when you push for tension it's not aggressive; it's like a "blanket of warm fuzzy tension." This is a slow-evolving type of vibe by design — keep that in mind. (You could probably get more aggressive results with the synth basses or by playing synth patches with more transients.)

**Mic positions.** There's a Mix 1 and Mix 2, plus FX positions. The **close condenser mic** is especially special — "the kind of mic I really crave," though it can get a little sharp. Use the **ribbon** mic to roll off those sharp edges. Mix 1 introduces more depth and a sense of being further back in space. The **FX2** position sounded like going through an old echo/delay machine — not delaying so much as filtering, focusing on the mids (a little less high, a little less low). Each new mic position is its own flavor worth auditioning.

**Articulations evolve live.** "Motion" and "Cirrus" are articulations. You can hold down notes, *change articulations*, then introduce a new note — the old ones keep playing and you shift the vibe underneath. (Switched from Motion down to Cirrus mid-hold and a little gliss-up appeared; the whole vibe changed.)

**The basses.** Just bass / contrabass / synth bass. These aren't typical bass recordings — the haunting vibe is amazing on studio speakers (and admittedly won't translate on an iPhone). A solo player situated in the center of the recording space.

**The eDNA engine.** To access eDNA you go up to "synth." If you see things filtered out it's confusing — you need both "standard synth" *and* "synth" selected to enable all the options below; with only one selected, some controls grey out. eDNA gives you an A and a B section, and you select from individual samples for each. (Reviewer note: "I produced a whole album of eDNA things a few years ago from Spitfire Tundra — eDNA Earth Vol 5 — and it was one of my favorite periods, just writing and messing around with sounds. eDNA is so fun.") Cells' eDNA doesn't have *quite* the variety Tundra had — many sounds here are similar-sounding — but there's a *ton* to pick from. You pick, e.g., "short sub" for A and "ratchet" for B, then automate movement back and forth between them: filters, pitches, volumes, high-passes, envelopes. There's a **gate sequencer** — turn it on for different rhythmic patterns. (Heard occasional clicks on some presets; source unknown.)

The **synth cells** break everything out individually and have a bunch of combis; lots of one-shots in the synths, plus a DI. Lots of individual synth cells, plus a lot of the same ideas as the strings.

**Overall character.** It feels endless. It all has that gentle, smooth, usually-warm, embracing tone — even the tension is warm. The aesthetic is very clear but very *broad* within itself: it can do happy, wonder, amazement. The learning curve is the velocity + key-tonality setup, but it's well put together and not that hard. The eDNA is amazing.
