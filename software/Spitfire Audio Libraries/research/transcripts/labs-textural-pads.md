## What the pack is
Textural Pads is one of the free LABS packs. The reviewer's general take on LABS: he really likes them, they're free, and crucially they have a "quirky nature" — you generally do NOT get a generic piano or generic trumpet; there's always some weird twist on the theme, which is what makes them inspiring. (He contrasts with the prior two LABS releases being more pluck-oriented: a Mandolin, and Monochord — a unique instrument from a theatre production, which he'd already used in a composition.)

His verdict on Textural Pads: eight pad patches, "fabulous," "really really nice," really usable, and with their own character and quirk. His pitch: if you skipped LABS before because you thought it was "a bit out there / a bit weird," these pads are nice AND usable.

## How it was made (read from the Spitfire page)
Inspired by sci-fi soundtracks. Over lockdown, Spitfire developer **Mark Sumrall** sampled a selection of instruments at his home studio:
- **bowed and scratched bass guitar**
- **kalimba**
- **cello**
- **vocals**

He combined these with synths from developer **Harnek Mudhar**, then used **bespoke granular effects** to transform the recordings into "otherworldly textures." They are genuinely otherworldly.

## The 8 patch names
Cyclosa, Deneb, Osny, Petrichor ("the smell after a rain shower"), Suddadi, and others (the names are obscure/invented — "I don't know what languages these are coming from"). The rest of the video is the patches played as a long evolving demo.

## Takeaway for this rig
A free, granular-textured, sci-fi-flavoured pad set built from acoustic sources (bowed bass, kalimba, cello, vocals) mangled with granular FX — exactly the kind of "real instrument, processed wrong" wall material to layer under a banjo/baritone. The quirk IS the point.
