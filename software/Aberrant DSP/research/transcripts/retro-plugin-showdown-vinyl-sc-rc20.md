"Retro Plugin Showdown: Vinyl vs. Sketch Cassette vs. RC-20" · (producer channel, beats-focused)

Head-to-head of iZotope Vinyl (free) vs SketchCassette II vs XLN RC-20 on a piano melody. Distilled (auto-sub, cleaned).

## On SketchCassette II
- Positioned as THE go-to if you're making lo-fi: "if you're looking to make lo-fi music this is probably the go-to plugin for you." Focus = tape (vs Vinyl's vinyl focus).
- New-vs-old: the big bottom knob = how worn vs new the tape sounds (Age).
- Tempo sync called out as a SketchCassette II addition over v1 — "all the wobbles are in sync with your tempo."
- Even as a plain tape-saturation plugin it "works really well" — set to reset/default and just add Saturation as you go.
- Experimental presets = "enough to make you dizzy" (heavy wow/flutter).
- He keeps it because "you really can't capture the same sound of tape distortion that you get with Sketch Cassette" elsewhere. Tape distortion character is its niche.

## On RC-20 (the rival the user owns)
- Most expensive (~$100), "the almighty," gained notoriety in the HIP-HOP SAMPLE community.
- Has things SketchCassette/Vinyl DON'T: a **built-in reverb (Space knob)** and a **Magnitude knob = global wet/dry mix** for all its effects at once.
- He uses Vinyl 2 preset a lot, tends to remove crackle.

## On iZotope Vinyl (free, for reference)
- Decade selector (2000s→30s), 1960 = his sweet spot. Dust knob removes crackle. Has a Lo-Fi setting (oversaturated artifacts) + Warp/Wear = warble. DJ spin-down can be automated.

## The "run all three" verdict
- Stacking Vinyl + SketchCassette + RC-20 together = "a little bit of overkill but not bad." He actually uses all three for DIFFERENT jobs, not stacked. Implication for the rig: pick ONE degrade tool per source; SketchCassette = tape distortion/wow-flutter, RC-20 = when you want its reverb/global-mix/cartoon destruction.
